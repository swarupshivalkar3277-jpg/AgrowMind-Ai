# Irrigation Scheduling in Agriculture

# Overview

Irrigation scheduling is the process of deciding **when to irrigate**, **how much water to apply**, and **how frequently irrigation should be given** to crops. Proper irrigation scheduling helps farmers provide water according to crop needs, soil moisture status, weather conditions, and crop growth stage.

Efficient irrigation scheduling helps:

* Save water
* Improve crop yield
* Reduce water stress
* Prevent over-irrigation
* Improve nutrient uptake
* Reduce disease incidence
* Improve farm profitability

Irrigation scheduling is a key part of modern, climate-smart, and water-efficient agriculture.

---

# What is Irrigation Scheduling?

Irrigation scheduling means planning irrigation based on crop water requirement rather than irrigating randomly or only by habit.

It answers three important questions:

1. When should irrigation be applied?
2. How much water should be applied?
3. How often should irrigation be repeated?

A good irrigation schedule balances crop water demand with available soil moisture.

---

# Importance of Irrigation Scheduling

Proper irrigation scheduling is important because both water shortage and excess water can harm crops.

## Benefits

* Maintains optimum soil moisture
* Improves root growth
* Enhances fertilizer efficiency
* Reduces water wastage
* Prevents nutrient leaching
* Reduces waterlogging
* Improves crop quality
* Supports sustainable water use

---

# Problems Caused by Poor Irrigation Scheduling

## Under-Irrigation

Under-irrigation occurs when crops do not receive enough water.

Effects include:

* Wilting
* Stunted growth
* Poor flowering
* Flower drop
* Reduced fruit setting
* Low yield
* Poor produce quality

---

## Over-Irrigation

Over-irrigation occurs when too much water is applied.

Effects include:

* Waterlogging
* Root suffocation
* Nutrient leaching
* Root rot
* Increased disease incidence
* Wastage of water and energy

# Crop Growth Stage

Water requirement changes during different crop growth stages.

Critical stages need special attention because water stress during these periods can severely reduce yield.

---

# Soil Type

Soil texture affects water-holding capacity.

## Sandy Soils

Characteristics:

* Low water-holding capacity
* Fast drainage
* Require frequent light irrigation

---

## Loamy Soils

Characteristics:

* Good water-holding capacity
* Good drainage
* Ideal for many crops

---

## Clay Soils

Characteristics:

* High water-holding capacity
* Slow drainage
* Require less frequent irrigation

Over-irrigation should be avoided in clay soils.

---

# Weather Conditions

Weather strongly affects irrigation needs.

## Hot and Dry Weather

* Increases evaporation
* Increases crop water demand
* Requires more frequent irrigation

---

## Cool and Humid Weather

* Reduces evaporation
* Reduces irrigation need

---

## Rainy Weather

* Irrigation may be reduced or stopped
* Drainage should be monitored

---

# Root Depth

Crops with deeper roots can access water from deeper soil layers.

## Shallow-Rooted Crops

Examples:

* Onion
* Leafy vegetables
* Potato

Require frequent irrigation.

---

## Deep-Rooted Crops

Examples:

* Cotton
* Pigeon pea
* Fruit trees

Can tolerate longer intervals between irrigation.

---

# Irrigation Method

The irrigation method affects scheduling.

## Flood Irrigation

* Longer irrigation interval
* Higher water use
* Higher risk of waterlogging

---

## Furrow Irrigation

* Suitable for row crops
* Moderate water efficiency

---

## Sprinkler Irrigation

* Uniform water distribution
* Suitable for many field crops

---

## Drip Irrigation

* Frequent and precise irrigation
* High water-use efficiency
* Best for vegetables, orchards, and high-value crops

# Crop Water Requirement

Crop water requirement is the total amount of water needed by a crop for normal growth, development, and yield.

It includes water used for:

* Transpiration
* Evaporation
* Plant growth
* Nutrient movement

Water requirement varies by crop, season, soil, and climate.

# Methods of Irrigation Scheduling

---

# 1. Calendar-Based Scheduling

In this method, irrigation is applied at fixed intervals such as every 3 days, 7 days, or 10 days.

## Advantages

* Simple and easy to follow
* Useful for small farmers
* Requires no instruments

## Limitations

* Does not consider rainfall
* Does not consider soil moisture
* May cause over-irrigation or under-irrigation

---

# 2. Soil Moisture-Based Scheduling

Irrigation is applied based on soil moisture condition.

## Methods

* Feel and appearance method
* Soil moisture sensors
* Tensiometers
* Gravimetric soil sampling

This method is more accurate than fixed calendar scheduling.

---

# 3. Crop Stage-Based Scheduling

Irrigation is planned according to crop growth stages.

More water is provided during critical stages and less water during non-critical stages.

## Benefits

* Better yield protection
* Efficient use of limited water
* Useful during drought periods

---

# 4. Weather-Based Scheduling

This method uses weather data to estimate crop water demand.

Factors considered:

* Temperature
* Humidity
* Wind speed
* Solar radiation
* Rainfall

This method is useful for precision farming.

---

# 5. Evapotranspiration-Based Scheduling

Evapotranspiration is the combined water loss through soil evaporation and plant transpiration.

Irrigation is scheduled based on crop evapotranspiration.

## Benefits

* Scientific method
* Accurate water estimation
* Useful for large farms and advanced irrigation systems

---

# 6. Sensor-Based Scheduling

Modern farms may use sensors to monitor soil moisture and automate irrigation.

Common tools:

* Soil moisture sensors
* Tensiometers
* Weather stations
* IoT irrigation controllers

## Benefits

* Precise irrigation
* Water saving
* Reduced labor
* Better crop performance

---

# Soil Moisture Monitoring

Soil moisture monitoring helps determine when irrigation is needed.

---

# Feel and Appearance Method

Farmers check soil by hand.

## Dry Soil

* Loose and powdery
* Does not form a ball
* Irrigation needed

## Moist Soil

* Forms a weak ball
* Slightly cool to touch
* Irrigation may not be needed immediately

## Wet Soil

* Sticky
* Forms a firm ball
* Irrigation should be avoided

---

# Soil Moisture Sensors

Sensors measure moisture levels in the root zone.

## Benefits

* Accurate data
* Prevents unnecessary irrigation
* Useful for drip irrigation systems

---

# Tensiometers

Tensiometers measure how strongly water is held in soil.

They help determine when crops are experiencing water stress.

---

# Irrigation Depth

Irrigation depth refers to the amount of water applied during one irrigation event.

It depends on:

* Root depth
* Soil water-holding capacity
* Crop stage
* Irrigation method

Deep irrigation is not always better. Water should remain within the active root zone.

---

# Irrigation Frequency

Irrigation frequency means how often water is applied.

## Frequent Irrigation Needed

* Sandy soils
* Shallow-rooted crops
* Hot weather
* Drip irrigation systems

---

## Less Frequent Irrigation Needed

* Clay soils
* Deep-rooted crops
* Cool weather
* Mulched fields

# Irrigation Scheduling by Season

---

# Summer Season

During summer:

* Crop water demand is high
* Evaporation is high
* Irrigation frequency should increase

Recommended:

* Irrigate early morning or evening
* Use mulching
* Avoid water stress

---

# Monsoon Season

During monsoon:

* Irrigation need decreases
* Drainage becomes important
* Avoid irrigation during heavy rainfall periods

Recommended:

* Monitor soil moisture
* Prevent waterlogging
* Maintain drainage channels

---

# Winter Season

During winter:

* Evaporation is lower
* Irrigation frequency decreases
* Over-irrigation should be avoided

Recommended:

* Irrigate during warmer parts of the day
* Avoid waterlogging
* Protect sensitive crops from frost stress

---

# Irrigation Timing During the Day

## Best Time

* Early morning
* Late evening

## Avoid

* Hot afternoon irrigation
* Irrigation during strong winds
* Irrigation during heavy rainfall

Early morning and evening irrigation reduce evaporation losses.

---

# Irrigation Scheduling for Drip Irrigation

Drip irrigation usually requires frequent and controlled irrigation.

## Recommended Approach

* Apply water daily or every few days depending on crop and soil
* Monitor root-zone moisture
* Adjust schedule according to weather
* Combine with fertigation when needed

## Benefits

* Saves water
* Maintains uniform moisture
* Improves nutrient efficiency

---

# Irrigation Scheduling for Sprinkler Irrigation

Sprinkler irrigation should be scheduled carefully to avoid water loss and disease risk.

## Recommended Approach

* Avoid irrigation during windy conditions
* Avoid frequent leaf wetness in disease-prone crops
* Irrigate during cooler hours

---

# Irrigation Scheduling for Flood Irrigation

Flood irrigation requires careful control to avoid waterlogging.

## Recommended Approach

* Apply only required water
* Avoid prolonged standing water
* Maintain field leveling
* Ensure proper drainage

---

# Irrigation Scheduling During Drought

During drought, water should be used strategically.

## Prioritize

1. High-value crops
2. Flowering crops
3. Fruiting crops
4. Seed production crops
5. Young orchards

## Recommended Practices

* Apply life-saving irrigation
* Use mulching
* Reduce water loss
* Avoid excess nitrogen application
* Use drip irrigation where possible

---

# Irrigation Scheduling During Rainy Periods

During rainy periods:

* Stop irrigation if soil is already moist
* Remove standing water
* Monitor root diseases
* Resume irrigation only after soil moisture declines

---

# Irrigation Scheduling and Fertilizer Management

Water and nutrients are closely related.

Proper irrigation improves:

* Nutrient availability
* Fertilizer uptake
* Root activity

Over-irrigation can cause:

* Nutrient leaching
* Fertilizer wastage
* Poor crop response

Fertigation should be scheduled according to crop growth stage.

---

# Irrigation Scheduling and Disease Management

Improper irrigation can increase disease risk.

## Excess Moisture Encourages

* Root rot
* Damping-off
* Bacterial wilt
* Leaf spot diseases

## Preventive Measures

* Avoid over-irrigation
* Use drip irrigation
* Improve drainage
* Avoid prolonged leaf wetness

---

# Irrigation Scheduling and Mulching

Mulching reduces irrigation frequency by conserving soil moisture.

Benefits include:

* Reduced evaporation
* Lower soil temperature
* Weed control
* Better moisture stability

Drip irrigation plus mulching gives excellent water-use efficiency.

# Common Mistakes in Irrigation Scheduling

Avoid:

* Irrigating without checking soil moisture
* Applying the same schedule in all seasons
* Over-irrigating clay soils
* Under-irrigating sandy soils
* Ignoring crop growth stages
* Irrigating during hot afternoons
* Continuing irrigation after rainfall
* Ignoring drainage problems

---

# Farmer Advisory Guidelines

## If Soil is Dry

* Irrigate based on crop stage and soil type.
* Use light irrigation for sandy soil.
* Apply deeper irrigation for deeper-rooted crops.

---

## If Soil is Moist

* Delay irrigation.
* Recheck after 1–2 days.
* Avoid unnecessary watering.

---

## If Soil is Wet or Waterlogged

* Stop irrigation.
* Improve drainage.
* Monitor root diseases.

---

## If Crop is Flowering

* Avoid water stress.
* Maintain uniform moisture.

---

## If Crop is Near Harvest

* Reduce excess irrigation where crop quality may be affected.
* Avoid waterlogging.
