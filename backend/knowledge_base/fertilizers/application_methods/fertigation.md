# Fertigation

## Overview

Fertigation is a fertilizer application method in which water-soluble fertilizers are applied through an irrigation system. It is most commonly used with drip irrigation, but it may also be used with sprinkler, micro-sprinkler, and other pressurized irrigation systems where suitable.

Fertigation allows farmers to apply nutrients in small, frequent, and controlled doses. It improves nutrient-use efficiency, reduces fertilizer wastage, saves labour, and supplies nutrients near the crop root zone.

Fertigation is especially useful for high-value crops, vegetables, fruit crops, orchards, protected cultivation, nurseries, floriculture, and crops grown under drip irrigation.

---

## 1. What Is Fertigation?

Fertigation is the combined application of fertilizer and irrigation water. The fertilizer is dissolved in water and injected into the irrigation system so that nutrients reach the crop root zone along with irrigation.

### Simple Definition

Fertigation means feeding the crop through irrigation water.

### Technical Definition

Fertigation is the application of soluble plant nutrients through an irrigation system using a fertilizer tank, venturi injector, dosing pump, or automated fertigation unit.

### Main Purpose

The main purpose of fertigation is to supply nutrients at the right time, in the right quantity, and near the active root zone of the crop.

### Farmer Guidance

Fertigation works best when the irrigation system is properly designed, filters are clean, fertilizers are fully soluble, and the crop nutrient schedule is based on soil test, water test, crop stage, and yield target.

---

## 2. Why Fertigation Is Important

Fertigation improves fertilizer efficiency because nutrients are delivered directly with water to the root zone. This reduces nutrient loss and improves nutrient availability.

### Benefits

* Improves fertilizer-use efficiency.
* Improves water-use efficiency.
* Reduces nutrient leaching.
* Reduces fertilizer wastage.
* Saves labour.
* Allows split application of nutrients.
* Supports crop-stage-wise nutrition.
* Improves crop growth and yield.
* Improves fruit size and quality.
* Reduces nutrient stress.
* Useful for precision farming.
* Suitable for drip irrigation and protected cultivation.
* Helps apply nutrients uniformly across the field when the system is well designed.

### Limitations

* Requires pressurized irrigation system.
* Requires water-soluble fertilizers.
* Requires filtration.
* Requires correct injector setup.
* Poor-quality water can cause clogging.
* Fertilizer incompatibility can cause precipitation.
* Initial system cost may be high.
* Incorrect scheduling can cause nutrient deficiency or excess.
* Uneven irrigation causes uneven nutrient distribution.
* Needs technical knowledge and regular maintenance.

---

## 3. Fertigation vs Traditional Fertilizer Application

| Point                 | Fertigation                              | Traditional Soil Application              |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Fertilizer placement  | Through irrigation water near root zone  | Broadcast, band, ring, or basal placement |
| Application frequency | Small and frequent doses                 | Larger doses at fewer times               |
| Labour requirement    | Lower after system setup                 | Higher                                    |
| Nutrient efficiency   | Usually higher                           | Can be lower due to losses                |
| Water requirement     | Combined with irrigation                 | Separate irrigation often needed          |
| Suitability           | Drip, sprinkler, micro-irrigation        | All fields                                |
| Risk                  | Clogging, wrong mixing, uneven injection | Runoff, leaching, uneven spreading        |
| Best for              | High-value and drip-irrigated crops      | Broad field crops and low-input systems   |

## 5. Fertilizer Injectors Used in Fertigation

Fertilizer injectors add fertilizer solution into the irrigation line.

## 5.1 Fertilizer Tank

A fertilizer tank is a pressure-resistant tank connected to the irrigation line. Water passes through the tank, dissolves fertilizer, and carries nutrients into the system.

### Advantages

* Simple design.
* Lower cost.
* Easy to operate.
* Suitable for small farms.

### Limitations

* Injection rate may not remain constant.
* Fertilizer concentration may be high at first and low later.
* Less precise than dosing pumps.
* Requires careful cleaning.

### Farmer Guidance

Fertilizer tanks are useful for simple fertigation, but uniformity may be lower than with dosing pumps.

---

## 5.2 Venturi Injector

A venturi injector uses water pressure difference to suck fertilizer solution from a tank into the irrigation line.

### Advantages

* No electricity required in many setups.
* Low cost.
* Easy to install.
* Suitable for small and medium farms.
* Common in drip irrigation systems.

### Limitations

* Requires pressure difference.
* Injection rate depends on pressure and flow.
* May reduce system pressure.
* Needs correct bypass arrangement.
* Less accurate than automated dosing pumps.

### Farmer Guidance

If a venturi injector is not sucking fertilizer, check pressure difference, blocked suction pipe, air leakage, clogged filter, or incorrect valve position.

---

## 5.3 Fertilizer Dosing Pump

A dosing pump injects fertilizer solution at a controlled rate into the irrigation system.

### Types

* Electric dosing pump.
* Hydraulic dosing pump.
* Piston dosing pump.
* Diaphragm dosing pump.
* Proportional injector.

### Advantages

* More accurate nutrient application.
* Better control of injection rate.
* Suitable for large farms.
* Useful for automation.
* Suitable for fertigation schedules.

### Limitations

* Higher cost.
* Needs maintenance.
* May require electricity or hydraulic pressure.
* Needs calibration.

### Farmer Guidance

Dosing pumps are preferred for precision fertigation, greenhouse farming, hydroponics, orchards, and high-value crops.

---

## 5.4 Automated Fertigation Unit

An automated fertigation unit controls fertilizer injection based on time, flow rate, EC, pH, irrigation schedule, or crop stage.

### Features

* Multiple fertilizer tanks.
* EC monitoring.
* pH monitoring.
* Automatic injection.
* Irrigation scheduling.
* Sensor-based control.
* Remote monitoring in advanced systems.

### Advantages

* High precision.
* Saves labour.
* Reduces human error.
* Useful for greenhouse and commercial farms.
* Allows crop-stage-based nutrient programs.

### Limitations

* High initial cost.
* Requires technical support.
* Sensors need calibration.
* System failure can affect crop quickly.

# Fertilizers Used in Fertigation

## 6. Characteristics of Good Fertigation Fertilizers

Fertilizers used in fertigation should dissolve completely in water and should not clog drippers.

### Good Fertigation Fertilizer Qualities

* Fully water-soluble.
* Low impurity content.
* Compatible with irrigation water.
* Compatible with other fertilizers in the tank.
* Low risk of precipitation.
* Suitable pH reaction.
* Suitable nutrient concentration.
* Crop-safe at recommended concentration.
* Low chloride and sodium where sensitive crops are grown.
* Free from sand, dust, and insoluble particles.

### Farmer Guidance

Never use ordinary granular fertilizers in fertigation unless they are specifically suitable and fully soluble. Insoluble material can clog filters, pipes, and emitters.

## 9. Fertilizer Compatibility

Fertilizer compatibility means whether fertilizers can be safely mixed together without forming precipitates, sludge, heat, gas, or insoluble particles.

### Incompatible Mixtures

* Calcium nitrate with phosphate fertilizers.
* Calcium nitrate with sulfate fertilizers.
* Calcium fertilizers with phosphoric acid without proper dilution and technical guidance.
* Magnesium sulfate with calcium fertilizers.
* Hard water with phosphate fertilizers.
* Fertilizers mixed with pesticides without compatibility check.

### Possible Problems

* Dripper clogging.
* White precipitate formation.
* Tank sludge.
* Filter blockage.
* Uneven nutrient application.
* Nutrient loss.
* Crop deficiency.
* System damage.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not mix calcium nitrate with phosphate or sulfate fertilizers in the same stock tank. Use separate tanks or apply on different fertigation days.

---

## 10. Jar Test for Fertilizer Mixing

A jar test helps check fertilizer compatibility before adding fertilizer to the main fertigation tank. Extension guidance commonly recommends testing fertilizer and irrigation-water compatibility before adding materials to the supply tank.

### Jar Test Steps

1. Take a clean transparent jar.
2. Fill it with the same irrigation water used on the farm.
3. Add small amounts of the fertilizers in the planned mixing order.
4. Shake or stir well.
5. Keep the jar undisturbed for several hours or overnight.
6. Observe for cloudiness, sediment, heat, gas, crystals, or sludge.
7. If precipitation appears, do not use that mixture in fertigation.

### Farmer Guidance

If the jar test forms sediment, the same problem can happen inside drip lines and drippers.

Water quality affects fertilizer solubility, nutrient availability, crop health, and dripper clogging.

### Important Water Parameters

* pH.
* Electrical conductivity, also called EC.
* Bicarbonates.
* Carbonates.
* Calcium.
* Magnesium.
* Sodium.
* Chloride.
* Iron.
* Manganese.
* Suspended solids.
* Algae.
* Biological contamination.
* Total dissolved salts.

### Farmer Guidance

Water testing is important before starting fertigation, especially for drip-irrigated orchards, vegetables, greenhouses, and high-value crops.

---

## 12. Water pH

Water pH affects fertilizer solubility and nutrient availability.

### General Guidance

* Very high pH can reduce availability of phosphorus, iron, zinc, and manganese.
* Very low pH can increase corrosion risk.
* High bicarbonates can cause precipitation and clogging.
* pH correction may be needed in advanced fertigation systems.

### Farmer Guidance

If water is hard or alkaline, avoid mixing phosphate and calcium fertilizers without expert advice.

---

## 13. Electrical Conductivity

Electrical conductivity, or EC, indicates the salt concentration in water or fertigation solution.

### High EC Risk

* Salt stress.
* Leaf burn.
* Poor germination.
* Reduced water uptake.
* Root injury.
* Lower yield.
* Fruit-quality issues in sensitive crops.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not apply highly concentrated fertilizer solution to young seedlings or salt-sensitive crops. Monitor EC in protected cultivation and hydroponic systems.

---

## 14. Filtration

Filtration is essential in fertigation because fertilizer particles, algae, sand, and precipitates can clog emitters.

### Filter Types

* Screen filter.
* Disc filter.
* Sand media filter.
* Hydrocyclone filter.

### Maintenance

* Clean filters regularly.
* Flush mainlines and laterals.
* Check pressure difference across filters.
* Remove sediment from tanks.
* Avoid undissolved fertilizer particles.
* Use clean fertilizer stock solution.

### Farmer Guidance

If drippers are clogging frequently, check fertilizer solubility, water quality, filter cleaning, algae growth, and line flushing.

# Fertigation Scheduling

## 15. What Is Fertigation Scheduling?

Fertigation scheduling means planning how much fertilizer to apply, when to apply it, and how often to apply it through irrigation.

### Scheduling Depends On

* Crop type.
* Crop growth stage.
* Soil test.
* Water test.
* Fertilizer recommendation.
* Expected yield.
* Root depth.
* Irrigation frequency.
* Weather.
* Soil type.
* Crop nutrient demand.
* Fertilizer solubility.
* System uniformity.

### Farmer Guidance

Fertigation should be crop-stage-wise. Young plants need different nutrients than flowering or fruiting plants.

---

## 16. Split Application

Split application means applying total fertilizer requirement in several small doses instead of one large dose.

### Benefits

* Reduces nutrient loss.
* Improves nutrient uptake.
* Reduces salt stress.
* Reduces leaching.
* Matches crop demand.
* Improves nutrient-use efficiency.
* Allows correction during crop growth.

### Farmer Guidance

Fertigation is best used as small and frequent feeding, not as a single heavy fertilizer dose.

---

## 17. Fertigation Timing During Irrigation

A common fertigation sequence has three parts.

### Step 1: Pre-Irrigation

Run clean water first to wet the root zone and stabilize system pressure.

### Step 2: Fertilizer Injection

Inject fertilizer solution during the middle part of irrigation.

### Step 3: Flushing

Run clean water after fertigation to push nutrients out of pipes and into the root zone and to clean the irrigation system.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not inject fertilizer at the very end of irrigation without flushing. Fertilizer left in pipes can clog drippers or cause uneven next irrigation.

---

## 18. Fertigation Frequency

Fertigation frequency depends on crop, soil, irrigation system, and nutrient demand.

### Common Frequency Patterns

* Daily fertigation in greenhouses and hydroponic systems.
* 2 to 3 times per week in vegetables under drip.
* Weekly fertigation in some orchards.
* Stage-based fertigation in field crops.
* More frequent fertigation in sandy soils.
* Less frequent fertigation in heavy clay soils.

### Farmer Guidance

Sandy soils need smaller and more frequent nutrient applications because nutrients can leach quickly. Heavy soils may need slower irrigation and careful water management.

# Fertigation Calculation Basics

## 19. Basic Fertigation Calculation

The amount of fertilizer required depends on nutrient requirement and fertilizer nutrient percentage.

### Formula

Fertilizer required = Required nutrient amount ÷ Nutrient fraction in fertilizer

### Example

If crop needs 10 kg nitrogen and urea contains 46% nitrogen:

Fertilizer required = 10 ÷ 0.46 = 21.7 kg urea

### Farmer Guidance

Always calculate based on nutrient content, not only fertilizer bag weight.

---

## 20. NPK Fertilizer Calculation Example

If a farmer applies 10 kg of 19:19:19 fertilizer:

* Nitrogen supplied = 10 × 19 ÷ 100 = 1.9 kg N
* Phosphorus as P2O5 supplied = 10 × 19 ÷ 100 = 1.9 kg P2O5
* Potassium as K2O supplied = 10 × 19 ÷ 100 = 1.9 kg K2O

### Farmer Guidance

NPK grades show nutrient percentage by weight. A 19:19:19 fertilizer contains 19% nitrogen, 19% P2O5, and 19% K2O.

---

## 21. Injection Rate

Injection rate is the speed at which fertilizer solution enters the irrigation system.

### Factors Affecting Injection Rate

* Injector capacity.
* System pressure.
* Flow rate.
* Fertilizer concentration.
* Irrigation duration.
* Tank volume.
* Target nutrient dose.

### Farmer Guidance

If fertilizer injection is too fast, nutrients may not distribute evenly. If too slow, fertilizer may not finish before irrigation ends.

---

## 22. Fertigation Uniformity

Fertigation uniformity means all plants receive similar amounts of water and nutrients.

### Causes of Poor Uniformity

* Clogged emitters.
* Unequal pressure.
* Poor irrigation design.
* Long laterals.
* Damaged pipes.
* Blocked filters.
* Air locks.
* Wrong injector position.
* Uneven field slope.
* Incorrect fertigation duration.

### Farmer Guidance

If some plants are dark green and others are yellow, check irrigation uniformity, dripper clogging, pressure, and fertilizer distribution.

# Nutrient Management Through Fertigation

## 27. Nitrogen Fertigation

Nitrogen is commonly applied through fertigation because it is mobile in soil and needed throughout crop growth.

### Benefits

* Supports vegetative growth.
* Can be split into multiple doses.
* Reduces leaching compared with heavy basal application.
* Allows correction based on crop colour and growth.

### Excess Nitrogen Symptoms

* Excessive vegetative growth.
* Delayed flowering.
* Soft plants.
* Higher pest and disease susceptibility.
* Poor fruit colour.
* Poor storage quality.
* Lodging in some crops.

### Deficiency Symptoms

* Yellowing of older leaves.
* Slow growth.
* Reduced tillering or branching.
* Small leaves.
* Low yield.

---

## 28. Phosphorus Fertigation

Phosphorus supports root development, flowering, and energy transfer in plants.

### Important Notes

* Phosphorus may react with calcium and hard water.
* Phosphorus movement in soil is limited.
* Phosphorus fertigation requires soluble fertilizers.
* Avoid mixing phosphates with calcium in the same tank.

### Deficiency Symptoms

* Poor root growth.
* Stunted plants.
* Purplish leaf colour in some crops.
* Delayed maturity.
* Poor flowering.

---

## 29. Potassium Fertigation

Potassium is important for fruit quality, water regulation, disease tolerance, sugar movement, and crop strength.

### Benefits

* Improves fruit size.
* Improves fruit colour.
* Improves sweetness in many fruit crops.
* Improves shelf life.
* Improves stress tolerance.
* Improves grain filling and tuber development.

### Deficiency Symptoms

* Leaf-edge scorching.
* Weak stems.
* Poor fruit quality.
* Small fruits.
* Low sugar content.
* Poor stress tolerance.

---

## 30. Calcium Fertigation

Calcium is important for cell-wall strength, fruit firmness, root growth, and quality.

### Benefits

* Improves fruit firmness.
* Reduces some physiological disorders.
* Supports root growth.
* Improves shelf life.

### Important Notes

* Calcium moves slowly inside plants.
* Calcium nitrate is commonly used in fertigation.
* Do not mix calcium nitrate with phosphate or sulfate fertilizers in one stock tank.
* Calcium supply should be regular during fruit development.

### Calcium-Related Disorders

* Blossom-end rot in tomato and capsicum.
* Tip burn in leafy vegetables.
* Poor fruit firmness.
* Cracking in some fruits.
* Internal disorders in some crops.

---

## 31. Magnesium Fertigation

Magnesium is the central element in chlorophyll and is important for photosynthesis.

### Sources

* Magnesium sulfate.
* Magnesium nitrate.
* Water-soluble magnesium formulations.

### Deficiency Symptoms

* Yellowing between veins on older leaves.
* Reduced photosynthesis.
* Poor crop growth.
* Premature leaf drop in some crops.

---

## 32. Micronutrient Fertigation

Micronutrients are needed in small amounts but are essential for crop growth.

### Important Micronutrients

* Zinc.

# Fertigation Safety and Maintenance

## 33. Backflow Prevention

Backflow prevention stops fertilizer solution from flowing backward into the water source.

### Why It Is Important

* Protects wells.
* Protects ponds.
* Protects drinking water.
* Prevents fertilizer contamination.
* Improves farm safety.

### Devices

* Non-return valve.
* Check valve.
* Vacuum breaker.
* Air gap.
* Backflow prevention assembly.

### Farmer Guidance

Never run fertigation without backflow prevention, especially when using a well, borewell, pond, or shared irrigation source.

---

## 34. System Flushing

Flushing removes fertilizer residues, sediment, algae, and precipitates from irrigation lines.

### When to Flush

* After fertigation.
* After using phosphate fertilizer.
* After using calcium fertilizer.
* When emitters show clogging.
* After repairs.
* Periodically during the season.
* Before closing the system for a long period.

### Farmer Guidance

Open end caps or flush valves and run clean water until discharge is clear.

---

## 35. Emitter Clogging

Emitter clogging is a common fertigation problem.

### Causes

* Insoluble fertilizer.
* Fertilizer precipitation.
* Hard water.
* Sand.
* Silt.
* Algae.
* Iron bacteria.
* Organic matter.
* Poor filtration.
* Fertilizer incompatibility.
* Not flushing after fertigation.

### Symptoms

* Some plants remain dry.
* Uneven crop growth.
* Uneven leaf colour.
* Low discharge from drippers.
* Higher pressure before blocked lines.
* Fertilizer remaining in tank.

### Farmer Guidance

If clogging occurs, check filters, fertilizer solubility, water quality, emitter discharge, line flushing, and chemical precipitation.

---

## 36. Fertigation Troubleshooting

### Problem: Fertilizer Not Injecting

Possible causes:

* No pressure difference.
* Venturi installed incorrectly.
* Suction pipe blocked.
* Air leakage in suction line.
* Fertilizer tank valve closed.
* Injector too small or too large.
* Filter clogged.
* Pump pressure too low.

### Problem: Uneven Crop Growth

Possible causes:

* Poor irrigation uniformity.
* Clogged emitters.
* Uneven pressure.
* Fertilizer injected too late.
* No flushing.
* Wrong lateral length.
* Different soil moisture zones.
* Nutrient deficiency.

### Problem: White Deposit in Tank or Filter

Possible causes:

* Calcium mixed with phosphate.
* Calcium mixed with sulfate.
* Hard water reaction.
* High pH.
* Fertilizer impurity.
* Poor solubility.

### Problem: Leaf Burn After Fertigation

Possible causes:

* High EC.
* Excess fertilizer dose.
* Fertigation in dry soil.
* Young plants exposed to high concentration.
* Salt-sensitive crop.
* Poor irrigation after fertigation.

### Farmer Guidance

When fertigation fails, inspect the system before increasing fertilizer dose. The problem may be clogging, poor pressure, wrong mixing, or injection failure.

# Fertigation Planning

## 37. Steps to Plan Fertigation

1. Test soil.
2. Test irrigation water.
3. Identify crop nutrient requirement.
4. Decide crop-stage-wise fertilizer schedule.
5. Choose compatible water-soluble fertilizers.
6. Calculate nutrient dose.
7. Prepare stock solution.
8. Check injector and filter.
9. Start irrigation with clean water.
10. Inject fertilizer during middle irrigation period.
11. Flush with clean water.
12. Record fertigation details.
13. Monitor crop response.
14. Adjust schedule based on soil, leaf, water, and crop observations.

| Crop Stage                 | Nutrient Focus                 | Purpose                         |

Nutrient demand changes with crop stage. Fertigation should be adjusted instead of applying the same fertilizer throughout the season.

# Farmer Questions and Answers

## Q1. What is fertigation?

Fertigation is the application of water-soluble fertilizers through an irrigation system so nutrients reach the crop root zone with irrigation water.

## Q2. Which irrigation system is best for fertigation?

Drip irrigation is the most suitable system for fertigation because it applies water and nutrients directly near the root zone.

## Q3. Can ordinary fertilizer be used in fertigation?

Only fully water-soluble fertilizers should be used. Ordinary granular fertilizers may contain insoluble particles and can clog filters and drippers.

## Q4. What are common fertigation fertilizers?

Common fertigation fertilizers include water-soluble NPK, urea, calcium nitrate, potassium nitrate, mono ammonium phosphate, mono potassium phosphate, magnesium sulfate, and chelated micronutrients.

## Q5. Can calcium nitrate be mixed with 00:52:34 or 12:61:00?

No. Calcium nitrate should not be mixed with phosphate fertilizers such as 00:52:34 or 12:61:00 in the same stock tank because it can form precipitates and clog drippers.

## Q6. Can calcium nitrate be mixed with magnesium sulfate?

No. Calcium fertilizers and sulfate fertilizers can react and form precipitates. Apply them separately or use separate tanks with expert guidance.

## Q7. Why are drippers clogging after fertigation?

Possible reasons include poor filtration, hard water, insoluble fertilizer, incompatible fertilizer mixing, algae, sediment, iron, or not flushing the system after fertigation.

## Q8. When should fertilizer be injected during irrigation?

Fertilizer should usually be injected during the middle part of irrigation. Start with clean water, inject fertilizer, then flush with clean water.

## Q9. Why is flushing needed after fertigation?

Flushing removes fertilizer from pipes and drippers, prevents clogging, and pushes nutrients into the root zone.

## Q10. What is EC in fertigation?

EC means electrical conductivity. It indicates salt concentration in water or fertilizer solution. High EC can cause salt stress and root injury.

## Q11. What is pH in fertigation?

pH indicates whether water or fertilizer solution is acidic or alkaline. pH affects nutrient availability and fertilizer compatibility.

## Q12. What is a venturi injector?

A venturi injector is a device that uses pressure difference to suck fertilizer solution into the irrigation line.

## Q13. Why is backflow prevention important?

Backflow prevention stops fertilizer solution from flowing back into wells, ponds, or water sources.

## Q14. How often should fertigation be done?

Frequency depends on crop, soil, crop stage, and irrigation system. Vegetables may need fertigation several times per week, while orchards may follow weekly or stage-based schedules.

## Q15. Can fertigation reduce fertilizer wastage?

Yes. Fertigation can reduce wastage by applying nutrients near the active root zone in small and frequent doses.

## Q16. Can fertigation be used for micronutrients?

Yes. Soluble and compatible micronutrients, especially chelated forms, can be applied through fertigation when recommended.

## Q17. Why is crop growth uneven after fertigation?

Uneven growth may be due to clogged drippers, poor pressure, unequal water distribution, uneven fertilizer injection, soil variation, or nutrient deficiency.

## Q18. What should be checked before fertigation?

Check water quality, fertilizer solubility, compatibility, filter condition, injector working, pressure, irrigation uniformity, crop stage, and nutrient dose.

## Q19. Can fertigation burn plants?

Yes. Excess fertilizer, high EC, dry soil, young seedlings, or salt-sensitive crops can suffer root or leaf burn.

## Q20. Is fertigation useful in orchards?

Yes. Fertigation is useful in orchards because it supplies nutrients near active roots and allows stage-wise nutrition during flowering, fruiting, and post-harvest recovery.
