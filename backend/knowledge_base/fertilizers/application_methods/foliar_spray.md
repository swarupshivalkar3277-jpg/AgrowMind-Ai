# Foliar Spray Fertilizer Application

## 1. Overview

Foliar spray is a fertilizer application method in which nutrients are dissolved or suspended in water and sprayed directly on plant leaves. The nutrients are absorbed through the leaf surface, mainly through the cuticle, stomata, and other leaf openings.

Foliar spray is also called **foliar application**, **foliar feeding**, or **leaf nutrient spray**.

Foliar fertilizer application is useful when crops need quick nutrient support, when soil conditions reduce nutrient availability, or when a specific nutrient deficiency must be corrected rapidly. However, foliar spray is generally a **supplementary method**, not a complete substitute for soil fertilization.

## 2. Simple Definition

Foliar spray means applying liquid fertilizer solution directly on crop leaves so that nutrients are absorbed through the leaf surface.

## 3. Main Purpose of Foliar Spray

The main purpose of foliar spray is to provide quick nutrient correction or supplementary feeding during important crop growth stages.

Foliar spray helps in:

* Quick correction of nutrient deficiency
* Supplying nutrients during critical growth stages
* Improving flowering and fruit setting
* Supporting grain filling
* Improving crop quality
* Helping crops recover from temporary stress
* Supplying micronutrients in small quantities
* Supporting crops when root uptake is poor

## 4. How Foliar Spray Works

When fertilizer solution is sprayed on leaves, nutrient ions or molecules enter the plant through:

* Leaf cuticle
* Stomata
* Hydathodes
* Cracks or natural openings in the leaf surface
* Young tender tissues

After absorption, some nutrients move to other plant parts, while others remain mostly near the sprayed area. Nutrient mobility inside the plant depends on the nutrient.

## 5. Foliar Spray Is Supplementary, Not Primary

Foliar spray should not be considered a complete replacement for basal fertilizer, top dressing, soil application, organic manure, or fertigation.

### Why Foliar Spray Cannot Fully Replace Soil Fertilization

* Leaves can absorb only limited nutrient quantities.
* High concentration can burn leaves.
* Major nutrients are required in large amounts.
* Rain can wash off the spray.
* Poor coverage reduces absorption.
* Some nutrients do not move well inside the plant.
* Multiple sprays may be needed.
* Soil fertility is still necessary for long-term crop growth.

## 6. When Foliar Spray Is Useful

Foliar spray is useful in the following situations:

* Visible micronutrient deficiency
* Poor root activity
* Drought stress or temporary moisture stress
* Waterlogged soil where roots cannot absorb nutrients properly
* High soil pH causing zinc, iron, or manganese deficiency
* Low soil temperature reducing nutrient uptake
* Critical crop stages such as flowering, fruit setting, grain filling, or pod filling
* Rapidly growing vegetable crops
* Fruit crops requiring calcium or boron support
* Pulses and oilseeds needing flowering-stage support
* Correcting nutrient deficiency faster than soil application

## 7. When Foliar Spray Is Less Useful

Foliar spray may not work well when:

* Crop is severely nutrient deficient due to poor soil fertility
* Spray concentration is too low
* Spray concentration is too high and causes leaf burn
* Leaves are dusty, waxy, or damaged
* Rain occurs soon after spraying
* Spray is done during hot sunlight
* Humidity is too low and droplets dry quickly
* Spray coverage is poor
* Wrong nutrient is selected
* Deficiency is caused by root disease, salinity, or waterlogging but root problem is not corrected
* Crop is already too damaged to recover

# 8. Nutrients Commonly Applied by Foliar Spray

## 8.1 Nitrogen

Nitrogen can be applied through foliar spray in small quantities for quick greening or temporary correction.

### Common Foliar Nitrogen Sources

* Urea
* Water-soluble NPK fertilizers
* Amino acid-based fertilizers
* Liquid nitrogen formulations

### Use Cases

* Temporary nitrogen deficiency
* Crop recovery from stress
* Supporting vegetative growth
* Grain filling support in some crops

### Caution

High-concentration urea spray can cause leaf burn. Use only crop-specific recommended concentration.

## 8.2 Phosphorus

Phosphorus foliar sprays are used less commonly than soil application because phosphorus is required early and is usually better applied as basal fertilizer.

### Common Foliar Phosphorus Sources

* Water-soluble NPK fertilizers
* Mono potassium phosphate
* Mono ammonium phosphate
* Phosphite/phosphonate products, where registered and properly understood

### Use Cases

* Early root and shoot support
* Flowering support
* Stress recovery
* Supplementary feeding during low soil phosphorus availability

### Caution

Foliar phosphorus should not replace basal phosphorus in most crops.

## 8.3 Potassium

Potassium is commonly used in foliar sprays during fruit development, grain filling, and stress conditions.

### Common Foliar Potassium Sources

* Potassium nitrate
* Mono potassium phosphate
* Sulphate of potash, water-soluble grade
* Potassium thiosulphate
* Water-soluble NPK fertilizers

### Use Cases

* Fruit development
* Fruit size improvement
* Grain filling
* Sugar movement
* Quality improvement
* Drought or heat stress tolerance
* Flowering and pod filling support

### Caution

Use only fully water-soluble sources for foliar spraying. Ordinary MOP is generally not preferred for foliar spray unless specifically formulated and recommended.

## 8.4 Calcium

Calcium foliar spray is important in fruit and vegetable crops where calcium-related disorders occur.

### Common Calcium Sources

* Calcium nitrate
* Calcium chloride, where recommended
* Chelated calcium
* Liquid calcium formulations

### Use Cases

* Blossom-end rot in tomato and chilli
* Tip burn in leafy vegetables
* Bitter pit in apple
* Fruit firmness improvement
* Cell wall strengthening

### Important Note

Calcium is poorly mobile inside plants. Foliar calcium must directly reach the target leaves or fruits. Multiple sprays may be needed during fruit development.

## 8.5 Magnesium

Magnesium is a central part of chlorophyll and helps photosynthesis.

### Common Magnesium Sources

* Magnesium sulphate
* Water-soluble magnesium fertilizers
* Chelated magnesium

### Use Cases

* Interveinal chlorosis in older leaves
* Photosynthesis support
* Vegetable and fruit crop nutrition
* Correction of magnesium deficiency in acidic or sandy soils

## 8.6 Sulphur

Sulphur is sometimes applied through foliar formulations, especially in oilseeds and vegetables.

### Common Sources

* Water-soluble sulphate fertilizers
* Magnesium sulphate
* Potassium sulphate
* Ammonium sulphate solution, where recommended
* Liquid sulphur formulations

### Use Cases

* Oilseed crops
* Onion and garlic
* Brassica crops
* Protein and oil formation support

## 8.7 Zinc

Zinc is one of the most common micronutrients applied through foliar spray.

### Common Sources

* Zinc sulphate
* Chelated zinc
* Zinc oxide suspension formulations
* Multi-micronutrient mixtures

### Use Cases

* Zinc deficiency in rice, maize, citrus, vegetables, pulses, and other crops
* High-pH soils where zinc availability is low
* Early growth correction
* Leaf chlorosis and small leaf symptoms

### Caution

Zinc sulphate is often used with lime in some recommendations to reduce leaf scorching. Follow crop-specific advisory.

## 8.8 Iron

Iron foliar spray is used when iron deficiency causes interveinal chlorosis, especially in high-pH or calcareous soils.

### Common Sources

* Ferrous sulphate
* Chelated iron
* Liquid iron formulations

### Use Cases

* Interveinal chlorosis in young leaves
* Citrus, grapes, vegetables, and ornamentals
* High-pH soil conditions

### Caution

Soil correction may also be needed because iron deficiency often comes from high soil pH, poor root health, or waterlogging.

## 8.9 Boron

Boron foliar spray is used in crops where flowering, pollination, fruit setting, or root development is affected by boron deficiency.

### Common Sources

* Borax
* Solubor
* Boric acid
* Liquid boron formulations

### Use Cases

* Flowering support
* Fruit setting
* Pollen tube growth
* Hollow stem in cauliflower
* Cracking issues in some crops
* Poor pod or seed set in pulses and oilseeds

### Caution

Boron has a narrow safety range. A small excess can cause toxicity. Always follow crop-specific dose.

## 8.10 Manganese

Manganese may be applied as foliar spray where deficiency occurs.

### Common Sources

* Manganese sulphate
* Chelated manganese
* Multi-micronutrient mixtures

### Use Cases

* Interveinal chlorosis
* High-pH soils
* Cereals, pulses, oilseeds, and vegetables

## 8.11 Molybdenum

Molybdenum is important for nitrogen metabolism and nodulation in legumes.

### Common Sources

* Sodium molybdate
* Ammonium molybdate

### Use Cases

* Pulses and legumes
* Poor nodulation
* Nitrogen deficiency-like symptoms in legumes
* Acidic soils where molybdenum availability is low

### Caution

Molybdenum is needed in very small amounts. Follow exact recommendation.

## 8.12 Multi-Micronutrient Mixtures

Multi-micronutrient sprays contain several micronutrients.

### Common Contents

* Zinc
* Iron
* Manganese
* Copper
* Boron
* Molybdenum
* Magnesium
* Sulphur

### Use Cases

* General micronutrient deficiency correction
* Vegetable crops
* Fruit crops
* High-value crops
* Soil-test or leaf-test-based nutrient programs

### Caution

Use approved grades and avoid repeated unnecessary sprays.

# 10. General Foliar Spray Concentration

Foliar spray concentration depends on:

* Crop
* Crop stage
* Nutrient type
* Fertilizer source
* Product formulation
* Weather
* Spray volume
* Leaf sensitivity
* Deficiency severity

A broad general range for many foliar nutrient sprays is often around:

* **0.5% to 2.0% solution**

This means:

* 0.5% = 5 grams per litre of water
* 1.0% = 10 grams per litre of water
* 2.0% = 20 grams per litre of water

### Important Warning

Do not use this general range blindly. Some nutrients, especially boron, molybdenum, copper, and some chelated products, are used at much lower doses. Always follow label or crop-specific recommendation.

# 11. How to Calculate Foliar Spray Solution

## 11.1 Formula

To prepare a percentage solution:

```text
Required fertilizer amount in grams = Desired percentage × 10 × Water volume in litres
```

## 11.2 Examples

### Example 1: 1% Solution in 15 Litres

```text
1 × 10 × 15 = 150 grams
```

So, mix 150 grams fertilizer in 15 litres water.

### Example 2: 0.5% Solution in 15 Litres

```text
0.5 × 10 × 15 = 75 grams
```

So, mix 75 grams fertilizer in 15 litres water.

### Example 3: 2% Solution in 15 Litres

```text
2 × 10 × 15 = 300 grams
```

So, mix 300 grams fertilizer in 15 litres water.

### Example 4: 1% Solution in 200 Litres

```text
1 × 10 × 200 = 2000 grams = 2 kg
```

So, mix 2 kg fertilizer in 200 litres water.

# 12. Best Time for Foliar Spray

Foliar spray should be done when leaves can absorb nutrients and when risk of leaf burn is low.

## 12.1 Best Time of Day

* Early morning
* Late afternoon
* Evening

## 12.2 Avoid Spraying

* During hot midday sun
* During strong wind
* Before expected rain
* On drought-stressed wilted plants
* On waterlogged and severely stressed crops
* During frost or very cold conditions
* When leaves are wet from rain or dew, unless local recommendation allows

## 12.3 Weather Conditions

Foliar spray works best when:

* Temperature is moderate
* Relative humidity is moderate to high
* Wind speed is low
* Leaves are active and not wilted
* Rain is not expected soon
* Crop is not under severe stress

# 14. Crop-Wise Foliar Spray Guidance

## 14.1 Rice / Paddy

Foliar spray in rice may be used for zinc, iron, nitrogen, potassium, or stress recovery.

### Common Uses

* Zinc deficiency correction
* Iron deficiency correction
* Nitrogen support
* Grain filling support
* Micronutrient correction

### Farmer Advisory

Foliar spray is useful for quick correction, but basal and top-dressed nutrients are still important. Zinc deficiency in rice may require soil or foliar zinc depending on severity and local recommendation.

## 14.2 Wheat

Foliar spray in wheat may support tillering, booting, flowering, and grain filling.

### Common Uses

* Nitrogen support
* Zinc correction
* Potassium support
* Micronutrient spray
* Grain filling support

### Farmer Advisory

Do not rely only on foliar spray for wheat nutrition. Basal phosphorus and timely nitrogen top dressing are essential.

## 14.3 Maize

Foliar spray in maize may be used for zinc, magnesium, potassium, and stress recovery.

### Common Uses

* Zinc deficiency correction
* Magnesium deficiency correction
* Potassium support before tasseling
* Grain filling support
* Drought stress support

### Farmer Advisory

Maize has high nutrient demand. Foliar spray can supplement nutrition but cannot replace proper basal and top-dressed NPK.

## 14.4 Cotton

Cotton may benefit from foliar nutrient sprays during square formation, flowering, and boll development.

### Common Uses

* Magnesium correction
* Potassium support
* Boron support
* Micronutrient mixtures
* Boll retention support
* Stress recovery

### Farmer Advisory

Balanced soil nutrition is necessary. Foliar sprays are useful during reproductive stages but should not be overused.

## 14.5 Pulses

Pulses often respond to foliar nutrition during flowering and pod filling, especially under moisture stress.

### Common Uses

* DAP or water-soluble NPK spray where recommended
* Potassium support
* Boron support
* Molybdenum support
* Micronutrient correction

### Farmer Advisory

Foliar spray can help during flowering and pod filling, but seed treatment with Rhizobium and basal phosphorus are also important.

## 14.6 Oilseeds

Oilseeds may require sulphur, boron, potassium, and micronutrients.

### Common Uses

* Boron spray during flowering
* Sulphur support
* Potassium support
* Micronutrient correction
* Drought stress recovery

### Farmer Advisory

Use boron carefully because excess boron can damage crops.

## 14.7 Tomato

Tomato commonly receives foliar sprays for calcium, boron, potassium, magnesium, and micronutrients.

### Common Uses

* Calcium for blossom-end rot management
* Boron for flowering and fruit setting
* Potassium for fruit size and quality
* Magnesium for chlorophyll and photosynthesis
* Micronutrient correction

### Farmer Advisory

Foliar calcium helps only when sprayed repeatedly during fruit development and when water management is proper. Blossom-end rot is also affected by irregular irrigation and root health.

## 14.8 Chilli

Chilli may need foliar sprays during vegetative growth, flowering, and fruiting.

### Common Uses

* Calcium support
* Boron support
* Potassium support
* Magnesium support
* Micronutrient mixture
* Stress recovery

### Farmer Advisory

Avoid spraying high concentration during hot weather because chilli leaves and flowers can be sensitive.

## 14.9 Onion and Garlic

Onion and garlic may benefit from micronutrient, sulphur, potassium, and magnesium sprays.

### Common Uses

* Sulphur support
* Magnesium support
* Micronutrient spray
* Potassium support during bulb development

### Farmer Advisory

Foliar sprays can support bulb quality but should not replace basal sulphur and balanced NPK.

## 14.10 Fruit Crops

Fruit crops commonly use foliar sprays for micronutrients, calcium, boron, magnesium, and potassium.

### Common Uses

* Calcium for fruit firmness
* Boron for flowering and fruit setting
* Zinc for small leaf or rosetting symptoms
* Iron for chlorosis
* Magnesium for leaf chlorosis
* Potassium for fruit size and quality

### Farmer Advisory

In fruit crops, correct spray timing is very important. Calcium and boron sprays are often stage-specific and should follow crop-specific local recommendations.

# 15. Foliar Spray for Nutrient Deficiency Correction

## 15.1 Zinc Deficiency

### Common Symptoms

* Small leaves
* Short internodes
* Yellowing between veins
* Stunted growth
* White or pale patches in rice leaves

### Foliar Correction

Use zinc-containing foliar spray as per crop recommendation.

## 15.2 Iron Deficiency

### Common Symptoms

* Yellowing of young leaves
* Green veins with yellow leaf area
* Common in high-pH or calcareous soils

### Foliar Correction

Use iron chelate or ferrous sulphate spray as recommended.

## 15.3 Magnesium Deficiency

### Common Symptoms

* Yellowing between veins on older leaves
* Premature leaf fall in severe cases
* Poor photosynthesis

### Foliar Correction

Use magnesium sulphate spray as recommended.

## 15.4 Boron Deficiency

### Common Symptoms

* Poor flowering
* Poor fruit set
* Hollow stem
* Cracked fruits or roots
* Deformed growing points

### Foliar Correction

Use boron spray only at recommended low dose.

## 15.5 Calcium Deficiency

### Common Symptoms

* Blossom-end rot
* Tip burn
* Fruit cracking
* Bitter pit
* Poor fruit firmness

### Foliar Correction

Use calcium spray targeted to young fruits and growing points.

## 15.6 Potassium Deficiency

### Common Symptoms

* Marginal yellowing or scorching of older leaves
* Poor fruit size
* Weak stems
* Poor grain filling
* Reduced stress tolerance

### Foliar Correction

Use potassium nitrate, mono potassium phosphate, or suitable water-soluble potassium fertilizer as recommended.

# 16. Compatibility and Tank Mixing

Foliar fertilizers are often mixed with pesticides, bio-stimulants, or other fertilizers, but not all mixtures are safe.

## 16.1 General Compatibility Rules

* Read labels of all products.
* Do a jar test before tank mixing.
* Avoid mixing calcium with phosphate or sulphate fertilizers unless label allows.
* Avoid mixing strongly acidic and strongly alkaline products.
* Avoid mixing micronutrient salts with incompatible pesticides.
* Avoid mixing biofertilizers or microbial products with harsh chemicals.
* Use clean water.
* Maintain proper spray solution pH if product requires it.
* Spray the mixture immediately after preparation.
* Do not store mixed spray solution overnight.

## 16.2 Jar Test Method

1. Take a small transparent container.
2. Add water.
3. Add products in the same order and proportion planned for the spray tank.
4. Mix gently.
5. Wait for 15 to 30 minutes.
6. Check for sediment, curdling, heat, gas, layers, or lumps.
7. If incompatibility appears, do not mix those products.

# 17. Spray Water Quality

Water quality affects foliar spray performance.

## 17.1 Important Water Factors

* pH
* Hardness
* Salinity
* Turbidity
* Suspended soil particles
* Organic matter contamination
* Iron content

## 17.2 Guidance

* Use clean water.
* Avoid muddy water.
* Avoid very hard water if product is sensitive.
* Adjust pH only if label recommends.
* Filter water before filling sprayer.
* Do not use saline water for sensitive crops.

# 18. Spray Coverage

Good coverage is essential for foliar fertilizer absorption.

## 18.1 Good Coverage Means

* Fine droplets cover the leaf surface.
* Both upper and lower leaf surfaces are reached.
* Spray reaches young growing parts when needed.
* Spray reaches fruits when calcium or fruit-specific nutrients are applied.
* Leaves are wetted uniformly but not dripping heavily.

## 18.2 Poor Coverage Causes

* Low spray volume
* Wrong nozzle
* High wind
* Dense canopy
* Fast walking speed
* Poor sprayer pressure
* Blocked nozzle
* Spray only on upper canopy

# 19. Sprayer Selection

Foliar spray can be applied using:

* Knapsack sprayer
* Battery sprayer
* Power sprayer
* Boom sprayer
* Mist blower
* Drone sprayer, where recommended and legally allowed
* Orchard air-blast sprayer for fruit crops

## Sprayer Maintenance

* Clean nozzle before spraying.
* Calibrate sprayer.
* Check leakage.
* Use proper pressure.
* Clean tank after spraying.
* Do not use pesticide-contaminated sprayer for sensitive nutrient sprays without proper washing.

# 20. Advantages of Foliar Spray

* Quick nutrient correction
* Useful for micronutrients
* Useful during critical growth stages
* Can help when root uptake is poor
* Requires small nutrient quantity
* Can improve flowering, fruit setting, grain filling, or quality
* Useful in high-value crops
* Can supplement fertigation or soil fertilization
* Helps manage hidden hunger when leaf testing shows deficiency

# 21. Limitations of Foliar Spray

* Cannot supply large nutrient quantities
* Can cause leaf burn if concentration is high
* Rain can wash off spray
* Multiple sprays may be needed
* Absorption depends on weather
* Some nutrients move poorly inside the plant
* Poor coverage reduces effectiveness
* Wrong timing gives poor result
* Incompatible mixtures can damage leaves
* Severe soil deficiency cannot be solved by foliar spray alone

# 25. Foliar Spray Decision Process

Before foliar spray, follow this decision process:

1. Identify crop and crop stage.
2. Identify pest, disease, or nutrient issue correctly.
3. Confirm nutrient deficiency if possible through symptoms, soil test, or leaf analysis.
4. Select correct nutrient and formulation.
5. Check recommended concentration.
6. Check weather forecast.
7. Use clean water.
8. Do jar test if mixing products.
9. Spray during morning or evening.
10. Ensure full coverage.
11. Observe crop response.
12. Repeat only as recommended.

# 26. Common Mistakes in Foliar Spray

## 26.1 Spraying at Midday

Hot sunlight can dry droplets quickly and increase leaf burn risk.

## 26.2 Using High Concentration

High concentration may scorch leaves, flowers, or fruits.

## 26.3 Mixing Incompatible Products

Wrong tank mixes can reduce effectiveness or cause crop injury.

## 26.4 Spraying Before Rain

Rain may wash off nutrients before absorption.

## 26.5 Poor Pest or Deficiency Diagnosis

Nutrient sprays will not solve insect damage, disease, herbicide injury, salinity, or root rot.

## 26.6 Ignoring Water Quality

Hard, muddy, saline, or high-pH water may reduce performance.

## 26.7 Spraying Severely Stressed Crop

Wilted or severely stressed plants may absorb poorly and may be damaged by spray.

## 26.8 Repeating Sprays Without Need

Unnecessary foliar sprays increase cost and may cause nutrient imbalance.

# 27. Safety Precautions

Foliar fertilizers are generally safer than many pesticides, but safe handling is still necessary.

## 27.1 Personal Safety

* Wear gloves.
* Wear long sleeves.
* Use eye protection.
* Avoid inhaling spray mist.
* Do not eat, drink, or smoke during spraying.
* Wash hands and face after spraying.
* Wash contaminated clothes separately.
* Keep children and animals away from spraying area.

## 27.2 Crop Safety

* Follow recommended concentration.
* Test on a small area before spraying a new product or mixture.
* Avoid spraying during high temperature.
* Avoid spraying sensitive crops without recommendation.
* Avoid high-salt fertilizers on tender leaves.
* Avoid spraying during flowering if product may damage flowers.

## 27.3 Environmental Safety

* Avoid drift to nearby crops.
* Do not spray during strong wind.
* Do not contaminate ponds, wells, canals, or streams.
* Do not dump leftover solution in water bodies.
* Clean sprayer safely.

# 28. Crop Phytotoxicity Warning

Phytotoxicity means crop injury caused by chemical or fertilizer spray.

## Symptoms of Phytotoxicity

* Leaf burn
* Leaf spots
* Yellow patches
* Flower drop
* Fruit russeting
* Leaf curling
* Necrotic margins
* Stunted growth

## Causes

* High concentration
* Wrong fertilizer source
* Incompatible mixture
* Hot weather spraying
* Poor water quality
* Sensitive crop variety
* Repeated spraying
* Spraying on stressed crop

# 29. Farmer-Friendly Advisory

Foliar spray gives quick nutrient support, especially for micronutrient deficiency and critical crop stages. It should be sprayed in the morning or evening using clean water and correct concentration. Do not spray during hot sun, strong wind, or before rain. Foliar spray is useful, but it does not replace proper soil fertilization. Use soil test, leaf test, and local crop recommendation for best results.

# 30. AI/RAG Query Examples

## Query: What is foliar spray fertilizer application?

Foliar spray fertilizer application is the spraying of dissolved nutrients directly on crop leaves so that nutrients are absorbed through the leaf surface.

## Query: Is foliar spray a replacement for soil fertilizer?

No. Foliar spray is usually supplementary. It is useful for quick correction of nutrient deficiency and stage-specific feeding, but it cannot replace basal application, top dressing, organic manure, or fertigation in most crops.

## Query: When should foliar spray be done?

Foliar spray should be done during early morning, late afternoon, or evening when temperature is moderate, wind is low, and rain is not expected.

## Query: Which nutrients can be applied by foliar spray?

Nitrogen, potassium, calcium, magnesium, sulphur, zinc, iron, boron, manganese, molybdenum, and multi-micronutrient mixtures can be applied by foliar spray if suitable formulations and recommended concentrations are used.

## Query: What is the general concentration for foliar spray?

A general concentration range for many foliar nutrient sprays is around 0.5% to 2.0%, but exact concentration depends on crop, nutrient, formulation, and local recommendation. Some nutrients such as boron and molybdenum require much lower doses.

## Query: How much fertilizer is needed for 1% foliar spray?

For a 1% solution, use 10 grams fertilizer per litre of water. For 15 litres of water, use 150 grams fertilizer.

## Query: Can urea be sprayed on leaves?

Yes, urea can be sprayed on leaves at crop-specific recommended concentration. High concentration can cause leaf burn, so label guidance must be followed.

## Query: Can DAP be used as foliar spray?

DAP is sometimes used as foliar spray in specific crops and recommendations, but it must be fully dissolved and used at the correct concentration. Water-soluble fertilizers are generally safer for foliar application.

## Query: Why does foliar spray cause leaf burn?

Leaf burn may occur because of high concentration, hot weather, poor water quality, incompatible tank mix, sensitive crop stage, or spraying on stressed plants.

## Query: Can foliar spray correct zinc deficiency?

Yes. Zinc deficiency can often be corrected quickly through foliar zinc spray, but severe or recurring deficiency may also need soil application based on soil test.

## Query: Can foliar calcium stop blossom-end rot in tomato?

Foliar calcium can help reduce calcium deficiency symptoms if applied correctly during fruit development, but blossom-end rot is also caused by irregular irrigation, root damage, salinity, and poor calcium movement. Water management is important.

## Query: What is the best time for micronutrient spray?

The best time is usually morning or evening during moderate temperature and low wind, and when rain is not expected soon.

# 31. Do and Don't

## Do

* Use foliar spray as supplementary nutrition.
* Confirm nutrient deficiency before spraying.
* Use crop-specific recommended concentration.
* Use clean water.
* Spray during morning or evening.
* Ensure good coverage.
* Do jar test before tank mixing.
* Use water-soluble or foliar-grade fertilizers.
* Wear protective clothing.
* Repeat only as recommended.

## Don't

* Do not spray during hot midday sun.
* Do not spray before rain.
* Do not use high concentration.
* Do not mix incompatible products.
* Do not spray severely wilted crops.
* Do not use muddy or saline water.
* Do not treat foliar spray as a full replacement for soil fertilization.

# 33. Short Summary

Foliar spray is a fertilizer application method in which dissolved nutrients are sprayed directly on plant leaves for absorption through the leaf surface. It is useful for quick correction of nutrient deficiencies, micronutrient application, and supplementary feeding during critical growth stages such as flowering, fruit setting, grain filling, and pod filling. Common foliar nutrients include nitrogen, potassium, calcium, magnesium, zinc, iron, boron, manganese, molybdenum, and multi-micronutrient mixtures. Foliar spray should be done with clean water, correct concentration, good coverage, and suitable weather. It should be applied in the morning or evening and avoided during hot sun, strong wind, or before rain. Foliar spray is helpful but does not replace basal fertilizer, top dressing, organic manure, or fertigation.
