# Zinc Deficiency in Crops

## 1. Overview

Zinc deficiency is one of the most common micronutrient deficiencies in agricultural crops. Zinc is needed in small quantities, but it is essential for plant growth, enzyme activity, hormone production, chlorophyll formation, protein synthesis, pollen development, and crop yield.

Zinc deficiency reduces root growth, shoot growth, leaf size, flowering, grain formation, fruit quality, and resistance to stress. It is common in high-pH soils, calcareous soils, sandy soils, low-organic-matter soils, eroded soils, waterlogged soils, and soils receiving excessive phosphorus fertilizer.

In India, zinc deficiency is widely observed in rice, wheat, maize, cotton, sugarcane, pulses, oilseeds, vegetables, and fruit crops. Rice-based systems are especially prone to zinc deficiency because puddling, flooding, high bicarbonate, and high pH conditions can reduce zinc availability.

## 2. Simple Definition

Zinc deficiency is a plant nutrient disorder caused by insufficient available zinc in the soil or poor zinc uptake by roots. It usually results in stunted growth, small leaves, yellowing between veins, short internodes, poor tillering, and reduced yield.

## 3. Role of Zinc in Plants

Zinc performs many important functions in plants.

### 3.1 Main Functions

* Helps enzyme activation
* Supports auxin hormone production
* Supports chlorophyll formation
* Helps protein synthesis
* Supports carbohydrate metabolism
* Helps pollen formation and fertilization
* Supports root growth
* Improves plant height and internode length
* Supports seed and grain development
* Improves tolerance to stress
* Helps maintain membrane stability

### 3.2 Why Zinc Is Important

Without enough zinc, plants cannot grow normally even if nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium are sufficient. Zinc deficiency often causes hidden hunger before visible symptoms appear.

## 4. Zinc Mobility in Plants

Zinc has low to moderate mobility inside plants. Because of limited mobility, deficiency symptoms often appear first on younger leaves or newly developing tissues, although symptoms may vary by crop.

### Important Point

If new leaves are small, pale, narrow, or show interveinal chlorosis, zinc deficiency should be considered, especially in high-pH or zinc-deficient soils.

# 5. General Symptoms of Zinc Deficiency

## 5.1 Common Visual Symptoms

* Stunted plant growth
* Short internodes
* Small leaves
* Narrow leaves
* Interveinal chlorosis
* Yellowing of young leaves
* Pale green or whitish leaf patches
* Rosetting of leaves in fruit crops
* Poor tillering in cereals
* Delayed maturity
* Poor flowering
* Poor grain or fruit development
* Reduced yield
* Reduced root growth

## 5.2 Leaf Symptoms

Zinc-deficient leaves may show:

* Yellowing between veins
* Light green or white bands
* Brown spots or rusty patches in rice
* Mottling
* Smaller-than-normal leaf size
* Distorted leaves
* Narrow leaves
* Necrotic patches in severe cases

## 5.3 Growth Symptoms

Zinc deficiency causes:

* Reduced plant height
* Shortened internodes
* Bushy or rosette appearance
* Poor root development
* Weak crop stand
* Reduced tiller number in rice and wheat
* Poor crop uniformity

## 5.4 Reproductive Symptoms

Zinc deficiency can reduce:

* Flowering
* Pollen viability
* Fruit set
* Grain filling
* Seed development
* Crop quality

# 6. Causes of Zinc Deficiency

## 6.1 Low Available Zinc in Soil

Some soils naturally contain low available zinc, especially sandy, highly weathered, or eroded soils.

## 6.2 High Soil pH

Zinc availability decreases as soil pH increases. High-pH and calcareous soils often make zinc unavailable to plants even when total zinc in soil is present.

## 6.3 Calcareous Soils

Calcareous soils contain free calcium carbonate, which can reduce zinc solubility and zinc uptake.

## 6.4 Excess Phosphorus Fertilizer

Heavy phosphorus application can induce zinc deficiency. Excess phosphorus may reduce zinc uptake and create nutrient imbalance.

## 6.5 Low Organic Matter

Organic matter helps hold micronutrients and improve nutrient availability. Low-organic-matter soils are more prone to zinc deficiency.

## 6.6 Sandy Soils

Sandy soils have low nutrient-holding capacity and may have low available zinc.

## 6.7 Waterlogging and Flooding

Waterlogged and flooded conditions can reduce zinc availability, especially in rice soils.

## 6.8 Puddled Rice Soils

Puddling and continuous flooding in rice can create chemical conditions that reduce zinc availability to roots.

## 6.9 Cool and Wet Conditions

Cold, wet soils reduce root activity and slow zinc uptake.

## 6.10 Soil Erosion or Land Leveling

Topsoil generally contains more organic matter and available nutrients. Removal of topsoil by erosion or leveling may expose zinc-deficient subsoil.

## 6.11 Root Damage

Zinc uptake may be reduced by:

* Root rot
* Nematodes
* Insect damage
* Compacted soil
* Salinity
* Poor drainage
* Transplant shock

# 7. Soils Most Prone to Zinc Deficiency

Zinc deficiency is common in:

# 8. Crop-Wise Zinc Deficiency Symptoms

## 8.1 Rice / Paddy

Zinc deficiency in rice is common and is sometimes associated with **Khaira disease**.

### Symptoms in Rice

* Stunted plants
* Poor tillering
* Brown or rusty spots on leaves
* Dusty brown patches on younger leaves
* Chlorosis near leaf base
* Bronzing of leaves
* Delayed maturity
* Increased spikelet sterility
* Uneven crop growth
* Reduced grain yield

### Rice-Specific Notes

Zinc deficiency usually appears 2 to 4 weeks after transplanting in puddled and flooded fields. Symptoms may appear in patches across the field.

### Correction in Rice

* Apply zinc sulphate to soil before transplanting or during final puddling where recommended.
* Foliar zinc spray may be used for quick correction.
* Improve organic matter.
* Avoid excessive phosphorus.
* Improve drainage where waterlogging is severe.

## 8.2 Maize

Maize is very sensitive to zinc deficiency.

### Symptoms in Maize

* Broad white or yellow bands on both sides of the midrib
* Pale stripes on young leaves
* Shortened internodes
* Stunted plants
* Reduced leaf size
* Delayed growth
* Poor cob development
* Reduced grain yield

### Maize-Specific Notes

Zinc deficiency is common in high-pH soils, sandy soils, cold soils, and fields with high phosphorus application.

### Correction in Maize

* Apply zinc sulphate to soil based on soil test.
* Use zinc-enriched NPK where available.
* Foliar zinc spray can help correct early symptoms.
* Avoid over-application of phosphorus.

| Feature    | Zinc Deficiency              | Iron Deficiency                              |

| Chlorosis  | Interveinal, mottled, banded | Strong interveinal chlorosis on young leaves |
| Rosetting  | Common in fruit crops        | Less common                                  |
| Cause      | High pH, high P, low Zn      | High pH, calcareous soil, poor root function |

| Feature               | Zinc Deficiency                         | Magnesium Deficiency                  |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |

# 10. Diagnosis of Zinc Deficiency

## 10.1 Visual Diagnosis

Visual symptoms help identify zinc deficiency, but symptoms may be confused with other nutrient deficiencies, diseases, herbicide injury, or root problems.

## 10.2 Soil Testing

Soil testing is the best way to confirm zinc deficiency and decide soil application dose.

### Soil Test Indicators

* Low available zinc
* High soil pH
* Calcareous condition
* Low organic carbon
* High phosphorus levels

## 10.3 Plant Tissue Testing

Leaf or plant tissue testing can confirm zinc deficiency during crop growth.

### Useful For

* Fruit crops
* Vegetables
* High-value crops
* Hidden hunger diagnosis
* Confirming recurring deficiency

## 10.4 Field History

Check:

* Previous zinc application
* Phosphorus fertilizer history
* Soil pH
* Organic matter status
* Crop rotation
* Waterlogging history
* Land leveling or erosion
* Manure use
* Irrigation water quality

# 11. Zinc Fertilizer Sources

## 11.1 Zinc Sulphate

Zinc sulphate is the most common zinc fertilizer.

### Common Grades

* Zinc sulphate heptahydrate, around 21 percent Zn
* Zinc sulphate monohydrate, around 33 percent Zn

### Uses

* Soil application
* Foliar spray, where recommended
* Nursery treatment in some crops

## 11.2 Zinc Oxide

Zinc oxide contains high zinc but is less soluble than zinc sulphate. It may be used in special formulations or suspension products.

## 11.3 Chelated Zinc

Chelated zinc is more soluble and useful for foliar spray, fertigation, and high-value crops.

### Common Chelates

* Zn-EDTA
* Zn-DTPA
* Zn-HEDTA

## 11.4 Zinc-Enriched Fertilizers

Some NPK fertilizers are fortified with zinc to provide balanced nutrition.

## 11.5 Zincated Urea

Zinc-coated or zincated urea supplies nitrogen along with zinc and may improve zinc distribution in the field.

## 11.6 Organic Sources

Organic manures supply small amounts of zinc and improve zinc availability.

### Examples

* FYM
* Compost
* Vermicompost
* Green manure
* Crop residues
* Bio-enriched compost

# 12. Zinc Deficiency Correction Methods

## 12.1 Soil Application

Soil application is useful for long-term correction and building zinc availability in deficient soils.

### Common Soil Application Sources

* Zinc sulphate heptahydrate
* Zinc sulphate monohydrate
* Zinc-enriched NPK
* Chelated zinc in high-value crops
* Zincated urea

### General Soil Application Guidance

* Apply zinc based on soil test.
* Mix zinc fertilizer with soil.
* Apply during final land preparation or basal application.
* In rice, zinc sulphate can be broadcast and incorporated before transplanting or final puddling.
* Avoid mixing concentrated zinc fertilizer directly with seed.
* Use organic manure to improve zinc availability.

### Broad Dose Note

Many local recommendations use zinc sulphate soil application in deficient soils. Dose varies by crop, soil, formulation, and local advisory. For example, rice recommendations often mention zinc sulphate application before transplanting or final puddling. Always follow local agriculture university or soil-test recommendation.

## 12.2 Foliar Spray

Foliar zinc spray gives faster correction during crop growth.

### Suitable Situations

* Visible zinc deficiency symptoms
* Crop already standing in field
* High-value crops
* Fruit crops
* Soil zinc application is slow or not possible
* Temporary deficiency due to cool, wet, or high-pH conditions

### Common Foliar Sources

* Zinc sulphate
* Chelated zinc
* Zinc oxide suspension
* Multi-micronutrient mixture

### Foliar Spray Caution

* Use recommended concentration only.
* Spray in morning or evening.
* Avoid hot sunlight.
* Avoid spraying before rain.
* Use clean water.
* Do jar test before mixing with pesticides.
* Zinc sulphate may need lime in some crop recommendations to reduce leaf burn.
* Repeat only as per recommendation.

## 12.3 Seed Treatment

Some crops may use zinc seed treatment where recommended.

### Benefits

* Supports early growth
* Low dose
* Improves early zinc availability
* Useful in zinc-deficient soils

### Caution

Use only seed-treatment-grade products and recommended dose. Do not mix with incompatible biofertilizers unless advised.

## 12.4 Nursery Treatment

In crops like rice and vegetables, zinc may be applied in nursery or seedling stage where deficiency is common.

### Benefits

* Improves seedling vigor
* Helps early establishment
* Reduces transplant shock
* Supports early root growth

## 12.5 Fertigation

Zinc can be applied through fertigation in drip-irrigated crops using soluble zinc sources.

### Suitable For

* Vegetables
* Fruit crops
* Plantation crops
* Protected cultivation
* High-value crops

### Caution

Use only fertigation-grade soluble zinc products. Avoid incompatible mixing with phosphate or calcium fertilizers unless label allows.

# 13. Rice Zinc Deficiency Management

Rice is one of the crops most affected by zinc deficiency.

## 13.1 Why Rice Gets Zinc Deficiency

* Flooding reduces zinc availability.
* Puddling changes soil chemistry.
* High pH reduces zinc solubility.
* High bicarbonate reduces zinc uptake.
* Low organic matter reduces micronutrient availability.
* Excess phosphorus worsens zinc deficiency.
* Zinc may form insoluble compounds under submerged conditions.

## 13.2 Symptoms in Rice

* Stunted seedlings
* Poor tillering
* Brown spots on young leaves
* Rusty or dusty patches
* Chlorotic leaf base
* Bronzing
* Patchy field growth
* Delayed maturity
* Spikelet sterility
* Yield loss

## 13.3 Corrective Measures in Rice

* Apply zinc sulphate as basal where deficiency is known.
* Incorporate zinc into soil before transplanting.
* Use foliar zinc spray for standing crop symptoms.
* Avoid excessive phosphorus fertilizer.
* Add organic manure or green manure.
* Maintain proper water management.
* Use soil testing for future crop planning.

# 16. Prevention of Zinc Deficiency

## 16.1 Soil-Test-Based Fertilization

Test soil regularly and apply zinc where deficiency is detected.

## 16.2 Balanced NPK Use

Avoid excessive phosphorus, especially in high-pH soils.

## 16.3 Organic Matter Management

Apply FYM, compost, green manure, or crop residues to improve micronutrient availability.

## 16.4 Proper Water Management

Avoid prolonged waterlogging unless crop requires flooding. In rice, manage water properly and avoid unnecessary continuous stagnation where local practice allows.

## 16.5 Zinc-Enriched Fertilizers

Use zinc-fortified fertilizers where available and recommended.

## 16.6 Crop Rotation

Crop rotation with legumes, green manure, or deep-rooted crops can improve soil health and nutrient cycling.

## 16.7 Avoid Topsoil Loss

Prevent erosion and avoid excessive land leveling that removes fertile topsoil.

# 17. Zinc Toxicity Warning

Zinc is a micronutrient, so plants need it in small quantities. Excess zinc can cause toxicity.

## 17.1 Toxicity Causes

* Overuse of zinc fertilizers
* Repeated foliar sprays at high concentration
* Industrial contamination
* Sewage sludge or polluted irrigation water
* Wrong product dose

## 17.2 Toxicity Symptoms

* Stunted growth
* Leaf chlorosis
* Reduced root growth
* Iron deficiency-like symptoms
* Poor crop growth

## 17.3 Prevention

* Follow soil-test-based dose.
* Do not repeat zinc sprays unnecessarily.
* Do not exceed label concentration.
* Avoid mixing multiple micronutrient products without need.

# 18. Compatibility and Mixing

## 18.1 Foliar Compatibility

Zinc fertilizers may not be compatible with all pesticides or fertilizers.

### General Rules

* Do a jar test before tank mixing.
* Avoid mixing zinc with highly alkaline products.
* Avoid mixing zinc with phosphate fertilizers in the same spray tank unless label allows.
* Avoid mixing with calcium products unless compatible.
* Use clean water.
* Spray immediately after preparation.
* Do not store mixed solution overnight.

## 18.2 Soil Mixing

For soil application:

* Mix zinc fertilizer well with soil.
* Do not place concentrated zinc fertilizer directly with seed.
* Combine with organic manure where recommended.
* Avoid uneven broadcasting.

# 19. Farmer-Friendly Diagnosis Guide

## 19.1 Suspect Zinc Deficiency If

* Crop is stunted.
* New leaves are small or pale.
* Internodes are short.
* Rice leaves show brown rusty spots.
* Maize leaves show yellow or white bands.
* Fruit trees show small leaves and rosetting.
* Soil is high pH or calcareous.
* Field has received heavy phosphorus fertilizer.

## 19.2 Confirm Before Treatment

Use:

* Soil test
* Leaf tissue test
* Crop history

# 21. General Farmer Advisory

Zinc deficiency should be managed with both correction and prevention. For immediate correction, foliar zinc spray can help. For long-term management, apply zinc to soil based on soil test and improve organic matter. Avoid excessive phosphorus fertilizer and maintain proper water management. In rice, zinc deficiency is common in puddled and flooded fields, so zinc sulphate application before transplanting may be needed in deficient soils.

Khaira disease is a zinc deficiency disorder in rice. It causes brown or rusty spots, bronzing, stunted growth, and poor tillering.

# 23. Do and Don't

## Do

* Confirm zinc deficiency with soil or leaf testing where possible.
* Apply zinc based on soil-test recommendation.
* Use zinc sulphate, chelated zinc, or zinc-enriched fertilizer as recommended.
* Use foliar zinc spray for quick correction.
* Add FYM, compost, or green manure to improve zinc availability.
* Avoid excessive phosphorus fertilizer.
* Manage waterlogging.
* Spray zinc in morning or evening.
* Do jar test before tank mixing.

# 25. Short Summary

Zinc deficiency is a common micronutrient problem that reduces crop growth, flowering, grain filling, fruit quality, and yield. It usually causes stunted growth, short internodes, small leaves, interveinal chlorosis, poor tillering, and delayed maturity. In rice, zinc deficiency can cause Khaira disease with brown rusty spots and bronzing. In maize, it causes yellow or white bands on young leaves. Zinc deficiency is common in high-pH, calcareous, sandy, low-organic-matter, waterlogged, and high-phosphorus soils. It can be corrected by soil application of zinc sulphate, foliar zinc spray, chelated zinc, zinc-enriched fertilizers, and organic matter addition. Long-term management should be based on soil testing, balanced fertilization, water management, and prevention of excessive phosphorus use.
