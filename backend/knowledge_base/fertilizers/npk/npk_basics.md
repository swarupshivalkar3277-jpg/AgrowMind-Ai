# NPK Fertilizer Basics

## Overview

NPK fertilizer refers to fertilizers that supply the three primary plant nutrients:

* N = Nitrogen
* P = Phosphorus
* K = Potassium

These nutrients are called primary macronutrients because plants need them in relatively large quantities for growth, yield, and quality.

On most fertilizer bags, NPK is written as three numbers, such as 10:26:26, 19:19:19, 12:32:16, or 20:20:0. These numbers show the percentage of nitrogen, phosphate, and potash in the fertilizer.

The standard order is:

N - P2O5 - K2O

This means:

* First number = Nitrogen percentage
* Second number = Phosphorus expressed as P2O5 percentage
* Third number = Potassium expressed as K2O percentage

Example:

A 100 kg bag of 19:19:19 contains:

* 19 kg nitrogen
* 19 kg phosphate as P2O5
* 19 kg potash as K2O

---

## 1. What Is NPK?

NPK is a short form used to describe the three main nutrients supplied by many fertilizers.

### Simple Definition

NPK means nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium.

### Technical Definition

NPK fertilizers are fertilizers that contain one or more of the primary nutrients nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium, usually expressed on labels as N, P2O5, and K2O.

### Why NPK Is Important

NPK nutrients support:

* Leaf growth.
* Root growth.
* Flowering.
* Fruit development.
* Grain filling.
* Stem strength.
* Photosynthesis.
* Energy transfer.
* Water regulation.
* Crop yield.
* Crop quality.

### Farmer Guidance

NPK fertilizer should be selected based on soil test, crop requirement, crop stage, expected yield, irrigation system, and local agricultural recommendations.

---

## 2. Meaning of Fertilizer Grade

Fertilizer grade is the percentage of nutrients present in the fertilizer.

### Example: 10:26:26

A fertilizer grade of 10:26:26 means:

* 10% nitrogen
* 26% phosphate as P2O5
* 26% potash as K2O

If a farmer applies 100 kg of 10:26:26:

* Nitrogen supplied = 10 kg
* P2O5 supplied = 26 kg
* K2O supplied = 26 kg

### Example: 12:32:16

A fertilizer grade of 12:32:16 means:

* 12% nitrogen
* 32% phosphate as P2O5
* 16% potash as K2O

If a farmer applies 50 kg of 12:32:16:

* Nitrogen supplied = 50 × 12 ÷ 100 = 6 kg N
* P2O5 supplied = 50 × 32 ÷ 100 = 16 kg P2O5
* K2O supplied = 50 × 16 ÷ 100 = 8 kg K2O

### Farmer Guidance

The NPK numbers are percentages by weight. They are not the total fertilizer quantity.

---

## 3. Difference Between Nutrient and Fertilizer

Nutrient and fertilizer are related but not the same.

### Nutrient

A nutrient is the actual element or compound needed by the plant.

Examples:

* Nitrogen.
* Phosphorus.
* Potassium.
* Calcium.
* Magnesium.
* Sulfur.
* Zinc.
* Iron.
* Boron.

### Fertilizer

A fertilizer is a material that supplies nutrients to the crop.

Examples:

* Urea supplies nitrogen.
* DAP supplies nitrogen and phosphorus.
* MOP supplies potassium.
* 19:19:19 supplies nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium.
* Calcium nitrate supplies calcium and nitrogen.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not compare fertilizers only by bag weight. Compare them by how much actual nutrient they supply.

# Nitrogen

## 4. What Is Nitrogen?

Nitrogen is a primary nutrient needed for vegetative growth, chlorophyll formation, protein synthesis, and crop vigour.

### Main Functions of Nitrogen

* Promotes leaf growth.
* Supports chlorophyll formation.
* Improves green colour.
* Helps photosynthesis.
* Supports protein formation.
* Promotes tillering in cereals.
* Supports vegetative growth.
* Helps crop canopy development.
* Improves early crop vigour.

### Crops Need Nitrogen For

* Leafy growth.
* Stem growth.
* Tillering.
* Branching.
* Biomass production.
* Grain protein.
* Yield formation.

### Farmer Guidance

Nitrogen is important for growth, but excess nitrogen can create soft plants, pest attack, disease susceptibility, delayed flowering, lodging, and poor fruit quality.

---

## 5. Nitrogen Deficiency Symptoms

Nitrogen deficiency usually appears first on older leaves because nitrogen is mobile inside the plant.

### Common Symptoms

* Older leaves turn pale green.
* Older leaves become yellow.
* Slow plant growth.
* Reduced tillering.
* Thin stems.
* Small leaves.
* Poor canopy development.
* Early leaf drying.
* Poor yield.
* Reduced grain protein.
* Reduced leaf size in vegetables.

### Crop Examples

* Rice: yellow older leaves and poor tillering.
* Wheat: pale crop, reduced tillers.
* Maize: V-shaped yellowing from leaf tip on older leaves.
* Vegetables: pale green plants and slow growth.
* Fruit crops: poor shoot growth and small leaves.

### Farmer Guidance

If yellowing starts on older leaves, nitrogen deficiency may be possible. But also check waterlogging, root damage, sulfur deficiency, and general nutrient stress.

---

## 6. Nitrogen Excess Symptoms

Too much nitrogen can be harmful.

### Common Symptoms

* Very dark green leaves.
* Excessive vegetative growth.
* Soft and succulent plants.
* Delayed flowering.
* Delayed fruit maturity.
* Poor fruit colour.
* Low sugar content in some fruits.
* Lodging in cereals.
* Increased pest attack.
* Increased disease pressure.
* Poor storage quality.
* Nitrate accumulation in leafy vegetables.

### Farmer Guidance

More nitrogen does not always mean more yield. Balanced nutrition is better than heavy nitrogen use.

---

## 7. Common Nitrogen Fertilizers

### Urea

* Nutrient content: 46% nitrogen.
* Common use: basal or top dressing.
* Highly concentrated nitrogen source.
* Can lose nitrogen through volatilization if left on soil surface without incorporation or irrigation.

### DAP

* Nutrient content: commonly 18% N and 46% P2O5.
* Common use: basal application.
* Supplies nitrogen and phosphorus.
* Should not be overused only because it is popular.

### Ammonium Sulfate

* Supplies nitrogen and sulfur.
* Useful where sulfur is deficient.
* Acid-forming effect in soil over time.
* Contains lower nitrogen percentage than urea.

### Calcium Nitrate

* Supplies nitrogen and calcium.
* Commonly used in fertigation.
* Useful in vegetables and fruit crops.
* Should not be mixed with phosphate or sulfate fertilizers in the same stock tank.

### Potassium Nitrate

* Supplies nitrogen and potassium.
* Commonly used in fertigation.
* Useful during fruit development where potassium demand is high.

---

# Phosphorus

## 8. What Is Phosphorus?

Phosphorus is a primary nutrient important for root growth, energy transfer, flowering, seed formation, and early crop establishment.

In fertilizers, phosphorus is usually expressed as P2O5.

### Main Functions of Phosphorus

* Supports root development.
* Helps early crop establishment.
* Supports energy transfer inside the plant.
* Helps flowering.
* Supports seed formation.
* Helps cell division.
* Supports maturity.
* Improves nodulation in legumes when other conditions are suitable.
* Supports early vigour in seedlings.

### Farmer Guidance

Phosphorus is very important during early crop growth. It should often be placed near the root zone because phosphorus does not move easily in soil.

---

## 9. Phosphorus Deficiency Symptoms

Phosphorus deficiency can be difficult to identify because symptoms are not always clear.

### Common Symptoms

* Slow growth.
* Poor root development.
* Stunted plants.
* Delayed maturity.
* Poor flowering.
* Poor seed formation.
* Dark green leaves in some crops.
* Purplish or reddish leaf colour in some crops.
* Poor tillering.
* Weak seedlings.

### Crop Examples

* Maize: purplish older leaves in some conditions.
* Rice: poor tillering and stunted growth.
* Pulses: poor root growth and nodulation.
* Vegetables: weak seedlings and delayed growth.
* Fruit crops: poor flowering and weak root growth.

### Farmer Guidance

Phosphorus deficiency is best confirmed by soil testing because field symptoms can be unclear or confused with cold stress and root damage.

---

## 10. Phosphorus Excess Problems

Excess phosphorus can create nutrient imbalance.

### Possible Problems

* Zinc deficiency.
* Iron deficiency.
* Copper deficiency.
* Poor micronutrient availability.
* Unnecessary fertilizer cost.
* Environmental runoff risk.
* Algal growth in water bodies if phosphorus reaches water.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not apply high phosphorus every season without soil testing. Excess phosphorus can create hidden micronutrient problems.

---

## 11. Common Phosphorus Fertilizers

### DAP

* Common grade: 18:46:0.
* Supplies nitrogen and phosphorus.
* Often used as basal fertilizer.
* Popular in cereals, pulses, oilseeds, vegetables, and field crops.

### SSP

* Single Super Phosphate.
* Supplies phosphorus, sulfur, and calcium.
* Useful where sulfur is also required.
* Commonly used as basal fertilizer.

### MAP

* Mono Ammonium Phosphate.
* Often used in water-soluble form for fertigation.
* Supplies nitrogen and phosphorus.
* More suitable for fertigation when fully soluble grade is used.

### TSP

* Triple Super Phosphate.
* Supplies concentrated phosphorus.
* Does not supply sulfur like SSP.

### 12:61:00

* Water-soluble high-phosphorus fertilizer.
* Commonly used in fertigation.
* Often used during root development and early growth stages.

### 00:52:34

* Mono Potassium Phosphate.
* Supplies phosphorus and potassium.
* Commonly used during flowering and fruiting stages in fertigation.

---

# Potassium

## 12. What Is Potassium?

Potassium is a primary nutrient important for water regulation, enzyme activation, stress tolerance, sugar movement, fruit quality, grain filling, and plant strength.

In fertilizers, potassium is usually expressed as K2O.

### Main Functions of Potassium

* Regulates water movement in plants.
* Improves drought tolerance.
* Improves disease tolerance.
* Supports sugar transport.
* Improves fruit size.
* Improves fruit colour.
* Improves sweetness in many crops.
* Improves shelf life.
* Strengthens stems.
* Improves grain filling.
* Improves tuber development.
* Improves crop quality.

### Farmer Guidance

Potassium is very important during fruit development, grain filling, tuber formation, and stress periods.

---

## 13. Potassium Deficiency Symptoms

Potassium is mobile inside plants, so deficiency often appears first on older leaves.

### Common Symptoms

* Yellowing on older leaf edges.
* Leaf margin scorching.
* Brown leaf edges.
* Weak stems.
* Lodging in cereals.
* Poor drought tolerance.
* Poor disease tolerance.
* Small fruits.
* Poor fruit colour.
* Low sweetness.
* Poor grain filling.
* Poor tuber quality.
* Reduced shelf life.

### Crop Examples

* Banana: leaf margin yellowing and scorching.
* Tomato: poor fruit colour and quality.
* Potato: poor tuber size and quality.
* Rice and wheat: weak stems and poor grain filling.
* Cotton: leaf edge burning and poor boll development.

### Farmer Guidance

Potassium deficiency often looks like leaf-edge burning on older leaves. But similar burning can also occur due to salinity, drought, or pesticide injury.

---

## 14. Potassium Excess Problems

Too much potassium can interfere with other nutrients.

### Possible Problems

* Magnesium deficiency.
* Calcium deficiency.
* Nutrient imbalance.
* Poor fruit firmness in some cases due to calcium imbalance.
* Increased fertilizer cost.
* Salt stress if high rates are used.

### Farmer Guidance

Potassium improves quality, but excessive potassium can reduce calcium and magnesium uptake. Balance is important.

---

## 15. Common Potassium Fertilizers

### MOP

* Full name: Muriate of Potash.
* Nutrient content: commonly around 60% K2O.
* Contains chloride.
* Suitable for many field crops.
* Use carefully in chloride-sensitive crops.

### SOP

* Full name: Sulphate of Potash.
* Nutrient content: commonly around 50% K2O.
* Supplies potassium and sulfur.
* Lower chloride than MOP.
* Preferred in chloride-sensitive crops and many quality-sensitive horticultural crops.

### Potassium Nitrate

* Supplies potassium and nitrogen.
* Common in fertigation.
* Useful during fruit development.
* Fully soluble grades are used in drip irrigation.

### Mono Potassium Phosphate

* Also called MKP.
* Grade often 00:52:34.
* Supplies phosphorus and potassium.
* Common in fertigation during flowering and fruiting stages.

### 00:00:50

* Water-soluble potassium sulfate grade.
* Supplies potassium and sulfur.
* Used in fertigation for quality improvement stages.

# Understanding NPK Ratios and Grades

## 16. Balanced NPK Fertilizers

Balanced NPK fertilizers contain similar amounts of N, P2O5, and K2O.

### Examples

* 19:19:19
* 20:20:20
* 18:18:18
* 15:15:15

### Common Uses

* General crop growth.
* Early vegetative stage.
* Fertigation.
* Foliar feeding, where product is suitable.
* Nursery crops.
* Protected cultivation.

### Farmer Guidance

Balanced NPK does not mean it is correct for every crop stage. Crop nutrient demand changes over time.

---

## 17. High Nitrogen Fertilizers

High nitrogen fertilizers contain more nitrogen than phosphorus and potassium.

### Examples

* Urea.
* Calcium nitrate.
* Ammonium sulfate.
* NPK grades with high first number.

### Common Uses

* Vegetative growth.
* Leafy vegetables.
* Tillering stage.
* Early growth after establishment.
* Nitrogen deficiency correction.

### Farmer Guidance

High nitrogen should not be overused during flowering and fruiting because it may delay reproductive growth and reduce quality.

---

## 18. High Phosphorus Fertilizers

High phosphorus fertilizers contain more phosphorus than nitrogen and potassium.

### Examples

* DAP 18:46:0.
* 12:61:00.
* SSP.
* TSP.
* MAP.

### Common Uses

* Basal application.
* Early root development.
* Seedling establishment.
* Flower initiation.
* Legume establishment.
* Transplanting stage.

### Farmer Guidance

Phosphorus is most useful when placed near developing roots. Surface application may be less effective in some soils.

---

## 19. High Potassium Fertilizers

High potassium fertilizers contain more potassium than nitrogen and phosphorus.

### Examples

* MOP.
* SOP.
* 13:00:45.
* 00:52:34.
* 00:00:50.
* Potassium nitrate.

### Common Uses

* Fruit development.
* Grain filling.
* Tuber formation.
* Quality improvement.
* Sugar accumulation.
* Stress tolerance.
* Colour development.

### Farmer Guidance

High potassium fertilizers are often useful during fruiting, grain filling, and quality stages, but they should be balanced with calcium and magnesium.

---

## 20. Straight, Complex, Mixed, and Water-Soluble Fertilizers

### Straight Fertilizer

A straight fertilizer supplies mainly one primary nutrient.

Examples:

* Urea supplies nitrogen.
* MOP supplies potassium.
* SSP mainly supplies phosphorus, sulfur, and calcium.

### Complex Fertilizer

A complex fertilizer contains two or more nutrients chemically combined or granulated uniformly.

Examples:

* DAP.
* 10:26:26.
* 12:32:16.
* 20:20:0.

### Mixed Fertilizer

A mixed fertilizer is made by physically mixing two or more fertilizers.

### Water-Soluble Fertilizer

Water-soluble fertilizer dissolves in water and is commonly used in fertigation or foliar feeding.

Examples:

* 19:19:19.
* 12:61:00.
* 00:52:34.
* 13:00:45.
* Calcium nitrate.
* Potassium nitrate.

### Farmer Guidance

For drip fertigation, use only fully water-soluble fertilizers. Ordinary granular fertilizers can clog filters and drippers.

# NPK Fertilizer Calculations

## 21. Basic Formula

To calculate nutrient supplied by fertilizer:

Nutrient supplied = Fertilizer quantity × Nutrient percentage ÷ 100

### Example 1

If 100 kg of urea is applied:

* Urea contains 46% nitrogen.
* Nitrogen supplied = 100 × 46 ÷ 100 = 46 kg N.

### Example 2

If 50 kg of DAP is applied:

* DAP contains 18% N and 46% P2O5.
* Nitrogen supplied = 50 × 18 ÷ 100 = 9 kg N.
* P2O5 supplied = 50 × 46 ÷ 100 = 23 kg P2O5.

### Example 3

If 25 kg of MOP is applied:

* MOP contains about 60% K2O.
* K2O supplied = 25 × 60 ÷ 100 = 15 kg K2O.

---

## 22. Fertilizer Requirement Formula

To calculate fertilizer required:

Fertilizer required = Required nutrient ÷ Nutrient fraction in fertilizer

### Example 1: Urea Requirement

Crop needs 30 kg nitrogen.

Urea contains 46% nitrogen.

Fertilizer required = 30 ÷ 0.46 = 65.2 kg urea

### Example 2: MOP Requirement

Crop needs 30 kg K2O.

MOP contains 60% K2O.

Fertilizer required = 30 ÷ 0.60 = 50 kg MOP

### Example 3: DAP Requirement for Phosphorus

Crop needs 46 kg P2O5.

DAP contains 46% P2O5.

Fertilizer required = 46 ÷ 0.46 = 100 kg DAP

This 100 kg DAP also supplies 18 kg nitrogen.

### Farmer Guidance

When one fertilizer supplies more than one nutrient, subtract the supplied nutrient from the remaining fertilizer plan.

---

## 23. Understanding P2O5 and K2O

Fertilizer labels usually express phosphorus as P2O5 and potassium as K2O, not as elemental P and K.

### Conversion Factors

* P = P2O5 × 0.436
* P2O5 = P × 2.29
* K = K2O × 0.830
* K2O = K × 1.20

### Example

If a fertilizer supplies 46 kg P2O5:

* Elemental P = 46 × 0.436 = 20.1 kg P

If a fertilizer supplies 60 kg K2O:

* Elemental K = 60 × 0.830 = 49.8 kg K

### Farmer Guidance

Most fertilizer recommendations use N, P2O5, and K2O. Do not mix elemental P/K values with P2O5/K2O values unless converting correctly.

# Application Timing and Placement

## 24. Basal Application

Basal application means applying fertilizer before or at the time of sowing or transplanting.

### Common Basal Nutrients

* Phosphorus.
* Part of nitrogen.
* Part of potassium.
* Organic manure.
* Soil amendments.
* Micronutrients if required.

### Why Phosphorus Is Often Basal

Phosphorus moves slowly in soil, so it is often placed near the root zone early in the crop season.

### Farmer Guidance

Basal fertilizer should be placed safely. Direct contact between high fertilizer concentration and seed can reduce germination.

---

## 25. Top Dressing

Top dressing means applying fertilizer after crop emergence or during crop growth.

### Common Top-Dressed Nutrients

* Nitrogen.
* Potassium in some crops.
* Sulfur in some systems.

### Common Crop Stages

* Tillering.
* Vegetative growth.
* Knee-high stage in maize.
* Branching.
* Flowering support.
* Grain filling support.

### Farmer Guidance

Top dressing should be followed by irrigation or rainfall where appropriate to move nutrients into the root zone and reduce losses.

---

## 26. Split Application

Split application means applying fertilizer in multiple smaller doses.

### Benefits

* Reduces nutrient loss.
* Matches crop demand.
* Improves nutrient-use efficiency.
* Reduces salt injury.
* Reduces leaching.
* Reduces luxury consumption.
* Improves crop response.

### Common Split Nutrients

* Nitrogen.
* Potassium.
* Calcium.
* Magnesium.
* Water-soluble NPK in fertigation.

### Farmer Guidance

Nitrogen should often be split because it is mobile and can be lost through leaching, volatilization, denitrification, or runoff.

---

## 27. Fertigation

Fertigation is the application of fertilizer through irrigation water.

### Suitable Fertilizers

* Water-soluble NPK.
* Urea if dissolved and suitable.
* Calcium nitrate.
* Potassium nitrate.
* MAP.
* MKP.
* Magnesium sulfate.
* Chelated micronutrients.

### Advantages

* Small and frequent application.
* Better nutrient placement.
* Better nutrient-use efficiency.
* Suitable for drip irrigation.
* Useful for stage-wise nutrition.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not mix calcium nitrate with phosphate or sulfate fertilizers in the same stock tank. It can form precipitates and clog drip lines.

---

## 28. Foliar Feeding

Foliar feeding means applying nutrients on leaves through spray.

### Uses

* Quick correction of some deficiencies.
* Micronutrient application.
* Support during stress.
* Supplementary feeding.

### Limitations

* Cannot replace full soil nutrition.
* Leaf burn can occur.
* Not all nutrients absorb well through leaves.
* Spray concentration must be correct.
* Weather affects absorption.

### Farmer Guidance

Foliar feeding is a supplement, not a complete replacement for soil or fertigation nutrition.

---

# Soil Testing and Balanced Fertilization

## 29. Why Soil Testing Is Important

Soil testing helps identify available nutrients, pH, salinity, organic carbon, and soil problems.

### Soil Testing Helps Decide

* Whether fertilizer is needed.
* Which nutrient is deficient.
* Correct fertilizer dose.
* Soil pH correction.
* Salinity management.
* Micronutrient need.
* Organic matter improvement.
* Crop suitability.
* Fertilizer cost optimization.

### Farmer Guidance

Without soil testing, farmers may apply nutrients that are already sufficient and ignore nutrients that are actually deficient.

---

## 30. Balanced Fertilization

Balanced fertilization means applying nutrients in the right proportion according to crop demand and soil supply.

### Balanced Fertilization Includes

* Nitrogen.
* Phosphorus.
* Potassium.
* Secondary nutrients.
* Micronutrients.
* Organic matter.
* Soil amendments if needed.
* Correct timing.
* Correct placement.
* Correct irrigation management.

### Risks of Imbalanced Fertilization

* Low yield.
* Poor quality.
* Nutrient deficiency.
* Nutrient toxicity.
* Pest and disease susceptibility.
* Soil fertility decline.
* High cost.
* Environmental pollution.

### Farmer Guidance

NPK is important, but crops also need secondary nutrients and micronutrients. A good fertilizer plan is not only NPK.

---

## 31. The 4R Nutrient Stewardship Concept

The 4R concept means applying fertilizer using:

* Right source.
* Right rate.
* Right time.
* Right place.

### Right Source

Choose fertilizer that matches crop need, soil condition, irrigation system, and nutrient deficiency.

### Right Rate

Use correct dose based on soil test, crop requirement, and yield target.

### Right Time

Apply nutrients when the crop needs them most.

### Right Place

Place nutrients where roots can access them safely.

### Farmer Guidance

The same fertilizer can perform well or poorly depending on timing, dose, placement, and crop stage.

---

# Crop Stage NPK Guidance

## 32. Early Crop Stage

### Nutrient Focus

* Phosphorus for root growth.
* Moderate nitrogen for early growth.
* Balanced NPK for establishment.

### Suitable Fertilizer Types

* Basal NPK.
* DAP where recommended.
* SSP where sulfur and phosphorus are needed.
* 12:61:00 in fertigation.
* 19:19:19 in fertigation or foliar use if suitable.

### Farmer Guidance

Early crop nutrition supports root development and crop establishment.

---

## 33. Vegetative Stage

### Nutrient Focus

* Nitrogen for leaf and stem growth.
* Potassium for plant strength.
* Magnesium and sulfur for photosynthesis and protein formation.

### Suitable Fertilizer Types

* Urea.
* Ammonium sulfate.
* Calcium nitrate.
* Balanced NPK.
* Potassium nitrate where needed.

### Farmer Guidance

Avoid excess nitrogen that creates weak, lush growth.

---

## 34. Flowering Stage

### Nutrient Focus

* Balanced nitrogen.
* Adequate phosphorus.
* Potassium.
* Boron and calcium where required.

### Suitable Fertilizer Types

* MKP.
* Balanced NPK.
* Potassium nitrate.
* Crop-specific fertilizers.

### Farmer Guidance

Heavy nitrogen during flowering can reduce flowering and fruit set in some crops.

---

## 35. Fruit Development and Grain Filling Stage

### Nutrient Focus

* Potassium.
* Calcium in fruit crops.
* Magnesium.
* Controlled nitrogen.
* Balanced micronutrients.

### Suitable Fertilizer Types

* Potassium nitrate.
* SOP.
* MOP where suitable.
* 13:00:45.
* 00:00:50.
* Calcium nitrate.
* Magnesium sulfate.

### Farmer Guidance

Potassium is important for fruit size, sweetness, colour, grain filling, tuber bulking, and quality.

---

## 36. Maturity Stage

### Nutrient Focus

* Avoid excess nitrogen.
* Maintain potassium where needed.
* Support crop quality.
* Avoid late heavy fertilizer use unless recommended.

### Farmer Guidance

Late excess nitrogen may delay maturity and reduce quality in many crops.

# Crop-Wise NPK Notes

## 37. Rice

### NPK Importance

* Nitrogen supports tillering and leaf growth.
* Phosphorus supports root growth and early establishment.
* Potassium supports stem strength and grain filling.

### Deficiency Notes

* Nitrogen deficiency causes pale yellow crop and poor tillering.
* Phosphorus deficiency causes stunting and poor tillering.
* Potassium deficiency causes weak stems, leaf-edge drying, and poor grain filling.

### Farmer Guidance

Split nitrogen application is important in rice. Potassium helps reduce lodging and improves grain filling.

---

## 38. Wheat

### NPK Importance

* Nitrogen supports tillering and grain protein.
* Phosphorus supports root growth and early vigour.
* Potassium supports stem strength and stress tolerance.

### Farmer Guidance

Apply phosphorus as basal and split nitrogen according to crop stage and irrigation schedule.

---

## 39. Maize

### NPK Importance

* Nitrogen supports rapid vegetative growth.
* Phosphorus supports root growth and early vigour.
* Potassium supports stalk strength and grain filling.

### Farmer Guidance

Maize has high nitrogen demand during rapid growth. Potassium is important for stalk strength and drought tolerance.

---

## 40. Cotton

### NPK Importance

* Nitrogen supports vegetative growth and boll development.
* Phosphorus supports root growth and flowering.
* Potassium supports boll formation, fibre quality, and stress tolerance.

### Farmer Guidance

Too much nitrogen can cause excessive vegetative growth and delayed boll maturity. Potassium is important for cotton quality.

---

## 41. Soybean and Pulses

### NPK Importance

* Phosphorus supports root growth and nodulation.
* Potassium supports seed filling and stress tolerance.
* Nitrogen demand is partly met through biological nitrogen fixation when nodulation is effective.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not overapply nitrogen to legumes. Focus on phosphorus, potassium, sulfur, and micronutrients where deficient.

---

## 42. Sugarcane

### NPK Importance

* Nitrogen supports cane growth.
* Phosphorus supports root development.
* Potassium supports cane strength, sugar accumulation, and stress tolerance.

### Farmer Guidance

Sugarcane has high nutrient demand. Split application and fertigation can improve nutrient-use efficiency.

---

## 43. Tomato

### NPK Importance

* Nitrogen supports vegetative growth.
* Phosphorus supports roots and flowering.
* Potassium supports fruit size, colour, firmness, and quality.

### Farmer Guidance

During fruiting, potassium and calcium become very important. Excess nitrogen can delay flowering and reduce fruit quality.

---

## 44. Chilli and Capsicum

### NPK Importance

* Nitrogen supports canopy growth.
* Phosphorus supports root and flower development.
* Potassium supports fruit size, colour, pungency, and quality.

### Farmer Guidance

Balanced NPK is needed early, but potassium demand increases during fruit development.

---

## 45. Onion

### NPK Importance

* Nitrogen supports leaf growth.
* Phosphorus supports root growth.
* Potassium supports bulb size, storage quality, and disease tolerance.

### Farmer Guidance

Avoid late excess nitrogen in onion because it can reduce bulb storage quality.

---

## 46. Potato

### NPK Importance

* Nitrogen supports canopy growth.
* Phosphorus supports early root and tuber initiation.
* Potassium supports tuber size, dry matter, and quality.

### Farmer Guidance

Potassium is important for tuber quality. Excess nitrogen can delay tuber maturity.

---

## 47. Banana

### NPK Importance

* Nitrogen supports leaf growth.
* Phosphorus supports root growth.
* Potassium supports bunch development, fruit filling, and quality.

### Farmer Guidance

Banana has high potassium demand. Potassium deficiency can strongly affect bunch weight and fruit quality.

---

## 48. Grapes

### NPK Importance

* Nitrogen supports shoot growth.
* Phosphorus supports root growth and flowering.
* Potassium supports berry development, sugar accumulation, and quality.

### Farmer Guidance

Nutrient timing in grapes should match pruning, shoot growth, flowering, berry development, and post-harvest recovery.

# Common Mistakes in NPK Fertilizer Use

## 50. Mistakes Farmers Should Avoid

* Applying fertilizer without soil testing.
* Using only urea every season.
* Using DAP even when phosphorus is already high.
* Ignoring potassium.
* Ignoring sulfur, calcium, magnesium, and micronutrients.
* Applying all nitrogen at once.
* Applying fertilizer too close to seed.
* Applying fertilizer during drought stress.
* Applying fertilizer before heavy rain.
* Overusing high-nitrogen fertilizers during flowering.
* Using ordinary fertilizer in drip fertigation.
* Not considering soil pH.
* Not considering irrigation water quality.
* Mixing incompatible fertilizers.
* Copying another farmer’s fertilizer schedule without checking own soil.
* Believing more fertilizer always means more yield.

# Farmer Questions and Answers

## Q1. What does NPK mean?

NPK means nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. These are the three primary nutrients needed by crops in large amounts.

## Q2. What do fertilizer numbers like 19:19:19 mean?

The numbers show the percentage of nitrogen, phosphate, and potash in the fertilizer. A 19:19:19 fertilizer contains 19% N, 19% P2O5, and 19% K2O.

## Q3. What is the first number in NPK?

The first number is nitrogen percentage.

## Q4. What is the second number in NPK?

The second number is phosphorus expressed as P2O5 percentage.

## Q5. What is the third number in NPK?

The third number is potassium expressed as K2O percentage.

## Q6. What does nitrogen do in plants?

Nitrogen promotes leaf growth, green colour, chlorophyll formation, tillering, protein formation, and vegetative growth.

## Q7. What does phosphorus do in plants?

Phosphorus supports root development, early crop establishment, flowering, seed formation, energy transfer, and maturity.

## Q8. What does potassium do in plants?

Potassium supports water regulation, fruit quality, sugar movement, stress tolerance, stem strength, grain filling, and shelf life.

## Q9. Which nutrient is needed for leaf growth?

Nitrogen is most associated with leaf and vegetative growth.

## Q10. Which nutrient is needed for root growth?

Phosphorus is important for root development and early crop establishment.

## Q11. Which nutrient is important for fruit quality?

Potassium is very important for fruit size, colour, sweetness, firmness, and shelf life.

## Q12. Why are older leaves yellow?

Older leaves yellowing may indicate nitrogen deficiency, but it can also be caused by waterlogging, root stress, magnesium deficiency, or other problems.

## Q13. Why are leaf edges burning?

Leaf-edge burning on older leaves may indicate potassium deficiency, but salinity, drought, pesticide injury, or disease may also cause similar symptoms.

## Q14. Can too much nitrogen harm crops?

Yes. Excess nitrogen can cause lush growth, delayed flowering, lodging, pest attack, disease pressure, and poor fruit quality.

## Q15. Can too much phosphorus harm crops?

Yes. Excess phosphorus can reduce zinc, iron, or copper availability and may increase environmental runoff risk.

## Q16. Can too much potassium harm crops?

Yes. Excess potassium can interfere with magnesium and calcium uptake.

## Q17. Is 19:19:19 good for all crops?

19:19:19 is a balanced water-soluble fertilizer, but it is not suitable for every crop stage. Crop nutrient demand changes with growth stage.

## Q18. What is DAP used for?

DAP is commonly used as a basal fertilizer because it supplies nitrogen and phosphorus, especially useful for early root growth.

## Q19. What is urea used for?

Urea supplies nitrogen and is commonly used for vegetative growth and top dressing.

## Q20. What is MOP used for?

MOP supplies potassium and is used where potassium is needed. It contains chloride, so it should be used carefully in chloride-sensitive crops.

## Q21. What is SOP used for?

SOP supplies potassium and sulfur and is often preferred for chloride-sensitive or quality-sensitive horticultural crops.

## Q22. What is basal fertilizer?

Basal fertilizer is applied before or at sowing or transplanting to support early crop growth.

## Q23. What is top dressing?

Top dressing is fertilizer applied after the crop has emerged or during crop growth.

## Q24. Why should nitrogen be split?

Nitrogen is mobile and can be lost from soil. Splitting nitrogen improves efficiency and reduces losses.

## Q25. What is balanced fertilization?

Balanced fertilization means applying nutrients in the correct proportion based on crop requirement and soil nutrient status.

## Q26. Why is soil testing important before NPK application?

Soil testing helps identify which nutrients are low, sufficient, or high so fertilizer can be applied correctly and economically.

## Q27. Can NPK fertilizers be used in drip irrigation?

Only fully water-soluble NPK fertilizers should be used in drip irrigation. Ordinary granular fertilizers can clog the system.

## Q28. How much nitrogen is in 100 kg urea?

Urea contains 46% nitrogen, so 100 kg urea supplies 46 kg nitrogen.

## Q29. How much nutrient is in 50 kg DAP?

If DAP is 18:46:0, then 50 kg DAP supplies 9 kg nitrogen and 23 kg P2O5.

## Q30. How much K2O is in 25 kg MOP?

If MOP contains 60% K2O, then 25 kg MOP supplies 15 kg K2O.
