# Compost

## 1. Overview

Compost is a decomposed organic material prepared from crop residues, farm waste, animal dung, dry leaves, green biomass, kitchen waste, weeds, and other biodegradable materials. It is used as an organic fertilizer and soil amendment to improve soil health, nutrient availability, microbial activity, soil structure, and water-holding capacity.

Compost is not only a nutrient source but also a soil conditioner. It adds organic matter to the soil, supports beneficial microorganisms, improves soil aggregation, and helps crops tolerate stress better.

Compost is commonly used in:

* Field crops
* Vegetables
* Fruit crops
* Plantation crops
* Nursery production
* Organic farming
* Kitchen gardens
* Protected cultivation
* Soil reclamation
* Integrated nutrient management

## 2. Simple Definition

Compost is well-decomposed organic matter made from plant and animal waste. It is added to soil to improve fertility, soil structure, moisture retention, and biological activity.

## 3. Why Compost Is Important

Compost is important because many agricultural soils have declining organic carbon due to intensive cultivation, residue burning, erosion, low organic manure use, and excessive dependence on chemical fertilizers. Compost helps restore organic matter and improves long-term soil productivity.

### Compost Helps To

* Improve soil organic matter
* Improve soil structure
* Increase microbial activity
* Improve water-holding capacity
* Improve nutrient availability
* Reduce nutrient losses
* Improve root growth
* Reduce soil crusting
* Improve soil aeration
* Support balanced nutrient cycling
* Reduce dependency on only chemical fertilizers
* Recycle farm waste safely

## 4. Compost as Fertilizer and Soil Conditioner

Compost acts in two ways:

1. **As a nutrient source**: Compost supplies nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, sulphur, calcium, magnesium, and micronutrients in small to moderate quantities.
2. **As a soil conditioner**: Compost improves soil physical, chemical, and biological properties.

Compost usually releases nutrients slowly. Therefore, it is best used as part of **integrated nutrient management**, along with soil-test-based chemical fertilizers, biofertilizers, green manures, and crop residues.

## 5. Nutrient Content of Compost

The nutrient content of compost varies widely depending on raw materials, composting method, maturity, moisture, and storage.

### Common Nutrients Supplied by Compost

* Nitrogen
* Phosphorus
* Potassium
* Sulphur
* Calcium
* Magnesium
* Zinc
* Iron
* Manganese
* Copper
* Boron
* Molybdenum
* Organic carbon

### Important Note

Compost nutrient content is usually lower than chemical fertilizers, but compost improves soil health in ways that chemical fertilizers alone cannot.

### Why Compost Nutrient Content Varies

Compost nutrient value depends on:

* Type of raw material
* Amount of animal dung used
* Carbon-to-nitrogen ratio
* Composting temperature
* Turning frequency
* Moisture level
* Decomposition period
* Leaching during rain
* Addition of soil, rock phosphate, ash, or microbial culture
* Storage conditions

## 6. Carbon-to-Nitrogen Ratio

The carbon-to-nitrogen ratio, also called C:N ratio, is important for composting.

### What Is C:N Ratio?

C:N ratio means the amount of carbon compared with nitrogen in organic material. Carbon-rich materials provide energy for microbes, while nitrogen-rich materials help microbial growth and decomposition.

### Ideal C:N Ratio for Composting

A starting C:N ratio around **25:1 to 30:1** is commonly considered suitable for composting. Materials with too much carbon decompose slowly. Materials with too much nitrogen may produce bad smell and ammonia loss.

### Carbon-Rich Materials

* Dry leaves
* Straw
* Sawdust
* Dry crop residues
* Sugarcane trash
* Coconut husk
* Dry grass
* Paper or cardboard without toxic ink

### Nitrogen-Rich Materials

* Fresh green leaves
* Legume residues
* Fresh grass clippings
* Animal dung
* Poultry litter
* Kitchen vegetable waste
* Pulse crop residues
* Oilseed cakes, where recommended

## 7. Types of Compost

## 7.1 Farm Compost

Farm compost is prepared from farm wastes such as crop residues, weeds, straw, leaves, animal dung, and household biodegradable waste.

### Use

* Field crops
* Vegetables
* Fruit crops
* Soil organic matter improvement

## 7.2 Vermicompost

Vermicompost is prepared using earthworms and microorganisms. It is finer and often more nutrient-rich than ordinary farm compost.

### Common Earthworms Used

* Eisenia fetida
* Eudrilus eugeniae
* Perionyx excavatus

### Use

* Vegetables
* Nursery plants
* Fruit crops
* Flower crops
* Kitchen gardens
* High-value crops

### Caution

Vermicompost should not be exposed to direct hot sunlight. Earthworms need moist but not waterlogged conditions.

## 7.3 Enriched Compost

Enriched compost is compost improved by adding nutrient sources such as rock phosphate, zinc, biofertilizers, or microbial cultures.

### Examples

* Phospho-compost
* Zinc-enriched compost
* Bio-enriched compost
* Mineral-enriched compost
* Nitrogen-enriched compost

### Benefits

* Higher nutrient value
* Better phosphorus availability
* Improved microbial activity
* Better crop response than ordinary compost in some conditions

## 7.4 City Compost

City compost is made from segregated municipal biodegradable waste.

### Caution

Only use certified and quality-tested city compost. It should be free from plastic, glass, heavy metals, pathogens, and toxic materials.

## 7.5 NADEP Compost

NADEP compost is prepared in a brick tank using crop residues, dung slurry, soil, and water. It is an aerobic composting method widely promoted in India.

### Benefits

* Uses less dung
* Produces large quantity of compost
* Suitable for farm-level composting
* Good for dry regions if moisture is managed

## 7.6 Biodynamic Compost

Biodynamic compost is prepared using specific biodynamic preparations. It is mainly used in biodynamic and organic farming systems.

## 7.7 Co-Compost

Co-compost is prepared by composting two or more organic waste types together, such as crop residues plus animal manure, press mud plus dung, or poultry litter plus straw.

# 9. Composting Process

Composting is the controlled decomposition of organic materials by microorganisms. The process needs air, moisture, suitable carbon-to-nitrogen ratio, and proper pile size.

## 9.1 Main Requirements

* Organic raw materials
* Moisture
* Air or oxygen
* Microorganisms
* Suitable C:N ratio
* Proper particle size
* Shade or covering
* Turning or aeration
* Time for decomposition

## 9.2 Stages of Composting

### Stage 1: Initial Decomposition

Microorganisms begin breaking down easily decomposable materials such as sugars and soft plant tissues. Temperature begins to rise.

### Stage 2: Active Heating Stage

The compost pile becomes warm or hot due to microbial activity. This stage helps break down raw material and may reduce weed seeds and pathogens if temperature is high enough for sufficient time.

### Stage 3: Cooling Stage

As easily decomposable material reduces, temperature drops. Fungi and other organisms continue decomposing tougher material.

### Stage 4: Maturation or Curing

Compost stabilizes and becomes safe for crop use. Immature compost can harm plants, so curing is important.

# 10. Compost Preparation Method

## 10.1 Site Selection

Choose a composting site that is:

* Slightly elevated
* Well-drained
* Near water source
* Near raw material source
* Away from drinking water sources
* Protected from heavy rain runoff
* Protected from direct extreme sunlight if possible
* Accessible for turning and transport

## 10.2 Pit or Heap Preparation

Compost can be prepared in:

* Pit
* Trench
* Heap
* Brick tank
* Cement tank
* NADEP tank
* Windrow
* Plastic compost bin
* Vermicompost bed

## 10.3 Layering Method

A common farm composting method uses layers.

### Layer 1: Coarse Material

Place small branches, crop stalks, or coarse residues at the bottom for aeration.

### Layer 2: Dry Carbon Material

Add dry leaves, straw, husk, or dry crop residues.

### Layer 3: Green Material

Add green biomass, weeds before seed formation, vegetable waste, or fresh crop waste.

### Layer 4: Dung Slurry

Sprinkle cow dung slurry or compost culture to introduce microbes and nitrogen.

### Layer 5: Soil Layer

Add a thin soil layer to reduce smell and support microbial activity.

### Layer 6: Moisture

Sprinkle water to maintain moisture.

Repeat the layers until the pit or heap reaches the desired height.

## 10.4 Moisture Management

Moisture is very important for composting.

### Correct Moisture

The compost should feel moist like a squeezed sponge. It should not be dry and should not release excess water when squeezed.

### If Compost Is Too Dry

* Decomposition slows down.
* Microbial activity reduces.
* Composting takes longer.

### If Compost Is Too Wet

* Air spaces fill with water.
* Bad smell develops.
* Anaerobic decomposition occurs.
* Nutrient loss may increase.
* Flies may increase.

## 10.5 Turning

Turning mixes the compost and provides oxygen.

### Benefits of Turning

* Improves aeration
* Speeds decomposition
* Mixes dry and wet areas
* Reduces bad smell
* Helps uniform composting
* Prevents overheating
* Improves final quality

### Turning Frequency

Turning frequency depends on composting method, materials, moisture, and labor availability. Common practice is turning every 15 to 30 days in farm composting, while intensive composting may require more frequent turning.

## 10.6 Covering

Cover the compost heap or pit with:

* Dry leaves
* Straw
* Gunny sacks
* Polythene sheet, with aeration care
* Mud plaster in some traditional systems
* Shade net

### Benefits of Covering

* Conserves moisture
* Prevents nutrient leaching by rain
* Reduces flies
* Reduces smell
* Protects from excessive drying
* Prevents scattering by animals

# 11. Time Required for Composting

Composting time depends on materials, method, moisture, aeration, temperature, and particle size.

### Approximate Time

* Fast composting: 30 to 60 days under managed conditions
* Ordinary farm compost: 2 to 4 months
* Traditional pit compost: 3 to 6 months
* Vermicompost: often 45 to 90 days after pre-decomposition, depending on conditions
* Woody or coarse material: may take longer

### Factors That Speed Composting

* Chopping residues
* Maintaining moisture
* Proper C:N ratio
* Adding dung slurry
* Turning regularly
* Keeping pile warm
* Using microbial decomposer
* Protecting from heavy rain

# 12. Signs of Mature Compost

Mature compost is safe and ready for field application.

## 12.1 Visual and Physical Signs

* Dark brown to black color
* Earthy smell
* Crumbly texture
* Original raw materials not easily recognizable
* Cool temperature similar to surrounding air
* No foul smell
* No heating after turning
* No visible fresh waste
* Low fly activity

## 12.2 Immature Compost Signs

* Bad smell
* Heat still produced
* Fresh residues visible
* Sticky or slimy texture
* Ammonia smell
* Attracts flies
* Causes seedling burn
* Yellowing or poor plant growth after application

## 12.3 Why Mature Compost Matters

Immature compost may:

* Compete with crop roots for nitrogen
* Produce organic acids
* Cause seed germination problems
* Attract pests
* Spread pathogens or weed seeds
* Cause root injury

# 14. Limitations of Compost

Compost is beneficial, but it has limitations.

## 14.1 Low Nutrient Concentration

Compost contains lower nutrient concentration than chemical fertilizers. Large quantities may be needed to meet crop nutrient demand.

## 14.2 Slow Nutrient Release

Nutrients are released gradually. Compost may not meet immediate high nutrient demand unless combined with other fertilizers.

## 14.3 Variable Quality

Nutrient content and quality vary by raw material and preparation method.

## 14.4 Bulky Material

Compost is bulky and may require more labor for transport and application.

## 14.5 Risk of Contamination

Poor-quality compost may contain:

* Plastic
* Glass
* Weed seeds
* Pathogens
* Heavy metals
* Pesticide residues
* Excess salts

## 14.6 Immature Compost Risk

Immature compost can harm crops and reduce germination.

# 15. Compost Application Methods

## 15.1 Broadcasting

### Suitable For

* Field crops
* Cereals
* Pulses
* Oilseeds
* Fodder crops
* Soil improvement

## 15.2 Furrow Application

Compost is applied in furrows before planting.

### Suitable For

* Sugarcane
* Potato

* Cotton
* Vegetables
* Groundnut

## 15.3 Pit Application

Compost is mixed with soil in planting pits.

### Suitable For

* Fruit trees
* Plantation crops
* Forestry seedlings
* Banana
* Papaya
* Coconut
* Arecanut
* Mango
* Pomegranate
* Grapes

## 15.4 Ring Application

Compost is applied in a ring around the tree canopy area.

### Suitable For

* Fruit trees
* Coconut
* Arecanut
* Plantation crops

### Caution

Do not apply compost directly touching the trunk. Apply near the active feeder root zone.

## 15.5 Bed Application

Compost is mixed during raised-bed preparation.

### Suitable For

* Vegetables
* Nursery beds
* Flower crops
* Kitchen gardens

## 15.6 Potting Mixture

Compost is mixed with soil, sand, cocopeat, or other media.

### Suitable For

* Nursery seedlings
* Potted plants
* Kitchen gardens
* Terrace gardens

### Caution

Use mature and sieved compost. Immature compost can damage seedlings.

# 16. General Application Rates

Compost application rates depend on crop, soil type, compost quality, organic carbon status, nutrient requirement, and local recommendation.

### General Field Crop Range

* 2 to 5 tonnes per acre
* 5 to 12 tonnes per hectare

### Vegetable Crop Range

* 5 to 10 tonnes per acre
* 12 to 25 tonnes per hectare

### Fruit Crop Range

* Young plants: 5 to 20 kg per plant
* Bearing trees: 20 to 50 kg or more per tree, depending on crop age and canopy size

### Nursery or Potting Mix

* 10% to 30% mature compost in potting mixture, depending on crop sensitivity and compost quality

### Important Note

These are broad guidance ranges. Exact dose should follow local agriculture university recommendations, soil test, compost analysis, crop type, and nutrient management plan.

# 17. Crop-Wise Use of Compost

## 17.1 Rice / Paddy

Compost improves soil structure, microbial activity, and nutrient availability in paddy fields.

### Use

* Apply before puddling or land preparation.
* Incorporate well into soil.
* Combine with recommended NPK fertilizers.
* Use green manure and compost for long-term soil health.

### Farmer Advisory

Compost improves soil health but cannot replace all nitrogen requirement in high-yielding rice unless applied in very large and well-planned quantities.

## 17.2 Wheat

Compost supports soil structure and root growth in wheat.

### Use

* Apply during field preparation before sowing.
* Mix into soil.
* Combine with basal phosphorus and recommended nitrogen.

### Farmer Advisory

Compost improves fertilizer-use efficiency and soil moisture retention, especially in low-organic-carbon soils.

## 17.3 Maize

Maize benefits from compost because it has high nutrient demand and needs good root growth.

### Use

* Apply before sowing.
* Use with recommended NPK.
* Place in rows or broadcast and incorporate.

### Farmer Advisory

Compost alone may not meet maize nitrogen demand. Combine with soil-test-based fertilizers.

## 17.4 Cotton

Compost helps improve soil structure, root growth, and moisture retention in cotton fields.

### Use

* Apply during land preparation.
* Combine with recommended NPK, sulphur, and micronutrients.
* Useful in rainfed cotton for improving water-holding capacity.

### Farmer Advisory

Long-term compost use improves soil resilience in cotton-growing soils.

## 17.5 Sugarcane

Sugarcane responds well to organic matter addition.

### Use

* Apply compost or FYM in furrows before planting.
* Mix with soil before placing setts.
* Use with recommended NPK and micronutrients.

### Farmer Advisory

Compost helps improve soil structure and supports ratoon crop productivity.

## 17.6 Pulses

Compost improves soil health and supports nodulation indirectly by improving root-zone conditions.

### Use

* Apply before sowing.
* Combine with Rhizobium seed treatment and phosphorus.
* Avoid excess nitrogen-rich manure.

### Farmer Advisory

Mature compost supports root growth and beneficial microorganisms.

## 17.7 Oilseeds

Oilseeds benefit from compost because it improves sulphur and micronutrient availability.

### Use

* Apply before sowing.
* Combine with sulphur, phosphorus, and potassium as recommended.
* Use soil-test-based micronutrients.

### Farmer Advisory

Compost improves soil health, but oilseed crops may still require sulphur and boron where deficient.

## 17.8 Vegetables

Vegetables respond strongly to compost because of their high nutrient demand and shallow root systems.

### Use

* Mix compost during bed preparation.
* Use mature compost only.
* Combine with drip fertigation or split fertilizer application.
* Use sieved compost for nursery beds.

### Farmer Advisory

Avoid fresh or immature compost in vegetables because it can cause root injury, pests, and food-safety problems.

## 17.9 Fruit Crops

Compost is useful in orchards for improving soil organic matter and root-zone health.

### Use

* Apply in planting pits after mixing with soil.
* For established trees, apply around drip line or feeder root zone.
* Mulch after compost application where suitable.
* Combine with recommended fertilizers.

### Farmer Advisory

Do not dump compost against the trunk. Keep compost away from direct trunk contact to avoid collar rot and pest issues.

## 17.10 Nursery Crops

Compost can improve nursery media if it is mature, fine, and pathogen-free.

### Use

* Sieve compost before use.
* Mix with soil, sand, cocopeat, or other media.
* Avoid high rates in sensitive seedlings.
* Use sterilized or disease-free compost for nursery production.

# 20. Compost Storage

Compost quality can decline if stored poorly.

## 20.1 Storage Guidelines

* Store in shade.
* Keep slightly moist but not wet.
* Protect from heavy rain.
* Avoid direct sunlight for long periods.
* Cover with tarpaulin or straw.
* Keep away from pesticide storage.
* Avoid contamination with plastic, chemicals, or sewage.
* Use within a reasonable time after maturity.

## 20.2 Poor Storage Effects

* Nutrient leaching
* Drying and loss of microbial activity
* Weed contamination
* Rewetting and bad smell
* Fly breeding
* Loss of nitrogen
* Contamination by animals or waste

# 27. Common Mistakes in Compost Use

## 27.1 Using Immature Compost

Immature compost can cause seedling burn, nitrogen immobilization, bad smell, and poor crop growth.

## 27.2 Applying Compost Only on Surface in Dry Fields

Surface compost may not benefit roots quickly if not incorporated or irrigated.

## 27.3 Using Contaminated Compost

Compost made from mixed waste may contain plastic, heavy metals, glass, or chemicals.

## 27.4 Treating Compost as Complete Fertilizer

Compost is useful but may not supply enough nutrients for high-yield crops.

## 27.5 Poor Storage

Leaving compost in rain can leach nutrients.

## 27.6 Applying Fresh Manure as Compost

Fresh manure is not compost. It may contain pathogens, weed seeds, ammonia, and high salts.

## 27.7 Applying Too Close to Tree Trunk

Compost piled against tree trunks can increase moisture, pest, and disease risk.

# 28. Safety Precautions

## 28.1 Personal Safety

* Wear gloves while handling compost.
* Use a mask if compost is dusty.
* Wash hands after handling.
* Avoid handling compost with open wounds.
* Keep children away from compost pits.
* Use boots when working in large compost pits.
* Avoid inhaling moldy compost dust.

## 28.2 Crop Safety

* Use only mature compost.
* Avoid excessive compost in nursery media.
* Avoid saline compost in salt-sensitive crops.
* Avoid compost with visible plastic or chemical smell.
* Do a germination test for sensitive seedlings if compost quality is uncertain.

## 28.3 Environmental Safety

* Do not place compost pits near wells or drinking water sources.
* Prevent runoff from compost piles into ponds or streams.
* Avoid composting pesticide-contaminated residues.
* Avoid burning compostable crop residues.
* Use covers to prevent nutrient leaching by rain.

# 29. Simple Compost Germination Test

A simple germination test can help check compost maturity.

## Method

1. Mix one part compost with one part clean soil or sand.
2. Fill a small tray or pot.
3. Sow fast-germinating seeds such as mung bean, wheat, mustard, or cress.
4. Keep moist.
5. Observe germination and seedling growth for 3 to 5 days.

## Interpretation

* Good germination and healthy roots indicate mature compost.
* Poor germination, root browning, or stunted seedlings may indicate immature or toxic compost.

# 30. Farmer-Friendly Advisory

Compost is best used as a regular soil-health input. Apply mature compost before sowing, during land preparation, in planting pits, or around fruit trees. Mix it with soil and combine it with soil-test-based fertilizers. Do not use fresh waste or immature compost directly on crops. Good compost smells earthy, looks dark, and does not heat up after turning.

# 31. AI/RAG Query Examples

## Query: What is compost?

Compost is well-decomposed organic matter made from plant and animal waste. It is used as an organic fertilizer and soil conditioner to improve soil fertility, organic matter, water-holding capacity, microbial activity, and crop growth.

## Query: Is compost a fertilizer?

Yes. Compost is an organic fertilizer because it supplies nutrients, but it is also a soil conditioner because it improves soil structure, microbial activity, and water retention.

## Query: What are the benefits of compost?

Compost improves soil organic matter, soil structure, water-holding capacity, microbial activity, nutrient cycling, root growth, crop resilience, and long-term soil fertility. It also recycles farm waste and reduces residue burning.

## Query: Can compost replace chemical fertilizer?

Compost can partly reduce chemical fertilizer requirement, but it usually cannot fully replace chemical fertilizers for high-yield crops because its nutrient concentration is low and nutrients release slowly.

## Query: How to know compost is ready?

Compost is ready when it is dark brown or black, crumbly, earthy-smelling, cool, and the original raw materials are not easily recognizable. It should not smell bad or produce heat after turning.

## Query: What happens if immature compost is used?

Immature compost can cause seedling burn, poor germination, nitrogen immobilization, bad smell, pest attraction, and poor crop growth.

## Query: What materials can be used for compost?

Crop residues, dry leaves, green leaves, straw, weeds before seed formation, animal dung, vegetable waste, fruit waste, grass clippings, press mud, and other biodegradable farm waste can be used.

## Query: What materials should not be used in compost?

Plastic, glass, metal, rubber, pesticide-contaminated material, diseased plant waste, mature weeds with seeds, industrial waste, heavy-metal-contaminated waste, and non-biodegradable materials should not be used.

## Query: How much compost should be applied?

General rates may range from 2 to 5 tonnes per acre for field crops, 5 to 10 tonnes per acre for vegetables, and 5 to 50 kg per fruit tree depending on age and crop. Exact dose should follow soil test, crop requirement, compost quality, and local recommendation.

## Query: What is the difference between compost and vermicompost?

Compost is made by microbial decomposition of organic waste, while vermicompost is made with earthworms and microbes. Vermicompost is usually finer and often used at lower rates in vegetables, nurseries, and high-value crops.

## Query: Why does compost smell bad?

Bad smell usually means the compost is too wet, lacks oxygen, contains too much nitrogen-rich material, or is decomposing anaerobically. Turning, adding dry carbon material, and improving drainage can reduce smell.

## Query: Can compost improve sandy soil?

Yes. Compost improves sandy soil by increasing water-holding capacity, nutrient retention, microbial activity, and root-zone moisture.

## Query: Can compost improve clay soil?

Yes. Compost improves clay soil by improving aggregation, aeration, infiltration, drainage, and root growth.

# 32. Do and Don't

## Do

* Use mature compost.
* Apply compost before sowing or during land preparation.
* Mix compost into soil where possible.
* Use compost regularly to improve soil organic matter.
* Combine compost with soil-test-based fertilizers.
* Keep compost moist during preparation.
* Turn compost for aeration.
* Cover compost to prevent nutrient loss.
* Use clean raw materials.
* Store compost in shade.

## Don't

* Do not use immature compost.
* Do not compost plastic, glass, metal, or chemical waste.
* Do not use pesticide-contaminated residues.
* Do not apply compost directly touching tree trunks.
* Do not leave compost exposed to heavy rain.
* Do not assume compost supplies all nutrients.
* Do not use foul-smelling compost in nursery beds.
* Do not use compost with visible contamination.
* Do not allow compost runoff into wells, ponds, or streams.

# 34. Short Summary

Compost is well-decomposed organic matter made from plant and animal waste. It is used as an organic fertilizer and soil conditioner. Compost supplies nutrients slowly and improves soil organic matter, soil structure, water-holding capacity, microbial activity, nutrient cycling, and long-term fertility. Good compost is dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling, mature, and free from plastic, weed seeds, pathogens, and toxic contamination. Compost can be applied by broadcasting, furrow application, pit application, ring application, bed application, or potting mixture. It is useful in field crops, vegetables, fruit crops, nurseries, plantation crops, and organic farming. Compost should be used with soil-test-based fertilizers because it usually cannot fully replace chemical fertilizers in high-yielding crops.
