# Farmyard Manure

## 1. Overview

Farmyard manure, commonly called **FYM**, is a bulky organic manure prepared from animal dung, urine, urine-soaked bedding, shed litter, leftover feed, straw, dry leaves, and other farm waste. It is one of the oldest and most widely used organic manures in agriculture.

FYM supplies nutrients slowly and improves soil physical, chemical, and biological properties. It is useful not only as a fertilizer but also as a soil conditioner. Regular use of well-decomposed FYM improves soil organic matter, microbial activity, water-holding capacity, nutrient retention, and long-term soil fertility.

FYM is commonly used in:

* Field crops
* Vegetables
* Fruit crops
* Plantation crops
* Fodder crops
* Nursery crops
* Organic farming
* Integrated nutrient management
* Soil improvement programs

## 2. Simple Definition

Farmyard manure is decomposed animal dung and urine mixed with bedding material, straw, litter, and farm waste. It is applied to soil to improve fertility, organic matter, soil structure, and crop growth.

## 3. What FYM Contains

Farmyard manure contains:

* Animal dung
* Animal urine
* Urine-soaked bedding
* Straw
* Dry leaves
* Leftover feed
* Shed waste
* Soil particles
* Microorganisms
* Partly decomposed organic matter
* Small amounts of crop nutrients

## 4. Why FYM Is Important

Many agricultural soils have low organic carbon because of intensive cultivation, residue burning, erosion, low manure use, and continuous chemical fertilizer use without organic inputs. FYM helps restore soil organic matter and improves the long-term productivity of soil.

### FYM Helps To

* Add organic matter to soil
* Improve soil structure
* Improve water-holding capacity
* Improve aeration
* Improve root growth
* Increase microbial activity
* Improve nutrient cycling
* Reduce nutrient losses
* Improve fertilizer-use efficiency
* Support long-term soil fertility
* Recycle livestock waste safely
* Reduce dependence on only chemical fertilizers

## 5. FYM as Fertilizer and Soil Conditioner

FYM works in two major ways:

1. **As an organic fertilizer**: It supplies nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, sulphur, calcium, magnesium, and micronutrients in small to moderate quantities.
2. **As a soil conditioner**: It improves soil structure, soil organic carbon, microbial activity, and water retention.

FYM usually releases nutrients slowly. Therefore, it should be used with soil-test-based chemical fertilizers, biofertilizers, crop residues, compost, green manure, and micronutrients under integrated nutrient management.

## 6. Nutrient Content of FYM

The nutrient content of FYM varies widely depending on animal type, feed quality, bedding material, urine collection, decomposition method, storage, moisture, and nutrient losses.

### Approximate Average Nutrient Content

| Nutrient         | Approximate Content in Well-Decomposed FYM |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Nitrogen, N      | 0.4% to 0.8%                               |
| Phosphorus, P2O5 | 0.2% to 0.4%                               |
| Potassium, K2O   | 0.4% to 0.8%                               |
| Organic carbon   | Variable                                   |
| Micronutrients   | Present in small amounts                   |

### Important Note

These values are general estimates. Actual FYM quality should ideally be tested, especially for high-value crops or large-scale nutrient planning.

## 7. Why FYM Nutrient Value Varies

FYM quality depends on:

* Type of animal
* Animal feed
* Amount of urine collected
* Type of bedding material
* Amount of straw or litter used
* Collection method
* Composting or decomposition method
* Storage duration
* Exposure to sun and rain
* Leaching losses
* Nitrogen volatilization losses
* Moisture content
* Degree of decomposition
* Contamination with soil or waste

## 8. Importance of Animal Urine in FYM

Animal urine contains valuable nitrogen and potassium. In traditional systems, urine is often lost because it drains away from cattle sheds. This reduces the nutrient value of FYM.

### Why Urine Conservation Matters

* Urine contains easily available nitrogen.
* Urine contains potassium.
* Urine-soaked bedding increases manure value.
* Preventing urine loss improves FYM quality.
* Bedding material can absorb urine and reduce nutrient loss.

### Good Practice

Use bedding materials such as straw, dry leaves, sawdust, crop residues, or soil in animal sheds to absorb urine. Add the soaked material to the manure pit.

## 9. Types of Farmyard Manure

## 9.1 Cattle FYM

Prepared mainly from cow or bullock dung, urine, and bedding. It is the most common type of FYM in India.

### Features

* Moderate nutrient content
* Good soil-conditioning value
* Useful for field crops, vegetables, and orchards

## 9.2 Buffalo FYM

Prepared from buffalo dung, urine, and bedding.

### Features

* Similar to cattle FYM
* Nutrient content depends on feed and bedding
* Often moist and dense

## 9.3 Mixed Livestock FYM

Prepared from mixed dung and urine of cattle, buffalo, goats, sheep, or other farm animals.

### Features

* Nutrient content varies
* May be richer if sheep/goat droppings are included
* Should be well decomposed before application

## 9.4 Shed Manure

Collected from animal sheds where dung, urine, bedding, feed waste, and litter mix together.

### Features

* Better quality if urine is absorbed
* Needs proper decomposition
* Should be protected from rain

## 9.5 Partially Decomposed Manure

Manure that is not fully decomposed.

### Caution

Partially decomposed FYM may harm crops, attract pests, release ammonia, contain weed seeds, or cause nitrogen immobilization.

# 13. Preparation of Good FYM

## 13.1 Site Selection

Select a manure pit or storage area that is:

* Near the cattle shed
* Slightly elevated
* Protected from rainwater runoff
* Away from wells and drinking water sources
* Shaded if possible
* Easy to access for filling and removal
* Not prone to flooding

## 13.2 Collection of Materials

Collect:

* Dung
* Urine-soaked bedding
* Straw
* Dry leaves
* Shed litter
* Leftover feed
* Crop residues
* Animal shed sweepings

## 13.3 Pit Method

A common FYM preparation method is the pit method.

### Steps

1. Dig a pit or prepare a manure storage trench.
2. Add daily cattle shed waste, dung, urine-soaked litter, and bedding.
3. Keep the material moist but not waterlogged.
4. Cover the pit with soil, straw, or organic cover.
5. Protect from direct rain and runoff.
6. Allow decomposition for several months.
7. Turn or mix if required and practical.
8. Use only after the manure is well decomposed.

## 13.4 Heap Method

In the heap method, dung and bedding are collected in a compact heap.

### Steps

1. Select a shaded and well-drained place.
2. Spread a layer of dry absorbent material.
3. Add dung and urine-soaked litter.
4. Add more dry material if too wet.
5. Compact lightly to reduce excessive drying.
6. Cover with soil, straw, leaves, or tarpaulin.
7. Protect from rain and sun.
8. Allow decomposition until mature.

## 13.5 Improved FYM Preparation

Improved FYM preparation focuses on reducing nutrient losses.

### Improved Practices

* Use bedding to absorb urine.
* Collect dung and urine-soaked litter daily.
* Store manure in a covered pit.
* Protect manure from rain.
* Prevent leaching.
* Avoid leaving dung exposed in open sun.
* Keep manure moist but not waterlogged.
* Add crop residues and dry leaves.
* Cover with soil or straw.
* Use decomposer culture where recommended.
* Apply well-decomposed FYM to field.

# 14. Nutrient Losses from FYM

FYM can lose nutrients during collection, storage, and handling.

## 14.1 Main Types of Losses

* Loss of urine
* Leaching by rainwater
* Nitrogen loss as ammonia gas
* Runoff losses
* Loss of fine particles
* Over-drying in sun
* Poor storage
* Burning or overheating
* Washing away of manure nutrients

## 14.2 Nitrogen Loss

Nitrogen can be lost when manure is exposed to air, sun, high temperature, or alkaline conditions. Urine nitrogen is especially prone to loss as ammonia.

## 14.3 Potassium Loss

Potassium is highly soluble and may be lost through urine drainage or leaching.

## 14.4 How to Reduce Nutrient Loss

* Use bedding material to absorb urine.
* Store FYM under shade or cover.
* Prevent rainwater entry.
* Avoid keeping manure in open heaps for long periods.
* Compact heap lightly.
* Cover manure with soil or straw.
* Use pits or lined storage where possible.
* Apply FYM before monsoon only if it can be incorporated.
* Do not allow runoff from manure piles.

# 15. Maturity Signs of Good FYM

Well-decomposed FYM is safer and more useful for crops.

## 15.1 Signs of Well-Decomposed FYM

* Dark brown to black color
* Earthy smell
* Crumbly or soft texture
* Original bedding material partly decomposed
* No strong ammonia smell
* Not hot inside
* No visible fresh dung
* Lower fly activity
* Easy to mix with soil

## 15.2 Signs of Poor or Immature FYM

* Strong bad smell
* Fresh dung visible
* Heating inside heap
* Sticky or slimy material
* Flies and maggots
* Weed seeds visible
* Strong ammonia smell
* Causes seedling burn
* Poor crop response after application

## 15.3 Why Decomposition Is Important

Immature FYM may:

* Damage seedlings
* Release ammonia
* Attract termites or flies
* Contain viable weed seeds
* Spread pathogens
* Cause temporary nitrogen deficiency
* Increase root disease risk in sensitive crops

# 17. Limitations of FYM

## 17.1 Low Nutrient Concentration

FYM contains low nutrient concentration compared with chemical fertilizers. Large quantities are needed to supply major nutrients.

## 17.2 Slow Nutrient Release

Nutrients release slowly, so FYM cannot always meet immediate crop demand.

## 17.3 Bulky Material

FYM is bulky and needs more labor for transport, spreading, and incorporation.

## 17.4 Variable Quality

Nutrient content changes depending on animal, feed, storage, urine conservation, and decomposition.

## 17.5 Risk of Weed Seeds

Poorly decomposed FYM may contain viable weed seeds.

## 17.6 Pathogen Risk

Fresh or poorly decomposed FYM may contain harmful microorganisms.

## 17.7 Nutrient Loss During Storage

Poor storage can reduce FYM value through leaching and nitrogen volatilization.

## 17.8 Not a Complete Fertilizer for High-Yield Crops

FYM improves soil health, but high-yield crops usually need additional soil-test-based fertilizers.

# 18. FYM Application Methods

## 18.1 Broadcasting and Incorporation

FYM is spread uniformly over the field and mixed into the soil during land preparation.

### Suitable For

* Rice
* Wheat
* Maize
* Pulses
* Oilseeds
* Cotton
* Fodder crops
* Soil organic matter improvement

### Advantages

* Simple method
* Improves whole-field soil condition
* Suitable for field crops
* Can be combined with final land preparation

## 18.2 Furrow Application

FYM is applied in furrows before planting.

### Suitable For

* Sugarcane
* Potato
* Maize
* Cotton
* Vegetables
* Groundnut

### Advantages

* Places organic matter near root zone
* Useful for row crops
* Reduces quantity needed compared with full broadcasting
* Supports early root growth

## 18.3 Pit Application

FYM is mixed with soil in planting pits.

### Suitable For

* Mango
* Banana
* Papaya
* Coconut
* Arecanut
* Pomegranate
* Guava
* Citrus
* Grapes
* Plantation crops

### Caution

Do not place fresh or concentrated FYM directly against tender roots. Mix well with soil and use decomposed FYM.

## 18.4 Ring Application

FYM is applied in a ring or basin around the tree canopy.

### Suitable For

* Fruit trees
* Coconut
* Arecanut
* Plantation crops

### Caution

Apply near the active feeder root zone, not directly touching the trunk.

## 18.5 Bed Application

FYM is mixed into raised beds before sowing or transplanting.

### Suitable For

* Vegetables
* Floriculture
* Nursery beds
* Kitchen gardens
* Protected cultivation

### Caution

Use only well-decomposed FYM in vegetable and nursery beds.

# 19. General Application Rates

FYM application rate depends on crop, soil fertility, FYM quality, organic carbon status, irrigation, and yield target.

### General Field Crops

* 5 to 10 tonnes per hectare
* Around 2 to 4 tonnes per acre

### Vegetables

* 10 to 25 tonnes per hectare
* Around 4 to 10 tonnes per acre

### Sugarcane and Banana

* 20 to 50 tonnes per hectare depending on soil and local recommendation

### Fruit Trees

* Young plants: 5 to 20 kg per plant
* Bearing trees: 20 to 50 kg or more per tree depending on age, canopy, and crop

### Nursery and Potting Mix

* Use well-decomposed and sieved FYM at low to moderate proportion
* Avoid excessive FYM in sensitive seedlings

### Important Note

These are broad ranges. Exact dose should follow soil test, crop recommendation, local agriculture university guidance, and FYM quality.

# 20. Best Time to Apply FYM

## 20.1 Before Sowing

Apply FYM during land preparation and incorporate into soil.

## 20.2 Before Transplanting

Apply FYM before transplanting vegetables, paddy, or nursery-raised crops.

## 20.3 Before Monsoon

In rainfed crops, FYM is often applied before monsoon and incorporated.

## 20.4 Before Orchard Flush or Active Growth

In fruit crops, apply FYM before active root growth or before rainy season, depending on crop and region.

## 20.5 Avoid

* Applying fresh FYM immediately before sowing
* Leaving FYM exposed on soil surface for long periods
* Applying FYM before heavy rainfall without incorporation
* Applying FYM directly touching seedlings or tree trunks

# 21. Crop-Wise FYM Use

## 21.1 Rice / Paddy

FYM improves soil organic matter and supports microbial activity in paddy fields.

### Use

* Apply before puddling or field preparation.
* Incorporate well.
* Combine with recommended NPK fertilizers.
* Use with green manure where available.

### Farmer Advisory

FYM improves soil health but should not fully replace nitrogen fertilizer in high-yielding rice unless nutrient planning is done carefully.

## 21.2 Wheat

FYM improves soil structure, root growth, and moisture retention.

### Use

* Apply before sowing during field preparation.
* Incorporate into soil.
* Combine with basal phosphorus and recommended nitrogen.

### Farmer Advisory

FYM is useful in low-organic-carbon soils and improves response to chemical fertilizers.

## 21.3 Maize

Maize benefits from FYM because it has high nutrient demand and needs good early root growth.

### Use

* Apply before sowing.
* Use with recommended NPK.
* Band or furrow application can be useful in row-planted maize.

### Farmer Advisory

FYM alone is usually not enough for high-yielding maize. Use integrated nutrient management.

## 21.4 Cotton

Cotton benefits from FYM in both irrigated and rainfed conditions.

### Use

* Apply before sowing.
* Incorporate during land preparation.
* Combine with recommended NPK, sulphur, and micronutrients.

### Farmer Advisory

FYM improves moisture retention and root-zone conditions in rainfed cotton.

## 21.5 Sugarcane

Sugarcane responds well to bulky organic manures.

### Use

* Apply FYM in furrows before planting.
* Mix with soil before placing cane setts.
* Combine with recommended NPK fertilizers.

### Farmer Advisory

FYM improves cane establishment and ratoon crop performance.

## 21.6 Pulses

Pulses benefit from FYM because it improves root growth and soil biological activity.

### Use

* Apply well-decomposed FYM before sowing.
* Combine with phosphorus and Rhizobium seed treatment.
* Avoid excessive nitrogen-rich manure.

### Farmer Advisory

FYM supports nodulation indirectly by improving root-zone conditions.

## 21.7 Oilseeds

FYM improves soil health and supports nutrient availability.

### Use

* Apply before sowing.
* Combine with sulphur, phosphorus, potassium, and micronutrients where needed.

### Farmer Advisory

Oilseed crops often respond to sulphur and boron; FYM should be part of balanced nutrition.

## 21.8 Vegetables

Vegetables respond strongly to FYM because of high nutrient demand and shallow roots.

### Use

* Apply well-decomposed FYM during bed preparation.
* Mix thoroughly with soil.
* Combine with fertigation or split fertilizer application.
* Use only mature FYM for nursery beds.

### Farmer Advisory

Never use fresh FYM immediately before planting vegetables because it may cause root burn, pests, weeds, and food-safety risk.

## 21.9 Fruit Crops

FYM is important in orchard nutrient management.

### Use

* Mix FYM with soil in planting pits.
* For established trees, apply FYM in ring or basin near active root zone.
* Apply before rainy season or active growth stage.
* Combine with recommended chemical fertilizers.

### Farmer Advisory

Do not dump FYM against the trunk. Keep manure away from direct trunk contact to avoid collar rot and pest problems.

## 21.10 Nursery Crops

FYM can be used in nursery media only if it is well decomposed, sieved, and safe.

### Use

* Mix with soil, sand, cocopeat, or compost.
* Use at moderate rate.
* Avoid fresh FYM.
* Sterilize or solarize nursery media where disease risk is high.

# 22. FYM and Integrated Nutrient Management

FYM is best used as part of integrated nutrient management.

## 22.1 Integrated Sources

* FYM
* Compost
* Vermicompost
* Green manure
* Biofertilizers
* Crop residues
* Chemical fertilizers
* Micronutrients
* Soil amendments

## 22.2 Why Integration Is Better

* FYM improves soil health.
* Chemical fertilizers provide precise nutrients.
* Biofertilizers improve nutrient availability.
* Green manures add biomass and nitrogen.
* Micronutrients correct specific deficiencies.
* Soil testing avoids overuse and deficiency.

## 22.3 FYM Cannot Always Replace Chemical Fertilizers

FYM can partly reduce chemical fertilizer need, but complete replacement is difficult in high-yield crops because FYM has low nutrient concentration and slow nutrient release.

# 24. FYM Storage

Proper storage preserves FYM quality.

## 24.1 Storage Guidelines

* Store in a covered pit or heap.
* Protect from direct rain.
* Protect from strong sunlight.
* Keep moist but not waterlogged.
* Prevent runoff.
* Use bedding to conserve urine.
* Keep away from water sources.
* Cover with soil, straw, leaves, or tarpaulin.
* Avoid mixing with plastic or chemicals.

## 24.2 Poor Storage Effects

* Nitrogen loss
* Potassium leaching
* Bad smell
* Fly breeding
* Weed contamination
* Reduced nutrient value
* Pathogen survival
* Runoff pollution

# 27. FYM and Disease/Pest Considerations

FYM is not a pesticide, but it can improve plant health through better soil conditions. However, poor-quality or fresh FYM can create pest and disease problems.

## 27.1 Possible Benefits

* Better root health
* Stronger crop growth
* Better microbial activity
* Improved soil balance
* Better resilience against stress

## 27.2 Possible Risks

* Termite attraction if undecomposed straw is present
* Weed seed spread
* Root rot risk in poorly drained soils
* Pathogen risk from fresh manure
* Fly breeding
* Collar rot if piled around tree trunks

# 28. Food Safety Caution

Fresh manure may contain harmful microorganisms. This is especially important in vegetables, leafy greens, fruits touching soil, and crops eaten raw.

### Safe Use Practices

* Use well-decomposed FYM.
* Apply before planting, not close to harvest.
* Incorporate into soil.
* Avoid splashing manure onto edible plant parts.
* Wash produce properly.
* Follow organic certification or food-safety rules where applicable.

# 29. Common Mistakes in FYM Use

## 29.1 Using Fresh FYM

Fresh FYM can damage seedlings and spread weeds or pathogens.

## 29.2 Leaving FYM Exposed to Rain

Rain leaches nutrients and reduces manure value.

## 29.3 Losing Urine

Urine loss greatly reduces nitrogen and potassium value.

## 29.4 Applying FYM Too Late

FYM releases nutrients slowly. It should be applied before planting or early enough for decomposition.

## 29.5 Treating FYM as Complete Fertilizer

FYM alone may not supply enough nutrients for high-yield crops.

## 29.6 Applying FYM Directly to Tree Trunk

This can increase moisture and disease risk around the trunk.

## 29.7 Using Contaminated FYM

FYM contaminated with plastic, chemicals, pesticides, or industrial waste can harm soil and crops.

# 30. Simple FYM Maturity Test

A simple field test can help judge FYM maturity.

## Method

1. Take a handful of FYM.
2. Check color and smell.
3. Squeeze it gently.
4. Observe whether it is hot, foul-smelling, or sticky.
5. Mix some FYM with soil and sow a few fast-germinating seeds such as mung, wheat, or mustard.

## Good FYM Result

* Earthy smell
* Dark color
* No heating
* Crumbly texture
* Good seed germination
* Healthy roots

## Poor FYM Result

* Bad smell
* Heating
* Poor germination
* Root browning
* Fly attraction
* Fresh dung visible

# 31. Farmer-Friendly Advisory

Farmyard manure should be well decomposed before field application. Apply FYM during land preparation and mix it into the soil. Protect FYM from rain and sunlight during storage to reduce nutrient losses. Use bedding material in animal sheds to absorb urine because urine contains valuable nitrogen and potassium. FYM improves soil health but should be combined with soil-test-based fertilizers for better yield.

# 32. AI/RAG Query Examples

## Query: What is farmyard manure?

Farmyard manure, or FYM, is decomposed animal dung, urine, bedding material, shed litter, straw, and farm waste. It is used as an organic fertilizer and soil conditioner.

## Query: What is FYM made from?

FYM is made from cattle or livestock dung, urine, urine-soaked bedding, straw, leftover feed, shed litter, dry leaves, and other farm waste.

## Query: What are the benefits of FYM?

FYM improves soil organic matter, soil structure, water-holding capacity, microbial activity, nutrient cycling, root growth, fertilizer-use efficiency, and long-term soil fertility.

## Query: What nutrients are present in FYM?

FYM contains nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, sulphur, calcium, magnesium, micronutrients, and organic carbon. Nutrient content varies depending on animal type, feed, bedding, urine conservation, and storage.

## Query: Can FYM replace chemical fertilizer?

FYM can partly reduce chemical fertilizer requirement, but it usually cannot fully replace chemical fertilizers in high-yield crops because its nutrient concentration is low and nutrients release slowly.

## Query: How much FYM should be applied?

General FYM rates are about 5 to 10 tonnes per hectare for field crops, 10 to 25 tonnes per hectare for vegetables, and 5 to 50 kg per fruit tree depending on age and crop. Exact dose should follow soil test and local recommendation.

## Query: When should FYM be applied?

FYM should usually be applied before sowing or transplanting during land preparation. It should be incorporated into soil before planting.

## Query: Is fresh cow dung same as FYM?

No. Fresh cow dung is not the same as well-decomposed FYM. Fresh dung can harm seedlings, spread pathogens, and contain weed seeds. FYM should be decomposed before use.

## Query: Why should FYM be covered during storage?

FYM should be covered to reduce nitrogen loss, prevent nutrient leaching by rain, conserve moisture, reduce flies, and maintain manure quality.

## Query: Why is urine important in FYM?

Animal urine contains valuable nitrogen and potassium. If urine is lost from cattle sheds, the nutrient value of FYM decreases.

## Query: How to identify good FYM?

Good FYM is dark brown or black, earthy-smelling, crumbly, cool, and well decomposed. It should not contain fresh dung, bad smell, plastic, or weed seeds.

## Query: Can FYM be used in vegetables?

Yes. Well-decomposed FYM is useful in vegetables, but fresh FYM should not be used immediately before planting because it may cause root burn, weeds, pests, and food-safety risk.

## Query: What is the difference between FYM and compost?

FYM is mainly made from animal dung, urine, bedding, and shed waste. Compost can be made from many types of plant and animal organic waste. Both improve soil health, but their composition and preparation differ.

* Use well-decomposed FYM.
* Apply FYM before sowing or transplanting.
* Mix FYM into soil.
* Store FYM under cover.
* Use bedding to absorb animal urine.
* Protect FYM from rainwater.
* Combine FYM with soil-test-based fertilizers.
* Use clean and uncontaminated manure.
* Apply near active root zone in fruit crops.
* Keep manure records where possible.

## Don't

* Do not use fresh dung directly on sensitive crops.
* Do not leave FYM exposed to rain and sun.
* Do not allow urine to drain away from cattle sheds.
* Do not apply FYM directly touching tree trunks.
* Do not use FYM contaminated with plastic, chemicals, or industrial waste.
* Do not assume FYM supplies all crop nutrients.
* Do not apply immature FYM in nursery beds.
* Do not dump FYM near wells, ponds, or streams.
* Do not harvest raw-eaten vegetables soon after fresh manure application.
* Do not spread FYM without considering soil test and crop needs.

# 35. Short Summary

Farmyard manure, or FYM, is a bulky organic manure made from decomposed animal dung, urine, bedding, shed litter, straw, and farm waste. It supplies small to moderate amounts of nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, sulphur, micronutrients, and organic carbon. FYM improves soil structure, water-holding capacity, microbial activity, nutrient retention, root growth, and long-term soil fertility. Good FYM should be well decomposed, dark, earthy-smelling, cool, and free from fresh dung, plastic, weed seeds, and contamination. FYM should be stored under cover to reduce nutrient losses from leaching and ammonia volatilization. It is best applied before sowing or transplanting and incorporated into soil. FYM improves soil health but should be combined with soil-test-based fertilizers for high-yield crops.
