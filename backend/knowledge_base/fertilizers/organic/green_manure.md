# Green Manure

## Overview

Green manure is an organic soil fertility practice where a crop is grown mainly to be incorporated into the soil or used as surface mulch to improve soil health. Green manure crops are usually grown before the main crop, between two main crops, during fallow periods, or in orchards between rows.

Green manure improves soil organic matter, nutrient cycling, soil structure, microbial activity, water-holding capacity, and long-term soil fertility. Legume green manure crops can also fix atmospheric nitrogen through root nodules when suitable rhizobium bacteria are present.

Green manure is an important part of organic farming, natural farming, regenerative agriculture, conservation agriculture, integrated nutrient management, and sustainable crop production.

---

## 1. What Is Green Manure?

Green manure means growing plants and then incorporating their fresh biomass into the soil, or using the biomass as mulch, to improve soil fertility and soil health.

### Simple Definition

Green manure is a crop grown to feed the soil instead of being harvested for grain, fruit, or sale.

### Technical Definition

Green manure is a crop, often a legume or fast-growing cover crop, grown for the purpose of adding organic matter, nutrients, and biological activity to the soil when it is incorporated, mulched, or decomposed.

### Main Purpose

The main purpose of green manure is to improve soil fertility and soil structure naturally.

### Farmer Guidance

Green manure should be selected according to season, soil type, rainfall, irrigation availability, cropping system, time available before the main crop, and purpose such as nitrogen addition, weed suppression, erosion control, or soil improvement.

---

## 2. Difference Between Green Manure and Farmyard Manure

| Point           | Green Manure                            | Farmyard Manure                              |
| --------------- | --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Source          | Fresh plant biomass grown in field      | Animal dung, urine, bedding, and waste       |
| Application     | Grown and incorporated or mulched       | Collected, decomposed, and applied           |
| Main benefit    | Adds fresh organic matter and nutrients | Adds decomposed organic matter and nutrients |
| Nitrogen source | Legumes can fix atmospheric nitrogen    | Depends on animal feed and storage           |
| Transport need  | Usually grown in same field             | Needs transport to field                     |
| Decomposition   | Decomposes after incorporation          | Already partly decomposed                    |
| Best use        | Soil improvement before main crop       | Basal organic nutrient source                |

### Farmer Guidance

Green manure and farmyard manure are complementary. Green manure improves in-field biomass and soil biology, while farmyard manure adds decomposed organic matter and nutrients.

---

## 3. Difference Between Green Manure and Cover Crop

Green manure and cover crop are closely related terms.

### Green Manure

A green manure crop is grown mainly to be incorporated into the soil or decomposed to add nutrients and organic matter.

### Cover Crop

A cover crop is grown mainly to cover the soil, prevent erosion, suppress weeds, reduce nutrient leaching, and improve soil structure.

### Important Point

The same crop can act as a cover crop first and green manure later. For example, a legume crop can cover the soil during fallow and later be incorporated before sowing the main crop.

### Farmer Guidance

If the crop is mainly protecting soil, it is called a cover crop. If it is incorporated to feed the soil, it is called green manure. Many crops do both.

# Benefits of Green Manure

## 4. Soil Fertility Improvement

Green manure improves soil fertility by adding organic matter and recycling nutrients.

### Benefits

* Adds fresh plant biomass to soil.
* Improves nutrient cycling.
* Increases soil microbial activity.
* Improves nitrogen availability when legumes are used.
* Helps release nutrients gradually.
* Reduces dependence on chemical fertilizers.
* Improves long-term soil productivity.

### Farmer Guidance

Green manure is not a complete replacement for all fertilizers in high-yielding crops, but it can reduce fertilizer requirement and improve fertilizer efficiency.

---

## 5. Nitrogen Addition

Legume green manure crops can add nitrogen to the soil through biological nitrogen fixation.

### How It Works

Legume roots form nodules with rhizobium bacteria. These bacteria convert atmospheric nitrogen into forms that plants can use. When the green manure crop decomposes, part of this nitrogen becomes available to the next crop.

### Legume Green Manure Crops

* Sunn hemp.
* Dhaincha.
* Cowpea.
* Green gram.
* Black gram.
* Sesbania.
* Cluster bean.
* Horse gram.
* Berseem.
* Lucerne.
* Stylosanthes.
* Fava bean.
* Vetch.
* Clover.

### Farmer Guidance

Legume green manure gives best nitrogen benefit when nodulation is good, crop growth is healthy, and biomass is incorporated before the stems become too woody.

---

## 6. Organic Matter Addition

Green manure adds organic residues to the soil.

### Effects of Organic Matter

* Improves soil structure.
* Improves aggregation.
* Improves water-holding capacity.
* Improves aeration.
* Improves root penetration.
* Supports beneficial microorganisms.
* Improves nutrient retention.
* Reduces crusting.
* Reduces erosion risk.

### Farmer Guidance

Regular green manuring over multiple seasons gives better soil improvement than one-time use.

---

## 7. Soil Structure Improvement

Green manure improves soil physical condition.

### Benefits

* Makes heavy soil more friable.
* Helps sandy soil hold more water and nutrients.
* Reduces surface crusting.
* Improves root-zone aeration.
* Supports earthworms.
* Reduces compaction over time.
* Improves infiltration.
* Reduces runoff.

### Deep-Rooted Green Manure Crops

* Sunn hemp.
* Sesbania.
* Cowpea.
* Lupin.
* Mustard.
* Radish-type cover crops.
* Sorghum-sudan grass.

### Farmer Guidance

Deep-rooted green manure crops can help open compacted soil layers, but severe hardpan may still need mechanical correction.

---

## 8. Weed Suppression

Green manure can reduce weed growth by covering the soil, shading weed seedlings, and competing for space.

### Weed Suppression Mechanisms

* Fast canopy cover.
* Shading of soil surface.
* Competition for light and nutrients.
* Smothering effect.
* Mulch after cutting.
* Reduced weed seed germination.
* Some crops may have allelopathic effects.

### Good Weed-Suppressing Green Manure Crops

* Sunn hemp.
* Cowpea.
* Sesbania.
* Sorghum-sudan grass.
* Mustard.
* Buckwheat.
* Rye.
* Oats.
* Pearl millet.

### Farmer Guidance

Green manure suppresses weeds best when it establishes quickly and covers the soil before weeds dominate.

---

## 9. Erosion Control

Green manure protects soil from wind and water erosion.

### How Green Manure Reduces Erosion

* Covers bare soil.
* Reduces raindrop impact.
* Holds soil with roots.
* Slows runoff.
* Improves infiltration.
* Adds mulch after cutting.
* Reduces soil loss on slopes.

### Farmer Guidance

On sloping land, green manure or cover crops should be part of soil-conservation planning.

---

## 10. Moisture Conservation

Green manure can help conserve soil moisture, especially when used as surface mulch.

### Benefits

* Reduces evaporation.
* Improves infiltration.
* Adds organic matter.
* Reduces soil temperature extremes.
* Improves water-holding capacity over time.

### Important Note

A growing green manure crop also uses water. In dry areas, moisture availability must be considered before growing green manure.

### Farmer Guidance

In rainfed areas, green manure should be timed carefully so it does not use too much moisture needed for the next crop.

---

## 11. Nutrient Recycling

Green manure crops absorb nutrients from soil and return them to the surface layer when decomposed.

### Nutrients Recycled

* Nitrogen.
* Phosphorus.
* Potassium.
* Sulfur.
* Calcium.
* Magnesium.
* Micronutrients.

### Deep Nutrient Capture

Deep-rooted green manure crops can absorb nutrients from deeper soil layers and return them to the topsoil through biomass decomposition.

### Farmer Guidance

Green manure does not create all nutrients from nothing. Legumes can add nitrogen, but other nutrients are mostly recycled from soil reserves.

---

## 12. Soil Biological Activity

Green manure feeds soil microorganisms.

### Biological Benefits

* Increases microbial biomass.
* Supports beneficial fungi and bacteria.
* Improves decomposition.
* Improves nutrient cycling.
* Supports earthworms.
* Improves soil food web.
* Can help disease-suppressive soil development over time.

### Farmer Guidance

Soil organisms need food. Green manure provides fresh organic material that supports active soil life.

# Types of Green Manure

## 13. Legume Green Manure

Legume green manures are crops from the legume family that can fix nitrogen through rhizobium bacteria.

### Examples

* Sunn hemp.
* Dhaincha.
* Cowpea.
* Green gram.
* Black gram.
* Horse gram.
* Cluster bean.
* Berseem.
* Lucerne.
* Clover.
* Vetch.
* Fava bean.
* Stylosanthes.
* Sesbania.

### Benefits

* Adds nitrogen.
* Improves soil organic matter.
* Supports beneficial microbes.
* Improves soil fertility.
* Often decomposes faster than cereal residues.
* Useful before cereals, vegetables, cotton, sugarcane, and rice.

### Limitations

* Requires suitable rhizobium.
* May perform poorly in very acidic, saline, or waterlogged conditions depending on species.
* Can host some pests or diseases if rotation is not planned.
* Seed availability may limit adoption.

### Farmer Guidance

Legumes are preferred when the main goal is nitrogen addition.

---

## 14. Non-Legume Green Manure

Non-legume green manure crops do not fix atmospheric nitrogen, but they produce biomass, protect soil, suppress weeds, and recycle nutrients.

### Examples

* Mustard.
* Oats.
* Rye.
* Pearl millet.
* Sorghum-sudan grass.
* Maize as green biomass.
* Buckwheat.
* Radish-type cover crops.
* Sunflower.

### Benefits

* Produces high biomass.
* Suppresses weeds.
* Protects soil from erosion.
* Captures leftover nutrients.
* Improves soil structure.
* Some species reduce nutrient leaching.
* Some species have biofumigation or pest-suppression effects.

### Limitations

* Does not add new nitrogen through fixation.
* High-carbon residues may temporarily immobilize nitrogen.
* Some crops may become weeds if allowed to seed.

### Farmer Guidance

Non-legumes are useful when the main goal is biomass, soil cover, weed suppression, or erosion control.

---

## 15. Mixed Green Manure

Mixed green manure uses two or more species together.

### Common Mixtures

* Cereal plus legume.
* Grass plus legume.
* Brassica plus legume.
* Deep-rooted crop plus shallow-rooted crop.
* Fast-growing crop plus nitrogen-fixing crop.

### Benefits

* Balanced biomass and nitrogen.
* Better soil cover.
* Improved root diversity.
* Better weed suppression.
* More stable performance under variable weather.
* Better nutrient cycling.
* Lower risk than single-species failure.

### Example Mixtures

* Oats plus vetch.
* Sorghum-sudan grass plus cowpea.
* Pearl millet plus sunn hemp.
* Mustard plus pea.
* Rye plus clover.

### Farmer Guidance

Mixtures are useful when the farmer wants multiple benefits, such as nitrogen addition, soil cover, biomass, and weed suppression.

---

## 16. In-Situ Green Manuring

In-situ green manuring means growing the green manure crop in the same field where it will be incorporated.

### Examples

* Dhaincha grown before rice and incorporated.
* Sunn hemp grown before sugarcane or vegetables and incorporated.
* Cowpea grown during fallow and mixed into soil before main crop.

### Benefits

* No transport cost.
* Roots improve soil structure in the same field.
* Biomass is produced where needed.
* Efficient soil improvement method.

### Farmer Guidance

In-situ green manuring is usually the most practical method for field crops.

---

## 17. Green Leaf Manuring

Green leaf manuring means collecting green leaves or tender twigs from trees, shrubs, or plants and applying them to the field.

### Common Green Leaf Sources

* Gliricidia.
* Sesbania leaves.
* Pongamia leaves.
* Neem leaves.
* Subabul leaves.
* Glyricidia hedge prunings.
* Wild shrubs where safe and allowed.

### Uses

* Rice fields.
* Vegetable fields.
* Plantation crops.
* Orchards.
* Organic farming systems.

### Farmer Guidance

Use only non-toxic and locally recommended green leaf materials. Avoid leaves from plants treated with pesticides or contaminated by industrial pollution.

# Common Green Manure Crops

## 18. Sunn Hemp

Sunn hemp is a fast-growing legume green manure crop.

### Features

* Produces high biomass.
* Fixes nitrogen.
* Suppresses weeds.
* Improves soil organic matter.
* Suitable for warm seasons.
* Useful before cereals, vegetables, cotton, and sugarcane.

### Incorporation Timing

Usually incorporated at tender flowering stage or before stems become woody.

### Farmer Guidance

Sunn hemp is one of the best green manure crops where warm weather and moisture are available.

---

## 19. Dhaincha

Dhaincha, commonly Sesbania, is a widely used green manure crop, especially before rice.

### Features

* Fast-growing legume.
* Tolerates wet conditions better than many crops.
* Adds organic matter.
* Fixes nitrogen.
* Suitable for rice-based systems.
* Can grow in poor soils.

### Uses

* Before transplanted rice.
* In lowland fields.
* In paddy systems.
* As green leaf manure in some areas.

### Farmer Guidance

Dhaincha is commonly incorporated before puddling or transplanting rice.

---

## 20. Cowpea

Cowpea is a legume crop that can be used as green manure, cover crop, fodder, or pulse crop.

### Features

* Nitrogen-fixing crop.
* Fast soil cover.
* Good weed suppression.
* Tolerates warm conditions.
* Useful in dryland areas compared with some legumes.
* Can provide fodder if not incorporated.

### Uses

* Before cereals.
* Before vegetables.
* In orchards.
* As intercrop.
* As cover crop in rainfed systems.

### Farmer Guidance

Cowpea is useful when the farmer wants both soil cover and nitrogen addition.

---

## 21. Green Gram and Black Gram

Green gram and black gram can be used as short-duration legumes for soil fertility improvement.

### Features

* Short duration.
* Nitrogen fixation.
* Can fit between two crops.
* Provides pulse yield if harvested.
* Residues improve soil.

### Uses

* Summer fallow.
* Between cereal crops.
* Before vegetables.
* Rainfed systems.

### Farmer Guidance

If the crop is harvested for grain, less biomass and nitrogen are returned compared with full green manure incorporation.

---

## 22. Berseem and Clover

Berseem and clover are cool-season legumes used as fodder and green manure.

### Features

* Nitrogen fixation.
* Good biomass production.
* Improves soil fertility.
* Can provide fodder.
* Useful in winter season where suitable.

### Farmer Guidance

If used for fodder, return manure or residues to maintain soil fertility benefits.

---

## 23. Mustard and Brassica Green Manure

Mustard and related brassica crops are non-legume green manures.

### Features

* Fast growth.
* Good biomass.
* Deep roots.
* Nutrient capture.
* Some biofumigation potential.
* Weed suppression.

### Uses

* Cover crop.
* Soil structure improvement.
* Biofumigation support where managed properly.
* Nutrient recycling.

### Farmer Guidance

Brassica green manure should not be allowed to become a weed or produce unwanted seeds.

---

## 24. Sorghum-Sudan Grass

Sorghum-sudan grass is a high-biomass non-legume green manure crop.

### Features

* Produces large biomass.
* Strong root system.
* Good weed suppression.
* Useful for soil organic matter.
* Helps break compacted layers over time.

### Limitations

* High-carbon residues may temporarily tie up nitrogen.
* Needs chopping before incorporation.
* Can be difficult to incorporate if too mature.

### Farmer Guidance

Incorporate while still manageable and avoid letting stems become too hard.

---

## 25. Buckwheat

Buckwheat is a fast-growing short-duration green manure and cover crop.

### Features

* Quick soil cover.
* Weed suppression.
* Can mobilize some phosphorus.
* Attracts beneficial insects when flowering.
* Fits into short fallow windows.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not allow buckwheat to set seed if volunteer plants are a concern.

# Green Manure Management

## 32. Sowing Time

Sowing time depends on crop species, rainfall, irrigation, and cropping system.

### General Guidance

* Sow before monsoon if moisture is available.
* Sow during fallow period.
* Sow after harvest of previous crop.
* Sow before rice transplanting in rice systems.
* Sow between orchard rows during rainy season.
* Sow short-duration crops in small windows between main crops.

### Farmer Guidance

Green manure should have enough time to produce biomass before the main crop.

---

## 33. Seed Rate and Establishment

Seed rate depends on species, seed size, purpose, and sowing method.

### Establishment Tips

* Use good-quality seed.
* Prepare a suitable seedbed.
* Sow at proper depth.
* Use rhizobium inoculation for legumes where needed.
* Ensure soil moisture.
* Control weeds during early establishment if necessary.
* Avoid very deep sowing of small seeds.

### Farmer Guidance

Poor establishment reduces biomass, nitrogen fixation, and weed suppression.

---

## 34. Rhizobium Inoculation

Rhizobium inoculation helps legume green manure crops fix nitrogen when suitable native rhizobium is absent or low.

### Benefits

* Better nodulation.
* Better nitrogen fixation.
* Better crop growth.
* Higher nitrogen contribution.
* Improved soil fertility benefit.

### Farmer Guidance

Use crop-specific rhizobium culture where recommended. Keep inoculated seed away from direct sunlight and sow soon after treatment.

---

## 35. Best Stage for Incorporation

Green manure is usually incorporated when the crop is tender and has produced enough biomass but before it becomes too woody.

### Common Best Stage

* Before flowering.
* At early flowering.
* Around 40 to 60 days for many fast-growing green manure crops, depending on species and climate.
* Before seed formation.

### Why Timing Matters

* Tender biomass decomposes faster.
* Nitrogen release is better.
* Woody stems decompose slowly.
* Mature plants may immobilize nitrogen.
* Seed formation may create volunteer weeds.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not wait until green manure becomes hard and woody. Incorporate before flowering or at early flowering for better decomposition.

---

## 36. Incorporation Method

Green manure can be incorporated using tillage, chopping, puddling, or mulching depending on the system.

### Methods

* Ploughing into soil.
* Rotavator incorporation.
* Disc harrow incorporation.
* Chopping and mixing.
* Puddling in rice field.
* Cutting and using as surface mulch.
* Roller-crimping in conservation systems.
* Composting and applying elsewhere.

### Farmer Guidance

Chop tall biomass before incorporation to speed decomposition and avoid machinery problems.

---

## 37. Decomposition Period Before Main Crop

After incorporation, green manure needs time to decompose before sowing or transplanting the next crop.

### General Guidance

* Wait until residues soften and start decomposing.
* Common waiting period is about 2 to 3 weeks in warm and moist soil.
* Decomposition is faster in warm, moist, aerated soil.
* Decomposition is slower in cold, dry, or waterlogged soil.
* Very woody biomass needs more time.

### Farmer Guidance

Planting immediately after heavy green manure incorporation may cause temporary nutrient immobilization or seedling problems.

---

## 38. Moisture Management After Incorporation

Moisture is needed for decomposition.

### Good Practices

* Incorporate when soil has moisture.
* Irrigate lightly after incorporation if soil is dry.
* Avoid severe waterlogging unless rice system requires puddling.
* Maintain moisture for microbial activity.
* Avoid leaving incorporated biomass in dry soil.

### Farmer Guidance

Dry soil slows decomposition and reduces the benefit of green manure.

---

## 39. Nutrient Release from Green Manure

Nutrient release depends on crop type, biomass quality, soil moisture, temperature, and incorporation timing.

### Faster Nutrient Release

* Tender legume biomass.
* Low carbon-to-nitrogen ratio.
* Warm moist soil.
* Chopped biomass.
* Good microbial activity.

### Slower Nutrient Release

* Mature woody biomass.
* High-carbon grass residues.
* Dry soil.
* Cold soil.
* Poor aeration.
* Large unchopped stems.

### Farmer Guidance

Legume green manure usually releases nitrogen faster than mature grass residues.

# Green Manure and Fertilizer Planning

## 40. Can Green Manure Replace Chemical Fertilizer?

Green manure can reduce fertilizer requirement, especially nitrogen fertilizer when legumes are used. However, it may not fully replace fertilizers in high-yield crops.

### Factors Affecting Replacement

* Biomass produced.
* Legume or non-legume crop.
* Nitrogen fixation.
* Decomposition rate.
* Soil fertility.
* Main crop demand.
* Yield target.
* Rainfall and irrigation.
* Management quality.

### Farmer Guidance

Use green manure as part of integrated nutrient management, along with soil testing, compost, farmyard manure, biofertilizers, and need-based fertilizers.

---

## 41. Green Manure and Nitrogen Fertilizer

Legume green manure can supply part of the nitrogen requirement of the next crop.

### Important Notes

* Nitrogen contribution varies widely.
* More biomass usually means more nitrogen.
* Good nodulation increases nitrogen fixation.
* Some nitrogen becomes available quickly.
* Some nitrogen remains in soil organic matter.
* Not all nitrogen is available immediately.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not completely remove nitrogen fertilizer without observing crop response and following local recommendations.

---

## 42. Green Manure and Phosphorus

Green manure can improve phosphorus cycling but does not create phosphorus like legumes fix nitrogen.

### How It Helps

* Roots absorb phosphorus from soil.
* Decomposition returns phosphorus to topsoil.
* Organic acids may improve phosphorus availability.
* Microbial activity may improve nutrient cycling.

### Farmer Guidance

If soil phosphorus is low, phosphorus fertilizer or amendments may still be needed.

---

## 43. Green Manure and Potassium

Green manure crops can recycle potassium from soil biomass.

### How It Helps

* Plant roots absorb potassium.
* Biomass returns potassium after decomposition.
* Deep-rooted crops can bring potassium from deeper layers.
* Residues release potassium during decomposition.

### Farmer Guidance

For high-potassium-demand crops like banana, sugarcane, potato, and fruiting vegetables, green manure helps but may not fully meet potassium needs.

---

# Limitations and Risks

## 49. Limitations of Green Manure

Green manure has many benefits but also some limitations.

### Limitations

* Requires land for a period.
* Requires seed.
* Requires moisture.
* May delay main crop sowing.
* May compete for water in dry areas.
* Needs labour or machinery for incorporation.
* Benefits may be slow.
* Poor biomass gives poor benefit.
* May host pests or diseases if crop choice is wrong.
* Mature residues may decompose slowly.
* May temporarily immobilize nitrogen.
* May become weed if allowed to seed.

### Farmer Guidance

Green manure should be planned as part of the crop calendar, not added at the last minute.

---

## 50. Pest and Disease Considerations

Green manure can reduce some pest and disease problems but can also host others if poorly selected.

### Good Effects

* Breaks crop rotation cycles.
* Supports beneficial insects.
* Improves soil biology.
* Some brassicas may help suppress soil pathogens.
* Reduces weeds that host pests.

### Risks

* Legumes may host some nematodes.
* Brassicas may host brassica pests.
* Grasses may host cereal pests.
* Poor residue management can increase damping-off risk.
* Dense wet biomass may shelter pests.

### Farmer Guidance

Avoid green manure species closely related to the next main crop if pest or disease carryover is a concern.

---

## 51. Nitrogen Immobilization

Nitrogen immobilization happens when soil microbes temporarily use available nitrogen to decompose high-carbon residues.

### Common Causes

* Mature grass green manure.
* Woody stems.
* High-carbon residues.
* Incorporation just before planting.
* Low soil nitrogen.

### Symptoms in Next Crop

* Pale seedlings.
* Slow growth.
* Temporary nitrogen deficiency.
* Poor early vigour.

### Prevention

* Incorporate green manure while tender.
* Use legume or legume mixture.
* Chop residues.
* Allow decomposition time.
* Apply starter nitrogen where recommended.
* Avoid planting immediately after heavy mature residues.

### Farmer Guidance

Tender legumes release nitrogen faster. Mature grasses may temporarily tie up nitrogen.

---

## 52. Water Competition

A growing green manure crop uses water.

### High-Risk Situations

* Rainfed dry areas.
* Limited irrigation.
* Low soil moisture before main crop.
* Long-duration green manure before water-sensitive crop.
* Orchard inter-row cover in dry season.

### Prevention

* Choose short-duration crop.
* Terminate green manure early.
* Use drought-tolerant species.
* Use mulch instead of living cover during dry periods.
* Irrigate if available.
* Avoid competition with young orchards.

### Farmer Guidance

In dryland farming, the timing of green manure termination is critical to save moisture for the next crop.

# Practical Green Manure Calendar

## 53. General Green Manure Workflow

1. Identify purpose: nitrogen, biomass, weed control, erosion control, or soil improvement.
2. Select suitable species.
3. Arrange quality seed.
4. Prepare field or sow with minimum disturbance.
5. Ensure soil moisture.
6. Inoculate legume seed with rhizobium if needed.
7. Grow until tender biomass stage.
8. Cut, incorporate, mulch, or roll as planned.
9. Maintain moisture for decomposition.
10. Wait for decomposition before sowing sensitive crops.
11. Adjust fertilizer plan for next crop.
12. Record crop, biomass, incorporation date, and next crop response.

## 54. Example Green Manure Plans

### Before Transplanted Rice

* Grow dhaincha or Sesbania.
* Incorporate before transplanting.
* Maintain moisture.
* Proceed with rice establishment after local recommended interval.

### Before Vegetables

* Grow sunn hemp, cowpea, or buckwheat.
* Incorporate before flowering.
* Allow decomposition.
* Prepare clean seedbed.
* Transplant or sow vegetables.

### Orchard Rainy Season

* Grow cowpea, sunn hemp, or legume-grass mixture between rows.
* Avoid competition near young trees.
* Cut before seed formation.
* Use biomass as mulch.

### Short Fallow Window

* Grow green gram, black gram, cowpea, or buckwheat.
* Terminate early if next crop sowing date is near.

# Farmer Questions and Answers

## Q1. What is green manure?

Green manure is a crop grown mainly to be incorporated into soil or used as mulch to improve soil fertility, organic matter, and soil health.

## Q2. Why is green manure used?

Green manure is used to add organic matter, improve soil structure, fix nitrogen with legumes, suppress weeds, reduce erosion, conserve moisture, and improve soil biological activity.

## Q3. Which crops are used as green manure?

Common green manure crops include sunn hemp, dhaincha, cowpea, green gram, black gram, Sesbania, berseem, clover, vetch, mustard, oats, buckwheat, and sorghum-sudan grass.

## Q4. Which green manure crop is best for nitrogen?

Legume crops such as sunn hemp, dhaincha, cowpea, Sesbania, green gram, black gram, clover, and vetch are good for nitrogen addition.

## Q5. Which green manure is good before rice?

Dhaincha or Sesbania is commonly used before rice because it grows well in wet conditions and adds organic matter and nitrogen.

## Q6. When should green manure be incorporated?

Green manure is usually incorporated before flowering or at early flowering, when the biomass is still tender and decomposes easily.

## Q7. Why should green manure not be allowed to mature too much?

Mature green manure becomes woody, decomposes slowly, may tie up nitrogen temporarily, and may produce seeds that become weeds.

## Q8. How long should farmers wait after incorporating green manure?

Farmers commonly wait around 2 to 3 weeks in warm, moist conditions, but the exact time depends on crop biomass, soil moisture, temperature, and next crop sensitivity.

## Q9. Can green manure replace urea?

Legume green manure can reduce nitrogen fertilizer requirement, but it may not fully replace urea in high-yield crops. Soil test and crop response should guide fertilizer reduction.

## Q10. Can green manure replace all fertilizers?

No. Green manure improves soil fertility and can add nitrogen through legumes, but phosphorus, potassium, sulfur, and micronutrients may still be needed based on soil test.

## Q11. Is green manure useful in dryland farming?

Yes, but it must be timed carefully because green manure crops use water. Terminate early enough to conserve moisture for the next crop.

## Q12. Can green manure suppress weeds?

Yes. Fast-growing green manure crops suppress weeds by covering the soil, shading weed seedlings, and competing for space.

## Q13. Can green manure improve soil structure?

Yes. Green manure adds organic matter, improves aggregation, supports roots and microbes, and improves soil tilth over time.

## Q14. What is green leaf manuring?

Green leaf manuring means collecting green leaves or tender twigs from trees or shrubs and applying them to the field to improve soil fertility.

## Q15. What is the difference between green manure and cover crop?

A cover crop mainly protects the soil, while green manure is mainly incorporated or decomposed to feed the soil. The same crop can serve both purposes.

## Q16. Can green manure be used in orchards?

Yes. Green manure or cover crops can be grown between orchard rows to improve soil cover, reduce erosion, suppress weeds, and add biomass.

## Q17. Can green manure cause problems?

Yes. If poorly managed, it can compete for water, delay sowing, host pests, decompose slowly, immobilize nitrogen, or become a weed if allowed to seed.

## Q18. What is nitrogen immobilization?

Nitrogen immobilization is temporary nitrogen shortage caused when microbes use soil nitrogen to decompose high-carbon residues like mature grass biomass.

## Q19. Should green manure be chopped before incorporation?

Tall or woody green manure should be chopped before incorporation to speed decomposition and make soil mixing easier.

## Q20. What should an AI advisory system ask before suggesting green manure?

It should ask crop rotation, season, soil type, irrigation availability, time before next crop, purpose, weed pressure, rainfall, and whether the farmer wants nitrogen addition, weed control, or soil improvement.
