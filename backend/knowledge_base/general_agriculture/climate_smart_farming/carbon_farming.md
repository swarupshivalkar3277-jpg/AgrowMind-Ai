# Carbon Farming

# Overview

Carbon farming is a climate-smart agricultural approach that focuses on increasing carbon storage in soil, plants, trees, and farm ecosystems while reducing greenhouse gas emissions from agricultural activities. It includes farming practices that improve soil organic carbon, enhance soil health, reduce carbon dioxide release, and support long-term climate resilience.

Carbon farming helps farmers:

* Improve soil fertility
* Increase soil organic matter
* Improve water retention
* Reduce soil erosion
* Enhance crop resilience
* Reduce greenhouse gas emissions
* Support climate-smart agriculture
* Create possible income opportunities through carbon credits

Carbon farming is not a single practice. It is a combination of soil, crop, livestock, water, and land management practices that increase carbon sequestration and reduce emissions.

---

# What is Carbon Farming?

Carbon farming means managing agricultural land in a way that captures atmospheric carbon dioxide and stores it in soil organic matter, crop biomass, trees, grasses, and long-term farm ecosystems.

Plants absorb carbon dioxide during photosynthesis. Some of this carbon is stored in:

* Roots
* Crop residues
* Soil organic matter
* Trees
* Pastures
* Compost
* Biochar

When farms are managed properly, more carbon can remain stored in the soil instead of returning quickly to the atmosphere.

---

# Importance of Carbon Farming

Carbon farming is important because agriculture is both affected by climate change and contributes to greenhouse gas emissions.

Carbon farming helps:

* Reduce climate change impact
* Improve soil health
* Increase farm productivity
* Improve drought tolerance
* Reduce fertilizer dependency
* Improve biodiversity
* Support sustainable farming
* Improve long-term farm profitability

Healthy carbon-rich soils are more productive and more resilient to drought, floods, heatwaves, and erosion.

---

# Carbon Sequestration in Agriculture

Carbon sequestration is the process of capturing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and storing it in soil, plants, trees, and organic matter.

In farming systems, carbon sequestration mainly occurs through:

1. Plant photosynthesis
2. Root growth
3. Crop residue addition
4. Organic manure application
5. Reduced soil disturbance
6. Agroforestry and tree planting
7. Permanent soil cover
8. Biochar application

---

# Soil Organic Carbon

Soil organic carbon is the carbon component of soil organic matter. It is one of the most important indicators of soil health.

Higher soil organic carbon improves:

* Soil structure
* Water-holding capacity
* Nutrient availability
* Microbial activity
* Root growth
* Soil aeration
* Crop resilience

Low soil organic carbon may lead to poor fertility, low productivity, erosion, and reduced water retention.

# Benefits of Carbon Farming

## Improved Soil Fertility

Carbon-rich soils contain more organic matter and support better nutrient cycling.

Benefits include:

* Better nitrogen availability
* Improved phosphorus use
* Increased microbial activity
* Better root development

---

## Better Water Retention

Soil organic matter improves the soil’s ability to hold water.

This helps crops during:

* Dry spells
* Drought
* Heatwaves
* Irregular rainfall

---

## Reduced Soil Erosion

Carbon farming practices such as cover crops, mulching, and reduced tillage protect the soil surface.

This reduces:

* Wind erosion
* Water erosion
* Topsoil loss
* Nutrient loss

## Improved Crop Resilience

Healthy soils help crops tolerate stress better.

Carbon farming improves resilience against:

* Drought
* Flooding
* High temperature
* Nutrient stress
* Soil degradation

---

## Reduced Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Carbon farming can reduce emissions by:

* Improving nitrogen fertilizer efficiency
* Reducing fuel use from tillage
* Improving manure management
* Increasing soil carbon storage
* Reducing residue burning

---

## Biodiversity Improvement

Carbon farming supports beneficial organisms such as:

* Earthworms

# Major Carbon Farming Practices

---

# 1. Conservation Tillage

Conservation tillage reduces soil disturbance and helps preserve soil organic carbon.

## Benefits

* Reduces carbon loss from soil
* Improves soil structure
* Reduces erosion
* Saves fuel and labor
* Improves moisture conservation

## Examples

* Minimum tillage
* Reduced tillage
* Strip tillage
* Zero tillage

## Farmer Advisory

Avoid unnecessary deep ploughing where soil and crop conditions allow reduced tillage.

---

# 2. Cover Cropping

Cover crops are grown to cover the soil when the main crop is not present.

## Examples

* Legumes
* Cowpea
* Sunhemp
* Clover
* Mustard
* Fodder grasses

## Benefits

* Adds biomass to soil
* Prevents erosion
* Improves nitrogen fixation
* Suppresses weeds
* Enhances soil microbial life

## Farmer Advisory

Use cover crops during fallow periods to protect soil and add organic carbon.

---

# 3. Crop Residue Management

Crop residues such as straw, stalks, leaves, and husks can add organic matter to soil.

## Good Practices

* Incorporate residues into soil
* Use residues as mulch
* Compost residues
* Avoid burning residues

## Benefits

* Increases soil carbon
* Improves soil moisture
* Reduces erosion
* Provides food for soil organisms

## Avoid

* Burning crop residues
* Removing all biomass from the field
* Leaving residues unmanaged in disease-prone situations

---

# 4. Organic Manure Application

Organic manures add carbon and nutrients to soil.

## Common Sources

* Farmyard manure
* Compost
* Vermicompost
* Poultry manure
* Green manure
* Biogas slurry

## Benefits

* Improves soil organic carbon
* Enhances nutrient availability
* Improves soil texture
* Supports microbial activity

## Farmer Advisory

Use well-decomposed organic manure to avoid weed seeds, pests, and nutrient imbalance.

---

# 5. Composting

Composting converts organic waste into stable organic manure.

## Materials Used

* Crop residues
* Animal dung
* Farm waste
* Kitchen waste
* Dry leaves
* Plant biomass

## Benefits

* Recycles farm waste
* Adds stable organic matter
* Improves soil health
* Reduces waste burning

---

# 6. Vermicomposting

Vermicomposting uses earthworms to convert organic waste into nutrient-rich manure.

## Benefits

* Improves soil microbial activity
* Provides nutrients in available form
* Enhances soil structure
* Supports sustainable farming

## Suitable Use

Vermicompost is useful for vegetables, fruits, nurseries, flowers, and high-value crops.

---

# 7. Green Manuring

Green manuring involves growing plants and incorporating them into the soil before flowering or maturity.

## Common Green Manure Crops

* Sunhemp
* Dhaincha
* Cowpea
* Green gram
* Sesbania

## Benefits

* Adds organic matter
* Improves nitrogen availability
* Enhances soil fertility
* Improves soil structure

---

# 8. Agroforestry

Agroforestry integrates trees with crops, livestock, or both.

## Benefits

* Stores carbon in trees and soil
* Provides shade
* Reduces wind erosion
* Improves biodiversity
* Provides timber, fodder, fruit, or fuelwood
* Improves microclimate

## Examples

* Coconut with intercrops
* Fruit trees with vegetables
* Boundary tree planting
* Silvopasture
* Windbreaks and shelterbelts

---

# 9. Tree Planting and Shelterbelts

Trees store carbon in biomass and roots.

## Benefits

* Reduces wind speed
* Prevents soil erosion
* Improves farm biodiversity
* Provides additional income
* Improves farm climate resilience

## Suitable Trees

Selection depends on region, rainfall, soil, and farm purpose.

Examples:

* Gliricidia
* Subabul
* Neem
* Casuarina
* Fruit trees
* Fodder trees

---

# 10. Mulching

Mulching covers the soil with organic or inorganic material.

## Organic Mulches

* Straw
* Dry leaves
* Crop residues
* Sugarcane trash
* Grass clippings

## Benefits

* Reduces evaporation
* Adds organic carbon when decomposed
* Reduces weed growth
* Protects soil from erosion
* Moderates soil temperature

---

# 11. Biochar Application

Biochar is a carbon-rich material produced by heating biomass under limited oxygen conditions.

## Benefits

* Adds stable carbon to soil
* Improves water retention
* Improves nutrient holding capacity
* Supports microbial activity
* May reduce nutrient leaching

## Farmer Advisory

Use biochar carefully and preferably with compost or organic manure. Application rate should depend on soil type and expert recommendation.

---

# 12. Integrated Nutrient Management

Integrated nutrient management combines organic and inorganic nutrient sources.

## Components

* Organic manures
* Chemical fertilizers
* Biofertilizers
* Crop residues
* Green manures
* Micronutrients

## Benefits

* Improves nutrient efficiency
* Reduces excessive fertilizer use
* Improves soil organic matter
* Supports long-term productivity

---

# 13. Precision Fertilizer Use

Excess nitrogen fertilizer can increase greenhouse gas emissions.

Good practices include:

* Soil testing
* Split fertilizer application
* Fertigation
* Slow-release fertilizers where suitable
* Avoiding excess nitrogen
* Applying fertilizer at correct time and dose

## Benefits

* Reduces emissions
* Saves cost
* Improves crop response
* Reduces nutrient loss

---

# 14. Improved Water Management

Water management affects soil carbon and emissions.

## Good Practices

* Drip irrigation
* Sprinkler irrigation
* Irrigation scheduling
* Mulching
* Rainwater harvesting
* Avoiding over-irrigation

## Benefits

* Reduces water stress
* Improves biomass growth
* Reduces nutrient leaching
* Saves energy

---

# 15. Rice Water Management

Flooded rice fields can produce methane.

Climate-smart rice water practices may include:

* Alternate wetting and drying
* Proper drainage
* Efficient irrigation
* Balanced fertilizer use
* Residue management

## Benefits

* Reduces methane emissions
* Saves water
* Improves root health
* Maintains productivity when properly managed

---

# 16. Livestock Manure Management

Livestock manure can release methane and nitrous oxide if poorly managed.

## Better Practices

* Composting manure
* Biogas production
* Covered manure storage
* Timely field application
* Avoiding runoff into water bodies

## Benefits

* Reduces emissions
* Produces organic fertilizer
* Improves farm nutrient recycling
* Supports clean energy through biogas

---

# 17. Rotational Grazing

Rotational grazing manages livestock movement across grazing areas.

## Benefits

* Prevents overgrazing
* Encourages grass regrowth
* Increases root biomass
* Improves soil carbon
* Reduces soil erosion

---

# 18. Pasture Improvement

Healthy pastures store carbon in roots and soil.

## Practices

* Reseeding degraded pastures
* Avoiding overgrazing
* Adding legumes
* Applying compost
* Maintaining ground cover

# Carbon Credits in Agriculture

Carbon credits are certificates representing reduced or removed greenhouse gas emissions.

In agriculture, carbon credits may be generated when farmers adopt practices that:

* Store more carbon in soil or trees
* Reduce methane emissions
* Reduce nitrous oxide emissions
* Reduce fossil fuel use

## Important Note

Carbon credit programs require measurement, verification, documentation, and compliance with program rules. Farmers should understand contract terms before joining any carbon farming scheme.

# Carbon Farming and Greenhouse Gases

Agriculture mainly influences three greenhouse gases:

## Carbon Dioxide

Released through:

* Soil disturbance
* Residue burning
* Fuel use
* Land degradation

Reduced through:

* Soil carbon storage
* Tree planting
* Reduced tillage
* Organic matter addition

---

## Methane

Produced from:

* Flooded rice fields
* Ruminant livestock
* Poor manure management

Reduced through:

* Improved rice water management
* Better livestock feeding
* Biogas systems
* Manure composting

---

## Nitrous Oxide

Produced from:

* Excess nitrogen fertilizer
* Poor manure application
* Wet soils with high nitrogen

Reduced through:

* Efficient nitrogen management
* Soil testing
* Split application
* Avoiding over-irrigation

# Carbon Farming Advisory Guidelines

## If Soil Organic Matter is Low

Recommended actions:

* Apply compost or farmyard manure.
* Retain crop residues.
* Grow green manure crops.
* Avoid excessive tillage.

---

## If Farm is Drought-Prone

Recommended actions:

* Use mulching.
* Adopt conservation tillage.
* Plant drought-tolerant crops.
* Improve rainwater harvesting.

---

## If Farm Has Livestock

Recommended actions:

* Compost manure.
* Use biogas slurry.
* Avoid unmanaged manure piles.
* Apply manure at proper time.

---

## If Farm Has Orchards

Recommended actions:

* Use cover crops.
* Apply basin mulch.
* Recycle pruning biomass.
* Plant windbreaks.

---

## If Farmer Wants Carbon Credits

Recommended actions:

* Maintain farm records.
* Test soil organic carbon.
* Understand contract terms.
* Work with trusted organizations.
* Avoid unrealistic income expectations.

---

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

Farmers should avoid:

* Burning crop residues
* Removing all crop biomass from fields
* Excess deep tillage
* Applying undecomposed manure directly in excess
* Overusing nitrogen fertilizers
* Joining carbon credit schemes without understanding rules
* Expecting immediate soil carbon increase
* Ignoring soil testing
* Leaving soil bare during off-season

---

# Farmer Recommendations

Farmers are advised to:

1. Increase organic matter through compost, manure, and crop residues.
2. Keep soil covered with mulch, cover crops, or crop residues.
3. Reduce unnecessary tillage where suitable.
4. Avoid burning crop residues.
5. Use crop rotation and intercropping.
6. Plant trees on bunds, boundaries, and suitable farm areas.
7. Improve fertilizer and irrigation efficiency.
8. Manage livestock manure properly.
9. Maintain records if participating in carbon credit programs.
10. Treat carbon farming as a long-term soil health strategy.
