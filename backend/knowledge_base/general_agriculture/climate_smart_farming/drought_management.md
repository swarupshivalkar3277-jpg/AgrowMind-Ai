# Drought Management in Climate-Smart Farming

# Overview

Drought management is a key part of climate-smart farming. Drought occurs when rainfall is insufficient for crop growth, soil moisture becomes low, water sources decline, and crops experience moisture stress. In agriculture, drought can reduce germination, crop growth, flowering, fruit setting, yield, fodder availability, livestock health, and farm income.

Climate-smart drought management focuses on:

* Conserving soil moisture
* Improving water-use efficiency
* Selecting drought-tolerant crops and varieties
* Harvesting and storing rainwater
* Managing irrigation carefully
* Improving soil organic matter
* Reducing crop stress
* Protecting livestock and fodder resources
* Reducing climate-related farm losses

Drought management should begin before drought occurs. Prepared farms can reduce crop failure risk and recover faster after dry periods.

---

# What is Agricultural Drought?

Agricultural drought occurs when soil moisture is not enough to meet crop water requirements. Even if total seasonal rainfall appears normal, long dry spells during sensitive crop stages can cause serious crop damage.

Agricultural drought may happen due to:

* Delayed monsoon
* Long dry spells
* Below-normal rainfall
* High temperature
* Low soil moisture
* Poor water storage
* Excess evaporation
* Overuse of groundwater
* Poor soil organic matter
* Inefficient irrigation practices

---

# Difference Between Drought and Dry Spell

| Term                   | Meaning                                                               |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Dry Spell              | A short period without rainfall during the crop season                |
| Drought                | A prolonged shortage of rainfall or water causing serious crop stress |
| Agricultural Drought   | Soil moisture shortage affecting crop growth                          |
| Hydrological Drought   | Shortage of water in wells, rivers, reservoirs, or groundwater        |
| Meteorological Drought | Rainfall much below normal for a region                               |

A dry spell can turn into agricultural drought if it continues during critical crop growth stages.

# Why Drought Management is Important

Drought management helps farmers:

* Reduce crop failure
* Maintain crop growth during dry periods
* Save irrigation water
* Improve soil moisture retention
* Protect crop yield
* Reduce livestock stress
* Improve food and fodder security
* Increase farm resilience against climate change

Drought is one of the most common climate risks faced by rainfed and semi-arid farming regions.

# Impact of Drought on Agriculture

## Poor Germination

Low soil moisture after sowing can cause:

* Uneven germination
* Seedling mortality
* Poor crop stand
* Re-sowing requirement

---

## Reduced Plant Growth

Drought reduces:

* Leaf expansion
* Stem growth
* Root activity
* Photosynthesis
* Biomass production

---

## Flower Drop

Moisture stress during flowering may cause:

* Flower abortion
* Poor pollination
* Reduced fruit setting
* Lower yield

---

## Poor Grain or Fruit Development

Drought during grain filling or fruit development may cause:

* Small grains
* Shrivelled grains
* Small fruits
* Poor quality produce
* Reduced market value

---

## Increased Pest and Disease Susceptibility

Drought-stressed crops become weaker and may be more vulnerable to:

* Aphids
* Whiteflies
* Thrips
* Spider mites
* Stem borers
* Wilt diseases

---

## Fodder Shortage

Drought reduces pasture growth and fodder crop production.

This may cause:

* Livestock feed shortage
* Reduced milk yield
* Weight loss in animals
* Higher feed cost

---

## Reduced Farm Income

Drought may reduce income through:

* Yield loss
* Crop failure
* Increased irrigation cost
* Increased livestock feed cost
* Poor produce quality
* Distress sale of animals or produce

# Crop Symptoms of Drought Stress

## Early Symptoms

* Leaf rolling
* Mild wilting during afternoon
* Slow growth
* Reduced leaf size
* Dull leaf color

---

## Moderate Symptoms

* Persistent wilting
* Yellowing of older leaves
* Flower drop
* Fruit drop
* Poor branching
* Reduced root growth

---

## Severe Symptoms

* Leaf drying
* Stem drying
* Plant death
* Poor grain filling
* Severe yield loss
* Complete crop failure

# Drought Management Before Sowing

## Select Suitable Crops

Choose crops according to rainfall expectation and water availability.

### Low Rainfall Areas

Recommended crops:

* Pearl millet
* Sorghum
* Sesame
* Cowpea
* Guar

### Moderate Rainfall Areas

Recommended crops:

* Groundnut
* Chickpea
* Green gram
* Black gram
* Pigeon pea

### Irrigated or High-Value Areas

Suitable crops may include:

* Tomato
* Chilli
* Onion
* Banana
* Coconut

These crops require assured irrigation.

---

## Use Short-Duration Varieties

Short-duration varieties complete their life cycle faster.

They help farmers:

* Escape late-season drought
* Reduce climate risk
* Harvest earlier
* Save water

Useful during delayed monsoon or uncertain rainfall.

---

## Use Drought-Tolerant Varieties

Drought-tolerant varieties may have:

* Deep roots
* Better water-use efficiency
* Early maturity
* Better stress recovery
* Better yield under moisture stress

Farmers should select varieties recommended by local agricultural universities, Krishi Vigyan Kendras, or agriculture departments.

---

## Seed Treatment

Seed treatment improves seedling establishment under stress.

Benefits include:

* Better germination
* Disease protection
* Stronger seedlings
* Improved early crop growth

Seed treatment is especially important in dryland farming.

---

## Adjust Sowing Time

Sowing should be planned based on rainfall.

Recommended approach:

* Sow after sufficient soil moisture is available.
* Avoid dry sowing unless locally recommended.
* Delay sowing if rainfall is uncertain.
* Use short-duration crops if monsoon is delayed.

## Benefits

* Reduces evaporation
* Maintains soil moisture
* Controls weeds
* Reduces soil temperature
* Protects soil from erosion
* Improves crop survival during dry spells

## Organic Mulches

Examples:

* Straw
* Dry leaves
* Crop residues
* Sugarcane trash
* Grass clippings

## Plastic Mulch

Useful for:

* Tomato
* Chilli
* Watermelon
* Cucumber
* Strawberry
* High-value vegetables

Plastic mulch conserves moisture and reduces weed growth.

---

# Conservation Tillage

Conservation tillage reduces soil disturbance.

## Benefits

* Reduces evaporation
* Improves infiltration
* Protects soil organic matter
* Reduces erosion
* Saves fuel and labor

Examples include:

* Minimum tillage
* Zero tillage
* Reduced tillage
* Strip tillage

# Crop Residue Retention

Leaving crop residues on the soil surface helps conserve moisture.

Benefits:

* Reduces evaporation
* Adds organic matter
* Protects soil from heat
* Improves soil biological activity
* Reduces erosion

Avoid burning crop residues.

# Efficient Irrigation During Drought

Efficient irrigation is essential when water is limited.

---

# Drip Irrigation

Drip irrigation delivers water directly to the root zone.

## Benefits

* Saves water
* Reduces evaporation
* Improves fertilizer efficiency
* Maintains uniform moisture
* Reduces weed growth

Suitable for:

* Tomato
* Chilli
* Coconut
* Banana
* Grapes
* Pomegranate
* Vegetables
* Orchards

---

# Sprinkler Irrigation

Sprinkler irrigation applies water like rainfall.

Useful for:

* Groundnut
* Pulses
* Millets
* Fodder crops
* Sandy soils

Avoid sprinkler irrigation during strong wind or extreme midday heat.

---

# Micro-Sprinklers

Micro-sprinklers are useful for orchards and nurseries.

Benefits:

* Lower water use
* Better coverage around root zone
* Suitable for young plants

---

# Irrigation Scheduling

Irrigation scheduling means applying water at the right time and in the right amount.

Scheduling should consider:

* Crop stage
* Soil moisture
* Soil type
* Weather forecast
* Water availability

During drought, avoid unnecessary irrigation and focus on critical stages.

---

# Life-Saving Irrigation

Life-saving irrigation is limited irrigation applied to prevent crop death.

Use it when water is scarce.

Priority should be given to:

1. High-value crops
2. Flowering crops
3. Fruiting crops
4. Young orchards
5. Seed production crops
6. Fodder crops for livestock survival

---

# Irrigation Timing

Best irrigation times during drought:

* Early morning
* Late evening

Avoid irrigation during hot afternoon because evaporation losses are high.

# Crop Management During Drought

## Weed Control

Weeds compete with crops for water.

Drought-time weed control helps:

* Save soil moisture
* Reduce nutrient competition
* Improve crop survival

Remove weeds early.

---

## Thinning

In severe drought, reducing plant population may reduce water competition.

Useful when:

* Crop stand is too dense
* Rainfall is very low
* Irrigation water is limited

---

## Gap Filling

If germination is poor due to dry conditions, gap filling may be done early when soil moisture is available.

---

## Intercultural Operations

Light intercultural operations can break soil crust and reduce evaporation.

Avoid deep cultivation during severe drought as it may damage roots.

---

## Avoid Excess Nitrogen

Excess nitrogen encourages soft vegetative growth and increases water demand.

During drought:

* Avoid heavy nitrogen application.
* Use balanced fertilizer.
* Apply nutrients only when moisture is available.

---

* Small fruits
* Poor yield

Management:

* Use drip irrigation.
* Apply mulch.
* Maintain uniform soil moisture.

* Provide calcium and balanced nutrition where needed.

---

# Coconut

Drought effects:

* Button shedding
* Reduced nut size
* Poor nut setting
* Leaf drying

Management:

* Basin mulching.
* Regular irrigation during dry months.
* Apply organic manure.
* Use drip irrigation where possible.
* Grow suitable intercrops and cover crops.

---

# Banana

Drought effects:

* Leaf drying
* Poor bunch development
* Reduced fruit size
* Plant stress

Management:

* Mulch around plants.
* Use drip irrigation.
* Protect from hot winds.
* Maintain regular moisture.
* Avoid severe water stress during bunch development.

# Rice

Drought effects:

* Poor tillering
* Panicle sterility
* Poor grain filling

Management:

* Use drought-tolerant varieties in drought-prone areas.
* Practice alternate wetting and drying where suitable.
* Maintain water during critical stages.
* Use direct seeded rice where locally recommended.

---

# Wheat

Drought effects:

* Poor tillering
* Reduced grain filling
* Low grain weight

Management:

* Irrigate at crown root initiation, flowering, and grain filling.
* Use timely sowing.
* Use residue retention where possible.
* Select suitable varieties.

# Pulses

* Flower drop
* Poor pod formation
* Small seeds

Management:

* Practice moisture conservation.

# Post-Drought Crop Recovery

After rainfall or irrigation returns, crops may recover if damage is not severe.

Recommended actions:

* Assess plant survival.
* Remove dead plants.
* Control weeds.
* Apply balanced nutrition.
* Avoid sudden excessive fertilizer.
* Monitor pests and diseases.
* Repair irrigation and soil conservation structures.

---

# Re-Sowing After Drought

Re-sowing may be needed when crop stand is poor.

Consider re-sowing if:

* Germination failure is severe
* Plant population is below economic level
* Rainfall resumes early enough
* Short-duration crops are available
* Re-sowing cost is justified

If the season is already too late, choose short-duration or fodder crops.

---

# Drought Contingency Planning

Drought contingency planning helps farmers decide what to do when rainfall is delayed.

---

# If Monsoon is Delayed by 1–2 Weeks

Recommended:

* Use normal crops if sufficient rainfall occurs.
* Treat seeds properly.
* Monitor soil moisture.

---

# If Monsoon is Delayed by 2–4 Weeks

Recommended:

* Shift to short-duration varieties.
* Reduce plant population where needed.
* Prefer millets, pulses, and oilseeds.

---

# If Monsoon is Delayed More Than 4 Weeks

Recommended:

* Avoid long-duration crops.
* Choose short-duration crops like green gram, cowpea, sesame, or fodder crops.
* Prepare for limited production.
* Protect livestock fodder supply.

---

# If Mid-Season Drought Occurs

Recommended:

* Control weeds.
* Apply mulch.
* Provide life-saving irrigation.
* Avoid top dressing without moisture.
* Reduce crop competition if necessary.

---

# If Terminal Drought Occurs

Terminal drought occurs near flowering, grain filling, or maturity.

Recommended:

* Prioritize irrigation at reproductive stages.
* Harvest early if crop is failing.
* Use produce as fodder if grain recovery is not possible.
* Plan next crop based on moisture availability.

# Farmer Advisory Guidelines

## If Soil Moisture is Low

* Apply mulch.
* Reduce weed competition.
* Irrigate if water is available.
* Avoid heavy fertilizer application.

---

## If Crop is Wilting

* Check soil moisture.
* Apply life-saving irrigation.
* Avoid pesticide or fertilizer spray during peak heat.
* Monitor recovery after irrigation.

---

## If Water is Limited

* Prioritize high-value and reproductive-stage crops.
* Use drip irrigation where possible.
* Avoid irrigating low-priority areas.
* Use stored rainwater carefully.

---

## If Rainfall is Delayed

* Delay sowing until adequate moisture is available.
* Use short-duration crops.
* Avoid long-duration high-water crops.

---

## If Crop Failure is Likely

* Assess remaining crop stand.
* Consider re-sowing with short-duration crops.
* Salvage crop as fodder if suitable.
* Protect livestock resources.
