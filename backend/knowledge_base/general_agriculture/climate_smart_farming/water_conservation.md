# Water Conservation in Climate-Smart Farming

# Overview

Water conservation is the practice of using, storing, and managing water efficiently so that crops, livestock, soil, and farm ecosystems receive adequate water while reducing wastage. In climate-smart farming, water conservation is essential because rainfall is becoming more irregular, droughts are increasing, groundwater levels are declining, and irrigation costs are rising.

Water conservation helps farmers:

* Save irrigation water
* Improve crop survival during dry spells
* Reduce drought risk
* Improve soil moisture
* Increase water-use efficiency
* Reduce production costs
* Improve farm resilience
* Protect groundwater resources
* Support sustainable agriculture

Water conservation is not only about using less water. It is about using the right amount of water at the right time, storing rainwater, improving soil moisture retention, and reducing unnecessary losses.

---

# What is Water Conservation in Agriculture?

Water conservation in agriculture means protecting available water resources and using water efficiently for crop and livestock production.

It includes:

* Rainwater harvesting
* Soil moisture conservation
* Efficient irrigation
* Proper irrigation scheduling
* Mulching
* Drainage management
* Groundwater recharge
* Crop selection according to water availability
* Reducing runoff, evaporation, and leakage

A good water conservation plan combines field-level practices, irrigation technologies, soil management, and climate-based decision-making.

---

# Importance of Water Conservation

Water conservation is important because agriculture depends heavily on water. Poor water management can lead to drought stress, low yield, waterlogging, soil degradation, salinity, and increased farming costs.

Water conservation helps:

* Maintain crop growth during dry periods
* Improve yield stability
* Reduce irrigation frequency
* Reduce pumping cost
* Improve fertilizer efficiency
* Protect soil health
* Reduce groundwater depletion
* Improve climate resilience
* Support long-term farm productivity

# Major Causes of Water Loss in Farming

---

# Evaporation Loss

Evaporation occurs when water is lost from the soil surface, open water bodies, and irrigation channels.

Evaporation increases during:

* Hot weather
* Dry winds
* Bare soil conditions
* Midday irrigation
* Poor mulching

---

# Runoff Loss

Runoff occurs when rainwater or irrigation water flows away from the field instead of entering the soil.

Runoff is common in:

* Sloping fields
* Compacted soils
* Unleveled fields
* Heavy rainfall areas
* Fields without bunds or contour structures

---

# Deep Percolation Loss

Deep percolation occurs when excess water moves below the crop root zone.

This water becomes unavailable to crops and may also carry nutrients away.

Deep percolation happens due to:

* Over-irrigation
* Sandy soils
* Poor irrigation scheduling
* Excessive flood irrigation

---

# Seepage Loss

Seepage occurs when water leaks from canals, field channels, ponds, or unlined watercourses.

It can reduce water availability before water reaches the crop field.

---

# Weed Competition

Weeds consume water that should be available to crops.

Heavy weed growth can significantly reduce soil moisture and crop yield.

---

# Poor Soil Organic Matter

Soils with low organic matter cannot hold water effectively.

Such soils dry quickly and require more frequent irrigation.

---

# Water Conservation Principles

Effective water conservation follows these principles:

1. Capture rainwater where it falls.
2. Store excess water for dry periods.
3. Reduce evaporation from soil.
4. Improve water infiltration.
5. Apply irrigation only when needed.
6. Use efficient irrigation methods.
7. Match crops with available water.
8. Maintain soil organic matter.
9. Prevent runoff and erosion.
10. Monitor soil moisture and weather.

---

# Rainwater Harvesting

Rainwater harvesting means collecting and storing rainwater for later use.

It is one of the most important water conservation practices in climate-smart agriculture.

## Benefits

* Provides water during dry spells
* Supports life-saving irrigation
* Improves groundwater recharge
* Reduces runoff
* Reduces soil erosion
* Improves drought preparedness

---

# Farm Ponds

Farm ponds are small water storage structures used to collect runoff water.

## Uses

* Supplemental irrigation
* Livestock drinking water
* Nursery irrigation
* Fish farming where suitable
* Emergency drought management

## Management Tips

* Desilt ponds regularly.
* Repair pond bunds before monsoon.
* Prevent contamination.
* Use pond water carefully during drought.
* Fence ponds for livestock and child safety.

---

# Check Dams

Check dams are small barriers built across drainage lines or streams to slow water flow.

## Benefits

* Reduces runoff speed
* Improves groundwater recharge
* Reduces soil erosion
* Increases water availability in nearby wells

---

# Percolation Tanks

Percolation tanks collect rainwater and allow it to slowly seep into the ground.

## Benefits

* Recharge groundwater
* Improve well water levels
* Reduce runoff
* Support long-term water security

---

# Recharge Pits and Recharge Wells

Recharge pits and wells help rainwater enter underground aquifers.

## Suitable For

* Farms with borewells
* Areas with declining groundwater
* Farm buildings with roof runoff
* Hard soil areas where natural infiltration is poor

---

# Rooftop Rainwater Harvesting

Rooftop rainwater harvesting collects water from farm buildings, sheds, houses, and livestock shelters.

## Uses

* Nursery watering
* Livestock drinking water
* Household farm use
* Small-scale irrigation
* Sprayer water use

---

# Field Bunding

Field bunds are raised soil barriers constructed around fields.

## Benefits

* Retain rainwater
* Reduce runoff
* Increase infiltration
* Reduce soil erosion
* Improve moisture availability

Bunding is especially useful in rainfed and dryland farming.

---

# Contour Bunding

Contour bunding is used on sloping land.

Bunds are made along contour lines to slow water movement.

## Benefits

* Reduces runoff
* Improves infiltration
* Prevents soil erosion
* Conserves rainwater in the field

---

# Soil Moisture Conservation

Soil moisture conservation means keeping water stored in the root zone for crop use.

---

# Mulching

Mulching covers the soil surface with organic or inorganic materials.

## Benefits

* Reduces evaporation
* Maintains soil moisture
* Reduces soil temperature
* Controls weeds
* Reduces erosion
* Improves crop growth during dry periods

## Organic Mulches

Examples:

* Straw
* Dry leaves
* Crop residues
* Sugarcane trash
* Grass clippings
* Coconut leaves
* Farm waste compost

## Plastic Mulch

Useful for:

* Tomato
* Chilli
* Cucumber
* Watermelon
* Strawberry
* High-value vegetables

Plastic mulch conserves water and controls weeds but should be disposed of responsibly.

---

# Crop Residue Retention

Crop residues protect the soil and conserve moisture.

## Benefits

* Reduces direct sun exposure
* Reduces evaporation
* Improves soil organic matter
* Reduces erosion
* Supports soil organisms

Farmers should avoid burning crop residues because burning wastes biomass and reduces soil carbon.

---

## Benefits

* Conserves soil moisture
* Reduces erosion
* Preserves soil organic matter
* Saves fuel and labor
* Improves water infiltration

# Efficient Irrigation Methods

Efficient irrigation helps apply water directly and uniformly to crops.

---

# Drip Irrigation

Drip irrigation delivers water directly to the root zone through emitters.

## Benefits

* Saves water
* Reduces evaporation
* Reduces weed growth
* Improves fertilizer efficiency
* Maintains uniform soil moisture
* Suitable for high-value crops

## Suitable Crops

* Tomato
* Chilli
* Coconut
* Banana
* Grapes
* Pomegranate
* Mango
* Vegetables
* Flowers

---

# Sprinkler Irrigation

Sprinkler irrigation applies water like rainfall.

## Benefits

* Suitable for uneven land
* Useful for sandy soils
* Reduces runoff compared to flood irrigation
* Suitable for field crops and fodder

## Suitable Crops

* Groundnut
* Millets
* Pulses
* Wheat
* Fodder crops
* Vegetables where leaf disease risk is low

---

# Micro-Sprinklers

Micro-sprinklers apply water in small droplets around the plant root zone.

## Suitable For

* Orchards
* Nurseries
* Young plantations
* Greenhouses
* Coconut and fruit crops

---

# Furrow Irrigation

Furrow irrigation applies water in small channels between crop rows.

## Water-Saving Tips

* Use properly shaped furrows.
* Avoid excessive flow.
* Stop irrigation once the root zone is wet.
* Use alternate furrow irrigation where suitable.

---

# Alternate Furrow Irrigation

In alternate furrow irrigation, water is applied to every alternate furrow instead of every furrow.

## Benefits

* Saves water
* Reduces irrigation cost
* Maintains crop growth under limited water
* Useful for row crops

---

# Raised Bed Cultivation

Raised beds improve water-use efficiency and drainage.

## Benefits

* Better root aeration
* Reduced waterlogging

* Reduced water wastage
* Better crop establishment

Suitable for:

* Tomato

# Irrigation Scheduling

Irrigation scheduling means deciding when and how much water to apply.

Good scheduling prevents both water stress and over-irrigation.

## Scheduling Should Consider

* Crop type
* Crop growth stage
* Soil type
* Soil moisture
* Weather forecast
* Irrigation method
* Water availability

---

Water should be prioritized at critical stages.

## Cereals

* Flowering
* Grain filling

## Pulses

* Flowering
* Pod formation

## Oilseeds

* Flowering
* Seed development

## Vegetables

* Flowering
* Fruit setting
* Fruit development

## Fruit Crops

* Flowering
* Fruit set
* Fruit growth

# Best Time for Irrigation

Best irrigation times:

* Early morning
* Late evening

Avoid irrigation during:

* Hot afternoon
* Strong winds
* Heavy rainfall
* Waterlogged conditions

Cool-hour irrigation reduces evaporation loss.

# Soil Type-Based Water Conservation

---

# Sandy Soils

Sandy soils drain quickly and hold less water.

## Recommendations

* Use frequent light irrigation.
* Apply organic manure.
* Use mulching.
* Use drip irrigation.
* Avoid excess irrigation that causes deep percolation.

---

# Loamy Soils

Loamy soils are ideal for water conservation.

## Recommendations

* Maintain organic matter.
* Use moderate irrigation.
* Practice mulching.
* Monitor soil moisture.

---

# Clay Soils

Clay soils hold water for longer but drain slowly.

## Recommendations

* Avoid over-irrigation.
* Maintain drainage.
* Use raised beds for sensitive crops.
* Apply water slowly.
* Avoid field work when soil is too wet.

* Use life-saving irrigation.
* Prioritize high-value crops.
* Apply mulch.
* Control weeds.
* Avoid excess nitrogen.
* Use drip irrigation where possible.
* Reduce water wastage.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

Farmers should avoid:

* Flood irrigation without need
* Irrigating during hot afternoon
* Ignoring leaks in pipes or channels
* Leaving soil bare
* Burning crop residues
* Growing high-water crops in water-scarce areas
* Applying irrigation without checking soil moisture
* Over-irrigating clay soils
* Ignoring rainwater harvesting opportunities
* Delaying weed control

# Farmer Recommendations

Farmers are advised to:

1. Harvest rainwater through farm ponds, bunds, and recharge structures.
2. Use drip irrigation or sprinkler irrigation where suitable.
3. Apply mulch to reduce evaporation.
4. Improve soil organic matter through compost and crop residues.
5. Schedule irrigation according to crop stage and soil moisture.
6. Control weeds early to save water.
7. Repair leaks in pumps, pipes, and channels.
8. Select crops according to water availability.
9. Avoid over-irrigation and waterlogging.
10. Follow weather-based advisories for irrigation decisions.

# Water Conservation Decision Guide

| Farm Situation    | Recommended Action                         |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Low rainfall area | Drought-tolerant crops and farm ponds      |
| Sandy soil        | Frequent light irrigation and mulch        |
| Clay soil         | Avoid over-irrigation and improve drainage |
| Vegetable crop    | Drip irrigation with mulch                 |
| Orchard crop      | Basin mulching and micro-irrigation        |
| Delayed monsoon   | Delay sowing or use short-duration crops   |
| Dry spell         | Life-saving irrigation and weed control    |
| Water scarcity    | Prioritize critical crop stages            |
| Heavy rainfall    | Store rainwater and maintain drainage      |
| High evaporation  | Irrigate early morning or evening          |
