# Crop Rotation Plans

# Overview

Crop rotation plans are planned sequences of crops grown on the same field across different seasons or years. A good rotation plan improves soil fertility, reduces pest and disease buildup, improves water-use efficiency, reduces weed pressure, and increases farm profitability.

Rotation planning is important because different crops have different effects on soil, nutrients, pests, diseases, water use, and farm income. A well-designed rotation should include cereals, pulses, oilseeds, vegetables, fodder crops, green manure crops, or cover crops according to local soil, rainfall, irrigation, market demand, and climate risk.

A crop rotation plan helps farmers:

* Maintain soil fertility
* Reduce soil-borne diseases
* Break pest life cycles
* Improve weed control
* Balance nutrient use
* Improve soil organic matter
* Reduce crop failure risk
* Diversify income
* Improve climate resilience
* Support sustainable farming

---

# What is a Crop Rotation Plan?

A crop rotation plan is a field-level schedule that decides which crop should be grown after another crop.

Example:

* Kharif season: Maize
* Rabi season: Chickpea
* Summer season: Green manure

This plan is better than growing the same crop repeatedly because it gives the soil time to recover and reduces buildup of crop-specific pests and diseases.

---

# Why Rotation Plans are Needed

Without rotation planning, farmers may repeat crops based only on habit or short-term market price. This can create long-term problems.

Continuous cropping may cause:

* Soil nutrient imbalance
* Pest buildup
* Disease buildup
* Declining yield
* More fertilizer use
* More pesticide use
* Weed dominance
* Lower soil organic matter
* Reduced profit over time

Rotation planning helps farmers make better agronomic and economic decisions.

---

# Main Goals of Crop Rotation Planning

The main goals are:

1. Improve soil fertility.
2. Reduce pests and diseases.
3. Improve soil structure.
4. Include legumes for nitrogen fixation.
5. Match crops with rainfall and irrigation.
6. Reduce weed problems.
7. Improve farm income stability.
8. Reduce climate and market risk.
9. Maintain long-term productivity.
10. Support sustainable and climate-smart agriculture.

# Principles for Designing Crop Rotation Plans

---

# 1. Rotate Crop Families

Crops from the same family often share pests and diseases. Avoid growing crops from the same family continuously.

Example:

Avoid:

* Tomato → Chilli → Brinjal → Potato

Better:

* Tomato → Maize → Cowpea → Onion

---

# 2. Include Legumes

Legumes help improve soil nitrogen through biological nitrogen fixation.

Useful legumes include:

* Chickpea
* Cowpea
* Green gram
* Black gram
* Pigeon pea
* Soybean
* Groundnut
* Lentil

Including legumes reduces dependency on nitrogen fertilizers and improves soil fertility.

---

# 3. Rotate Heavy Feeders with Soil-Restoring Crops

Heavy feeder crops use large amounts of nutrients.

Heavy feeders include:

* Maize
* Rice
* Wheat
* Sugarcane
* Banana
* Tomato
* Cabbage
* Cauliflower

Soil-restoring crops include:

* Pulses
* Green manure crops
* Cover crops
* Fodder legumes

A good rotation balances nutrient demand and soil recovery.

---

# 4. Rotate Deep-Rooted and Shallow-Rooted Crops

Deep-rooted crops use nutrients and water from deeper layers.

Examples:

* Pigeon pea
* Cotton
* Sorghum
* Sunflower

Shallow-rooted crops use nutrients from upper soil layers.

Examples:

* Onion
* Garlic
* Potato
* Leafy vegetables

Rotating root systems improves soil structure and nutrient use.

---

# 5. Include Cover Crops or Green Manure

Cover crops and green manure improve soil organic matter and reduce erosion.

Examples:

* Sunhemp
* Dhaincha
* Cowpea
* Green gram
* Fodder grasses

They are useful between main crops or during fallow periods.

---

# 6. Match Crops with Water Availability

Water-demanding crops should be grown only when irrigation or rainfall is reliable.

High water requirement crops:

* Rice
* Sugarcane
* Banana
* Leafy vegetables

Low water requirement crops:

* Pearl millet
* Sorghum
* Sesame
* Cowpea
* Chickpea

---

# 7. Consider Market Demand

A rotation plan should be agronomically suitable and economically practical.

Farmers should consider:

* Local market price
* Processing demand
* Storage possibility
* Labor availability
* Transport access
* Buyer linkage

# Rotation Plan for Tomato Fields

## Good Rotation

Tomato → Maize → Cowpea → Onion

## Alternative Rotation

Tomato → Sorghum → Chickpea → Cabbage

## Avoid

Tomato → Chilli → Brinjal → Potato

## Reason

Tomato, chilli, brinjal, and potato belong to the same family and share diseases such as wilt, root rot, bacterial wilt, nematodes, and some insect pests.

Recommended rotation:

Cereal → Pulse → Green manure

Recommended rotation:

Vegetable → Cereal → Pulse → Non-host crop

Recommended rotation:

# Step-by-Step Rotation Planning Method

## Step 1: Write Previous Crop History

Record crops grown in the field for the last 2–3 seasons.

---

## Step 2: Identify Crop Family

Avoid repeating crops from the same family.

---

## Step 3: Identify Field Problems

Check for pest, disease, weed, water, or fertility issues.

---

## Step 4: Add a Legume

Include a pulse, fodder legume, or green manure crop.

---

## Step 5: Balance Water Requirement

Avoid high-water crops when irrigation is not assured.

---

## Step 6: Consider Market Demand

Choose crops that have market or household value.

---

## Step 7: Prepare a Seasonal Calendar

Plan crops for Kharif, Rabi, and summer.

---

## Step 8: Review After Each Season

After harvest, record:

* Yield
* Pest problems
* Disease problems
* Soil condition
* Market price
* Profit

Use records to improve the next rotation plan.

# Farmer Advisory Guidelines

## If Farmer Grows Tomato Continuously

Advise:

* Stop continuous tomato cultivation.
* Rotate with maize, sorghum, cowpea, chickpea, or onion.
* Avoid chilli, brinjal, and potato immediately after tomato.

---

## If Farmer Has Low Soil Fertility

Advise:

* Include pulses and green manure.
* Add compost or farmyard manure.
* Avoid continuous heavy feeder crops.

---

## If Farmer Has Drought Risk

Advise:

* Use millets, pulses, and sesame.
* Avoid long-duration water-demanding crops.
* Practice mulching and rainwater harvesting.

---

## If Farmer Has Waterlogging Risk

Advise:

* Use raised beds.
* Improve drainage.
* Avoid sensitive crops in low-lying areas.
* Choose crops tolerant to excess moisture where needed.

---

## If Farmer Wants More Income

Advise:

* Include high-value crops carefully.
* Do not ignore soil-restoring crops.
* Use market-linked production.
* Rotate cash crops with legumes and cereals.

---

# Common Rotation Planning Mistakes

Farmers should avoid:

* Growing the same crop every season
* Rotating crops from the same family
* Ignoring legumes
* Ignoring pest and disease history
* Leaving soil bare for long periods
* Selecting crops without water availability
* Selecting crops without market access
* Burning crop residues
* Not maintaining field records
* Using only short-term profit as the planning basis

---

# Farmer Recommendations

Farmers are advised to:

1. Plan crop rotation before the season starts.
2. Keep a record of crops grown in each field.
3. Avoid repeating crops from the same family.
4. Include legumes regularly.
5. Rotate heavy feeder crops with soil-restoring crops.
6. Use green manure or cover crops where possible.
7. Match crops with soil type and water availability.
8. Rotate vegetables with cereals or pulses.
9. Use drought-tolerant crops in dryland areas.
10. Review the rotation plan every year based on yield and market results.
