# Soil Testing

# Overview

Soil testing is the scientific process of analyzing soil to understand its fertility, nutrient status, pH, salinity, organic carbon, and other important soil properties. It helps farmers know what nutrients are available in the soil and what nutrients need to be added for healthy crop growth.

Soil testing is one of the most important practices in modern agriculture because it supports balanced fertilizer use, reduces unnecessary input cost, improves crop yield, prevents nutrient imbalance, and protects long-term soil health.

Soil testing helps farmers:

* Know soil fertility status
* Apply fertilizers according to crop need
* Reduce fertilizer wastage
* Correct nutrient deficiencies
* Manage soil pH
* Improve crop productivity
* Reduce production cost
* Protect soil health
* Improve water and nutrient efficiency
* Plan long-term farm management

Soil testing should be done regularly, especially before major cropping seasons, new orchard establishment, high-value crop cultivation, or when crops show poor growth.

---

# What is Soil Testing?

Soil testing is the laboratory analysis of a representative soil sample collected from a field.

A soil test may measure:

* Soil pH
* Electrical conductivity
* Organic carbon
* Available nitrogen
* Available phosphorus
* Available potassium
* Sulfur
* Micronutrients
* Soil texture
* Salinity or alkalinity problems
* Lime or gypsum requirement where needed

The test report gives information about the soil condition and fertilizer recommendations for crops.

---

# Why Soil Testing is Important

Farmers often apply fertilizers based on habit, guesswork, or advice from input sellers. This can lead to overuse of some nutrients and shortage of others.

Soil testing helps avoid this problem.

## Benefits

* Saves fertilizer cost
* Improves nutrient balance
* Prevents overuse of nitrogen
* Identifies hidden deficiencies
* Improves crop response to fertilizers
* Supports soil pH correction
* Reduces nutrient leaching
* Improves yield and quality
* Supports sustainable agriculture

---

# Objectives of Soil Testing

The main objectives of soil testing are:

1. Assess soil fertility.
2. Identify nutrient deficiencies.
3. Identify excess nutrients or salinity problems.
4. Understand soil pH condition.
5. Recommend suitable fertilizers and amendments.
6. Improve fertilizer-use efficiency.
7. Support crop planning.
8. Maintain long-term soil productivity.

---

# What Soil Testing Can Tell Farmers

A soil test can help answer questions such as:

* Is the soil acidic, neutral, or alkaline?
* Is the soil low in organic carbon?
* Does the soil have enough nitrogen?
* Is phosphorus available to crops?
* Is potassium sufficient?
* Are micronutrients deficient?
* Is soil salinity affecting crop growth?
* Does the soil need lime or gypsum?
* Which fertilizer should be applied?
* How much fertilizer is needed?

# Important Soil Test Parameters

---

# Soil pH

Soil pH shows whether the soil is acidic, neutral, or alkaline.

## Importance

Soil pH affects:

* Nutrient availability
* Microbial activity
* Root growth
* Fertilizer efficiency
* Crop suitability

## General Interpretation

| Soil pH   | Meaning                         |
| --------- | ------------------------------- |
| Below 5.5 | Strongly acidic                 |
| 5.5–6.5   | Moderately acidic               |
| 6.5–7.5   | Near neutral                    |
| 7.5–8.5   | Moderately alkaline             |
| Above 8.5 | Strongly alkaline or sodic risk |

---

# Electrical Conductivity

Electrical conductivity, or EC, indicates soluble salt concentration in soil.

## Importance

High EC may indicate salinity.

Salinity can cause:

* Poor germination
* Leaf burning
* Stunted growth
* Poor water uptake
* Patchy crop stand

---

# Organic Carbon

Organic carbon indicates soil organic matter status.

## Importance

Organic carbon affects:

* Soil fertility
* Water-holding capacity
* Soil structure
* Microbial activity
* Nutrient cycling
* Climate resilience

Low organic carbon means the soil needs more compost, manure, crop residues, green manure, or cover crops.

---

# Available Nitrogen

Nitrogen supports:

* Leaf growth
* Green color
* Photosynthesis
* Protein formation
* Vegetative growth

## Low Nitrogen Symptoms

* Yellowing of older leaves
* Poor growth
* Small leaves
* Low tillering or branching
* Reduced yield

---

# Available Phosphorus

Phosphorus supports:

* Root growth
* Early crop development
* Flowering
* Seed formation
* Energy transfer

## Low Phosphorus Symptoms

* Poor root growth
* Stunted plants
* Delayed maturity
* Poor flowering
* Purple coloration in some crops

---

# Available Potassium

Potassium supports:

* Water regulation
* Disease resistance
* Stress tolerance
* Stem strength
* Fruit quality
* Grain filling

## Low Potassium Symptoms

* Scorching on leaf edges
* Weak stems
* Poor fruit size
* Low stress tolerance
* Poor crop quality

---

# Sulfur

Sulfur supports protein formation, enzyme activity, and oilseed quality.

## Deficiency Symptoms

* Yellowing of younger leaves
* Poor growth
* Reduced oil content in oilseeds

---

# Micronutrients

Micronutrients are needed in small quantities but are essential.

Commonly tested micronutrients include:

* Zinc
* Iron
* Manganese
* Copper
* Boron

## Importance

Micronutrients affect:

* Flowering
* Fruit setting
* Enzyme activity
* Leaf development
* Grain formation
* Crop quality

---

# When Should Soil Testing Be Done?

Soil testing should be done:

* Before sowing a major crop
* Before establishing orchards
* Before planting vegetables
* Before applying large fertilizer doses
* Once every 2–3 years for regular monitoring
* Every year for intensive vegetable or high-value crops
* When crop growth is poor despite fertilizer use
* When nutrient deficiency symptoms appear
* When salinity, acidity, or alkalinity is suspected

---

# Best Time for Soil Sampling

The best time for soil sampling is usually:

* After harvest
* Before fertilizer or manure application
* Before land preparation
* When the field is not too wet
* Before the next crop season

Avoid collecting soil immediately after fertilizer application because the result may not represent the actual soil condition.

---

# Soil Sampling Importance

Correct soil sampling is the most important step in soil testing.

A laboratory can only analyze the sample sent by the farmer. If the sample is not representative, the test result will be wrong.

Poor sampling leads to:

* Wrong fertilizer recommendation
* Extra fertilizer cost
* Poor crop response
* Incorrect pH correction
* Misdiagnosis of soil problems

---

# Tools Required for Soil Sampling

Farmers may use:

* Soil auger
* Khurpi
* Spade
* Clean bucket
* Plastic sheet
* Sample bag
* Marker pen
* Label
* Notebook

Tools should be clean and free from fertilizer, pesticide, manure, or chemical contamination.

---

# Soil Sampling Depth

Sampling depth depends on crop type.

| Crop Type                     | Recommended Sampling Depth    |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Field crops                   | 0–15 cm                       |
| Vegetables                    | 0–15 cm                       |
| Orchards and plantation crops | 0–30 cm or as advised         |
| Deep-rooted crops             | 0–30 cm may be useful         |
| Salinity diagnosis            | Multiple depths may be needed |

For fruit trees and coconut, deeper sampling may be useful because roots explore deeper soil layers.

---

# How to Collect Soil Sample

## Step 1: Divide the Field

Divide the farm into separate sampling units based on:

* Soil type
* Crop history
* Fertilizer history
* Irrigation source
* Slope
* Drainage condition
* Field appearance

Do not mix soil from very different fields.

---

## Step 2: Avoid Unusual Spots

Avoid collecting soil from:

* Field bunds
* Manure heaps
* Fertilizer storage areas
* Water channels
* Tree shade areas
* Burnt patches
* Compost pits
* Animal resting areas
* Wet depressions
* Areas near roads

These spots may give misleading results.

---

## Step 3: Collect Soil from Several Points

Collect soil from 8–15 points in a zigzag pattern across the field.

This helps create a representative sample.

---

## Step 4: Remove Surface Litter

Remove dry leaves, stones, crop residues, and surface trash before sampling.

Do not remove topsoil.

---

## Step 5: Take Soil Slice

Dig a V-shaped pit with a spade.

Take a thin slice of soil from the side of the pit from the required depth.

---

## Step 6: Mix Samples

Put all soil slices from the same field into a clean bucket.

Mix thoroughly.

---

## Step 7: Reduce Sample Size

Spread mixed soil on a clean plastic sheet.

Remove stones, roots, and plant pieces.

Divide into four parts.

Discard two opposite parts and mix the remaining two.

Repeat until about 500 grams of soil remains.

---

## Step 8: Dry in Shade

If soil is wet, dry it in shade.

Do not dry soil in direct sunlight, on a fertilizer bag, or near chemicals.

---

## Step 9: Pack and Label

Pack soil in a clean bag.

Label should include:

* Farmer name
* Field name or number
* Village
* Crop to be grown
* Previous crop
* Sampling date
* Irrigation source
* Contact number

# Soil Sampling for Orchards

For orchards and plantation crops, collect soil from the active root zone.

## Recommended Method

* Collect soil from the basin or canopy drip line.
* Avoid trunk base.
* Take samples from multiple trees.
* Mix samples from similar tree blocks.
* Avoid recently fertilized basins.

For coconut, mango, banana, and other fruit crops, root-zone sampling is more useful than random sampling from unused spaces.

---

# Soil Sampling for Problem Areas

If a field has patches of poor growth, collect separate samples:

1. One sample from healthy area
2. One sample from affected area

Do not mix them.

This helps diagnose whether the problem is due to pH, salinity, nutrient deficiency, drainage, or other soil issues.

---

# Soil Testing for Salinity

When salinity is suspected, test both soil and irrigation water.

Symptoms may include:

* White salt crust on soil surface
* Poor germination
* Leaf tip burning
* Patchy crop growth
* Poor water uptake
* Hard soil surface

In saline or sodic fields, deeper sampling may be required.

---

# Soil Testing for Acidic Soil

Acidic soils are common in high rainfall areas and some naturally acidic regions.

Soil testing helps determine:

* pH level
* Lime requirement
* Calcium and magnesium status
* Possible aluminum toxicity risk
* Crop suitability

Lime should be applied only as recommended.

---

# Soil Testing for Alkaline or Sodic Soil

Alkaline or sodic soils may show:

* High pH
* Poor water infiltration
* Hard crust
* Patchy crop growth
* Micronutrient deficiency
* Poor germination

Soil testing helps decide whether gypsum, organic matter, drainage improvement, or micronutrient correction is needed.

---

# Soil Test Report

A soil test report usually includes:

* Sample details
* Soil pH
* Electrical conductivity
* Organic carbon
* Available NPK
* Micronutrient status
* Fertilizer recommendation
* Amendment recommendation
* Remarks

Farmers should read both the test values and recommendations.

---

# Understanding Soil Test Categories

Soil test results are often shown as:

* Low
* Medium
* High
* Deficient
* Sufficient
* Excess

## Meaning

| Category   | Meaning                                         |
| ---------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Low        | Nutrient is not enough; application needed      |
| Medium     | Moderate level; balanced application needed     |
| High       | Enough or excess; avoid unnecessary application |
| Deficient  | Correction required                             |
| Sufficient | No immediate correction needed                  |
| Excess     | Avoid over-application                          |

---

# Using Soil Test Results

Soil test results should be used to prepare a nutrient plan.

## Steps

1. Read pH and EC first.
2. Check organic carbon.
3. Check nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium.
4. Check micronutrients.
5. Compare with crop requirement.
6. Plan organic manure and fertilizer.
7. Apply amendments if needed.
8. Keep records for future comparison.

---

# Soil Testing and Fertilizer Planning

Soil testing helps farmers apply fertilizers more accurately.

## Example

If soil phosphorus is high:

* Avoid unnecessary phosphorus fertilizer.
* Save cost.
* Reduce nutrient imbalance.

If potassium is low:

* Apply potassium fertilizer based on crop need.
* Improve fruit quality and stress tolerance.

If organic carbon is low:

* Add compost, FYM, green manure, and residues.

---

# Soil Testing and Organic Farming

Soil testing is useful in organic farming too.

It helps organic farmers:

* Monitor soil organic carbon
* Plan compost and manure application
* Identify nutrient deficiencies
* Correct pH
* Track long-term soil health
* Avoid nutrient mining

Organic farming still requires nutrient planning.

---

# Soil Testing and Integrated Nutrient Management

Integrated nutrient management combines:

* Soil testing
* Organic manures
* Chemical fertilizers where used
* Biofertilizers
* Green manure
* Crop residues
* Micronutrients

Soil testing helps decide the correct balance.

---

# Soil Testing and Crop Rotation

Soil testing helps plan crop rotation.

Examples:

* Low nitrogen soil may benefit from legumes.
* Low organic carbon soil may need green manure.
* Saline soil may need tolerant crops.
* Acidic soil may need crops tolerant to acidity or lime correction.

---

# Soil Testing and Water Management

Soil testing should be combined with irrigation water testing when problems are suspected.

Water quality can affect:

* Soil pH
* Salinity
* Sodicity
* Nutrient availability
* Crop growth

Testing borewell water is useful in areas with high salt or bicarbonate problems.

---

# Soil Testing in Tomato Cultivation

Tomato is a nutrient-demanding crop and responds well to balanced nutrition.

Soil testing helps identify:

* pH suitability
* Organic carbon level
* Nitrogen requirement
* Phosphorus and potassium status
* Calcium and micronutrient problems
* Salinity risk

## Recommendations

* Test soil before planting.
* Maintain pH around suitable range.
* Add well-decomposed organic manure.
* Apply NPK according to test.
* Monitor calcium and boron for fruit quality.
* Avoid excess nitrogen.

---

# Soil Testing in Coconut Cultivation

Coconut is a long-duration perennial crop, so soil testing helps long-term nutrient planning.

Soil testing helps identify:

* Soil pH
* Organic carbon
* Potassium status
* Magnesium status
* Micronutrient deficiencies
* Salinity risk in coastal areas

## Recommendations

* Test soil from coconut root zone.
* Apply organic manure in basins.
* Use coconut leaf mulch.
* Apply fertilizers according to tree age and soil test.
* Test irrigation water in coastal or saline areas.

---

# Soil Testing in Mango Cultivation

Mango orchards need balanced nutrition for flowering, fruit set, and tree health.

Soil testing helps:

* Plan nutrient application
* Identify pH issues
* Detect micronutrient problems
* Improve fruit quality
* Avoid excess nitrogen

## Recommendations

* Collect soil from the canopy drip line.
* Test before fertilizer planning.
* Apply compost or FYM.
* Monitor zinc and boron where deficiency is common.

---

# Soil Testing in Grain Crops

For rice, wheat, maize, and millets, soil testing helps balance NPK.

Recommendations:

* Test before season.
* Apply nitrogen in split doses.
* Avoid unnecessary phosphorus if soil is high.
* Apply zinc in rice where deficient.
* Include pulses in rotation to improve fertility.

---

# Soil Testing in Pulses

Pulses require good pH, phosphorus, sulfur, and micronutrients for nodulation and yield.

Recommendations:

* Check soil pH.
* Apply phosphorus and sulfur if deficient.
* Use Rhizobium seed treatment.
* Avoid waterlogging.
* Use molybdenum where recommended.

---

# Soil Testing in Vegetable Crops

Vegetables are high-value and nutrient-demanding.

Recommendations:

* Test soil before each major vegetable crop.
* Apply organic manure.
* Use balanced NPK.
* Correct micronutrients.
* Monitor pH and salinity.
* Avoid repeated excessive fertilizer use.

---

# Common Soil Testing Mistakes

Farmers should avoid:

* Collecting soil from only one spot
* Mixing different fields in one sample
* Sampling after fertilizer application
* Sampling from manure heaps or bunds
* Sending wet soil without drying
* Not labeling samples
* Ignoring pH and EC values
* Applying fertilizer without reading the report
* Not keeping past soil test records
* Testing once and never repeating

---

# Farmer Recommendations

Farmers are advised to:

1. Test soil regularly before major crop planning.
2. Collect representative soil samples properly.
3. Avoid unusual spots during sampling.
4. Label samples clearly.
5. Read pH, EC, organic carbon, NPK, and micronutrients.
6. Apply fertilizers based on soil test recommendations.
7. Add organic matter when organic carbon is low.
8. Correct pH only according to recommendation.
9. Keep soil test reports for comparison.
10. Combine soil testing with crop records and yield records.

---

# Soil Testing Decision Guide

| Situation                 | Recommended Action                             |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Poor crop growth          | Test soil and compare healthy vs affected area |
| High fertilizer cost      | Use soil test-based fertilizer planning        |
| Low organic matter        | Apply compost, FYM, green manure               |
| Yellowing leaves          | Test soil and diagnose nutrient deficiency     |
| Poor tomato fruit quality | Check calcium, boron, pH, and irrigation       |
| Coconut yield decline     | Test basin soil and review nutrient plan       |
| White salt crust          | Test soil EC and irrigation water              |
| Acidic soil suspected     | Test pH and lime requirement                   |
| Alkaline soil suspected   | Test pH, EC, and micronutrients                |
| New orchard planting      | Test soil before planting                      |

# Soil Testing Checklist

## Before Sampling

* Select field or plot.
* Avoid recently fertilized fields.
* Keep tools clean.
* Prepare sample bag and label.
* Decide sampling depth.

## During Sampling

* Collect from 8–15 points.
* Use zigzag pattern.
* Avoid unusual spots.
* Mix soil well.
* Remove stones and roots.
* Reduce to about 500 grams.

## After Sampling

* Dry in shade if wet.
* Pack in clean bag.
* Label properly.
* Send to soil testing lab.
* Keep report safely.
* Use recommendations for nutrient planning.
