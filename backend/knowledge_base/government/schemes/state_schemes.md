# State Government Agriculture Schemes

# Overview

State government agriculture schemes are farmer support programs implemented by state governments to improve crop production, irrigation, farm mechanization, input access, soil health, horticulture, livestock, fisheries, storage, marketing, climate resilience, and farmer income.

These schemes are important because agricultural conditions differ from state to state. Soil, rainfall, irrigation, crops, pests, markets, disasters, and farmer needs vary across regions. State schemes help address local agricultural problems more directly.

State schemes may support:

* Seed subsidy
* Fertilizer or input support
* Irrigation support
* Drip and sprinkler subsidy
* Farm pond subsidy
* Farm mechanization
* Horticulture development
* Organic farming
* Crop diversification
* Pest and disease control
* Livestock and dairy support
* Fisheries support
* Farmer training
* Market linkage
* Storage and post-harvest infrastructure
* Disaster compensation
* Crop loss relief
* Farmer welfare programs

Farmers should always verify current scheme details from the state agriculture department, district agriculture office, horticulture department, animal husbandry department, fisheries department, revenue office, Common Service Centre, official state portal, or local extension officer.

---

# Why State Schemes are Important

State schemes help farmers access support that is specific to local farming needs.

State schemes are important because they can:

* Address local crop problems
* Support region-specific crops
* Help farmers manage drought, flood, cyclone, or hailstorm risk
* Improve irrigation in water-scarce areas
* Support horticulture and cash crops
* Provide subsidies based on state priorities
* Support small and marginal farmers
* Promote climate-resilient farming
* Improve market access
* Support farmer groups and FPOs
* Help farmers recover after natural calamities

A farmer should check both central and state schemes because many benefits are implemented through state departments.

---

# Difference Between Central and State Schemes

| Scheme Type                | Main Funding or Control   | Example                                                           |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Central Scheme             | Government of India       | PM-KISAN, PMFBY, KCC-related support                              |
| State Scheme               | State Government          | State crop subsidy, state farm pond scheme, state disaster relief |
| Centrally Sponsored Scheme | Central and State sharing | Irrigation, horticulture, mechanization, soil health programs     |
| Department Scheme          | Specific state department | Agriculture, horticulture, animal husbandry, fisheries            |

Farmers should not assume that a scheme available in one state is automatically available in another state.

# State Scheme Application Process

---

# Step 1: Identify the Scheme

Farmer should identify which scheme matches the farm need.

Examples:

* Drip irrigation subsidy
* Farm pond scheme
* Seed subsidy
* Horticulture orchard scheme
* Livestock unit scheme
* Organic farming scheme

---

# Step 2: Check Eligibility

Eligibility may depend on:

* State
* District
* Crop
* Landholding
* Farmer category
* Caste category
* Gender
* Irrigation status
* Bank account
* Aadhaar
* Previous subsidy received
* Scheme target
* Budget availability

---

# Step 3: Collect Documents

Common documents may include:

* Aadhaar card
* Bank passbook
* Land record
* 7/12 extract or equivalent
* Crop details
* Mobile number
* Farmer registration number
* Caste certificate where applicable
* Income certificate where applicable
* Passport-size photo
* Quotation for machinery
* Project report for larger schemes
* Water source proof for irrigation schemes
* FPO or SHG documents where applicable

---

# Step 4: Apply Through Official Channel

Application channels may include:

* State agriculture portal
* Agriculture department office
* Horticulture department office
* Animal husbandry office
* Fisheries office
* CSC center
* Gram panchayat
* District agriculture office
* Bank branch for credit-linked schemes
* FPO or cooperative

---

# Step 5: Verification

Officials may verify:

* Land records
* Farm location
* Farmer identity
* Crop details
* Water source
* Machinery quotation
* Project feasibility
* Farmer category
* Previous subsidy history

---

# Step 6: Approval or Sanction

If approved, farmer may receive:

* Subsidy sanction

* Equipment approval

* Training selection

# Step 7: Implementation

Farmer should complete the approved activity according to scheme rules.

Examples:

* Install drip system
* Construct farm pond
* Plant orchard
* Purchase machinery
* Build livestock shed
* Start compost unit

---

# Step 8: Inspection

Many schemes require inspection after completion.

Officials may check:

* Asset installation
* Quality of work
* Actual farm location
* Crop planting
* Machinery purchase
* Beneficiary contribution
* Bills and receipts

# Step 9: Subsidy Release or Benefit Delivery

Subsidy or benefit may be released:

* Directly to farmer bank account
* To vendor
* Through bank loan adjustment
* As input material
* As equipment
* As training support
* As reimbursement after verification

Farmer should save:

# Common Reasons for Scheme Rejection

Applications may be rejected due to:

* Incomplete documents
* Wrong land record
* Aadhaar mismatch
* Bank account error
* Duplicate application
* Ineligible farmer category
* Scheme not active in district
* Budget exhausted
* Deadline missed
* Wrong crop or activity
* Previous subsidy already received
* No water source proof
* Poor project feasibility
* Failed inspection
* Unauthorized vendor purchase

---

# How to Avoid Rejection

Farmers should:

1. Read scheme guidelines.
2. Apply before deadline.
3. Upload clear documents.
4. Use correct Aadhaar and bank details.
5. Match land record with applicant name.
6. Keep mobile number active.
7. Use approved vendors where required.
8. Do not start work before approval if scheme requires prior sanction.
9. Save application number.
10. Follow up with department office.

# Grievance Process

If a scheme application is delayed or rejected unfairly, farmer should:

1. Check status online or at department office.
2. Ask for rejection reason.
3. Correct missing documents.
4. Submit grievance through official portal or office.
5. Keep complaint number.
6. Contact taluka or district agriculture office.
7. Use helpline if available.
8. Keep all receipts and screenshots.

---

# State Scheme Safety and Fraud Advisory

Farmers should avoid fake agents and unofficial payment demands.

Do not share:

* OTP
* UPI PIN
* ATM PIN
* Aadhaar OTP
* Bank password
* Internet banking password

Use only:

* Official state government portals
* Agriculture department offices
* CSC centers
* Bank branches
* Authorized officers
* Official helplines

No private agent can guarantee scheme approval.

---

# Farmer Recommendations

Farmers are advised to:

1. Check state schemes before each season.
2. Keep Aadhaar, bank, and land records updated.
3. Apply through official channels only.
4. Maintain farm records and receipts.
5. Use schemes based on actual farm need.
6. Avoid duplicate or false applications.
7. Track application status regularly.
8. Complete inspections and document corrections quickly.
9. Join FPOs or groups for better access.
10. Verify current rules because state schemes change frequently.
