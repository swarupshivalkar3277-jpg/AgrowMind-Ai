# Bacterial Canker Disease in Mango

## Overview

Bacterial canker is a serious disease of mango caused by the bacterium *Xanthomonas citri* pv. *mangiferaeindicae*. The disease affects leaves, twigs, branches, stems, and fruits, leading to significant yield and quality losses. It is particularly severe in regions with high humidity, frequent rainfall, and strong winds.

## Scientific Classification

* Disease Name: Bacterial Canker
* Causal Organism: *Xanthomonas citri* pv. *mangiferaeindicae*
* Disease Type: Bacterial Disease
* Affected Crop: Mango (*Mangifera indica*)

## Importance

Bacterial canker is one of the most destructive bacterial diseases of mango. Severe infections can reduce fruit production, lower fruit quality, and weaken orchard trees over time.

## Symptoms

### On Leaves

* Small water-soaked spots initially appear on leaves.
* Spots become dark brown to black.
* Lesions are often angular and surrounded by yellow halos.
* Severe infection causes leaf drying and premature leaf fall.

### On Twigs and Branches

* Cracks and cankers develop on young shoots.
* Dark lesions appear on twigs.
* Gum exudation may occur.
* Dieback of infected shoots may develop.

### On Fruits

* Small raised black spots appear on fruit surfaces.
* Lesions become corky and rough.
* Fruits may crack in severe infections.
* Market quality is significantly reduced.

### On Stems

* Bark cracking and canker formation.
* Gum oozing from infected tissues.
* Reduced vigor of affected branches.

## Favorable Conditions

The disease develops rapidly under:

* High humidity.
* Frequent rainfall.
* Temperatures between 25°C and 35°C.
* Strong winds causing plant injuries.
* Dense orchards with poor ventilation.
* Presence of infected plant material.

## Disease Cycle

1. The bacterium survives in infected twigs, leaves, and cankers.
2. Rain splash spreads bacteria to healthy tissues.
3. Entry occurs through wounds, stomata, and natural openings.
4. Bacteria multiply within plant tissues.
5. New infections develop during wet weather.

## Economic Impact

* Reduced fruit yield.
* Poor fruit appearance and quality.
* Increased fruit cracking.
* Orchard decline under severe infection.
* Higher management costs.

## Identification Features

* Water-soaked leaf lesions.
* Black raised fruit spots.
* Yellow halos around lesions.
* Gum exudation from infected branches.
* Bark cracking and canker formation.

## Integrated Disease Management

### Cultural Practices

* Remove and destroy infected twigs and branches.
* Prune diseased shoots during dry weather.
* Improve air circulation through canopy management.
* Avoid overhead irrigation.
* Maintain orchard sanitation.

### Use Healthy Planting Material

* Plant disease-free nursery stock.
* Avoid using infected grafting material.

### Tool Sanitation

* Disinfect pruning tools before use.
* Sterilize equipment between trees.
* Avoid spreading bacteria through contaminated tools.

## Chemical Management

### Copper-Based Sprays

Recommended protective sprays:

* Copper Oxychloride @ 3 g/L water.
* Bordeaux Mixture 1%.

### Antibiotic-Based Treatments

Where permitted by local agricultural regulations:

* Streptocycline combined with copper fungicides may be used according to agricultural recommendations.

Always follow local extension guidelines and label instructions.

## Prevention

* Use certified disease-free planting material.
* Avoid injuries to plants.
* Maintain proper tree spacing.
* Conduct regular orchard inspections.
* Remove infected plant parts promptly.

## Monitoring Checklist

* Inspect leaves for water-soaked lesions.
* Check fruits for raised black spots.
* Observe branches for gum exudation.
* Monitor disease spread during rainy periods.
* Record infected trees for follow-up management.

## Best Management Practices

* Follow strict orchard sanitation.
* Combine pruning with copper-based protection.
* Monitor orchards closely during monsoon season.
* Use integrated disease management strategies.
* Remove heavily infected branches immediately.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What causes black raised spots on mango fruits?

Bacterial canker commonly produces raised black corky lesions on mango fruits.

### Why is gum oozing from mango branches?

Gum exudation can be a symptom of bacterial canker infection in branches and stems.

### How does bacterial canker spread?

The disease spreads mainly through rain splash, contaminated tools, infected planting material, and wounds.
