# Dieback Disease in Mango

## Overview

Dieback is a serious fungal disease of mango that causes drying and death of twigs, branches, and shoots from the tip backwards. If left unmanaged, the disease can spread to larger branches and significantly reduce fruit production. Dieback commonly occurs in orchards with poor sanitation, mechanical injuries, nutrient deficiencies, or prolonged humid conditions.

## Scientific Classification

* Disease Name: Dieback
* Causal Organism: *Lasiodiplodia theobromae* (syn. *Botryodiplodia theobromae*)
* Disease Type: Fungal Disease
* Affected Crop: Mango (*Mangifera indica*)

## Symptoms

### Early Symptoms

* Drying of young twigs from the tip.
* Leaves on affected shoots turn brown and wither.
* Gradual death of terminal branches.

### Advanced Symptoms

* Progressive drying of branches towards the trunk.
* Dark brown discoloration under the bark.
* Cracks and cankers may develop on branches.
* Gum exudation may be observed in severe cases.

### On Nursery Plants

* Wilting and drying of young plants.
* Reduced growth and vigor.

## Favorable Conditions

* High humidity.
* Frequent rainfall.
* Poor orchard sanitation.
* Mechanical injuries caused during pruning or harvesting.
* Nutrient stress and drought stress.
* Dense tree canopy with poor ventilation.

## Disease Cycle

1. Fungus survives in infected twigs, branches, and plant debris.
2. Spores spread through wind, rain splash, and pruning tools.
3. Infection enters through wounds and cuts.
4. Fungus colonizes vascular tissues.
5. Branches gradually dry and die back.

## Economic Importance

* Reduction in vegetative growth.
* Decreased flowering and fruiting.
* Lower fruit yield.
* Weakening of orchard trees.
* Increased management costs.

## Identification Features

* Drying starts from the tip and progresses backward.
* Dark brown internal tissue beneath bark.
* Dead twigs remain attached to the tree.
* Gum exudation may occur around infected areas.

## Integrated Disease Management

### Cultural Practices

* Remove and destroy infected twigs and branches.
* Prune affected branches 15–20 cm below infected tissue.
* Burn infected plant debris away from the orchard.
* Improve orchard sanitation.
* Maintain proper tree spacing.

### Nutrient Management

* Apply balanced fertilizers.
* Correct micronutrient deficiencies.
* Ensure adequate irrigation during dry periods.

### Tool Sanitation

* Disinfect pruning tools before and after use.
* Use 1% sodium hypochlorite or recommended disinfectants.

### Biological Control

* Apply beneficial microorganisms such as *Trichoderma harzianum* around the root zone and pruning wounds where recommended.

### Chemical Control

#### After Pruning

Apply protective fungicidal paste on cut surfaces:

* Copper Oxychloride paste.
* Bordeaux paste.

#### Fungicide Sprays

* Copper Oxychloride @ 3 g/L water.
* Carbendazim @ 1 g/L water.
* Mancozeb @ 2.5 g/L water.

Apply sprays according to local agricultural recommendations and disease severity.

## Prevention

* Avoid injuries to branches.
* Prune trees annually.
* Maintain proper canopy structure.
* Remove diseased plant material promptly.
* Follow balanced fertilization schedules.

## Monitoring Checklist

* Inspect orchards monthly for drying shoots.
* Check pruning wounds for infection.
* Observe branches for cankers and gum exudation.
* Record disease spread and affected trees.

## Best Management Practices

* Conduct regular orchard sanitation.
* Remove dead branches immediately.
* Use disease-free planting material.
* Improve air circulation within the canopy.
* Follow integrated disease management practices.
