# Maturity Indices for Mango Harvesting

## Overview

Harvesting mangoes at the correct stage of maturity is essential for obtaining high fruit quality, better shelf life, improved market value, and optimal flavor. Fruits harvested too early may fail to ripen properly, while overripe fruits are more susceptible to damage during handling and transportation.

Maturity indices are physical and physiological indicators used to determine the appropriate time for harvesting mango fruits.

---

# Importance of Proper Harvest Timing

Harvesting at the correct maturity stage helps:

* Improve fruit quality.
* Enhance sweetness and flavor.
* Increase shelf life.
* Reduce post-harvest losses.
* Improve market acceptance.
* Ensure uniform ripening.

---

# Common Maturity Indicators

## 1. Fruit Age

The number of days from flowering to harvest varies among varieties.

Typical maturity period:

| Variety      | Days After Fruit Set |
| ------------ | -------------------- |
| Alphonso     | 100–120 days         |
| Kesar        | 110–120 days         |
| Banganapalli | 110–130 days         |
| Dashehari    | 100–115 days         |

Fruit age should be used together with other maturity indicators.

---

## 2. Change in Fruit Shape

Mature fruits show:

* Well-developed shoulders.
* Full fruit size.
* Rounded appearance.
* Less prominent beak.

Immature fruits often appear narrow and flattened.

---

## 3. Shoulder Development

A reliable maturity indicator.

Mature fruits:

* Shoulders rise above the stem-end attachment point.
* Fruit appears fuller and more rounded.

---

## 4. Skin Color Changes

Color changes depend on variety.

Common signs include:

* Light green to greenish-yellow color.
* Reduced dark green appearance.
* Development of characteristic variety color.

Note:

Color alone should not be used as the only maturity indicator.

---

## 5. Fruit Surface Characteristics

Mature fruits generally have:

* Smooth surface.
* Waxy appearance.
* Better shine.
* Reduced skin roughness.

---

## 6. Latex Flow

When the fruit stalk is cut:

### Immature Fruits

* Produce abundant milky latex.
* Latex is thick and sticky.

### Mature Fruits

* Latex flow decreases.
* Latex becomes less dense.

---

## 7. Specific Gravity Test

Mature fruits generally have higher specific gravity.

Simple test:

* Place fruits in water.
* Mature fruits often sink.
* Immature fruits may float.

This method should be used together with other indicators.

---

## 8. Pulp Color

Mature fruits show:

* Creamy yellow pulp.
* Increased carotenoid development.

Immature fruits usually have:

* Pale white pulp.
* Low sweetness.

---

## 9. Seed Development

Mature fruits have:

* Fully developed seed.
* Hard seed coat.
* Complete embryo formation.

# Variety-Specific Maturity Indicators

## Alphonso

Indicators:

* Shoulders become well developed.
* Skin turns light green.
* Fruit develops characteristic shape.
* Harvest generally 100–120 days after fruit set.

---

## Kesar

Indicators:

* Rounded fruit shape.
* Full shoulder development.
* Slight yellow coloration near stem.

---

## Banganapalli

Indicators:

* Large fruit size.
* Full shoulder formation.
* Reduced latex flow.

---

## Dashehari

Indicators:

* Characteristic elongated shape.
* Light green color.
* Mature shoulder development.

---

# Harvesting Methods

## Manual Harvesting

Recommended method.

Use:

* Harvesting poles.
* Fruit pickers.
* Secateurs.

Benefits:

* Minimizes fruit damage.
* Maintains fruit quality.

---

## Harvesting Precautions

Avoid:

* Pulling fruits by hand.
* Dropping fruits on the ground.
* Harvesting during rainfall.

Recommended:

* Leave a short stalk attached.
* Harvest during cooler hours.
* Handle fruits carefully.

---

# Post-Harvest Handling

Immediately after harvest:

* Remove field heat.
* Grade fruits.
* Sort damaged fruits.
* Pack carefully.

Benefits:

* Reduced spoilage.
* Improved shelf life.
* Better market value.

---

# Common Harvesting Mistakes

### Harvesting Too Early

Results:

* Poor ripening.
* Low sweetness.
* Inferior fruit quality.

### Harvesting Too Late

Results:

* Short shelf life.
* Increased bruising.
* Higher post-harvest losses.

---

# Monitoring Checklist

Before harvest verify:

* Fruit age.
* Shoulder development.
* Fruit size.
* Skin color changes.
* Reduced latex flow.
* Seed maturity.

---

# Best Management Practices

* Use multiple maturity indicators.
* Harvest during cool morning hours.
* Use proper harvesting tools.
* Avoid mechanical injury.
* Grade fruits after harvest.
* Follow recommended post-harvest practices.

---

# Frequently Asked Questions

## How can I identify a mature mango?

Look for full shoulder development, proper fruit size, slight color change, reduced latex flow, and maturity based on variety-specific harvest period.

## Why should mangoes not be harvested too early?

Immature fruits often fail to ripen properly and have poor flavor and market quality.

## What is the most reliable maturity indicator?

Shoulder development combined with fruit age is considered one of the most reliable indicators.

## Can skin color alone determine maturity?

No. Skin color varies among varieties and should be used together with other maturity indicators.
