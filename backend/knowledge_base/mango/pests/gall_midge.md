# Gall Midge in Mango

## Overview

Gall midge is an important pest of mango that attacks tender leaves, flower panicles, buds, and young fruits. The larvae feed inside plant tissues, causing gall formation, tissue distortion, flower damage, and fruit loss.

Severe infestations can significantly reduce flowering, fruit set, and overall yield. Gall midge is particularly problematic during flushing and flowering periods.

---

# Scientific Classification

Common Name: Mango Gall Midge

Scientific Name: Procontarinia spp.

Order: Diptera

Family: Cecidomyiidae

---

# Distribution

Gall midge occurs in major mango-growing regions and is commonly observed during:

- New vegetative flushes
- Flowering season
- Periods of moderate temperature and humidity

---

# Identification

## Adult Midge

Characteristics:

- Tiny mosquito-like insect.
- Delicate body.
- Long legs.
- Short lifespan.

Size:

- Approximately 2–3 mm long.

Adults are difficult to observe due to their small size.

---

## Eggs

Characteristics:

- Tiny and transparent.
- Deposited on tender leaves, buds, or flower tissues.

---

## Larvae

Characteristics:

- Small maggots.
- Creamy white to yellowish.
- Feed within plant tissues.

This stage causes most of the damage.

---

## Pupae

Characteristics:

- Develop in soil or within plant debris.
- Brownish in color.

---

# Nature of Damage

Gall midge larvae feed internally within plant tissues.

Affected plant parts:

- Tender leaves
- Flower buds
- Flower panicles
- Young fruits

---

# Symptoms

## Leaf Gall Formation

Symptoms:

- Swollen structures on leaves.
- Distorted leaf growth.
- Curling and deformation.

---

## Damage to Flower Panicles

Symptoms:

- Drying of flowers.
- Flower abortion.
- Reduced fruit set.

---

## Bud Damage

Symptoms:

- Failure of bud development.
- Drying of young shoots.

---

## Fruit Damage

Symptoms:

- Young fruit drop.
- Reduced fruit retention.
- Poor fruit development.

---

# Life Cycle

## Egg Stage

Duration:

- 2–4 days

Eggs are laid on tender plant tissues.

---

## Larval Stage

Duration:

- 7–14 days

Larvae feed within plant tissues causing gall formation and tissue damage.

---

## Pupal Stage

Duration:

- 5–10 days

Pupation usually occurs in soil beneath the tree canopy.

---

## Adult Stage

Adults emerge from pupae and begin reproduction.

Several generations may occur during the growing season.

---

# Favorable Conditions

Gall midge populations increase under:

- Moderate temperatures.
- High humidity.
- Dense tree canopies.
- Presence of tender flushes and flowers.

---

# Economic Importance

Heavy infestations can cause:

- Loss of flowers.
- Reduced fruit set.
- Premature fruit drop.
- Yield reduction.

Young orchards may experience severe growth suppression.

---

# Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

## Orchard Sanitation

Recommendations:

- Remove fallen leaves.
- Destroy infested plant material.
- Maintain orchard cleanliness.

Benefits:

- Reduces breeding sites.
- Interrupts pest life cycle.

---

## Pruning

Recommended:

- Remove overcrowded branches.
- Improve sunlight penetration.
- Improve air circulation.

Benefits:

- Reduces favorable conditions for pest development.

---

## Soil Cultivation

Light cultivation around trees:

- Exposes pupae.
- Reduces adult emergence.

---

# Monitoring

Regularly inspect:

- New leaf flushes.
- Flower panicles.
- Bud development.
- Young fruits.

Early detection improves management success.

---

# Biological Control

Natural enemies may help suppress populations.

Examples:

- Predatory insects.
- Parasitoids.
- Beneficial microorganisms.

Conservation of beneficial organisms is recommended.

---

# Chemical Management

When infestations become economically significant:

- Use insecticides recommended by local agricultural authorities.
- Follow label instructions carefully.
- Apply treatments during vulnerable pest stages.

Important:

- Avoid unnecessary pesticide use.
- Follow integrated pest management principles.

---

# Monitoring Checklist

Inspect orchards weekly for:

- Leaf galls.
- Flower damage.
- Bud drying.
- Premature fruit drop.
- Presence of adult midges.

---

# Best Management Practices

- Maintain orchard sanitation.
- Monitor new flushes regularly.
- Prune trees annually.
- Remove infested tissues.
- Encourage beneficial insects.
- Practice integrated pest management.
- Follow local agricultural recommendations.

---

# Frequently Asked Questions

## What is the main symptom of gall midge infestation?

The most common symptom is gall formation on leaves along with flower and bud damage.

## Can gall midge reduce mango yield?

Yes. Severe infestations can reduce flowering, fruit set, and overall production.

## When is gall midge most active?

The pest is most active during flushing and flowering periods when tender plant tissues are available.

## Can pruning help manage gall midge?

Yes. Proper pruning improves air circulation and reduces favorable conditions for pest development.
