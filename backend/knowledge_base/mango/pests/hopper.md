# Mango Hopper

## Overview

Mango hopper is one of the most serious insect pests of mango and is considered a major cause of yield loss in mango orchards. The pest attacks flower panicles, tender shoots, leaves, and young fruits by sucking plant sap.

Severe infestations can lead to flower drying, poor fruit set, premature fruit drop, and significant yield reduction.

---

# Scientific Classification

Common Name: Mango Hopper

Scientific Names:

- Idioscopus clypealis
- Idioscopus nitidulus
- Amritodus atkinsoni

Order: Hemiptera

Family: Cicadellidae

---

# Distribution

Mango hoppers are found in almost all mango-growing regions and are particularly active during:

- Flowering season
- New vegetative flushes
- Warm and humid conditions

---

# Identification

## Adult Hopper

Characteristics:

- Small wedge-shaped insect.
- Light brown to dark brown color.
- Active and quick-moving.
- Usually found on flower panicles and tender shoots.

Size:

- Approximately 3–5 mm long.

---

## Nymph

Characteristics:

- Yellowish-green.
- Wingless.
- Active and mobile.

Nymphs and adults both cause damage.

---

# Nature of Damage

Mango hoppers feed by sucking sap from:

- Flower panicles.
- Tender leaves.
- Young shoots.
- Developing fruits.

---

# Symptoms

## Flower Damage

Symptoms:

- Drying of flowers.
- Wilting of flower panicles.
- Reduced fruit set.

---

## Sap Loss

Effects:

- Weakening of plant tissues.
- Reduced flowering efficiency.

---

## Honeydew Production

Hoppers excrete sugary honeydew.

Consequences:

- Sticky plant surfaces.
- Development of sooty mould fungus.
- Reduced photosynthesis.

---

## Fruit Loss

Symptoms:

- Poor fruit setting.
- Premature fruit drop.
- Reduced yield.

---

# Life Cycle

## Egg Stage

Duration:

- 4–7 days

Eggs are inserted into tender plant tissues.

---

## Nymph Stage

Duration:

- 10–15 days

Nymphs feed actively on plant sap.

---

## Adult Stage

Duration:

- Several weeks

Adults continue feeding and reproduction.

Several generations may occur annually.

---

# Favorable Conditions

Population increases under:

- Warm temperatures.
- High humidity.
- Dense tree canopy.
- Flowering period.

---

# Economic Importance

Heavy infestations may cause:

- 20–80% reduction in yield.
- Flower drying.
- Poor fruit retention.
- Significant economic losses.

The flowering stage is particularly vulnerable.

---

# Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

## Orchard Sanitation

Recommendations:

- Remove weeds.
- Remove dead branches.
- Maintain orchard cleanliness.

Benefits:

- Reduces pest breeding sites.

---

## Pruning

Benefits:

- Improves air circulation.
- Increases sunlight penetration.
- Reduces favorable conditions for pest multiplication.

---

## Monitoring

Inspect orchards regularly during flowering.

Check:

- Flower panicles.
- Tender shoots.
- New flushes.

Early detection is critical.

---

# Mechanical Control

Recommended practices:

- Remove heavily infested shoots where feasible.
- Maintain clean orchard conditions.

---

# Biological Control

Natural enemies include:

- Ladybird beetles.
- Spiders.
- Lacewings.
- Predatory insects.

Conservation of beneficial insects is encouraged.

---

# Chemical Management

If infestation reaches economic threshold levels:

Use insecticides recommended by agricultural authorities.

Important guidelines:

- Apply according to label recommendations.
- Avoid spraying during peak pollinator activity.
- Follow integrated pest management principles.

---

# Sooty Mould Association

Mango hopper infestations often lead to:

- Honeydew accumulation.
- Growth of black sooty mould fungus.

Consequences:

- Reduced photosynthesis.
- Poor tree vigor.
- Reduced fruit quality.

---

# Monitoring Checklist

Inspect regularly for:

- Hopper populations.
- Flower drying.
- Sticky honeydew deposits.
- Sooty mould development.
- Fruit set reduction.

---

# Best Management Practices

- Monitor flowering orchards regularly.
- Maintain orchard sanitation.
- Prune trees annually.
- Encourage natural enemies.
- Manage infestations early.
- Avoid unnecessary pesticide applications.
- Follow integrated pest management strategies.

---

# Frequently Asked Questions

## Why are mango flowers drying suddenly?

Mango hopper infestation is one of the most common causes of flower drying and poor fruit set.

## How does mango hopper damage trees?

The pest sucks sap from flowers, shoots, and leaves, weakening the tree and reducing fruit production.

## What is the black coating on leaves after hopper attack?

This is often sooty mould fungus growing on honeydew secreted by mango hoppers.

## When is mango hopper most active?

The pest is most active during the flowering season.
