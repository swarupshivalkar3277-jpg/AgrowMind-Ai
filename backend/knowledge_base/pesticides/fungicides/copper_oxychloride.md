# Copper Oxychloride

# Overview

Copper oxychloride is a copper-based contact fungicide used for managing several fungal and bacterial diseases in crops. It is commonly used as a protective fungicide in vegetables, fruits, plantation crops, spices, and field crops.

Copper oxychloride helps protect plants by forming a protective layer on the plant surface. When fungal spores or some bacterial cells come in contact with treated surfaces, copper ions interfere with their biological processes and reduce infection.

Copper oxychloride is mainly used for:

* Leaf spots
* Blights
* Fruit rots
* Cankers
* Downy mildew in some crops
* Bud rot
* Foot rot
* Some bacterial diseases where copper fungicides are recommended

Copper oxychloride is not a systemic fungicide. It does not move inside the plant. It protects only the plant surface where spray coverage is present.

Farmers should always follow the product label, local agricultural university recommendation, and agriculture officer guidance before using copper oxychloride.

---

# What is Copper Oxychloride?

Copper oxychloride is an inorganic copper fungicide. It is used as a protectant fungicide to prevent disease infection on leaves, stems, fruits, and other plant parts.

It is commonly available as:

* Copper oxychloride 50% WP
* Copper oxychloride 50% WG
* Copper oxychloride combinations with other fungicides or bactericides

The exact dose, crops, diseases, waiting period, and safety instructions depend on formulation and label approval.

---

# Fungicide Type

Copper oxychloride is a contact fungicide.

## Contact Fungicide Meaning

A contact fungicide remains mostly on the plant surface.

It does not move inside the plant through sap flow.

This means:

* Good spray coverage is very important.
* It protects only treated plant surfaces.
* New growth after spraying may remain unprotected.
* It is better for prevention than cure.
* Repeat spraying may be needed under disease-favourable weather.

---

# Mode of Action

Copper oxychloride releases copper ions on the plant surface.

Copper ions affect fungal spores and some bacterial cells by interfering with:

* Protein function
* Enzyme activity
* Cell membrane processes
* Spore germination
* Pathogen growth on plant surface

Copper fungicides are generally multi-site protectant fungicides. This means they affect pathogens at multiple biological points rather than one single target site.

---

# FRAC Group

Copper fungicides are generally classified under:

* FRAC Group M01
* Multi-site contact activity
* Low to medium resistance risk compared with many single-site fungicides

Even though resistance risk is lower, overuse should still be avoided. Copper should be used as part of an integrated disease management plan.

# Copper Oxychloride in Tomato

Copper oxychloride is commonly used in tomato disease management where recommended.

## Target Diseases in Tomato

It may be used for:

* Early blight
* Late blight
* Leaf spot
* Some bacterial leaf spot problems where copper products are recommended

## Symptoms Managed

### Early Blight

Symptoms may include:

* Brown circular leaf spots
* Concentric rings in spots
* Yellowing around spots
* Older leaves affected first
* Leaf drying and defoliation

### Late Blight

Symptoms may include:

* Water-soaked lesions
* Brown to black patches on leaves
* Rapid spread in cool and humid weather
* Stem lesions
* Fruit infection
* Severe crop loss if uncontrolled

### Leaf Spot

Symptoms may include:

* Small brown or dark spots
* Yellowing leaves
* Leaf drying
* Reduced photosynthesis

## Tomato Advisory

Copper oxychloride works best as a preventive or early-stage spray. It should be used before disease becomes severe, especially during humid, cloudy, rainy, or high-dew conditions.

# When to Use Copper Oxychloride

Copper oxychloride should be used:

* Before disease appears in high-risk weather
* At first disease symptoms
* After pruning where protective spray is recommended
* During rainy or humid periods
* When leaf wetness is high
* When disease has occurred in previous season
* As part of preventive disease program

It is less effective if disease is already severe and widespread.

---

# Weather Conditions Favoring Use

Copper oxychloride may be useful when disease risk increases due to:

* Rainfall
* High humidity
* Cloudy weather
* Heavy dew
* Poor air circulation
* Waterlogging
* Dense canopy
* Frequent irrigation wetting foliage
* History of disease in field

---

# Dose and Spray Concentration

The dose depends on:

* Formulation
* Crop
* Disease
* Spray volume
* Plant canopy size
* Local recommendation
* Product label

Common field recommendations for copper oxychloride 50% WP are often around 2 to 3 grams per litre of water, but this must be confirmed from the product label and local agricultural recommendation.

Do not exceed label dose.

Excess copper can cause:

* Leaf burn
* Fruit russeting
* Flower injury
* Soil copper buildup
* Beneficial microbe disturbance
* Residue issues

# How to Prepare Spray Solution

## Step 1: Read the Label

Check:

* Crop
* Disease
* Dose
* Waiting period
* Safety instructions
* Compatibility
* Protective equipment
* Re-entry interval where mentioned

## Step 2: Measure Clean Water

Use clean water for spray solution.

Avoid muddy or highly alkaline water.

## Step 3: Make Slurry

Mix the required quantity of copper oxychloride in a small amount of water to make a smooth slurry.

## Step 4: Add to Spray Tank

Pour slurry into the spray tank filled halfway with water.

## Step 5: Stir Well

Agitate properly.

## Step 6: Add Remaining Water

Fill remaining water and mix continuously.

## Step 7: Spray Immediately

Use the spray solution the same day.

Do not store mixed spray solution overnight.

---

# Spray Method

For effective control:

* Use clean sprayer.
* Use proper nozzle.
* Spray both upper and lower leaf surfaces.
* Cover stems and fruit where disease risk exists.
* Avoid runoff.
* Avoid spraying during strong wind.
* Avoid spraying during very hot afternoon.
* Avoid spraying before heavy rain.
* Spray during calm morning or evening conditions.

---

# Importance of Coverage

Copper oxychloride is a contact fungicide.

Good coverage is essential because:

* Unsprayed leaves remain unprotected.
* New growth after spray is not protected.
* Dense canopy may hide lower leaves.
* Disease can start from untreated areas.

Use enough water volume to cover the crop canopy properly.

---

# Spray Interval

Spray interval depends on:

* Disease pressure
* Weather
* Crop growth stage
* Rainfall
* Irrigation method
* Product label
* Local recommendation

During high disease pressure, shorter intervals may be required according to expert guidance.

After heavy rain, protective copper deposits may reduce, and repeat spray may be needed if allowed by label.

---

# Waiting Period

Waiting period, also called pre-harvest interval, is the minimum gap between last spray and harvest.

Waiting period varies by:

* Crop
* Formulation
* Dose
* Label approval
* Country or market requirement
* Buyer residue standard

Farmers must follow the waiting period mentioned on the product label.

Do not harvest immediately after spraying unless label allows it.

---

# Compatibility

Copper oxychloride should not be mixed randomly with other agrochemicals.

Avoid mixing with:

* Strongly acidic products
* Strongly alkaline products
* Some foliar fertilizers
* Some insecticides
* Oils or oil-based sprays unless recommended
* Microbial biopesticides unless compatibility is confirmed

Before tank mixing:

1. Read labels of both products.
2. Ask agriculture officer or company expert.
3. Conduct jar test.
4. Spray small area first.

---

# Phytotoxicity Risk

Phytotoxicity means crop injury due to chemical spray.

Copper oxychloride may cause injury if:

* Dose is too high
* Sprayed during hot weather
* Sprayed on stressed crops
* Mixed with incompatible chemicals
* Used on sensitive crop stage
* Repeated excessively
* Sprayed with poor water quality
* Applied close to flowering in sensitive crops

## Symptoms of Phytotoxicity

* Leaf burn
* Brown spots
* Yellowing
* Leaf drop
* Fruit russeting
* Flower drop
* Stunted growth

Stop use and consult an expert if injury appears.

# Safety Precautions

Copper oxychloride should be handled carefully.

## Personal Protective Equipment

Use:

* Gloves
* Mask
* Goggles
* Long-sleeved clothing
* Long pants
* Shoes or boots
* Head cover where needed

## During Mixing

* Do not inhale dust.
* Avoid contact with eyes and skin.
* Mix in open or well-ventilated area.
* Do not eat, drink, or smoke.
* Keep children and animals away.

## During Spraying

* Spray with wind direction.
* Avoid drift to people, animals, ponds, and nearby crops.
* Do not spray in strong wind.
* Avoid walking into spray mist.
* Do not allow unprotected workers in treated area.

## After Spraying

* Wash hands and face with soap.
* Bathe after spraying.
* Wash contaminated clothes separately.
* Clean sprayer safely.
* Dispose empty container according to label instructions.

# First Aid

If exposure occurs:

## Skin Contact

* Remove contaminated clothing.
* Wash skin with plenty of water and soap.
* Seek medical help if irritation continues.

## Eye Contact

* Rinse eyes with clean water for several minutes.
* Do not rub eyes.
* Seek medical help.

## Inhalation

* Move person to fresh air.
* Loosen tight clothing.
* Seek medical help if breathing difficulty occurs.

## Accidental Swallowing

* Do not induce vomiting unless medical professional advises.
* Take product label or container to doctor.
* Seek medical help immediately.

# Environmental Precautions

Copper can accumulate in soil if used excessively.

Farmers should avoid:

* Repeated unnecessary copper sprays
* Overdose
* Spray drift into water bodies
* Washing sprayers in ponds
* Copper use without disease risk
* Excessive use in orchards over many years

Copper can affect aquatic organisms and soil microorganisms when misused.

---

# Resistance Management

Copper fungicides have multi-site action, so resistance risk is generally lower than many single-site fungicides.

However:

* Do not overuse copper.
* Rotate with other recommended fungicides.
* Use disease forecasting where available.
* Combine with sanitation and cultural practices.
* Avoid repeated sprays when disease risk is low.
* Follow label limits.

---

# Integrated Disease Management

Copper oxychloride works best when combined with other disease management practices.

## Cultural Practices

* Use disease-free seed or seedlings.
* Maintain proper spacing.
* Avoid overhead irrigation where possible.
* Improve drainage.
* Remove infected plant parts.
* Avoid dense canopy.
* Practice crop rotation.
* Avoid working in wet fields when disease spreads easily.

## Nutrient Management

* Avoid excess nitrogen.
* Maintain potassium and calcium.
* Use balanced nutrition.
* Improve soil organic matter.

## Field Sanitation

* Remove diseased leaves and fruits.
* Destroy infected residues safely.
* Avoid leaving rotten fruits in field.
* Clean tools and crates.

## Resistant Varieties

Use resistant or tolerant varieties where available.

# Copper Oxychloride and Organic Farming

Copper-based fungicides may be allowed in some organic systems under restricted conditions, depending on certification standard.

Organic farmers should:

* Check certification rules before use.
* Use only approved products.
* Maintain spray records.
* Avoid excessive copper buildup.
* Use preventive cultural practices first.
* Follow allowed dose and frequency limits.

Do not assume all copper products are automatically allowed in certified organic farming.

---

# Copper Oxychloride and Biofungicides

Copper oxychloride may harm beneficial microbial biofungicides if mixed directly.

Avoid direct tank mixing with:

* Trichoderma
* Pseudomonas fluorescens
* Bacillus-based biofungicides
* Other live microbial products

If using biofungicides and copper fungicide in the same crop program, keep a suitable time gap and follow expert guidance.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

Farmers should avoid:

* Spraying after disease becomes severe and expecting full cure
* Using overdose
* Poor spray coverage
* Spraying during hot afternoon
* Spraying before heavy rain
* Mixing with unknown chemicals
* Ignoring waiting period
* Not wearing protective equipment
* Reusing pesticide containers
* Applying repeatedly without disease risk
* Using copper as a substitute for drainage and sanitation
* Ignoring label instructions

# Farmer Recommendations

Farmers are advised to:

1. Use copper oxychloride mainly as a preventive or early-stage disease spray.
2. Confirm crop, disease, dose, and waiting period from the label.
3. Use good spray coverage because it is a contact fungicide.
4. Avoid overdose and unnecessary repeated sprays.
5. Do not mix with other chemicals unless compatibility is confirmed.
6. Wear protective equipment during mixing and spraying.
7. Avoid spraying during hot afternoon, strong wind, or before rain.
8. Combine copper use with sanitation, drainage, crop rotation, and balanced nutrition.
9. Keep spray records for residue safety and farm planning.
10. Consult agriculture experts for severe disease outbreaks.
