# Fungicide Types

## Overview

Fungicides are crop-protection products used to prevent, suppress, or control diseases caused by fungi and fungus-like organisms. They are commonly used in agriculture to protect leaves, stems, roots, fruits, seeds, and stored produce from diseases such as powdery mildew, downy mildew, rust, blight, leaf spot, anthracnose, damping-off, fruit rot, and root rot.

Fungicides do not work equally against all diseases. A fungicide should be selected based on the crop, disease, disease stage, weather conditions, infection pressure, product label, mode of action, and local agricultural recommendations.

Common fungicide classification methods include:

* Classification by activity: protectant, curative, eradicant.
* Classification by movement in plant: contact, systemic, translaminar, locally systemic.
* Classification by application method: seed treatment, soil application, foliar spray, post-harvest treatment.
* Classification by chemical group or mode of action: copper fungicides, sulfur fungicides, triazoles, strobilurins, dithiocarbamates, benzimidazoles, phenylamides, SDHI fungicides, biological fungicides.
* Classification by resistance risk: multi-site fungicides and single-site fungicides.

---

## 1. Protectant Fungicides

Protectant fungicides are applied before disease infection occurs. They form a protective layer on the plant surface and prevent fungal spores from germinating or entering the plant tissue.

### Key Features

* Best used before disease appears or at the very early stage.
* Mostly remain on the plant surface.
* Usually need good spray coverage.
* New plant growth after spraying may remain unprotected.
* Rain, irrigation, and sunlight can reduce effectiveness.
* Often used as preventive sprays during disease-favorable weather.

### Examples

* Mancozeb
* Copper oxychloride
* Copper hydroxide
* Chlorothalonil
* Sulfur
* Bordeaux mixture

### Common Use Cases

* Preventive control of leaf spot, blight, anthracnose, rust, mildew, and fruit rot.
* Protection during rainy or humid weather.
* Used before expected disease outbreak.
* Used in resistance-management programs because many protectant fungicides have multi-site action.

### Farmer Guidance

Use protectant fungicides when disease has not yet spread widely. They are most useful when applied before infection, especially before rain, high humidity, cloudy weather, or known disease-prone crop stages.

### Limitations

Protectant fungicides generally cannot cure established infection inside the plant. They mainly protect healthy tissue from new infection.

---

## 2. Contact Fungicides

Contact fungicides stay mostly on the surface where they are applied. They do not move significantly inside the plant.

### Key Features

* Require complete spray coverage.
* Protect only treated plant parts.
* Do not protect new growth after spraying.
* Can be washed off by rain if not rainfast.
* Usually act as preventive fungicides.

### Examples

* Mancozeb
* Copper oxychloride
* Sulfur
* Chlorothalonil
* Captan
* Zineb
* Ziram

### Common Use Cases

* Leaf surface protection.
* Fruit protection.
* Vegetable disease prevention.
* Seedling and nursery disease prevention.

### Farmer Guidance

Spray contact fungicides evenly on both upper and lower leaf surfaces. Poor coverage gives poor control. Repeat sprays may be required during continuous rainy weather or rapid crop growth.

---

## 3. Systemic Fungicides

Systemic fungicides are absorbed by the plant and move inside plant tissues. Some move upward through the xylem, while others move locally within the treated area.

### Key Features

* Can protect some internal plant tissues.
* May control early infections after the fungus has entered the plant.
* Often have specific modes of action.
* Usually require lower spray coverage than contact fungicides, but good coverage is still important.
* Many systemic fungicides have higher resistance risk if used repeatedly.

### Examples

* Carbendazim
* Hexaconazole
* Propiconazole
* Tebuconazole
* Metalaxyl
* Azoxystrobin
* Tricyclazole
* Fosetyl-Al
* Difenoconazole

### Common Use Cases

* Powdery mildew
* Rust
* Blast disease
* Sheath blight
* Downy mildew, depending on active ingredient
* Early-stage leaf spot and blight diseases

### Farmer Guidance

Systemic fungicides should not be used repeatedly from the same chemical group. Rotate fungicides with different modes of action to reduce resistance risk.

### Limitations

Systemic fungicides are not magic cures. They are most effective when applied at early disease stages. Severe infection, dead tissue, and advanced disease cannot be fully reversed.

---

## 4. Translaminar Fungicides

Translaminar fungicides move from the sprayed leaf surface to the opposite side of the leaf. They do not move throughout the entire plant like fully systemic fungicides.

### Key Features

* Useful when spray reaches only one side of the leaf.
* Can protect leaf tissue near the sprayed area.
* Limited movement compared with systemic fungicides.
* Often effective against foliar diseases.

### Examples

* Azoxystrobin
* Trifloxystrobin
* Some triazole fungicides
* Some SDHI fungicides

### Common Use Cases

* Powdery mildew
* Leaf spot
* Rust
* Blight
* Diseases affecting leaf surfaces

### Farmer Guidance

Even though translaminar fungicides can move across the leaf, farmers should still aim for full spray coverage for best control.

---

## 5. Curative Fungicides

Curative fungicides act after infection has started but before the disease is fully developed. They help stop early fungal growth inside the plant.

### Key Features

* Work only during early infection stages.
* Do not restore dead or badly damaged plant tissue.
* Often systemic or locally systemic.
* Timing is very important.
* Useful when disease symptoms are just beginning.

### Examples

* Tebuconazole
* Propiconazole
* Hexaconazole
* Difenoconazole
* Carbendazim
* Metalaxyl, for some oomycete diseases

### Common Use Cases

* Early powdery mildew infection.
* Early rust infection.
* Initial leaf spot symptoms.
* Early downy mildew or blight infection, depending on product.

### Farmer Guidance

Apply curative fungicides as soon as early symptoms are noticed. Do not wait until the crop is heavily infected. Curative fungicides should be combined with preventive measures and proper rotation.

### Important Note

The term “curative” does not mean that the fungicide can fully heal a diseased plant. It means the product may stop or slow the infection if applied very early.

---

## 6. Eradicant Fungicides

Eradicant fungicides reduce or destroy fungal growth already present on or in plant tissue. They are used after visible disease development, but they are most useful at early disease stages.

### Key Features

* Suppress active fungal growth.
* May reduce spore production.
* Usually effective only when disease is not too advanced.
* Often used with follow-up protectant sprays.

### Examples

* Sulfur, for powdery mildew
* Lime sulfur, in some orchard uses
* Some systemic fungicides with eradicant activity
* Some triazole fungicides, depending on disease and timing

### Common Use Cases

* Powdery mildew suppression.
* Early-stage foliar fungal growth.
* Orchard disease management.
* Reducing disease spread after initial symptoms.

### Farmer Guidance

Use eradicant fungicides only as per label and crop recommendation. Do not rely only on eradicant sprays; combine with sanitation, resistant varieties, proper spacing, and preventive fungicides.

---

## 7. Multi-Site Fungicides

Multi-site fungicides affect several biological processes in the fungus. Because they attack the pathogen at multiple points, they generally have lower resistance risk.

### Key Features

* Lower risk of fungicide resistance.
* Mostly protectant/contact in nature.
* Broad-spectrum disease control.
* Useful in fungicide rotation and mixture programs.
* Often used to protect high-risk systemic fungicides from resistance development.

### Examples

* Mancozeb
* Copper fungicides
* Chlorothalonil
* Captan
* Sulfur

### Common Use Cases

* Preventive disease control.
* Resistance management.
* Broad-spectrum protection in vegetables, fruits, cereals, and pulses.

### Farmer Guidance

Multi-site fungicides are good options for preventive sprays and for mixing or rotating with single-site fungicides where allowed by label and local recommendations.

---

## 8. Single-Site Fungicides

Single-site fungicides target one specific biochemical process in the fungus. They are often highly effective but may have higher resistance risk if overused.

### Key Features

* Specific mode of action.
* Often systemic or translaminar.
* Can be highly effective against target diseases.
* Higher resistance risk when used repeatedly.
* Should be rotated with different FRAC groups.

### Examples

* Triazoles
* Strobilurins
* Benzimidazoles
* Phenylamides
* SDHI fungicides

### Common Use Cases

* Targeted control of specific diseases.
* Early curative action.
* High-value crop disease management.
* Disease control during high infection pressure.

### Farmer Guidance

Avoid repeated sprays of the same single-site fungicide or same FRAC group. Rotate with other modes of action and include multi-site fungicides when recommended.

# Fungicides by Chemical Group

## 9. Copper Fungicides

Copper fungicides are inorganic, mostly contact fungicides. They are widely used as protective fungicides against many fungal and bacterial diseases.

### Common Active Ingredients

* Copper oxychloride
* Copper hydroxide
* Copper sulfate
* Bordeaux mixture
* Tribasic copper sulfate

### Disease Targets

* Leaf spot
* Anthracnose
* Bacterial leaf spot
* Downy mildew
* Fruit rot
* Blight diseases

### Common Crops

* Tomato
* Potato
* Grapes
* Citrus
* Mango
* Pomegranate
* Chilli
* Cucurbits
* Onion
* Banana

### Advantages

* Broad-spectrum protection.
* Useful against some bacterial diseases.
* Lower resistance risk because of multi-site action.
* Useful in preventive disease management.

### Limitations

* Can cause phytotoxicity if misused.
* May leave visible residue on leaves and fruits.
* Should not be overused in sensitive crops.
* Effectiveness depends on coverage and weather.

### Farmer Guidance

Copper fungicides should be applied preventively. Avoid spraying during very hot conditions or on stressed crops unless recommended. Follow label dose carefully.

---

## 10. Sulfur Fungicides

Sulfur fungicides are mainly contact fungicides used especially against powdery mildew and mites in some crops.

### Common Forms

* Wettable sulfur
* Dusting sulfur
* Lime sulfur

### Disease Targets

* Powdery mildew
* Some rust diseases
* Some fungal surface infections

### Common Crops

* Grapes
* Mango
* Cucurbits
* Peas
* Apple
* Rose
* Chilli

### Advantages

* Effective against powdery mildew.
* Lower resistance risk.
* Can be used in some organic farming systems, depending on certification rules.
* Relatively broad surface activity.

### Limitations

* Can cause leaf burn in hot weather.
* Should not be mixed with certain oils or sprayed close to oil sprays.
* May be less effective in cool conditions.
* Strong smell and dust irritation may occur.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not apply sulfur during high temperature periods. Avoid use on sulfur-sensitive crops. Follow local crop-specific recommendations.

### Common Active Ingredients

* Zineb

* Potato
* Tomato

* Onion
* Chilli
* Cucurbits

### Advantages

* Broad-spectrum disease control.
* Useful as preventive fungicides.

### Limitations

* Requires good coverage.
* Can be washed off by heavy rain.
* Must follow pre-harvest interval and residue guidelines.

### Farmer Guidance

Use dithiocarbamates before disease spread. They are useful when weather is favorable for disease but severe infection has not yet occurred.

## 12. Benzimidazole Fungicides

Benzimidazoles are systemic fungicides with specific action against fungal cell division. They have been widely used but resistance can develop quickly.

### Common Active Ingredients

* Carbendazim
* Thiophanate-methyl
* Benomyl, older active ingredient

### Disease Targets

* Anthracnose
* Powdery mildew
* Leaf spot
* Fruit rot
* Some seed-borne diseases

### Common Crops

* Mango
* Grapes
* Pulses
* Vegetables
* Cereals
* Fruits

### Advantages

* Systemic action.
* Useful for some seed treatments and foliar sprays.
* Can control early infection.

### Limitations

* High resistance risk.
* Repeated use can reduce effectiveness.
* Not suitable for all fungal diseases.
* Should not be used continuously.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not spray carbendazim repeatedly. Rotate with different fungicide groups and use only when recommended for the target disease.

## 13. Triazole Fungicides

Triazoles are systemic fungicides that inhibit sterol biosynthesis in fungi. They are commonly used for powdery mildew, rusts, leaf spots, and many foliar diseases.

### Common Active Ingredients

* Tebuconazole
* Propiconazole
* Hexaconazole
* Difenoconazole
* Myclobutanil
* Triadimefon

### Disease Targets

* Powdery mildew
* Rust
* Leaf spot
* Sheath blight
* Anthracnose
* Scab
* Fruit rot

### Common Crops

* Wheat
* Rice
* Grapes
* Mango
* Apple
* Chilli
* Groundnut
* Soybean
* Vegetables

### Advantages

* Systemic movement.
* Protective and early curative action.
* Effective against many foliar fungal diseases.
* Useful during early disease development.

### Limitations

* Resistance risk if overused.
* Some products may affect plant growth if misused.
* Not equally effective against all fungi.
* Should not be applied at wrong crop stage or excessive dose.

### Farmer Guidance

Rotate triazoles with other fungicide groups. Use correct dose and spray interval. Avoid unnecessary sprays when disease pressure is low.

## 14. Strobilurin Fungicides

Strobilurins, also called QoI fungicides, affect fungal respiration. They are often used for foliar diseases and may provide protective and some early curative activity.

### Common Active Ingredients

* Azoxystrobin
* Trifloxystrobin
* Pyraclostrobin
* Kresoxim-methyl

### Disease Targets

* Powdery mildew
* Downy mildew, depending on product
* Leaf spot
* Rust
* Blight
* Anthracnose

### Common Crops

* Grapes
* Rice
* Wheat
* Soybean
* Vegetables
* Fruits
* Pulses

### Advantages

* Broad disease-control spectrum.
* Translaminar or locally systemic movement.
* Good preventive activity.
* May improve green leaf retention in some crops.

### Limitations

* High resistance risk in many pathogens.
* Should not be used repeatedly.
* Not all strobilurins control all diseases.
* Resistance can develop quickly under high disease pressure.

### Farmer Guidance

Limit the number of strobilurin sprays per season. Rotate with different FRAC groups and avoid back-to-back applications.

## 16. Phenylamide Fungicides

Phenylamide fungicides are systemic fungicides mainly used against oomycete diseases such as downy mildew and some blights.

### Common Active Ingredients

* Benalaxyl

### Disease Targets

* Downy mildew
* Phytophthora diseases
* Pythium diseases
* Some damping-off diseases

### Common Crops

* Grapes
* Onion
* Potato
* Cucurbits
* Vegetables
* Nursery crops

### Advantages

* Strong systemic activity.
* Useful against oomycete pathogens.
* Can protect new growth to some extent.

### Limitations

* High resistance risk.
* Should not be used alone repeatedly.
* Not effective against many true fungi such as rust and powdery mildew.
* Often combined with protectant fungicides.

### Farmer Guidance

Use metalaxyl-type fungicides only when the disease is caused by oomycetes such as downy mildew, Pythium, or Phytophthora. Rotate with other fungicide groups.

* Bacillus subtilis
* Bacillus amyloliquefaciens
* Ampelomyces quisqualis

### Disease Targets

* Seedling diseases
* Soil-borne fungal diseases
* Powdery mildew, for some biological products

### Common Uses

* Seed treatment
* Soil application
* Nursery treatment
* Compost enrichment
* Drip application
* Foliar spray, depending on product

### Advantages

* Useful in integrated disease management.
* Can improve soil microbial balance.
* Lower chemical residue concern.
* Helpful in organic and low-residue production systems, depending on certification.

### Limitations

* Slower action than chemical fungicides.
* Effectiveness depends on temperature, moisture, soil condition, and product quality.
* May not control severe disease alone.
* Requires correct storage and application.

### Farmer Guidance

Use biofungicides preventively. Apply in moist soil or suitable environmental conditions. Avoid mixing with incompatible chemical fungicides unless recommended.

# Fungicides by Application Method

## 18. Seed Treatment Fungicides

Seed treatment fungicides are applied to seeds before sowing to protect them from seed-borne and soil-borne pathogens.

### Disease Targets

* Seed rot
* Seedling blight
* Damping-off
* Smut
* Bunt
* Some root rots

### Examples

* Thiram
* Carbendazim
* Captan
* Metalaxyl
* Tebuconazole
* Trichoderma-based biofungicides

### Benefits

* Protects seeds during germination.
* Reduces early crop loss.
* Uses small quantity of fungicide.
* Improves plant stand in disease-prone soils.

### Farmer Guidance

Use recommended seed-treatment dose only. Mix uniformly with seeds. Treated seeds should not be used for food, feed, or oil extraction.

# Fungicide Selection Guide

## Selection Based on Disease Stage

### Before Disease Appears

Use protectant or contact fungicides.

Examples:

* Mancozeb
* Copper oxychloride
* Chlorothalonil
* Sulfur
* Captan

### At Early Disease Stage

Use systemic, translaminar, or curative fungicides where recommended.

Examples:

* Tebuconazole
* Propiconazole
* Hexaconazole
* Difenoconazole
* Azoxystrobin
* Carbendazim

### At Severe Disease Stage

Fungicides may only reduce further spread. Remove infected plant parts where possible and combine chemical control with cultural practices.

Recommended approach:

* Remove heavily infected leaves or fruits.
* Improve air circulation.
* Avoid overhead irrigation.
* Apply recommended systemic plus protectant fungicide if allowed.
* Rotate mode of action.
* Prevent further spread to healthy plants.

# Fungicide Resistance Management

Fungicide resistance occurs when a fungal pathogen becomes less sensitive to a fungicide. This can happen when the same fungicide or same mode-of-action group is used repeatedly.

## Causes of Resistance

* Repeated use of the same fungicide.
* Repeated use of the same FRAC group.
* Using lower than recommended dose.
* Using poor-quality or expired product.
* Spraying after disease is already severe.
* Not combining fungicides with cultural disease management.
* High disease pressure and frequent pathogen reproduction.

## Resistance Prevention Practices

* Rotate fungicides with different modes of action.
* Avoid repeated sprays from the same chemical group.
* Use multi-site fungicides in disease-management programs.
* Use fungicide mixtures only when recommended.
* Apply fungicides at correct dose and interval.
* Use resistant crop varieties where available.
* Remove infected crop residues.
* Avoid dense planting and poor aeration.
* Manage irrigation to reduce leaf wetness.
* Follow FRAC and local agricultural recommendations.

## High Resistance-Risk Groups

These groups need careful rotation:

* Strobilurins
* Benzimidazoles
* Phenylamides
* SDHI fungicides
* Some triazoles, depending on pathogen and usage pattern

## Lower Resistance-Risk Groups

These groups generally have lower resistance risk because of multi-site action:

* Copper fungicides
* Sulfur
* Mancozeb
* Chlorothalonil
* Captan

# Integrated Disease Management with Fungicides

Fungicides work best when used as part of integrated disease management.

## Important Non-Chemical Practices

* Use disease-free seeds and seedlings.
* Choose resistant or tolerant varieties.
* Practice crop rotation.
* Remove infected plant debris.
* Avoid waterlogging.
* Improve field drainage.
* Maintain proper plant spacing.
* Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilizer.
* Use balanced nutrition.
* Control weeds that may host pathogens.
* Use clean tools and equipment.
* Avoid overhead irrigation where foliar disease is common.
* Monitor weather conditions and disease symptoms regularly.

## Why Fungicides Alone May Fail

Fungicides may fail if:

* Wrong fungicide is selected.
* Disease is not fungal.
* Disease is too advanced.
* Spray coverage is poor.
* Rain washes off fungicide.
* Pathogen has developed resistance.
* Dose is incorrect.
* Application timing is wrong.
* Product is expired or fake.
* Crop is stressed due to nutrient deficiency, drought, or waterlogging.

# Safety Guidelines for Fungicide Use

## Before Spraying

* Identify the disease correctly.
* Read the product label.
* Check recommended crop and disease.
* Confirm dose and dilution.
* Wear protective clothing.
* Check sprayer condition.
* Avoid spraying during strong wind.
* Avoid spraying during extreme heat.
* Do not mix products unless compatibility is known.

## During Spraying

* Wear gloves, mask, goggles, long sleeves, and boots.
* Do not eat, drink, or smoke while spraying.
* Avoid inhaling spray mist.
* Spray in the direction of wind movement, not against it.
* Avoid drift to animals, water bodies, and nearby people.
* Keep children and livestock away from the spray area.

## After Spraying

* Wash hands, face, and exposed skin.
* Wash spray clothes separately.
* Clean sprayer safely.
* Dispose empty containers according to local rules.
* Do not reuse pesticide containers.
* Follow re-entry interval.
* Follow pre-harvest interval before harvesting.

# Farmer Questions and Answers

## Q1. What is the best fungicide type for disease prevention?

Protectant or contact fungicides are usually best for prevention. Examples include mancozeb, copper oxychloride, sulfur, chlorothalonil, and captan. They should be applied before disease infection occurs.

## Q2. What is the difference between contact and systemic fungicides?

Contact fungicides remain on the plant surface and protect only the sprayed area. Systemic fungicides are absorbed by the plant and move inside plant tissues to protect or treat early infection.

## Q3. Can fungicides cure a diseased plant?

Fungicides cannot fully repair damaged or dead plant tissue. Curative fungicides may stop early infection, but advanced disease cannot be completely cured.

## Q4. Which fungicide type has lower resistance risk?

Multi-site fungicides such as mancozeb, copper fungicides, sulfur, chlorothalonil, and captan generally have lower resistance risk.

## Q5. Why should farmers rotate fungicides?

Rotation prevents the fungus from becoming resistant. Farmers should avoid repeated use of the same fungicide or same FRAC group.

## Q6. Are biological fungicides effective?

Biological fungicides can be effective when used preventively and under suitable conditions. They are especially useful for soil-borne diseases, seed treatment, nursery management, and integrated disease management.

## Q7. Which fungicide is useful for powdery mildew?

Sulfur, triazoles, strobilurins, and some biological fungicides are commonly used for powdery mildew, depending on crop and local recommendation.

## Q8. Which fungicide is useful for downy mildew?

Downy mildew is commonly managed using metalaxyl combinations, mancozeb, copper fungicides, cymoxanil combinations, or other oomycete-specific fungicides, depending on crop and disease severity.

## Q9. Why did the fungicide spray fail?

Possible reasons include wrong disease diagnosis, wrong fungicide selection, late application, poor spray coverage, rain wash-off, resistance, incorrect dose, expired product, or poor crop health.

## Q10. Can copper fungicide control bacterial disease?

Copper fungicides can help manage some bacterial plant diseases, but effectiveness depends on disease, crop, timing, and coverage. They should be used according to label and local recommendations.
