# Pre-Emergence Herbicides

# Overview

Pre-emergence herbicides are herbicides applied before weeds emerge from the soil. They are mainly used to control weed seedlings at the germination or early emergence stage. In most crops, pre-emergence herbicides are applied after sowing or transplanting the crop but before weeds appear.

Pre-emergence herbicides help farmers:

* Control early weed competition
* Reduce manual weeding cost
* Improve crop establishment
* Protect crop yield during early growth
* Reduce weed pressure during critical crop stages
* Improve nutrient, water, and light availability for crops
* Support timely weed management
* Reduce labor dependency

Pre-emergence herbicides do not usually control large established weeds. They work best when applied at the correct time on a well-prepared, moist soil surface.

---

# What is a Pre-Emergence Herbicide?

A pre-emergence herbicide is a weed control chemical applied before weeds emerge.

It may be applied:

* After sowing but before weed emergence
* After transplanting but before weeds emerge
* Before crop emergence in some crops where label allows
* Soon after irrigation or rainfall where soil moisture is adequate
* On prepared soil surface before weed germination

Pre-emergence herbicides usually form a herbicidal layer in the upper soil zone. When weed seeds germinate, the emerging roots or shoots absorb the herbicide and die or fail to establish.

---

# Difference Between Pre-Emergence and Post-Emergence Herbicides

| Herbicide Type           | Application Timing             | Main Target                               |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------- |
| Pre-emergence herbicide  | Before weeds emerge            | Germinating weed seeds and tiny seedlings |
| Post-emergence herbicide | After weeds emerge             | Existing visible weeds                    |
| Pre-plant herbicide      | Before crop sowing or planting | Existing weeds before crop establishment  |

Pre-emergence herbicides prevent weed establishment. Post-emergence herbicides control weeds after they are already visible.

---

# Why Pre-Emergence Weed Control is Important

Early weed competition can reduce crop yield because weeds compete with the crop for:

* Soil moisture
* Nutrients
* Sunlight
* Space
* Air movement
* Root zone resources

Early weed control is especially important in crops that grow slowly during early stages, such as:

* Onion
* Chilli
* Tomato
* Cotton
* Pulses
* Oilseeds
* Maize
* Sugarcane
* Groundnut
* Soybean
* Vegetables

If weeds are allowed to grow during the critical early period, later removal may not fully recover yield loss.

---

# How Pre-Emergence Herbicides Work

Pre-emergence herbicides work mainly in the soil.

They may act by:

* Inhibiting root growth
* Inhibiting shoot growth
* Stopping cell division
* Blocking photosynthesis in emerging seedlings
* Interrupting lipid or protein synthesis
* Preventing weed seedling establishment

Most pre-emergence herbicides affect weeds when they are germinating or very young.

They do not usually kill dormant weed seeds permanently.

---

# Important Features of Pre-Emergence Herbicides

Pre-emergence herbicides:

* Need correct timing
* Need soil moisture for activation
* Need uniform soil coverage
* Work best on fine seedbed
* Are affected by soil type
* May be affected by organic matter
* May have crop selectivity limits
* May injure crop if misused
* May not control all weed species
* May need follow-up hand weeding or post-emergence control

---

# Conditions Required for Good Performance

---

# Soil Moisture

Soil moisture is very important for pre-emergence herbicides.

Good soil moisture helps:

* Activate the herbicide
* Move herbicide slightly into the weed germination zone
* Improve weed seedling exposure
* Improve control efficiency

If soil is too dry, pre-emergence herbicide performance may be poor.

If heavy rain occurs after spraying, herbicide may move too deep or cause crop injury depending on product and soil.

---

# Fine Seedbed

A fine and level seedbed improves herbicide performance.

Good seedbed should be:

* Free from large clods
* Level
* Moist
* Firm but not compacted
* Free from heavy crop residue where herbicide coverage is blocked

Large clods reduce herbicide contact with weed germination zones.

---

# Uniform Spray Coverage

Uniform spray coverage is essential.

Poor coverage causes:

* Weed escape patches
* Uneven control
* Crop injury from overlapping spray
* Poor value from herbicide cost

Farmers should use proper nozzle, pressure, walking speed, and water volume.

---

# Correct Timing

Pre-emergence herbicides must be applied at the correct time.

Common timing:

* Within 0 to 3 days after sowing, depending on crop and label
* Within 0 to 5 days after sowing for some crops and herbicides
* Soon after transplanting where recommended
* Before weed seedlings emerge

Late application after weeds have emerged may reduce effectiveness.

---

# Proper Water Volume

Use sufficient spray water for uniform soil coverage.

Many field recommendations use higher water volume than foliar pesticide sprays because the herbicide must cover the soil surface evenly.

Water volume depends on:

* Crop
* Herbicide
* Sprayer type
* Soil surface
* Label recommendation
* Local extension guidance

Use clean water and proper calibration.

# Weed Types Controlled

Pre-emergence herbicides may control:

* Annual grasses
* Broadleaf weeds
* Sedges in some cases
* Small-seeded weeds
* Germinating weed seedlings

They may not control:

* Large established weeds
* Deep-rooted perennial weeds
* Some sedges
* Dormant weed seeds
* Weeds emerging after herbicide effect declines
* Weeds not covered by herbicide spectrum

---

Depending on crop and herbicide, pre-emergence herbicides may help manage weeds such as:

* Echinochloa species
* Amaranthus species
* Trianthema species
* Chenopodium species
* Cyperus species in some cases
* Digitaria species
* Eleusine species
* Phalaris minor in some crop programs
* Portulaca species

* Parthenium seedlings in some cases

Local weed flora should guide herbicide choice.

---

# Crop Selectivity

A herbicide must be selective to the crop at the recommended dose.

Crop selectivity depends on:

* Crop species
* Variety or hybrid sensitivity
* Growth stage
* Sowing depth
* Soil type
* Soil moisture
* Dose
* Formulation
* Application timing
* Weather after application

Using the wrong herbicide can injure or kill the crop.

---

# Herbicide Dose

Dose depends on:

* Active ingredient
* Formulation
* Crop
* Weed spectrum
* Soil type
* Organic matter
* Label recommendation
* Local agricultural university recommendation

Farmers should never guess herbicide dose.

Use herbicide dose according to label and local recommendation.

---

# Why Overdose is Dangerous

Overdose may cause:

* Crop injury
* Poor germination
* Root damage
* Yellowing
* Stunted growth
* Reduced yield
* Soil residue risk
* Injury to next crop
* Higher cost
* Environmental contamination

---

# Why Underdose is Also Problematic

Underdose may cause:

* Poor weed control
* Weed escapes
* More manual weeding
* Lower yield
* Increased resistance risk
* Money wasted

Correct dose is essential.

---

# Soil Type and Herbicide Performance

Soil type affects herbicide activity.

## Sandy Soil

In sandy soil:

* Herbicide may move deeper
* Crop injury risk may increase
* Residual control may be shorter
* Lower dose may be recommended for some products

## Clay Soil

In clay soil:

* Herbicide may bind strongly
* Higher dose may be required for some products
* Activation may need good moisture
* Residual activity may last longer

## High Organic Matter Soil

Organic matter can bind herbicides and reduce availability.

Some herbicides may need adjusted dose according to label.

## Low Organic Matter Soil

Crop injury risk may increase with some herbicides because more herbicide remains active in soil water.

---

# Weather Effect

Weather affects pre-emergence herbicide results.

## Dry Weather

* Poor activation
* Weak weed control
* Delayed effect
* Need irrigation if label allows

## Light Rain or Irrigation

* Helps activate herbicide
* Improves weed control

## Heavy Rain

* May move herbicide below weed zone
* May cause crop injury
* May reduce control
* May cause runoff to nearby fields

## High Temperature

* May increase evaporation of some herbicides
* May increase crop stress
* May reduce safety margin

---

# Application Equipment

Use suitable equipment for uniform spray.

## Recommended Sprayers

* Knapsack sprayer
* Battery sprayer
* Tractor-mounted boom sprayer
* Power sprayer with proper nozzle
* Boom sprayer for large fields

## Recommended Nozzle

Flat fan nozzle is commonly preferred for herbicide spray because it gives uniform coverage on the soil surface.

Avoid cone nozzles for soil-applied herbicides unless specifically recommended.

---

# Sprayer Calibration

Calibration means adjusting sprayer output to apply correct herbicide dose per area.

Calibration prevents:

* Overdose
* Underdose
* Uneven spray
* Herbicide waste
* Crop injury
* Poor weed control

## Calibration Factors

* Nozzle type
* Nozzle output
* Walking speed
* Spray pressure
* Boom height
* Water volume
* Field area

Farmers should calibrate sprayer before spraying herbicides.

# Application Steps

---

# Step 1: Identify Crop and Weed Problem

Before choosing herbicide, identify:

* Crop
* Crop stage
* Weed species
* Previous weed history
* Soil type
* Irrigation availability
* Rain forecast
* Next crop plan

---

# Step 2: Select Recommended Herbicide

Use only recommended herbicide for the crop and weed.

Check:

* Label approval
* Crop safety
* Weed spectrum
* Dose
* Timing
* Waiting period if applicable
* Residual effect
* Rotational crop restrictions

---

# Step 3: Prepare Field

Field should be:

* Well-prepared
* Fine
* Level
* Moist
* Free from large clods
* Sown at correct depth

---

# Step 4: Check Soil Moisture

Apply when soil has adequate moisture.

If soil is dry, irrigate before or after application only if recommended.

---

# Step 5: Prepare Spray Solution

* Wear protective equipment.
* Measure herbicide accurately.
* Mix herbicide in small water first.
* Add to spray tank.
* Fill water to required volume.
* Agitate properly.
* Use spray solution the same day.

---

# Step 6: Spray Uniformly

* Use flat fan nozzle.
* Maintain steady walking speed.
* Avoid overlap.
* Avoid missed strips.
* Avoid spraying in strong wind.
* Avoid spraying before heavy rain.
* Keep nozzle height uniform.

---

# Step 7: Avoid Soil Disturbance

After applying pre-emergence herbicide, avoid disturbing the soil surface.

Do not:

* Hoe immediately
* Deep interculture soon after spray
* Break herbicide layer
* Flood heavily unless recommended

Soil disturbance can reduce weed control.

---

# Step 8: Monitor Field

After spraying, observe:

* Weed control
* Crop germination
* Crop injury symptoms
* Weed escapes
* Need for hand weeding
* Need for post-emergence herbicide

---

# Safety Precautions

Herbicides must be handled carefully.

## Personal Protective Equipment

Use:

* Gloves
* Mask
* Goggles
* Long-sleeved shirt
* Long pants
* Shoes or boots
* Head cover

## During Mixing

* Do not inhale vapors or dust.
* Avoid skin contact.
* Do not eat, drink, or smoke.
* Mix in open air.
* Keep children and animals away.

## During Spraying

* Spray with wind direction.
* Avoid spray drift.
* Do not spray near water bodies.
* Do not allow animals into treated field.
* Avoid walking through spray mist.

## After Spraying

* Wash hands and body.
* Wash clothes separately.
* Clean sprayer carefully.
* Store leftover product safely.
* Dispose empty container properly.

---

# Environmental Safety

Pre-emergence herbicides act in soil, so environmental care is important.

Avoid:

* Overdose
* Spraying before heavy rain
* Runoff into ponds or canals
* Washing sprayer in water bodies
* Spraying near fish ponds
* Applying on cracked dry soil where movement is unpredictable
* Applying on slopes before rain

Use buffer zones near water bodies where label requires.

---

# Crop Injury Symptoms

Crop injury from pre-emergence herbicides may appear as:

* Poor germination
* Weak seedlings
* Root pruning
* Yellowing leaves
* Leaf burning
* Stunted growth
* Twisted shoots
* Thin plant stand
* Delayed emergence
* Patchy crop growth

If injury appears, farmers should consult an agriculture expert before taking corrective action.

---

# Causes of Crop Injury

Crop injury may occur due to:

* Overdose
* Wrong herbicide
* Wrong crop
* Shallow sowing
* Heavy rain after application
* Sandy soil
* Poor seed quality
* Waterlogging
* Overlapping spray
* Wrong nozzle
* Soil disturbance
* Herbicide carryover from previous crop
* Stress from drought or disease

---

# Herbicide Carryover

Some pre-emergence herbicides may remain active in soil and affect the next crop.

Carryover risk depends on:

* Herbicide persistence
* Dose
* Soil type
* Rainfall
* Irrigation
* Soil pH
* Organic matter
* Temperature
* Time gap before next crop

Farmers should check rotational crop restrictions on the label.

---

# Herbicide Resistance Management

Repeated use of the same herbicide can select resistant weeds.

To reduce resistance risk:

* Rotate herbicide mode of action.
* Use crop rotation.
* Use mechanical weeding.
* Use stale seedbed method.
* Use intercropping or cover crops.
* Avoid underdosing.
* Control weed escapes before seed set.
* Do not use the same herbicide year after year without rotation.

---

# Integrated Weed Management

Pre-emergence herbicides should be part of integrated weed management.

## Components

* Clean seed
* Field sanitation
* Crop rotation
* Stale seedbed
* Timely sowing
* Proper plant spacing
* Mulching
* Mechanical weeding
* Hand weeding
* Intercropping
* Cover crops
* Competitive crop varieties
* Pre-emergence herbicide
* Post-emergence herbicide where needed

Integrated weed management gives better long-term weed control than herbicide alone.

---

# Stale Seedbed Method

Stale seedbed is a useful weed management practice.

## Steps

1. Prepare field early.
2. Irrigate lightly or wait for rain.
3. Allow weeds to germinate.
4. Destroy weeds before sowing using shallow tillage or approved method.
5. Sow crop.
6. Apply pre-emergence herbicide if recommended.

This reduces early weed pressure.

# Pre-Emergence Herbicides in Rice

Rice uses several pre-emergence herbicides depending on crop establishment method.

## Transplanted Rice

Common pre-emergence or early herbicide options may include:

* Pretilachlor
* Butachlor
* Pyrazosulfuron-ethyl
* Bensulfuron-methyl plus pretilachlor
* Oxadiargyl

## Direct-Seeded Rice

Options differ and may include:

* Pendimethalin
* Pretilachlor where recommended
* Pyrazosulfuron-ethyl
* Other rice-specific herbicides

## Advisory

Rice herbicide choice depends on transplanted rice, wet direct-seeded rice, dry direct-seeded rice, water level, and weed flora.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicides in Wheat

Pre-emergence herbicides may be used to manage early weeds in wheat.

Common options may include:

* Pendimethalin
* Metribuzin in specific conditions and varieties where recommended

## Advisory

Wheat herbicide safety depends on soil type, variety, irrigation timing, and dose. Farmers should follow local recommendation carefully.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicides in Maize

Maize pre-emergence weed control may use:

* Atrazine
* Pendimethalin
* S-metolachlor
* Other maize-approved herbicides

## Advisory

Atrazine should be used carefully because it may affect sensitive crops in rotation and should not be misused near water bodies.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicides in Soybean

Soybean pre-emergence weed control may use:

* Pendimethalin
* Imazethapyr plus pendimethalin ready-mix where approved
* Other soybean-approved herbicides

## Advisory

Soybean is sensitive to poor drainage and herbicide injury under stress. Use correct dose and avoid waterlogging after application.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicides in Groundnut

Groundnut weed control may include:

* Pendimethalin
* Oxyfluorfen where recommended
* Other groundnut-approved herbicides

## Advisory

Groundnut fields should not be disturbed immediately after application. Later interculture may be needed after herbicide effect reduces.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicides in Cotton

Cotton pre-emergence weed control may use:

* Pendimethalin
* Diuron where recommended
* Fluchloralin as pre-plant incorporation in some systems
* Other cotton-approved herbicides

## Advisory

Cotton has a long crop duration, so pre-emergence herbicide may need follow-up interculture or post-emergence weed control.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicides in Sugarcane

Sugarcane pre-emergence weed control may use:

* Atrazine
* Metribuzin
* Pendimethalin
* Diuron where recommended
* Other sugarcane-approved herbicides

## Advisory

Sugarcane weed pressure remains for a long period. Combine herbicide with earthing up, trash mulching, and interculture.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicides in Onion

Onion is a slow-growing crop and suffers from weed competition.

Possible herbicides may include:

* Pendimethalin
* Oxyfluorfen where recommended

## Advisory

Onion is sensitive to herbicide injury. Use only crop-approved dose and avoid spraying on stressed seedlings.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicides in Tomato

Tomato pre-emergence herbicide use depends on whether tomato is direct-seeded or transplanted and on local approval.

Possible weed management options may include:

* Pendimethalin where recommended
* Oxyfluorfen in some transplanted systems where recommended
* Mulching
* Hand weeding
* Drip plus plastic mulch
* Mechanical weeding between rows

## Advisory

Tomato is sensitive to herbicide misuse. Many vegetable growers prefer mulching, stale seedbed, and careful manual or mechanical weeding to avoid crop injury.

Always verify herbicide approval for tomato before spraying.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicides in Chilli

Chilli weed management may use:

* Pendimethalin where recommended
* Oxyfluorfen in some transplanted systems where recommended
* Mulching
* Hand weeding
* Interculture

## Advisory

Avoid herbicide contact with young chilli plants unless label allows. Transplanted vegetables are often sensitive.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicides in Pulses

Pulses may use herbicides such as:

* Pendimethalin
* Pendimethalin plus imazethapyr ready-mix where approved
* Other crop-specific options

## Advisory

Herbicide selection differs among chickpea, black gram, green gram, pigeon pea, cowpea, and soybean. Do not use one pulse recommendation for another pulse without checking.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicide and Mulching

Mulching can reduce weed emergence and may reduce dependence on herbicides.

## Types of Mulch

* Organic mulch
* Plastic mulch
* Crop residue mulch
* Straw mulch
* Sugarcane trash mulch

## Benefits

* Reduces weeds
* Conserves moisture
* Reduces soil temperature fluctuation
* Improves soil health with organic mulch
* Reduces herbicide requirement

Do not apply soil-active herbicide over thick mulch unless label and expert guidance allow, because mulch may block herbicide from reaching soil.

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicide and Drip Irrigation

In drip-irrigated crops, weed emergence may be concentrated near wet zones.

Management options include:

* Pre-emergence herbicide where approved
* Plastic mulch
* Organic mulch
* Inter-row cultivation
* Hand weeding near plants
* Drip scheduling to avoid unnecessary wetting of inter-row areas

---

# Pre-Emergence Herbicide and Conservation Agriculture

In conservation agriculture, crop residues may cover the soil.

This affects herbicide performance because:

* Residue may intercept spray
* Herbicide may not reach soil
* Weed emergence pattern changes
* Moisture may improve activation
* Some herbicides may bind to residue

Farmers should use conservation agriculture-specific recommendations.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

Farmers should avoid:

* Spraying after weeds have already grown
* Using wrong herbicide for crop
* Guessing dose
* Using overdose
* Spraying on dry soil without activation
* Spraying before heavy rain
* Using cone nozzle instead of flat fan nozzle where uniform soil spray is needed
* Not calibrating sprayer
* Overlapping spray lines
* Disturbing soil soon after application
* Ignoring soil type
* Ignoring next crop restrictions
* Not wearing protective equipment

---

# Farmer Recommendations

Farmers are advised to:

1. Identify crop and weed species before selecting herbicide.
2. Use only crop-approved pre-emergence herbicides.
3. Apply at the correct time before weed emergence.
4. Ensure soil has adequate moisture.
5. Prepare a fine and level seedbed.
6. Use flat fan nozzle and calibrated sprayer.
7. Apply uniform spray without overlap.
8. Avoid soil disturbance after spraying.
9. Monitor field for weed escapes and crop injury.
10. Combine herbicide with integrated weed management.
