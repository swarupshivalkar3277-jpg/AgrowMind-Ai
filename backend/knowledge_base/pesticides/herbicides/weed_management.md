# Weed Management

## Overview

Weed management is the planned control of unwanted plants that compete with crops for nutrients, water, sunlight, space, and air. Weeds can reduce crop yield, increase pest and disease pressure, interfere with harvesting, reduce produce quality, contaminate seed lots, and increase farm production cost.

Weed management should not depend only on herbicides. The best approach is Integrated Weed Management, which combines preventive, cultural, mechanical, biological, and chemical methods.

Effective weed management depends on:

* Correct weed identification.
* Crop type and crop growth stage.
* Weed growth stage.
* Weed density.
* Soil type and soil moisture.
* Season and rainfall.
* Herbicide selectivity.
* Herbicide timing.
* Herbicide mode of action.
* Resistance-management practices.
* Safety and label instructions.

---

## 1. What Are Weeds?

Weeds are unwanted plants growing where they are not desired. A plant may be useful in one place but considered a weed in another place if it competes with the main crop.

### Common Effects of Weeds

* Reduce crop yield.
* Compete for water, nutrients, sunlight, and space.
* Increase pest and disease shelter.
* Interfere with irrigation and fertilizer application.
* Reduce harvesting efficiency.
* Lower crop quality.
* Contaminate grains, seeds, and fodder.
* Increase production cost.
* Produce seeds that remain in soil for many years.

### Examples of Common Agricultural Weeds

* Parthenium
* Cyperus rotundus
* Cynodon dactylon
* Echinochloa species
* Amaranthus species
* Chenopodium species
* Phalaris minor
* Portulaca oleracea
* Digitaria species
* Commelina species
* Trianthema portulacastrum

---

## 2. Types of Weeds

## 2.1 Grassy Weeds

Grassy weeds belong to the grass family. They usually have narrow leaves, parallel veins, fibrous roots, and hollow or jointed stems.

### Examples

* Echinochloa species
* Phalaris minor
* Cynodon dactylon
* Digitaria species
* Eleusine indica
* Avena fatua
* Dactyloctenium aegyptium

### Common Crops Affected

* Rice
* Wheat
* Maize
* Sugarcane
* Cotton
* Soybean
* Groundnut
* Vegetables

### Management Notes

Grassy weeds are difficult to control in cereal crops because they are biologically similar to the crop. Selective herbicide choice is very important.

---

## 2.2 Broadleaf Weeds

Broadleaf weeds usually have wider leaves, net-like leaf veins, and different growth habits compared with grasses.

### Examples

* Amaranthus species
* Chenopodium album
* Parthenium hysterophorus
* Portulaca oleracea
* Trianthema portulacastrum
* Euphorbia species
* Commelina benghalensis

### Common Crops Affected

* Pulses
* Oilseeds
* Cotton
* Vegetables
* Orchards
* Wheat
* Maize
* Sugarcane

### Management Notes

Many broadleaf weeds are controlled by broadleaf-specific herbicides, hand weeding, hoeing, mulching, crop rotation, and stale seedbed practices.

---

## 2.3 Sedges

Sedges are grass-like weeds but have triangular stems and underground tubers or rhizomes in many species. They are difficult to control because they can regrow from underground parts.

### Examples

* Cyperus rotundus
* Cyperus iria
* Fimbristylis miliacea

### Common Crops Affected

* Rice
* Sugarcane
* Vegetables
* Cotton
* Orchards
* Pulses

### Management Notes

Sedges require repeated management. Hand removal may fail if underground tubers remain in soil. Herbicide selection must specifically target sedges.

---

## 2.4 Annual Weeds

Annual weeds complete their life cycle in one season. They germinate, grow, flower, produce seeds, and die within a short period.

### Examples

* Amaranthus species
* Chenopodium album
* Echinochloa species
* Phalaris minor
* Portulaca oleracea

### Management Notes

Annual weeds should be controlled before flowering and seed production. Preventing seed formation reduces future weed pressure.

---

## 2.5 Perennial Weeds

Perennial weeds live for more than one season. They may reproduce by seeds, rhizomes, stolons, tubers, bulbs, or roots.

### Examples

* Cyperus rotundus
* Cynodon dactylon
* Convolvulus arvensis
* Saccharum spontaneum
* Imperata cylindrica

### Management Notes

Perennial weeds are harder to control than annual weeds. They often require repeated control measures, deep tillage, crop rotation, competitive crops, and suitable systemic herbicides.

---

## 2.6 Aquatic Weeds

Aquatic weeds grow in water bodies, irrigation canals, ponds, rice fields, and drainage systems.

### Examples

* Eichhornia crassipes
* Hydrilla
* Lemna
* Azolla
* Nymphaea species

### Management Notes

Aquatic weed management requires care because wrong herbicide use can harm fish, livestock, irrigation water, and the environment. Use only approved aquatic weed-management methods.

# Integrated Weed Management

## 3. What Is Integrated Weed Management?

Integrated Weed Management, also called IWM, is the combined use of different weed-control methods to reduce weed pressure and protect crop yield. It avoids dependence on a single method such as repeated herbicide spraying.

### Main Components of Integrated Weed Management

* Preventive weed control.
* Cultural weed control.
* Mechanical weed control.
* Biological weed control.
* Chemical weed control.
* Crop rotation.
* Mulching.
* Cover crops.
* Competitive crop varieties.
* Proper irrigation and fertilizer management.
* Herbicide rotation.
* Weed seedbank reduction.

### Why Integrated Weed Management Is Important

* Reduces crop yield loss.
* Reduces herbicide resistance risk.
* Reduces long-term weed pressure.
* Improves soil and crop health.
* Reduces dependency on chemical herbicides.
* Improves sustainability of farming.
* Helps manage difficult weeds.

---

## 4. Preventive Weed Management

Preventive weed management aims to stop weeds from entering or spreading in the field.

### Preventive Practices

* Use clean and certified crop seed.
* Avoid using weed-contaminated farmyard manure.
* Clean farm machinery before moving between fields.
* Control weeds along bunds, field borders, canals, and roadsides.
* Do not allow weeds to produce seeds.
* Use clean irrigation water where possible.
* Remove new invasive weeds immediately.
* Avoid moving soil from weed-infested fields to clean fields.
* Keep nursery areas weed-free.

### Farmer Guidance

The cheapest weed control is prevention. If weeds are allowed to produce seeds, the soil weed seedbank increases and weed problems become worse in future seasons.

---

## 5. Cultural Weed Management

Cultural methods make the crop more competitive against weeds.

### Important Cultural Practices

* Crop rotation.
* Proper crop spacing.
* Timely sowing.
* Use of vigorous varieties.
* Higher seed rate where recommended.
* Balanced fertilizer application.
* Proper irrigation scheduling.
* Intercropping.
* Cover cropping.
* Mulching.
* Transplanting instead of direct seeding in some crops.
* Maintaining good crop stand.

### Benefits

* Reduces weed emergence.
* Improves crop competition.
* Suppresses weed growth naturally.
* Reduces herbicide requirement.
* Improves crop yield stability.

### Farmer Guidance

Healthy and fast-growing crops suppress weeds better than weak crops. Poor crop stand allows weeds to dominate the field.

---

## 6. Mechanical Weed Management

Mechanical weed management involves physical removal, cutting, uprooting, burying, or damaging weeds.

### Methods

* Hand weeding.
* Hoeing.
* Intercultivation.
* Tillage.
* Harrowing.
* Rotary weeding.
* Conoweeding in rice.
* Mowing.
* Slashing.
* Deep ploughing.
* Weed pulling before seed formation.

### Advantages

* Immediate weed removal.
* Useful in organic farming.
* Reduces herbicide dependence.
* Effective for large weeds when done properly.
* Can be combined with other methods.

### Limitations

* Labour-intensive.
* Costly when labour is unavailable.
* May damage crop roots.
* Less effective against deep-rooted perennial weeds.
* Wet soil conditions can make mechanical weeding difficult.
* Repeated tillage may increase soil erosion.

### Farmer Guidance

Mechanical weeding is most effective when weeds are small. Weeds should be removed before flowering and seed production.

---

## 7. Mulching for Weed Control

Mulching means covering the soil surface to reduce weed emergence and conserve moisture.

### Types of Mulch

* Straw mulch.
* Crop residue mulch.
* Dry leaves.
* Sugarcane trash.
* Plastic mulch.
* Biodegradable mulch.
* Live mulch or cover crop.

### Benefits

* Blocks sunlight needed for weed germination.
* Conserves soil moisture.
* Reduces soil temperature fluctuations.
* Reduces soil erosion.
* Improves soil organic matter when organic mulch is used.
* Reduces herbicide need.

### Common Crops

* Vegetables.
* Fruit crops.
* Orchards.
* Sugarcane.
* Flowers.
* Nursery crops.

### Limitations

* Organic mulch may carry weed seeds if not clean.
* Plastic mulch needs proper disposal.
* Mulch may shelter insects or rodents in some conditions.
* Initial cost may be high.

### Farmer Guidance

Use clean mulch material. Avoid mulch made from mature weeds carrying seeds.

---

## 8. Stale Seedbed Technique

A stale seedbed is prepared before sowing the crop. Weed seeds are allowed to germinate and then destroyed before crop planting.

### Steps

1. Prepare the field.
2. Irrigate lightly or wait for rainfall.
3. Allow weeds to germinate.
4. Kill emerged weeds using shallow cultivation or non-selective herbicide where recommended.
5. Sow the crop with minimum soil disturbance.

### Benefits

* Reduces first flush of weeds.
* Useful for small-seeded crops.
* Reduces early crop-weed competition.
* Helps reduce weed seedbank.

### Common Use Cases

* Vegetables.
* Rice.
* Wheat.
* Onion.
* Direct-seeded crops.
* Nursery beds.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not disturb the soil deeply after killing the first weed flush, because deeper weed seeds may come to the surface and germinate.

---

## 9. Crop Rotation for Weed Management

Crop rotation means growing different crops in sequence across seasons. It helps break weed life cycles and allows use of different weed-management methods.

### Benefits

* Reduces dominance of specific weeds.
* Allows use of different herbicide groups.
* Changes crop planting time and weed competition pattern.
* Reduces herbicide resistance risk.
* Improves soil health.
* Reduces pest and disease carryover.

### Example Rotations

* Rice followed by wheat.
* Soybean followed by wheat.
* Maize followed by gram.
* Cotton followed by wheat.
* Groundnut followed by sorghum.
* Vegetable crop followed by cereal crop.

### Farmer Guidance

Repeatedly growing the same crop encourages the same weed species. Rotation helps disturb weed adaptation.

---

# Herbicide-Based Weed Management

## 10. What Are Herbicides?

Herbicides are pesticides used to kill, suppress, or manage weeds. They may be selective or non-selective, applied before planting, before weed emergence, or after weed emergence.

Herbicides should be used only after identifying:

* Crop.
* Weed type.
* Weed stage.
* Crop stage.
* Soil condition.
* Herbicide selectivity.
* Recommended dose.
* Application timing.
* Required water volume.
* Safety interval.
* Label restrictions.

---

## 11. Selective Herbicides

Selective herbicides control certain weeds without seriously damaging the crop when used correctly.

### Examples

* 2,4-D in some cereal crops for broadleaf weeds.
* Pendimethalin in several crops as pre-emergence herbicide.
* Butachlor in transplanted rice.
* Pretilachlor in rice.
* Atrazine in maize and sugarcane, where recommended.
* Clodinafop in wheat for grassy weeds.
* Metsulfuron-methyl in wheat for broadleaf weeds, where recommended.

### Advantages

* Can be used within standing crop.
* Useful for specific weed groups.
* Saves labour.
* Helps manage weeds during critical crop stages.

### Limitations

* Wrong herbicide can damage the crop.
* Wrong dose can cause phytotoxicity.
* Wrong crop stage can cause injury.
* Some herbicides control only grassy weeds or only broadleaf weeds.
* Resistance risk exists with repeated use.

### Farmer Guidance

Selective herbicide safety depends on correct crop, correct dose, correct timing, correct spray method, and correct field condition.

---

## 12. Non-Selective Herbicides

Non-selective herbicides can kill or injure most green plants. They should not touch the crop unless used in special systems or directed application.

### Examples

* Glyphosate.
* Paraquat, where legally permitted.
* Glufosinate, where registered.
* Some contact burn-down herbicides.

### Common Uses

* Pre-plant weed control.
* Stale seedbed.
* Orchard floor weed control with directed spray.
* Non-crop areas.
* Field bunds and irrigation channels.
* Perennial weed suppression.

### Important Warning

Non-selective herbicides can damage or kill the crop if spray drift reaches crop leaves, stems, young bark, or green tissues.

### Farmer Guidance

Use shielded or directed spraying near crops. Avoid spraying during windy conditions.

---

## 13. Pre-Plant Herbicides

Pre-plant herbicides are applied before crop sowing or transplanting.

### Uses

* Kill existing weeds before planting.
* Reduce early weed pressure.
* Help prepare clean seedbed.
* Manage perennial weeds before crop establishment.

### Examples

* Glyphosate before sowing, where recommended.
* Paraquat before sowing, where permitted.
* Pre-plant incorporated herbicides in some crops.

### Farmer Guidance

Follow the waiting period between herbicide application and sowing. Some herbicides can injure the crop if planted too soon.

---

## 14. Pre-Emergence Herbicides

Pre-emergence herbicides are applied after sowing but before weeds emerge, or before both crop and weeds emerge depending on crop recommendation. They create a chemical barrier in the top soil layer.

### Key Features

* Control weeds during early crop growth.
* Need adequate soil moisture for activation.
* Usually work on germinating weed seeds.
* Best applied to well-prepared soil.
* Poor soil coverage reduces effectiveness.

### Examples

* Pendimethalin.
* Pretilachlor.
* Butachlor.
* Atrazine.
* Oxyfluorfen.
* Metribuzin, crop-dependent.

### Common Crops

* Rice.
* Wheat.
* Soybean.
* Groundnut.
* Cotton.
* Onion.
* Potato.
* Maize.
* Sugarcane.
* Vegetables, depending on label.

### Advantages

* Controls first flush of weeds.
* Reduces early crop-weed competition.
* Saves labour.
* Useful when hand weeding is delayed.

### Limitations

* Requires proper soil moisture.
* Heavy rain may reduce effectiveness or cause crop injury.
* Dry soil may reduce activation.
* Wrong dose can injure crop.
* Does not control already established large weeds.

### Farmer Guidance

Pre-emergence herbicides should be applied uniformly soon after sowing or transplanting as recommended. Soil should be moist but not waterlogged unless the product label allows it.

---

## 15. Post-Emergence Herbicides

Post-emergence herbicides are applied after weeds have emerged. They may be selective or non-selective.

### Key Features

* Target visible weeds.
* Weed stage is very important.
* Young weeds are easier to control.
* Spray coverage is important.
* Weather conditions affect performance.

### Examples

* 2,4-D.
* Metsulfuron-methyl.
* Clodinafop-propargyl.
* Fenoxaprop-p-ethyl.
* Bispyribac-sodium.
* Quizalofop-ethyl.
* Imazethapyr.
* Glyphosate in non-crop or directed uses.

### Advantages

* Can target actual weed population.
* Useful when pre-emergence herbicide fails.
* Can control escaped weeds.
* Crop-specific options available.

### Limitations

* Large weeds are harder to kill.
* Crop injury can occur if sprayed at wrong stage.
* Spray drift can damage nearby crops.
* Rain after spraying may reduce effectiveness.
* Resistance risk increases with repeated use.

### Farmer Guidance

Post-emergence herbicides should usually be applied when weeds are young and actively growing. Avoid spraying stressed crops or drought-stressed weeds unless recommended.

---

## 16. Contact Herbicides

Contact herbicides kill only the plant parts they touch. They do not move much inside the plant.

### Key Features

* Fast visible burning effect.
* Require good coverage.
* Less effective on underground roots or rhizomes.
* Perennial weeds may regrow.
* Useful for small weeds.

### Examples

* Paraquat, where legally permitted.
* Some burn-down herbicides.
* Oxyfluorfen has contact activity in some situations.

### Farmer Guidance

Use good coverage for contact herbicides. Do not expect full control of deep-rooted perennial weeds from a single contact herbicide spray.

---

## 17. Systemic Herbicides

Systemic herbicides are absorbed and move inside the weed plant. They may move to roots, shoots, and growing points.

### Key Features

* Useful for perennial weeds.
* Slower visible symptoms than contact herbicides.
* Can control underground parts if applied correctly.
* Need actively growing weeds for best movement.
* Rain or drought stress can reduce performance.

### Examples

* Glyphosate.
* 2,4-D.
* Imazethapyr.
* Metsulfuron-methyl.
* Certain grass-killer herbicides.

### Farmer Guidance

Systemic herbicides work best when weeds are actively growing. Avoid cutting, grazing, or disturbing weeds immediately after spraying because the herbicide needs time to move inside the plant.

---

# Herbicide Mode of Action

## 18. Why Mode of Action Matters

Herbicide mode of action describes how a herbicide kills or suppresses weeds. Herbicides are grouped by their site of action or mode of action for resistance-management purposes.

Using the same mode of action repeatedly increases herbicide resistance risk. Herbicide rotation should be based on mode of action, not only on product brand name.

---

## 19. Common Herbicide Mode-of-Action Groups

## 19.1 ACCase Inhibitors

### Main Target

Mostly grassy weeds.

### Examples

* Clodinafop-propargyl.
* Fenoxaprop-p-ethyl.
* Quizalofop-ethyl.
* Cyhalofop-butyl.

### Common Uses

* Grass weed control in wheat, soybean, pulses, cotton, and rice depending on product label.

### Resistance Risk

High if used repeatedly against the same grass weed population.

---

## 19.2 ALS or AHAS Inhibitors

### Main Target

Many grassy, broadleaf, and sedge weeds depending on active ingredient.

### Examples

* Metsulfuron-methyl.
* Imazethapyr.
* Bispyribac-sodium.
* Pyrazosulfuron-ethyl.
* Chlorimuron-ethyl.

### Common Uses

* Rice.
* Wheat.
* Soybean.
* Pulses.
* Sugarcane.
* Other crops depending on label.

### Resistance Risk

High. Avoid repeated use of ALS inhibitors in the same field.

---

## 19.3 Photosystem II Inhibitors

### Main Target

Broadleaf and some grassy weeds.

### Examples

* Atrazine.
* Metribuzin.
* Diuron.
* Isoproturon, where registered and recommended.

### Common Uses

* Maize.
* Sugarcane.
* Potato.
* Wheat, depending on product and local regulations.

### Resistance Risk

Medium to high depending on weed species and repeated use.

---

## 19.4 Synthetic Auxins

### Main Target

Mostly broadleaf weeds.

### Examples

* 2,4-D.
* Dicamba.
* MCPA.
* Fluroxypyr.

### Common Uses

* Wheat.
* Maize.
* Sugarcane.
* Pastures.
* Non-crop areas.

### Important Safety Note

Synthetic auxin herbicides can cause serious injury to sensitive broadleaf crops through drift or wrong application.

### Sensitive Crops

* Cotton.
* Tomato.
* Grapes.
* Pulses.
* Vegetables.
* Fruit crops.
* Ornamentals.

---

## 19.5 EPSPS Inhibitors

### Main Target

Broad-spectrum weed control.

### Example

* Glyphosate.

### Common Uses

* Pre-plant weed control.
* Stale seedbed.
* Orchard floor directed spray.
* Non-crop areas.
* Perennial weed control.

### Resistance Risk

Resistance can develop with repeated use. Avoid depending only on glyphosate every season.

---

## 19.6 PPO Inhibitors

### Main Target

Broadleaf weeds and some grasses depending on active ingredient.

### Examples

* Oxyfluorfen.
* Fomesafen.
* Lactofen.
* Saflufenacil.

### Common Uses

* Onion.
* Soybean.
* Groundnut.
* Pulses.
* Vegetables depending on label.

### Notes

PPO inhibitors often show contact-type injury symptoms and require correct crop stage and dose.

---

## 19.7 Microtubule Inhibitors

### Main Target

Annual grasses and some broadleaf weeds during germination.

### Examples

* Pendimethalin.
* Trifluralin.

### Common Uses

* Pre-emergence weed control in many crops.

### Notes

These herbicides are commonly used for early-season weed suppression.

---

# Crop-Wise Weed Management Notes

## 20. Rice Weed Management

### Common Rice Weeds

* Echinochloa species.
* Cyperus species.
* Fimbristylis species.
* Eclipta.
* Monochoria.
* Ludwigia.
* Leptochloa.

### Weed Management Practices

* Use clean nursery.
* Maintain proper water level in transplanted rice.
* Puddle field where suitable.
* Use stale seedbed in direct-seeded rice.
* Use pre-emergence herbicides where recommended.
* Use post-emergence herbicides based on weed type.

* Prevent seed formation of escaped weeds.

* Early post-emergence: when weeds are small.

Rice fields may contain grasses, sedges, and broadleaf weeds together. Choose herbicide based on the dominant weed type.

# Critical Period of Weed Competition

## 28. What Is Critical Period of Weed Competition?

The critical period of weed competition is the crop growth stage when weeds cause the greatest yield loss. During this period, the crop should be kept weed-free.

### General Rule

Most crops are highly sensitive to weed competition during early growth. Weed control during the first few weeks after sowing or transplanting is very important.

### Examples

* Rice: early establishment stage.
* Wheat: early tillering stage.
* Maize: first few weeks after emergence.
* Soybean: first 30 to 45 days.
* Cotton: early vegetative stage.
* Vegetables: early growth and establishment stage.

### Farmer Guidance

Late weeding may improve field appearance but cannot recover yield already lost during early crop growth.

# Herbicide Application Guidelines

## 29. Correct Herbicide Application

Herbicide performance depends strongly on application quality.

### Key Factors

* Correct dose.
* Correct water volume.
* Correct nozzle.
* Uniform spraying.
* Correct weed stage.
* Correct crop stage.
* Correct soil moisture.
* Suitable weather.
* Proper sprayer calibration.
* No spray overlap.
* No spray drift.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not guess herbicide dose. Measure product and water accurately. Calibrate sprayer before application.

---

## 30. Sprayer Calibration

Sprayer calibration means adjusting the sprayer to apply the correct amount of herbicide per unit area.

### Why Calibration Is Important

* Prevents underdosing.
* Prevents overdosing.
* Reduces crop injury.
* Improves weed control.
* Saves chemical cost.
* Reduces environmental risk.

### Common Calibration Mistakes

* Using uneven walking speed.
* Using damaged nozzle.
* Spraying with leaking sprayer.
* Using wrong water volume.
* Mixing herbicide without measuring.
* Overlapping spray paths.
* Not maintaining constant pressure.

### Farmer Guidance

A poorly calibrated sprayer can make even the correct herbicide fail.

---

## 31. Weather Conditions for Herbicide Spraying

### Avoid Spraying When

* Wind speed is high.
* Rain is expected soon, unless label allows.
* Crop is drought-stressed.
* Weeds are drought-stressed.
* Temperature is very high.
* Field is waterlogged, unless product is recommended for such condition.
* Dew is excessive and spray runoff occurs.
* Nearby sensitive crops may be affected by drift.

### Good Conditions

* Calm weather.
* Moderate temperature.
* Active weed growth.
* Adequate soil moisture.
* No immediate heavy rain.
* No strong wind.

### Farmer Guidance

Weather can decide herbicide success or failure. Avoid spraying during strong wind and extreme heat.

---

## 32. Herbicide Phytotoxicity

Phytotoxicity means crop injury caused by herbicide.

### Symptoms

* Leaf yellowing.
* Leaf burning.
* Stunted growth.
* Twisted leaves.
* Root damage.
* Poor germination.
* Crop thinning.
* Delayed flowering.
* Reduced yield.
* Plant death in severe cases.

### Causes

* Wrong herbicide.
* Excess dose.
* Wrong crop stage.
* Wrong soil condition.
* Spray overlap.
* Herbicide drift.
* Tank contamination.
* Wrong mixing.
* Heavy rain after application.
* Sensitive crop variety.
* Residual herbicide carryover.

### Farmer Guidance

If crop injury appears after herbicide use, record product name, dose, crop stage, weed stage, weather, soil moisture, and spraying method. This helps diagnose the problem.

---

## 33. Herbicide Carryover

Herbicide carryover occurs when herbicide remains active in soil and injures the next crop.

### Risk Factors

* Long-residual herbicides.
* Low rainfall after application.
* High dose.
* High soil pH for some herbicides.
* Dry soil conditions.
* Short crop rotation interval.
* Sensitive next crop.

### Prevention

* Follow label crop-rotation restrictions.
* Avoid excessive dose.
* Know soil type and pH.
* Consider rainfall and irrigation after herbicide use.
* Avoid planting sensitive crops too soon.
* Keep herbicide application records.

### Farmer Guidance

Always check which crop can be safely grown after using a residual herbicide.

---

# Herbicide Resistance Management

## 34. What Is Herbicide Resistance?

Herbicide resistance is the ability of a weed population to survive a herbicide dose that normally controls that weed. Resistance develops over time when the same herbicide or same mode of action is used repeatedly.

### Herbicide Resistance Is Not the Same as Tolerance

* Resistance: a weed population that was once controlled becomes difficult to control after repeated selection.
* Tolerance: a weed species naturally survives a herbicide even without previous exposure.

---

## 35. Causes of Herbicide Resistance

* Repeated use of the same herbicide.
* Repeated use of the same mode of action.
* Using herbicide as the only weed-control method.
* Applying lower than recommended dose.
* Spraying large weeds.
* Poor spray coverage.
* Allowing surviving weeds to produce seeds.
* Monocropping.
* Lack of crop rotation.
* No mechanical or cultural control.
* High weed seed production.

---

## 36. Signs of Herbicide Resistance

* One weed species survives while other weeds are controlled.
* Surviving weeds appear in patches.
* Same herbicide worked in previous years but now fails.
* Correct dose and timing were used but control is poor.
* Surviving weeds are healthy and produce seeds.
* Nearby fields using the same herbicide show similar failure.

### Important Note

Poor weed control does not always mean resistance. It can also happen due to wrong dose, wrong timing, poor coverage, rainfall, drought stress, expired product, or wrong weed identification.

---

## 37. How to Prevent Herbicide Resistance

### Best Practices

* Rotate herbicides with different modes of action.
* Use tank mixtures only when both herbicides are effective against the target weed and label allows.
* Combine herbicides with mechanical and cultural control.
* Use crop rotation.
* Use clean seeds.
* Prevent weed seed production.
* Control field-border weeds.
* Use recommended dose.
* Spray weeds at correct stage.
* Avoid repeated use of the same herbicide group.
* Monitor fields after spraying.
* Remove surviving weeds before seed formation.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not rotate only product names. Rotate herbicide mode of action.

---

## 38. Managing Suspected Resistant Weeds

### Steps

1. Confirm correct weed identification.
2. Check herbicide dose and application timing.
3. Check weather and soil conditions during spraying.
4. Check sprayer calibration.
5. Compare with past herbicide history.
6. Remove surviving weeds before seed production.
7. Avoid using the same herbicide again.
8. Use a different effective mode of action.
9. Add mechanical control.
10. Use crop rotation and cultural suppression.

### Farmer Guidance

Never allow suspected resistant weeds to produce seeds. One resistant plant can create many resistant plants in future seasons.

# Weed Seedbank Management

## 39. What Is Weed Seedbank?

The weed seedbank is the reserve of viable weed seeds present in soil. Many weed seeds can survive for years and germinate when conditions become suitable.

### How Seedbank Increases

* Weeds produce seeds before control.
* Weed seeds enter through contaminated crop seed.
* Weed seeds spread through manure.
* Weed seeds move with irrigation water.
* Weed seeds move through machinery.
* Birds, animals, and wind spread seeds.

### How to Reduce Seedbank

* Prevent flowering and seed formation.
* Use stale seedbed.
* Rotate crops.
* Use mulching.
* Use cover crops.
* Clean machinery.
* Remove weeds on bunds and borders.
* Control escapes after herbicide application.
* Deep burial or shallow tillage depending on weed biology.

### Farmer Guidance

Long-term weed management means reducing weed seed production every season.

---

# Biological Weed Management

## 40. Biological Weed Control

Biological weed control uses living organisms to suppress weeds. It is more common for invasive weeds and long-term land management than for quick field-crop weed control.

### Examples

* Insects that feed on specific weeds.
* Fungal pathogens that infect weeds.
* Grazing animals in some systems.
* Competitive cover crops.
* Allelopathic crops or residues.

### Advantages

* Can reduce chemical dependence.
* Useful for some invasive weeds.
* Environmentally supportive when well managed.

### Limitations

* Slow action.
* Not suitable for all weeds.
* Requires expert guidance.
* May not provide complete control.
* Biological agents must be approved and safe.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not introduce insects, pathogens, or biological agents without expert approval.

### Common Conditions

* Rainfall supports rapid weed growth.

### Management Practices

* Early hand weeding or hoeing.
* Drainage management.

### Common Conditions

### Management Practices

* Mechanical weeding in wider-row crops.

### Management Practices

* Irrigation-channel weed control.
* Perennial weed suppression.
* Orchard weed management.

Weed control can fail due to many reasons.

### Common Reasons

* Wrong weed identification.
* Wrong herbicide selection.
* Wrong dose.
* Wrong application timing.
* Weeds were too large.
* Poor spray coverage.
* Low water volume.
* Wrong nozzle.
* Spray drift.
* Rain after spraying.
* Dry soil after pre-emergence herbicide.
* Drought-stressed weeds.
* Waterlogged field.
* Expired or fake herbicide.
* Herbicide resistance.
* Poor-quality mixing.
* Herbicide antagonism in tank mix.
* Crop residue blocking soil-applied herbicide.

### Farmer Guidance

Before repeating herbicide spray, diagnose the reason for failure. Re-spraying the same herbicide may waste money and increase resistance.

# Farmer Questions and Answers

## Q1. What is weed management?

Weed management is the process of preventing, reducing, or controlling unwanted plants that compete with crops for nutrients, water, sunlight, and space.

## Q2. What is Integrated Weed Management?

Integrated Weed Management is the combined use of preventive, cultural, mechanical, biological, and chemical methods to manage weeds sustainably.

## Q3. Why should farmers not depend only on herbicides?

Depending only on herbicides increases resistance risk, may increase cost, and may fail under poor application or weather conditions. Combining methods gives better long-term control.

## Q4. What is the difference between pre-emergence and post-emergence herbicide?

Pre-emergence herbicide is applied before weeds emerge and controls germinating weed seeds. Post-emergence herbicide is applied after weeds have emerged.

## Q5. What is the difference between selective and non-selective herbicide?

Selective herbicides control certain weeds without serious crop damage when used correctly. Non-selective herbicides can kill or injure most green plants, including crops.

## Q6. Why did my herbicide not work?

Possible reasons include wrong herbicide, wrong dose, wrong weed stage, poor spray coverage, rain after spraying, dry soil, drought-stressed weeds, expired product, or herbicide resistance.

## Q7. What is herbicide resistance?

Herbicide resistance is the ability of a weed population to survive a herbicide application that normally controls that weed.

## Q8. How can herbicide resistance be prevented?

Rotate herbicide modes of action, use recommended dose, spray at correct weed stage, combine herbicides with cultural and mechanical control, and prevent surviving weeds from producing seeds.

## Q9. What is the best time to control weeds?

Most crops should be kept weed-free during early growth because this is when weeds cause the greatest yield loss.

## Q10. What are grassy weeds?

Grassy weeds are narrow-leaved weeds with parallel veins and fibrous roots. Examples include Echinochloa, Phalaris minor, Digitaria, and Cynodon.

## Q11. What are broadleaf weeds?

Broadleaf weeds have wider leaves and net-like veins. Examples include Amaranthus, Chenopodium, Parthenium, Portulaca, and Trianthema.

## Q12. What are sedges?

Sedges are grass-like weeds with triangular stems. Cyperus rotundus is a common sedge weed.

## Q13. Can mulching control weeds?

Yes. Mulching blocks sunlight, reduces weed emergence, conserves soil moisture, and is useful in vegetables, orchards, and high-value crops.

## Q14. What is stale seedbed technique?

A stale seedbed is prepared before sowing. Weeds are allowed to germinate and then destroyed before planting the crop.

## Q15. Why is sprayer calibration important?

Sprayer calibration ensures correct herbicide dose per area. Poor calibration can cause weed-control failure or crop injury.

## Q16. What is herbicide phytotoxicity?

Herbicide phytotoxicity is crop injury caused by herbicide misuse, wrong dose, wrong timing, spray drift, carryover, or crop sensitivity.

## Q17. What is herbicide carryover?

Herbicide carryover occurs when herbicide remains active in soil and injures the next crop.

## Q18. How can farmers reduce weed seedbank?

Prevent weeds from producing seeds, use clean seed, control field borders, use stale seedbed, rotate crops, mulch, and remove escaped weeds.

## Q19. Which herbicide should be used for perennial weeds?

Perennial weeds often require systemic herbicides and repeated integrated management. Herbicide choice depends on crop, weed species, and label recommendation.

## Q20. Can herbicides damage nearby crops?

Yes. Spray drift from herbicides, especially non-selective and synthetic auxin herbicides, can damage nearby sensitive crops.
