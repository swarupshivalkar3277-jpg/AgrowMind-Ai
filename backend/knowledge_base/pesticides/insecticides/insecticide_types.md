# Types of Insecticides

## 1. Overview

Insecticides are pesticides used to manage insect pests that damage crops, stored grains, fruits, vegetables, plantation crops, ornamentals, and household or public-health areas. In agriculture, insecticides are used to control pests such as aphids, jassids, thrips, whiteflies, caterpillars, borers, beetles, hoppers, mealybugs, mites, termites, fruit flies, and sucking pests.

Insecticides can be classified in many ways:

* By mode of action
* By chemical group
* By target pest
* By how they enter the insect body
* By movement inside the plant
* By origin, such as synthetic, botanical, biological, or mineral
* By formulation type
* By use pattern, such as foliar spray, seed treatment, soil application, bait, fumigation, or trunk injection

For a RAG knowledge base, insecticide classification by **mode of action** is especially important because it helps answer farmer questions about pest control, product selection, and resistance management.

## 2. Simple Definition

An insecticide is a chemical, biological, botanical, or mineral-based product used to kill, repel, suppress, or disrupt insects that cause economic damage to crops or stored produce.

## 3. Major Classification of Insecticides

Insecticides may be grouped into the following major categories:

1. Contact insecticides
2. Stomach insecticides
3. Systemic insecticides
4. Translaminar insecticides
5. Fumigant insecticides
6. Repellents
7. Antifeedants
8. Ovicides
9. Larvicides
10. Adulticides
11. Insect growth regulators
12. Neurotoxic insecticides
13. Respiratory inhibitors
14. Midgut disruptors
15. Biological insecticides
16. Botanical insecticides
17. Mineral or inorganic insecticides
18. Baits and attract-and-kill products

Each type works differently and should be selected according to pest type, crop stage, pest life stage, infestation level, weather, safety requirement, and resistance-management plan.

# 4. Classification by Entry Route into Insect Body

## 4.1 Contact Insecticides

Contact insecticides kill insects when the spray directly touches the insect body or when insects walk over treated surfaces.

### Key Features

* Act through direct body contact
* Require good spray coverage
* Useful against exposed insects
* Less effective on hidden pests inside stems, fruits, soil, or curled leaves
* Often affected by rainfall and sunlight
* May need repeat application if pest pressure continues

### Common Target Pests

* Aphids
* Jassids
* Thrips
* Whiteflies
* Caterpillars
* Beetles
* Mealybugs
* Hoppers
* Mites, depending on product

### Examples of Contact Insecticide Groups

* Pyrethroids
* Organophosphates
* Carbamates
* Some botanical oils
* Some microbial or natural-contact products

### Farmer Advisory

Use contact insecticides when pests are visible and exposed. Spray both upper and lower leaf surfaces. Contact products may fail if insects hide under leaves, inside plant tissue, or in rolled leaves.

## 4.2 Stomach Insecticides

Stomach insecticides kill insects when they eat treated plant parts or bait.

### Key Features

* Insect must ingest the insecticide
* Very useful against chewing pests
* Less effective against pests that do not consume treated surface
* Coverage of feeding area is important
* Often used against caterpillars, beetles, and borers at early stages

### Common Target Pests

* Caterpillars
* Leaf-eating larvae
* Beetle larvae and adults
* Grasshoppers
* Some borers before entry into plant tissue

### Examples

* Bacillus thuringiensis products
* Some diamides
* Some organophosphates
* Some carbamates
* Some insect growth regulators

### Farmer Advisory

Stomach insecticides work best when applied while larvae are small and actively feeding. Once borers enter stems or fruits, stomach insecticides may become less effective.

## 4.3 Systemic Insecticides

Systemic insecticides are absorbed by the plant and move through plant tissues. Insects are controlled when they suck sap or feed on treated plant parts.

### Key Features

* Absorbed by roots, leaves, seeds, or stems
* Move inside the plant to some extent
* Useful against sucking pests
* Often used as seed treatment, soil drench, granule, or foliar spray
* Can protect new growth depending on product mobility
* Must be used carefully to protect pollinators and beneficial insects

### Common Target Pests

* Aphids
* Jassids
* Whiteflies
* Thrips
* Hoppers
* Mealybugs
* Scales
* Termites in some use patterns

### Examples of Systemic Groups

* Neonicotinoids
* Some organophosphates
* Some diamides
* Some ketoenols
* Some butenolides

### Farmer Advisory

Systemic insecticides are useful for sucking pests, but repeated use can quickly cause resistance. Rotate with different IRAC mode-of-action groups.

## 4.4 Translaminar Insecticides

Translaminar insecticides move from the upper leaf surface to the lower leaf surface but do not fully move through the whole plant like systemic insecticides.

### Key Features

* Penetrate leaf tissue
* Protect both sides of sprayed leaves
* Useful against pests feeding on underside of leaves
* Often effective against mites, leaf miners, thrips, and some caterpillars
* Require good coverage for best performance

### Common Target Pests

* Mites
* Thrips
* Leaf miners
* Whiteflies
* Small caterpillars

### Examples

* Abamectin
* Emamectin benzoate
* Some spinosyns
* Some diamides

### Farmer Advisory

Translaminar insecticides are useful where pests hide under leaves. However, they may not protect new leaves that emerge after spraying.

## 4.5 Fumigant Insecticides

Fumigants are pesticides that act as gases or vapors. They are mainly used in storage, soil treatment, quarantine, or enclosed-space pest control.

### Key Features

* Act through vapor or gas
* Require sealed conditions
* Highly hazardous if misused
* Usually applied by trained professionals
* Used for stored grain pests, soil pests, or quarantine treatment

### Common Target Pests

* Stored grain beetles
* Weevils
* Moths
* Termites
* Soil insects in some fumigation systems

### Examples

* Aluminium phosphide
* Magnesium phosphide
* Sulfuryl fluoride, depending on country and use approval

### Farmer Advisory

Fumigants are dangerous and should never be used casually. They require strict label compliance, trained handling, sealed storage structures, and safety precautions.

# 5. Classification by Mode of Action

Mode of action means the specific biological process in the insect that the insecticide affects. Mode-of-action classification is important for resistance management. Farmers should rotate insecticides from different mode-of-action groups, not just different brand names.

## 5.1 Nerve and Muscle Action Insecticides

These insecticides disturb the insect nervous system or muscle function. Many fast-acting insecticides belong to this category.

### 5.1.1 Acetylcholinesterase Inhibitors

These insecticides block acetylcholinesterase, an enzyme needed for normal nerve signal termination. This causes overstimulation, paralysis, and death.

#### Main Chemical Groups

* Organophosphates
* Carbamates

#### Common Examples

* Chlorpyrifos
* Quinalphos
* Profenofos
* Acephate
* Dimethoate
* Malathion
* Carbaryl
* Carbofuran
* Thiodicarb

#### Common Target Pests

* Sucking pests
* Caterpillars
* Borers
* Beetles
* Termites
* Soil insects

#### Notes

Organophosphates and carbamates are often broad-spectrum and may harm beneficial insects. Many products in these groups require careful handling because of higher mammalian toxicity concerns.

## 5.1.2 Sodium Channel Modulators

These insecticides affect sodium channels in insect nerves, causing rapid knockdown, tremors, paralysis, and death.

#### Main Chemical Group

* Pyrethroids

#### Common Examples

* Cypermethrin
* Deltamethrin
* Lambda-cyhalothrin
* Alpha-cypermethrin
* Fenvalerate
* Permethrin
* Bifenthrin

#### Common Target Pests

* Bollworms
* Caterpillars
* Fruit borers
* Shoot borers
* Beetles
* Mosquitoes
* Flies
* Some sucking pests

#### Notes

Pyrethroids often give quick knockdown but resistance is common in many pests. They can also flare mites or sucking pests by killing natural enemies.

## 5.1.3 Nicotinic Acetylcholine Receptor Competitive Modulators

These insecticides act on nicotinic acetylcholine receptors in the insect nervous system.

#### Main Chemical Groups

* Neonicotinoids
* Butenolides
* Sulfoximines
* Mesonicotinoids

#### Common Examples

* Imidacloprid
* Thiamethoxam
* Acetamiprid
* Clothianidin
* Dinotefuran
* Flupyradifurone
* Sulfoxaflor
* Triflumezopyrim

#### Common Target Pests

* Aphids
* Jassids
* Whiteflies
* Hoppers
* Thrips
* Mealybugs
* Termites in some use patterns

#### Notes

These are often effective against sucking pests. Some are systemic. They must be used carefully around flowering crops and pollinators.

## 5.1.4 Nicotinic Acetylcholine Receptor Allosteric Modulators

These insecticides also affect nicotinic acetylcholine receptors but in a different way from neonicotinoids.

#### Main Chemical Group

* Spinosyns

#### Common Examples

* Spinosad
* Spinetoram

#### Common Target Pests

* Thrips
* Caterpillars
* Leaf miners
* Fruit borers
* Diamondback moth

#### Notes

Spinosyns are useful in many IPM programs, but resistance can develop if used repeatedly. Avoid excessive applications in the same crop cycle.

## 5.1.5 GABA-Gated Chloride Channel Blockers

These insecticides interfere with chloride channels in the insect nervous system.

#### Main Chemical Groups

* Phenylpyrazoles
* Cyclodienes, older and restricted in many regions

#### Common Examples

* Fipronil

#### Common Target Pests

* Termites
* Stem borers
* Root pests
* Thrips in some crops
* Soil insects

#### Notes

Fipronil is often used as seed treatment, soil application, or targeted treatment. It must be used carefully because of environmental and pollinator concerns.

## 5.1.6 Glutamate-Gated Chloride Channel Allosteric Modulators

These insecticides disrupt chloride channels, causing paralysis and death.

#### Main Chemical Groups

* Avermectins
* Milbemycins

#### Common Examples

* Abamectin
* Emamectin benzoate
* Milbemectin

#### Common Target Pests

* Mites
* Thrips
* Leaf miners
* Caterpillars
* Fruit borers

#### Notes

Abamectin and emamectin often show translaminar activity. They are effective at low dose but should be rotated to prevent resistance.

## 5.1.7 Ryanodine Receptor Modulators

These insecticides disturb calcium release in insect muscles, causing feeding cessation, paralysis, and death.

#### Main Chemical Group

* Diamides

#### Common Examples

* Chlorantraniliprole
* Cyantraniliprole
* Flubendiamide
* Cyclaniliprole
* Tetraniliprole

#### Common Target Pests

* Caterpillars
* Fruit borers
* Stem borers
* Leaf folders
* Diamondback moth
* Armyworms

#### Notes

Diamides are highly effective against lepidopteran larvae. Resistance management is important because repeated use can select resistant populations.

## 5.2 Growth and Development Disruptors

These insecticides interfere with insect growth, moulting, egg development, or metamorphosis. They usually act more slowly than nerve poisons.

## 5.2.1 Juvenile Hormone Mimics

These insecticides imitate juvenile hormone and disturb normal insect development.

#### Common Examples

* Pyriproxyfen
* Methoprene
* Fenoxycarb

#### Common Target Pests

* Whiteflies
* Scales
* Mealybugs
* Mosquito larvae
* Some stored-product insects

#### Notes

Juvenile hormone mimics are often useful against immature stages and may reduce egg hatch or adult emergence.

## 5.2.2 Chitin Synthesis Inhibitors

These insecticides stop insects from forming normal cuticle during moulting.

#### Main Chemical Groups

* Benzoylureas
* Buprofezin-type products

#### Common Examples

* Diflubenzuron
* Lufenuron
* Novaluron
* Hexaflumuron
* Buprofezin

#### Common Target Pests

* Caterpillars
* Whiteflies
* Hoppers
* Mealybugs
* Termites
* Some beetle larvae

#### Notes

These insecticides work best on young immature stages. They are slower acting and should be applied before pest populations become severe.

## 5.2.3 Ecdysone Receptor Agonists

These insecticides trigger abnormal moulting and disrupt larval development.

#### Common Examples

* Tebufenozide
* Methoxyfenozide
* Chromafenozide

#### Common Target Pests

* Caterpillars
* Leaf rollers
* Armyworms
* Fruit borers

#### Notes

These are mainly used against lepidopteran larvae and are more selective than many broad-spectrum insecticides.

## 5.3 Respiration Disruptors

Respiration disruptors interfere with energy production in insect cells. Many miticides and some insecticides belong to this category.

### Common Groups

* METI acaricides and insecticides
* Mitochondrial complex inhibitors
* Oxidative phosphorylation disruptors
* ATP synthase inhibitors

### Common Examples

* Diafenthiuron
* Fenazaquin
* Pyridaben
* Spiromesifen
* Spirotetramat
* Chlorfenapyr

### Common Target Pests

* Mites
* Whiteflies
* Thrips
* Aphids
* Some caterpillars

### Notes

Respiration disruptors are often useful in mite and sucking pest programs. They should be rotated carefully because resistance can develop.

## 5.4 Midgut Disruptors

These insecticides damage the insect gut after ingestion.

### Main Example

* Bacillus thuringiensis, also called Bt

### Common Bt Types

* Bt kurstaki
* Bt aizawai
* Bt israelensis

### Common Target Pests

* Caterpillars
* Diamondback moth
* Fruit borers
* Leaf-eating larvae
* Mosquito larvae, depending on Bt strain

### Notes

Bt products are biological insecticides. They work best when larvae are small and actively feeding. They are generally safer for many beneficial insects but require correct timing and coverage.

# 6. Classification by Chemical Origin

## 6.1 Synthetic Insecticides

Synthetic insecticides are manufactured chemical insecticides. They often provide quick and reliable control when used properly.

### Examples

* Organophosphates
* Carbamates
* Pyrethroids
* Neonicotinoids
* Diamides
* Phenylpyrazoles
* Spinosyns
* Oxadiazines
* Pyrroles

### Advantages

* Fast action
* Broad availability
* Effective against many pests
* Many formulation choices

### Limitations

* Resistance risk
* Residue concerns
* Possible toxicity to humans, livestock, fish, bees, and beneficial insects
* Need for strict label compliance

## 6.2 Botanical Insecticides

Botanical insecticides are derived from plants.

### Common Examples

* Neem oil
* Azadirachtin
* Pyrethrum
* Rotenone, restricted or banned in many places
* Garlic extract
* Chilli extract
* Karanj oil

### Common Target Pests

* Aphids
* Whiteflies
* Thrips
* Jassids
* Mealybugs
* Early-stage caterpillars

### Advantages

* Useful in low-to-moderate pest pressure
* Often suitable for IPM
* Some have repellent, antifeedant, or growth-disrupting effects
* Lower residue concern in many cases

### Limitations

* Slower action
* Shorter persistence
* May need repeated applications
* Quality varies by product
* Less effective during heavy infestation

## 6.3 Biological Insecticides

Biological insecticides contain living organisms, microbial metabolites, or naturally derived biological toxins.

### Main Types

* Bacterial insecticides
* Fungal insecticides
* Viral insecticides
* Nematode-based biologicals
* Microbial metabolites

### Common Examples

* Bacillus thuringiensis
* Beauveria bassiana
* Metarhizium anisopliae
* Verticillium lecanii / Lecanicillium lecanii
* Nuclear polyhedrosis virus, also called NPV
* Spinosad, derived from Saccharopolyspora spinosa metabolites

### Common Target Pests

* Caterpillars
* Whiteflies
* Mealybugs
* Thrips
* Aphids
* Beetle larvae
* Soil insects
* Grubs

### Advantages

* Useful in IPM
* Often safer to natural enemies
* Lower residue concern
* Good option for organic or residue-sensitive crops, depending on certification

### Limitations

* Sensitive to heat, UV light, and dry conditions
* Slower action than many synthetic insecticides
* Require correct timing
* Need good humidity for fungal biologicals
* Product quality and storage are important

## 6.4 Mineral and Inorganic Insecticides

Mineral or inorganic insecticides are derived from mineral sources or inorganic compounds.

### Examples

* Sulfur, mainly used as fungicide and miticide
* Lime sulfur
* Diatomaceous earth
* Mineral oils
* Horticultural oils
* Kaolin clay

### Uses

* Mite suppression
* Scale insects
* Whiteflies
* Aphids
* Stored grain pests, in the case of diatomaceous earth
* Sunburn and pest deterrence, in the case of kaolin

### Notes

Mineral oils and horticultural oils can suffocate soft-bodied pests but may cause phytotoxicity if sprayed during high temperature or mixed with incompatible pesticides.

# 7. Classification by Pest Type

## 7.1 Aphicides

Aphicides are insecticides used mainly against aphids.

### Common Groups

* Neonicotinoids
* Sulfoximines
* Butenolides
* Pyrethroids
* Botanicals
* Biologicals
* Selective feeding blockers

### Crop Examples

* Vegetables
* Cotton
* Mustard
* Wheat
* Fruits
* Pulses

## 7.2 Miticides or Acaricides

Miticides control mites. Mites are not insects, but many crop-protection programs include acaricides with insecticides.

### Common Target Pests

* Red spider mite
* Yellow mite
* Two-spotted spider mite
* Broad mite
* Eriophyid mites

### Common Examples

* Abamectin
* Fenazaquin
* Propargite
* Spiromesifen
* Diafenthiuron
* Sulfur

## 7.3 Larvicides

Larvicides kill immature larval stages of insects.

### Common Target Pests

* Caterpillars
* Mosquito larvae
* Beetle larvae
* Grubs
* Leaf miners

### Examples

* Bt
* Emamectin benzoate
* Chlorantraniliprole
* Lufenuron
* Novaluron
* NPV

## 7.4 Ovicides

Ovicides kill insect or mite eggs or reduce egg hatch.

### Common Target Pests

* Mites
* Whiteflies
* Leaf miners
* Some caterpillars

### Examples

* Pyriproxyfen
* Spiromesifen
* Hexythiazox
* Clofentezine
* Some oils

## 7.5 Adulticides

Adulticides kill adult insects.

### Common Target Pests

* Mosquitoes
* Fruit flies
* Beetles
* Moths
* Whiteflies
* Hoppers

### Examples

* Pyrethroids
* Organophosphates
* Baits
* Attract-and-kill products

## 7.6 Termiticides

Termiticides are used against termites.

### Common Examples

* Fipronil
* Chlorpyrifos, where registered
* Imidacloprid
* Bifenthrin
* Termite bait systems

### Use Pattern

* Soil treatment
* Seed treatment
* Building treatment
* Nursery treatment
* Plantation crop root-zone treatment

# 8. Classification by Plant Mobility

## 8.1 Non-Systemic Insecticides

Non-systemic insecticides remain mostly on the plant surface.

### Features

* Need direct spray coverage
* Useful against exposed pests
* May be washed off by rain
* New growth is not protected
* Often contact or stomach poisons

### Examples

* Many pyrethroids
* Many botanicals
* Some organophosphates
* Bt products
* Oils and soaps

## 8.2 Systemic Insecticides

Systemic insecticides move inside the plant.

### Features

* Useful against sucking pests
* Can be applied as seed treatment, soil drench, granule, or foliar spray
* May protect new growth depending on chemical movement
* Can pose risk to pollinators if used during flowering

### Examples

* Imidacloprid
* Thiamethoxam
* Acetamiprid
* Dinotefuran
* Flupyradifurone
* Spirotetramat

## 8.3 Translaminar Insecticides

Translaminar insecticides move across leaf tissue.

### Features

* Useful against leaf underside pests
* Good for mites, leaf miners, and thrips
* Does not move strongly to distant plant parts
* Coverage still matters

### Examples

* Abamectin
* Emamectin benzoate
* Spinosad
* Spinetoram
* Some diamides

# 9. Classification by Formulation

## 9.1 EC - Emulsifiable Concentrate

Liquid formulation that forms an emulsion when mixed with water.

### Features

* Easy to measure
* Good spreading
* May have solvent odor
* Can cause phytotoxicity in sensitive crops
* Flammable risk in some cases

## 9.2 SC - Suspension Concentrate

Fine solid particles suspended in liquid.

### Features

* Less dust than WP
* Easy to mix
* Needs shaking before use
* Lower solvent content than EC

## 9.3 WP - Wettable Powder

Powder formulation mixed with water before spraying.

### Features

* Can produce dust during mixing
* Needs agitation
* May clog nozzles if not mixed properly
* Often stable in storage if kept dry

## 9.4 WG or WDG - Water Dispersible Granule

Granular product that disperses in water.

### Features

* Less dusty than WP
* Easy handling
* Good storage stability
* Needs proper mixing

## 9.5 GR - Granule

Solid granules applied to soil, whorl, or root zone.

### Features

* Used for soil insects, stem borers, and root-zone pests
* Lower drift than spray
* Requires moisture for activation in some cases

## 9.6 SL - Soluble Liquid

Liquid formulation that dissolves in water.

### Features

* Easy to mix
* Uniform solution
* Common for many systemic insecticides

## 9.7 SG - Soluble Granule

Granules that dissolve in water.

### Features

* Low dust
* Easy transport
* Good for some modern insecticides

## 9.8 ULV - Ultra Low Volume

Highly concentrated formulation used in specialized equipment.

### Features

* Low water requirement
* Used in public health, locust control, or specific field operations
* Requires special sprayer calibration

## 9.9 Bait Formulation

Bait combines attractant and toxicant.

### Features

* Useful against fruit flies, ants, cockroaches, termites, and rodents depending on product
* Reduces whole-field spraying
* Works when pest feeds on bait

# 10. Classification by Application Method

## 10.1 Foliar Spray

Applied to leaves and above-ground plant parts.

### Best For

* Aphids
* Thrips
* Whiteflies
* Caterpillars
* Mites
* Leaf miners
* Hoppers

### Key Requirement

Good coverage is essential.

## 10.2 Seed Treatment

Applied to seed before sowing.

### Best For

* Early sucking pests
* Termites
* Soil insects
* Early seedling protection

### Advantages

* Low dose per hectare
* Protects early crop stage
* Reduces need for early foliar spray

### Caution

Use only registered seed-treatment products. Treated seed should never be used as food or feed.

## 10.3 Soil Application

Applied to soil as drench, granule, or incorporation.

### Best For

* Termites
* Root grubs
* Cutworms
* Soil insects
* Early systemic protection

## 10.4 Whorl Application

Granules or sprays applied into crop whorl.

### Best For

* Maize fall armyworm
* Stem borers
* Leaf folders in some crops

## 10.5 Trunk Injection or Stem Application

Used in some tree crops.

### Best For

* Stem borers
* Sucking pests
* Tree-specific pest management

## 10.6 Fumigation

Used in closed spaces or storage.

### Best For

* Stored grain pests
* Quarantine pests
* Soil pests in specialized systems

# 11. Important Insecticide Chemical Groups

### Target Pests

* Aphids
* Jassids
* Thrips
* Caterpillars
* Borers
* Termites
* Hoppers

### Strengths

* Broad-spectrum
* Fast action
* Many uses historically

### Limitations

* Higher safety concerns
* Harmful to beneficial insects
* Resistance risk
* Some products banned or restricted

## 11.2 Carbamates

### Mode of Action

Acetylcholinesterase inhibition.

### Common Examples

* Carbaryl
* Carbofuran
* Methomyl
* Thiodicarb

### Target Pests

* Caterpillars
* Beetles
* Soil pests
* Sucking pests

### Limitations

* Toxicity concerns
* Some products restricted or banned
* Resistance risk

## 11.3 Pyrethroids

### Mode of Action

Sodium channel modulation.

### Common Examples

* Cypermethrin
* Deltamethrin
* Lambda-cyhalothrin
* Fenvalerate
* Bifenthrin
* Permethrin

### Target Pests

* Caterpillars
* Fruit borers
* Beetles
* Flies
* Mosquitoes
* Some sucking pests

### Strengths

* Quick knockdown
* Low dose
* Broad-spectrum

### Limitations

* Resistance common in many pests
* Can harm natural enemies
* May cause mite resurgence
* Not always effective against hidden pests

## 11.4 Neonicotinoids

### Mode of Action

Nicotinic acetylcholine receptor competitive modulation.

### Common Examples

* Imidacloprid
* Thiamethoxam
* Acetamiprid
* Clothianidin
* Dinotefuran

### Target Pests

* Aphids
* Whiteflies
* Jassids
* Hoppers
* Thrips
* Termites
* Mealybugs

### Strengths

* Systemic activity
* Useful for sucking pests
* Seed treatment and soil application options

### Limitations

* Pollinator concerns
* Resistance risk in whiteflies, aphids, and hoppers
* Avoid during flowering unless label allows and pollinator risk is managed

## 11.5 Phenylpyrazoles

### Mode of Action

GABA-gated chloride channel blocking.

### Common Example

* Fipronil

### Target Pests

* Termites
* Stem borers
* Thrips
* Soil insects
* Root pests

### Strengths

* Effective at low dose
* Useful in seed treatment and soil application

### Limitations

* Environmental and pollinator concerns
* Resistance risk
* Must follow label strictly

## 11.6 Spinosyns

### Mode of Action

Nicotinic acetylcholine receptor allosteric modulation.

### Common Examples

* Spinosad
* Spinetoram

### Target Pests

* Thrips
* Leaf miners
* Caterpillars
* Fruit borers
* Diamondback moth

### Strengths

* Useful in IPM
* Good activity on thrips and caterpillars
* Often lower impact than older broad-spectrum insecticides when used correctly

### Limitations

* Resistance can develop
* Avoid repeated use in same pest generation

## 11.7 Avermectins and Milbemycins

### Mode of Action

Glutamate-gated chloride channel modulation.

### Common Examples

* Abamectin
* Emamectin benzoate
* Milbemectin

### Target Pests

* Mites
* Thrips
* Leaf miners
* Caterpillars
* Fruit borers

### Strengths

* Strong translaminar activity
* Effective at low dose
* Useful against hidden leaf-surface pests

### Limitations

* Sensitive to sunlight degradation
* Resistance risk
* Toxic to aquatic organisms
* Avoid drift to water bodies

## 11.8 Diamides

### Mode of Action

Ryanodine receptor modulation.

### Common Examples

* Chlorantraniliprole
* Cyantraniliprole
* Flubendiamide
* Cyclaniliprole
* Tetraniliprole

### Target Pests

* Caterpillars
* Fruit borers
* Stem borers

# 13. Resistance Management

Insecticide resistance occurs when pest populations become less sensitive to an insecticide after repeated exposure. Resistance is common in pests with short life cycles, high reproduction, and repeated insecticide exposure.

## 13.1 Causes of Resistance

* Repeated use of same insecticide group
* Underdosing
* Overdosing
* Frequent sprays without scouting
* Use of broad-spectrum insecticides that kill natural enemies
* Spraying after pest population becomes very high
* Poor spray coverage
* Ignoring pest life stage
* Using mixtures without resistance strategy

## 13.2 Resistance Management Principles

* Rotate insecticides by IRAC mode-of-action group.
* Do not rotate only by brand name.
* Avoid repeated use of the same mode-of-action group in the same pest generation.
* Use recommended dose only.
* Spray only when pest crosses economic threshold.
* Use biological control and cultural practices.
* Preserve natural enemies.
* Use pest-specific products instead of broad-spectrum sprays when possible.
* Avoid unnecessary pesticide mixtures.
* Follow label restrictions on number of applications.

## 13.3 Example Rotation Concept

For caterpillar pests:

* First spray: Bt or NPV when larvae are small
* Next spray window: Diamide group product if infestation increases
* Next spray window: Spinosyn or avermectin group product
* Later spray: Insect growth regulator or other approved group

For sucking pests:

* First approach: neem, yellow sticky traps, sanitation, natural enemy conservation
* If population crosses threshold: selective systemic or translaminar product
* Next spray window: rotate to different IRAC group
* Avoid repeated neonicotinoid use for whitefly or aphid management

# 15. Safety Classification and Precautions

Insecticides vary widely in toxicity. Some are relatively safer when used correctly, while others are highly hazardous. Always follow the product label, local registration, crop recommendation, dose, waiting period, and safety instructions.

## 15.1 Basic PPE

* Gloves
* Long-sleeved shirt
* Long pants
* Shoes or boots
* Face mask or respirator where required
* Eye protection
* Apron during mixing
* Head cover during spraying

## 15.2 Safe Handling

* Do not eat, drink, smoke, or chew tobacco during mixing or spraying.
* Avoid inhaling spray mist or dust.
* Avoid skin and eye contact.
* Mix pesticides in a well-ventilated place.
* Do not use bare hands to stir pesticide solution.
* Keep children, animals, and untrained persons away.
* Wash hands and face after spraying.
* Wash contaminated clothes separately.
* Store pesticides in original labeled containers.
* Do not store pesticides near food, seed, feed, or drinking water.

## 15.3 Environmental Safety

* Do not spray near ponds, streams, wells, or canals.
* Avoid spray drift.
* Do not spray during strong wind.
* Avoid spraying during bee activity or crop flowering unless label allows.
* Do not wash sprayers in water bodies.
* Dispose of empty containers safely.
* Avoid repeated broad-spectrum insecticide sprays that harm natural enemies.

# 17. Common Mistakes in Insecticide Use

## 17.1 Wrong Pest Identification

Many farmers spray without confirming the pest. For example, mite damage, nutrient deficiency, virus symptoms, and fungal leaf spots may be confused with insect damage.

## 17.2 Spraying Too Late

Insecticides are less effective when pest populations become severe or when larvae enter fruits, stems, or pods.

## 17.3 Repeating Same Insecticide

Repeated use of the same chemical group increases resistance.

## 17.4 Poor Spray Coverage

Contact and stomach insecticides fail if the spray does not reach the pest.

## 17.5 Ignoring Weather

Rain, wind, high temperature, and strong sunlight can reduce insecticide performance.

## 17.6 Mixing Many Products

Unnecessary mixing can cause crop injury, residue issues, poor control, and higher cost.

# 18. Choosing the Right Insecticide Type

## 18.1 For Aphids

Prefer selective systemic or translaminar insecticides, botanicals, oils, soaps, and natural enemy conservation. Avoid repeated broad-spectrum sprays.

## 18.2 For Whiteflies

Use yellow sticky traps, remove virus-infected plants, control weeds, conserve natural enemies, and rotate insecticides. Insect growth regulators, selective systemics, and biologicals may help.

## 18.3 For Thrips

Use blue or yellow sticky traps, avoid water stress, remove weeds, and use translaminar or systemic insecticides when threshold is crossed.

## 18.4 For Caterpillars

Use pheromone traps for monitoring. Apply Bt, NPV, diamides, spinosyns, avermectins, or IGRs when larvae are small.

## 18.5 For Borers

Preventive timing is important. Once larvae enter stem, fruit, or pod, control is difficult. Use pheromone traps, remove infested parts, and apply recommended larvicides early.

## 18.6 For Mites

Avoid unnecessary pyrethroid sprays that can flare mites. Use miticides, sulfur, oils, or biological options according to crop and pest stage.

## 18.7 For Termites

Use seed treatment, soil treatment, field sanitation, organic matter management, and registered termiticides.

# 19. Short RAG Answers

## Query: What are insecticides?

Insecticides are pesticides used to control insect pests that damage crops, stored grain, fruits, vegetables, and other agricultural products.

## Query: What are the main types of insecticides?

The main types include contact insecticides, stomach insecticides, systemic insecticides, translaminar insecticides, fumigants, repellents, antifeedants, ovicides, larvicides, adulticides, insect growth regulators, biological insecticides, botanical insecticides, and mineral insecticides.

## Query: What is a contact insecticide?

A contact insecticide kills insects when it directly touches their body or when they walk over treated surfaces. It needs good spray coverage and works best on exposed pests.

## Query: What is a systemic insecticide?

A systemic insecticide is absorbed by the plant and moves inside plant tissues. It controls insects when they suck sap or feed on treated plant parts.

## Query: What is a stomach insecticide?

A stomach insecticide kills insects when they eat treated plant parts or bait. It is especially useful against chewing pests such as caterpillars and beetles.

## Query: What is a translaminar insecticide?

A translaminar insecticide moves from one leaf surface to the other through the leaf tissue. It is useful against pests that feed on the underside of leaves, such as mites, thrips, and leaf miners.

## Query: What insecticide is best for sucking pests?

Sucking pests are commonly managed with systemic, translaminar, selective, botanical, or biological insecticides. The best choice depends on the pest, crop, infestation level, and local recommendation.

## Query: What insecticide is best for caterpillars?

Caterpillars are commonly managed with stomach poisons, Bt, NPV, diamides, spinosyns, avermectins, and insect growth regulators. Spraying is most effective when larvae are small.

## Query: What is insecticide resistance?

Insecticide resistance is when pest populations become less sensitive to an insecticide because the same mode of action is used repeatedly.

## Query: How can farmers prevent insecticide resistance?

Farmers can prevent resistance by rotating IRAC mode-of-action groups, spraying only when needed, using recommended dose, preserving natural enemies, and combining insecticides with IPM practices.

## Query: Are botanical insecticides safe?

Botanical insecticides are often lower-risk than many synthetic insecticides, but they are not automatically harmless. Farmers should still follow label dose, PPE, and waiting period.

## Query: Are biological insecticides slow?

Many biological insecticides act more slowly than synthetic nerve poisons. They work best when applied early, under suitable weather conditions, and with good spray coverage.

# 22. Short Summary

Insecticides are pesticides used to manage insect pests in crops, stored produce, and other agricultural systems. They can be classified as contact, stomach, systemic, translaminar, fumigant, botanical, biological, synthetic, mineral, larvicidal, ovicidal, adulticidal, or growth-regulating products. Important chemical groups include organophosphates, carbamates, pyrethroids, neonicotinoids, phenylpyrazoles, spinosyns, avermectins, diamides, and insect growth regulators. Insecticide selection should be based on pest identification, crop stage, pest life stage, economic threshold, safety, resistance risk, and integrated pest management principles. Farmers should rotate IRAC mode-of-action groups, avoid unnecessary sprays, use PPE, protect pollinators, and follow label dose and waiting period.
