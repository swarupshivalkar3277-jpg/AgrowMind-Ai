# Safe Usage of Insecticides

# Overview

Safe usage of insecticides means using insecticides correctly, responsibly, and only when needed to control harmful insect pests while protecting farmers, consumers, beneficial insects, livestock, soil, water, and the environment.

Insecticides can reduce crop losses caused by pests such as aphids, whiteflies, thrips, fruit borers, stem borers, leaf miners, jassids, mealybugs, caterpillars, mites, beetles, and sucking pests. However, insecticides can also cause harm if used carelessly, at the wrong dose, on the wrong crop, near harvest, without protective equipment, or without following label instructions.

Safe insecticide use helps farmers:

* Protect crops from damaging pests
* Reduce pesticide poisoning risk
* Avoid crop injury
* Reduce pesticide residues
* Protect pollinators and natural enemies
* Prevent pest resistance
* Protect soil and water
* Reduce unnecessary input cost
* Improve market acceptance of produce
* Maintain long-term farm sustainability

Insecticides should always be used as part of Integrated Pest Management, also called IPM, and not as the first or only pest-control method.

---

# What is an Insecticide?

An insecticide is a pesticide used to kill, repel, suppress, or manage insect pests.

Insecticides may act on:

* Eggs
* Larvae
* Nymphs
* Adults
* Sucking insects
* Chewing insects
* Boring insects
* Soil insects
* Stored grain insects

Different insecticides have different modes of action, safety levels, waiting periods, pest targets, crop approvals, and resistance risks.

Farmers should use only registered insecticides that are approved for the specific crop and pest.

---

# Why Safe Use is Important

Unsafe insecticide use can cause serious problems.

Possible risks include:

* Farmer poisoning
* Skin irritation
* Eye injury
* Breathing difficulty
* Headache, dizziness, nausea, or vomiting
* Crop burn or phytotoxicity
* Residue above safe limits
* Harm to honeybees and pollinators
* Harm to natural enemies
* Fish and livestock poisoning
* Water contamination
* Soil contamination
* Pest resistance
* Secondary pest outbreaks
* Rejection of produce in markets

Safe use protects both crop production and human health.

---

# Basic Principles of Safe Insecticide Use

Safe insecticide use is based on five important principles:

1. Use insecticide only when needed.
2. Select the correct insecticide for the crop and pest.
3. Use the correct dose and water volume.
4. Apply at the correct time and crop stage.
5. Follow safety, waiting period, and label instructions.

---

# Integrated Pest Management First

Integrated Pest Management, or IPM, means combining different pest management methods before relying on chemical insecticides.

IPM includes:

* Regular pest monitoring
* Economic threshold-based spraying
* Resistant varieties
* Healthy seedlings
* Field sanitation
* Crop rotation
* Trap crops
* Yellow and blue sticky traps
* Pheromone traps
* Light traps where appropriate
* Biological control
* Neem-based products
* Biopesticides
* Mechanical removal of egg masses or larvae
* Need-based chemical insecticide use

Chemical insecticide should be used only when pest population reaches damaging level or when expert recommendation indicates spray is needed.

---

# Avoid Calendar Spraying

Calendar spraying means spraying insecticide at fixed intervals without checking pest level.

This should be avoided because it can:

* Waste money
* Kill beneficial insects
* Increase pest resistance
* Increase pesticide residues
* Increase health risk
* Cause secondary pest outbreaks
* Reduce natural pest control

Spray only after monitoring the crop and confirming pest need.

---

# Pest Monitoring Before Spraying

Before deciding to spray, farmers should inspect the field.

Check:

* Pest species
* Pest stage
* Pest population
* Damage symptoms
* Crop stage
* Natural enemies
* Weather condition
* Previous spray history
* Economic threshold level where available
* Disease or nutrient symptoms that may look like pest damage

Correct pest identification is essential. Wrong diagnosis leads to wrong insecticide use.

---

# Correct Pest Identification

Different pests need different control methods.

Examples:

* Aphids and whiteflies are sucking pests.
* Fruit borers are caterpillar pests.
* Thrips damage young leaves, flowers, and fruits.
* Leaf miners live inside leaves.
* Stem borers live inside stems.
* Mites are not insects and may need miticides or specific recommendations.

Do not use one insecticide for all pests without diagnosis.

---

# Selecting the Correct Insecticide

Before selecting insecticide, check:

* Is the pest correctly identified?
* Is the crop listed on the label?
* Is the pest listed on the label?
* Is the formulation suitable?
* What is the recommended dose?
* What is the waiting period?
* What is the mode of action?
* What safety equipment is required?
* Is it safe for pollinators?
* Is it compatible with other products?
* Is there a resistance concern?

Farmers should consult agriculture officers, KVK, agricultural university recommendations, or approved pesticide labels.

---

# Label Instructions

The pesticide label is the most important safety guide.

The label may include:

* Trade name
* Active ingredient
* Formulation
* Crop
* Target pest
* Dose
* Water volume
* Waiting period
* Safety precautions
* Antidote information
* Toxicity symbol
* Storage instructions
* Disposal instructions
* Manufacturer details
* Batch number
* Manufacturing date
* Expiry date

Always read the label before use.

---

# Use Only Registered and Approved Products

Farmers should use insecticides registered for use in the country and approved for the target crop and pest.

Avoid:

* Unregistered products
* Fake products
* Products without label
* Products with damaged packaging
* Expired products
* Loose pesticide sold without container
* Products recommended only by verbal advice without label support

Use of unapproved insecticide may cause crop residue problems, legal problems, crop injury, or poor pest control.

---

# Understanding Formulations

Insecticides are sold in different formulations.

Common formulations include:

* EC: Emulsifiable Concentrate
* SC: Suspension Concentrate
* SL: Soluble Liquid
* WP: Wettable Powder
* WG or WDG: Water Dispersible Granule
* GR: Granule
* SP: Soluble Powder
* CS: Capsule Suspension
* SG: Soluble Granule
* ULV: Ultra Low Volume formulation

Dose and mixing method depend on formulation.

Do not assume that all formulations of the same active ingredient have the same dose.

---

# Insecticide Toxicity Labels

Pesticide labels may show toxicity symbols or color bands depending on classification.

Farmers should understand that toxicity category indicates hazard level.

General safety rule:

* More toxic products need stricter handling.
* Avoid highly hazardous pesticides where safer alternatives are available.
* Never handle pesticides without protective equipment.
* Store all pesticides away from children, animals, food, and water.

---

# Personal Protective Equipment

Personal Protective Equipment, or PPE, reduces pesticide exposure. FAO and WHO pesticide safety guidance emphasizes reducing exposure through effective personal protection, including appropriate PPE selection and use.

## Basic PPE for Insecticide Handling

Farmers should use:

* Chemical-resistant gloves
* Face mask or respirator suitable for pesticide use
* Eye protection or goggles
* Long-sleeved shirt
* Long trousers
* Apron where mixing concentrated pesticide
* Shoes or boots
* Head cover
* Waterproof protection where required

## PPE During Mixing

Mixing concentrated insecticide is a high-risk activity.

Use:

* Gloves
* Mask
* Goggles
* Apron
* Closed footwear
* Long clothing

## PPE During Spraying

Use:

* Mask
* Gloves
* Long clothing
* Shoes or boots
* Eye protection where drift risk exists

## PPE After Spraying

Wash PPE separately from household clothes.

Do not reuse contaminated clothing without washing.

---

# Before Buying Insecticide

Before purchase, farmers should:

1. Identify pest correctly.
2. Check if spray is really needed.
3. Ask for crop-specific recommendation.
4. Buy from licensed pesticide dealer.
5. Check label and seal.
6. Check expiry date.
7. Check manufacturing date.
8. Check batch number.
9. Ask for bill.
10. Buy only required quantity.

Do not buy extra pesticide without need.

---

# Transporting Insecticides Safely

When transporting insecticides:

* Keep pesticides separate from food, feed, seed, and household items.
* Do not carry leaking containers.
* Keep containers upright.
* Do not carry pesticide on head, shoulder, or back.
* Do not transport pesticide with children or animals.
* Keep bill and label safe.
* Avoid carrying pesticide inside passenger area if possible.

Official safe-use guidance also warns against transporting pesticides along with food, fodder, or edible articles.

---

# Storing Insecticides Safely

Store insecticides carefully.

## Storage Guidelines

* Keep in original container.
* Store in locked place.
* Keep away from children.
* Keep away from livestock.
* Keep away from food, feed, seed, and drinking water.
* Store in cool and dry place.
* Avoid direct sunlight.
* Keep away from fire.
* Do not store near fertilizers unless safely separated.
* Keep labels intact.
* Do not store leftover spray solution.

## Storage Location

A safe pesticide storage area should be:

* Locked
* Ventilated
* Dry
* Away from living area
* Away from kitchen
* Away from animal shed
* Away from water source
* Clearly marked

---

# Preparing Spray Solution Safely

Preparing spray solution is one of the most dangerous stages because concentrated pesticide is handled.

## Steps

1. Read label carefully.
2. Wear PPE.
3. Use clean water.
4. Measure pesticide accurately.
5. Prepare only required quantity.
6. Mix in open or well-ventilated area.
7. Avoid splashing.
8. Keep children and animals away.
9. Do not smell the chemical.
10. Do not touch pesticide with bare hands.

PPQS safe-use guidance specifically advises using clean water, protective clothing, recommended dosage, and only preparing required solution.

---

# Measuring Insecticide

Use proper measuring tools.

Do not use:

* Household spoons
* Drinking cups
* Kitchen vessels
* Hands
* Bottle caps without calibration
* Approximate guessing

Use a pesticide measuring cup, syringe, scale, or measuring cylinder dedicated only to pesticide use.

Never reuse pesticide measuring tools for food or household use.

---

# Mixing Order

General mixing approach:

1. Fill spray tank halfway with clean water.
2. Add measured insecticide.
3. Stir or agitate.
4. Add remaining water.
5. Mix thoroughly.
6. Spray immediately.

For tank mixes, follow label mixing order and compatibility guidance.

Do not mix multiple pesticides unless recommended.

---

# Avoid Overdose

Overdose is dangerous.

Overdose can cause:

* Crop injury
* High residues
* Farmer poisoning
* Pollinator death
* Natural enemy destruction
* Pest resistance
* Soil and water pollution
* Extra cost
* Market rejection

Always use recommended dose.

---

# Avoid Underdose

Underdose is also harmful.

Underdose can cause:

* Poor pest control
* Pest survival
* Resistance development
* Need for repeat spray
* Yield loss
* Money waste

Correct dose is essential.

---

# Water Quality for Spraying

Use clean water.

Avoid:

* Muddy water
* Stagnant water
* Water with high suspended particles
* Highly alkaline water without expert guidance
* Polluted water

Dirty water can clog nozzles and reduce pesticide effectiveness.

---

# Sprayer Calibration

Sprayer calibration ensures correct pesticide dose per area.

Calibration helps avoid:

* Overdose
* Underdose
* Uneven spray
* Excess residue
* Crop injury
* Poor control
* Chemical waste

Calibrate sprayer before spraying.

---

# Sprayer Selection

Common sprayers include:

* Knapsack sprayer
* Battery-operated sprayer
* Power sprayer
* Mist blower
* Boom sprayer
* Tractor-mounted sprayer
* Drone sprayer where legally allowed and professionally operated

Choose sprayer based on crop, canopy, field size, pest location, and label instructions.

---

# Nozzle Selection

Nozzle affects spray coverage and drift.

Common nozzles:

* Hollow cone nozzle for many insecticide and fungicide foliar sprays
* Flat fan nozzle for herbicide sprays
* Flood jet nozzle for some specific uses
* Mist blower nozzles for orchards and dense canopy crops

Use correct nozzle for the target.

---

# Spray Timing

Best spray timing depends on pest and crop.

General safe timing:

* Early morning or late evening
* Low wind condition
* Moderate temperature
* No rainfall expected soon
* Bees less active where pollinator risk exists
* Crop not under severe stress

Avoid spraying:

* During strong wind
* During hot afternoon
* Before heavy rain
* During flowering when bees are active
* Near water bodies without buffer precautions
* When workers or animals are in field

---

# Weather Conditions

Weather affects spray safety and effectiveness.

## Wind

Strong wind causes drift.

Spray drift can harm:

* Neighboring crops
* People
* Animals
* Pollinators
* Water bodies

## Rain

Rain soon after spray can wash insecticide away and reduce effectiveness.

## Heat

High temperature can increase evaporation, crop stress, and exposure risk.

## Humidity

Humidity affects droplet drying and pest activity.

---

# Spray Direction

Spray with wind direction, not against it.

Do not walk into spray mist.

Avoid spraying toward:

* People
* Animals
* Homes
* Wells
* Ponds
* Beehives
* Vegetable harvest areas
* Neighboring sensitive crops

---

# During Spraying

Farmers should:

* Wear PPE.
* Keep steady speed.
* Maintain correct pressure.
* Avoid nozzle blockage.
* Avoid eating, drinking, or smoking.
* Avoid touching face.
* Avoid spraying against wind.
* Avoid excessive runoff.
* Avoid repeated overlap.
* Keep children and animals away.
* Stop if dizziness or illness occurs.

---

# After Spraying

After spraying:

1. Wash hands and face with soap.
2. Bathe as soon as possible.
3. Wash contaminated clothes separately.
4. Clean sprayer safely.
5. Store unused product safely.
6. Do not store leftover spray solution.
7. Dispose empty containers safely.
8. Record spray details.
9. Observe re-entry interval if label mentions.
10. Keep animals away from treated field until safe.

---

# Re-Entry Interval

Re-entry interval is the minimum time workers should wait before entering a treated field without protective equipment.

It depends on:

* Insecticide
* Crop
* Dose
* Label instruction
* Weather
* Spray type

If label gives a re-entry interval, follow it strictly.

If no re-entry interval is known, avoid entering the field until spray deposits are dry and it is safe according to local guidance.

---

# Waiting Period

Waiting period, also called pre-harvest interval, is the minimum gap between last insecticide spray and harvest.

Waiting period is very important for food safety.

It depends on:

* Crop
* Insecticide
* Dose
* Formulation
* Market requirement
* Label instruction

Never harvest before the waiting period is completed.

---

# Pesticide Residue Safety

Residues are small amounts of pesticide that remain on or in produce after spraying.

High residues may occur due to:

* Spraying near harvest
* Overdose
* Too many sprays
* Wrong insecticide
* Not following waiting period
* Poor washing and handling
* Use of unapproved insecticide

High residues can cause market rejection and health concerns.

---

# Protecting Pollinators

Pollinators such as honeybees are important for many crops.

To protect pollinators:

* Avoid spraying during flowering.
* Avoid spraying when bees are active.
* Spray late evening if necessary and label allows.
* Avoid highly toxic insecticides during bloom.
* Inform nearby beekeepers before spraying.
* Avoid drift to flowering weeds and nearby crops.
* Use bee-safe alternatives where possible.

---

# Protecting Natural Enemies

Natural enemies help control pests naturally.

Examples:

* Ladybird beetles
* Lacewings
* Parasitoid wasps
* Predatory bugs
* Spiders
* Predatory mites
* Dragonflies
* Trichogramma
* Chrysoperla

Unnecessary insecticide sprays can kill natural enemies and increase pest outbreaks later.

Use selective insecticides where possible.

---

# Protecting Water Bodies

Insecticides can harm fish and aquatic organisms.

Farmers should:

* Avoid spraying near ponds, rivers, wells, and canals.
* Maintain buffer zone.
* Avoid washing sprayers in water bodies.
* Avoid disposing leftover spray into drains.
* Avoid spraying before heavy rain.
* Prevent runoff from treated fields.

---

# Protecting Livestock and Poultry

Keep livestock and poultry away from:

* Pesticide storage
* Spray mixing area
* Treated fields
* Empty containers
* Contaminated water
* Freshly sprayed fodder

Do not feed sprayed crop residues to animals unless label and waiting period allow.

---

# Protecting Children and Family

Children are highly vulnerable to pesticide poisoning.

Safety rules:

* Never store pesticides inside home.
* Never keep pesticides in drink bottles.
* Never allow children near mixing or spraying.
* Never reuse pesticide containers.
* Wash work clothes separately.
* Keep pesticide cabinet locked.
* Teach family members that pesticide containers are dangerous.

---

# First Aid for Insecticide Exposure

If exposure occurs, act quickly.

## Skin Contact

* Remove contaminated clothing.
* Wash skin with soap and plenty of water.
* Seek medical help if irritation continues.

## Eye Contact

* Rinse eyes with clean water for several minutes.
* Do not rub eyes.
* Seek medical help immediately.

## Inhalation

* Move person to fresh air.
* Loosen tight clothing.
* Keep person calm.
* Seek medical help if breathing problem, dizziness, or weakness occurs.

## Swallowing

* Do not induce vomiting unless advised by medical professional.
* Do not give anything by mouth to an unconscious person.
* Take pesticide container or label to doctor.
* Seek emergency medical help immediately.

---

# Symptoms of Insecticide Poisoning

Symptoms vary by insecticide type.

Possible symptoms include:

* Headache
* Dizziness
* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Sweating
* Weakness
* Eye irritation
* Skin burning
* Excess salivation
* Stomach pain
* Blurred vision
* Breathing difficulty
* Muscle twitching
* Confusion
* Convulsions in severe cases
* Unconsciousness in severe cases

Any suspected pesticide poisoning should be treated as urgent.

---

# Emergency Action

In case of poisoning:

1. Stop exposure immediately.
2. Move person away from spray area.
3. Remove contaminated clothing.
4. Wash exposed skin.
5. Take pesticide label or container to hospital.
6. Do not delay medical help.
7. Inform doctor about product name and active ingredient.

---

# Antidote Information

Some insecticides have specific antidotes, while others do not.

The pesticide label may mention antidote or treatment information.

Farmers should not self-medicate.

Only qualified medical professionals should decide treatment.

---

# Cleaning Sprayer

After spraying:

* Clean sprayer in a safe place.
* Do not wash near wells, ponds, or canals.
* Rinse tank properly.
* Flush nozzle and hose.
* Use rinse water on labeled crop area if allowed.
* Do not dump rinse water in drains.
* Store sprayer safely.

Use separate sprayer for herbicides if possible to avoid crop injury from herbicide residues.

---

# Empty Container Disposal

Empty pesticide containers are dangerous.

Do not:

* Reuse for water, food, oil, seed, or household storage.
* Sell to unauthorized recyclers.
* Throw in field.
* Throw in canal, pond, or well.
* Burn near people or animals.

Follow label and local disposal guidance.

A common safe approach is triple rinsing, puncturing the container so it cannot be reused, and disposing through approved collection or local agriculture guidance.

---

# Leftover Spray Solution

Avoid preparing excess spray solution.

If leftover solution remains:

* Do not store for next day.
* Do not pour into water bodies.
* Do not dump near home.
* Apply only on labeled crop area if safe and allowed.
* Follow local pesticide disposal guidance.

PPQS guidance advises not using leftover spray solution after 24 hours of preparation.

---

# Tank Mixing Safety

Tank mixing means mixing two or more pesticides or nutrients in one spray tank.

Tank mixing can be risky.

Possible problems:

* Crop burn
* Reduced effectiveness
* Sedimentation
* Nozzle clogging
* Toxic fumes
* Higher residues
* Incompatibility
* Beneficial insect harm

Before tank mixing:

1. Read labels.
2. Confirm compatibility.
3. Conduct jar test.
4. Ask expert.
5. Test small area.
6. Avoid mixing many products together.

---

# Insecticide Resistance Management

Pests can develop resistance when the same insecticide is used repeatedly.

Resistance means the insecticide no longer controls the pest effectively.

## Causes

* Repeated use of same mode of action
* Underdosing
* Frequent unnecessary sprays
* Poor coverage
* Not controlling pest escapes
* Using long-residual products repeatedly
* Spraying without threshold

## Prevention

* Rotate insecticide mode of action.
* Avoid repeated same active ingredient.
* Use IPM.
* Use biological control.
* Spray only when needed.
* Use correct dose.
* Ensure good coverage.
* Preserve natural enemies.
* Avoid calendar spraying.

---

# Mode of Action Rotation

Insecticides are grouped by mode of action.

Farmers should rotate between different mode-of-action groups where recommended.

Do not simply change brand name.

Many brands may contain the same active ingredient or same mode of action.

Ask for active ingredient and IRAC group where available.

# Safe Use in Tomato

Tomato is attacked by pests such as:

* Fruit borer
* Whitefly
* Thrips
* Aphids
* Leaf miner
* Mites

Safe tomato insecticide use:

* Use pheromone traps for fruit borer.
* Use yellow sticky traps for whitefly and aphids.
* Use blue sticky traps for thrips.
* Remove damaged fruits.
* Avoid unnecessary sprays during flowering.
* Follow waiting period strictly.
* Rotate insecticide mode of action.
* Avoid spraying close to harvest.
* Use biopesticides and neem products where suitable.
* Use crop-specific label recommendations.

Tomato is harvested frequently, so waiting period is very important.

---

# Safe Use in Chilli

Chilli pests include:

* Thrips
* Mites
* Aphids
* Fruit borer
* Whitefly

Safe chilli insecticide use:

* Monitor young leaves and flowers.
* Avoid overdose because chilli can be sensitive.
* Follow waiting period.
* Avoid spraying during hot afternoon.
* Use recommended miticides for mites, not random insecticides.
* Rotate insecticide groups.
* Use sticky traps and sanitation.

---

# Safe Use in Cotton

Cotton pests include:

* Jassids
* Aphids
* Whiteflies
* Thrips
* Bollworms
* Mealybugs

Safe cotton insecticide use:

* Follow economic threshold levels.
* Conserve natural enemies.
* Use pheromone traps for bollworms.
* Avoid repeated pyrethroid use.
* Rotate modes of action.
* Use selective products where possible.
* Follow resistance management strategy.

---

# Safe Use in Rice

Rice pests include:

* Stem borer
* Leaf folder
* Brown planthopper
* Green leafhopper
* Gall midge

Safe rice insecticide use:

* Monitor pest level before spraying.
* Avoid unnecessary broad-spectrum sprays.
* Maintain recommended plant spacing.
* Avoid excess nitrogen because it can increase some pests.
* Avoid spraying when natural enemies are abundant and pest level is low.
* Follow water management recommendations.

---

# Safe Use in Coconut

Coconut pests include:

* Rhinoceros beetle
* Red palm weevil
* Black-headed caterpillar
* Eriophyid mite
* Scale insects

Safe coconut insecticide use:

* Use mechanical and biological methods where possible.
* Use pheromone traps for rhinoceros beetle or red palm weevil where recommended.
* Avoid unsafe climbing and spraying.
* Use trained climbers or proper equipment.
* Avoid contamination of coconut water and nearby livestock areas.
* Follow expert guidance for crown treatment.

---

# Safe Use in Stored Grains

Stored grain insecticides and fumigants require special care.

## Important Warning

Fumigants are highly dangerous and should only be used by trained and authorized persons according to label and legal rules.

For stored grains:

* Dry grain properly.
* Clean storage area.
* Use airtight storage where possible.
* Use recommended grain protectants only where approved.
* Never mix unknown pesticide directly with food grain.
* Follow waiting period and safety rules.
* Keep treated grain away from children and animals.

# Insecticides and Food Safety

Food safety is very important for market acceptance.

Farmers should:

* Use only crop-approved insecticides.
* Follow dose.
* Follow waiting period.
* Avoid repeated unnecessary sprays.
* Keep spray records.
* Avoid banned or restricted pesticides.
* Follow export or buyer residue standards where applicable.
* Wash and handle produce properly after harvest.

Farmers are advised to:

1. Use insecticides only when pest monitoring shows need.
2. Select insecticide based on crop, pest, label, and expert recommendation.
3. Use only registered and approved products.
4. Read and follow label instructions every time.
5. Wear protective equipment during mixing and spraying.
