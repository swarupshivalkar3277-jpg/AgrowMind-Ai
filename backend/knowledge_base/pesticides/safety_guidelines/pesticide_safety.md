# Pesticide Safety Guidelines

## Overview

Pesticide safety means handling, storing, mixing, applying, and disposing of pesticides in a way that protects farmers, workers, consumers, animals, beneficial organisms, soil, water, and the environment.

Pesticides include insecticides, fungicides, herbicides, acaricides, nematicides, rodenticides, bactericides, seed treatment chemicals, fumigants, and plant protection products. Although pesticides can protect crops from pests, diseases, and weeds, unsafe use can cause poisoning, crop injury, environmental contamination, pesticide residues, resistance, and harm to beneficial organisms.

The most important pesticide safety rule is: **always read and follow the product label**.

The product label provides crop approval, target pest, dose, dilution, application method, personal protective equipment, waiting period, re-entry interval, first aid, storage, disposal, and emergency instructions.

---

## Simple Definition

Pesticide safety is the correct and responsible use of pesticides to control pests while reducing risk to humans, crops, animals, food, water, soil, and the environment.

---

## Core Safety Principles

1. Use pesticides only when needed.
2. Identify the pest correctly before selecting a pesticide.
3. Use only pesticides approved for the crop and pest.
4. Read and follow the pesticide label.
5. Wear proper personal protective equipment.
6. Use the recommended dose only.
7. Avoid unnecessary pesticide mixtures.
8. Avoid spraying during unsafe weather.
9. Prevent pesticide drift.
10. Protect water sources, pollinators, livestock, and beneficial insects.
11. Observe re-entry interval and pre-harvest interval.
12. Store pesticides safely.
13. Dispose empty containers correctly.
14. Keep pesticide records.
15. Seek medical help immediately in case of poisoning.

# Important Pesticide Label Terms

## Active Ingredient

The active ingredient is the chemical or biological component responsible for controlling the pest.

Example:

* Imidacloprid
* Mancozeb
* Glyphosate
* Chlorantraniliprole
* Azadirachtin
* Copper oxychloride

## Formulation

Formulation describes the physical form of the pesticide product.

Common formulations:

* EC: Emulsifiable concentrate
* SC: Suspension concentrate
* SL: Soluble liquid
* WP: Wettable powder
* WG / WDG: Water-dispersible granule
* GR: Granule
* DP: Dusting powder
* FS: Flowable seed treatment
* CS: Capsule suspension

## Dose

Dose is the recommended quantity of pesticide product or active ingredient to use per acre, hectare, litre of water, or spray tank.

Using less than the recommended dose can cause poor control and resistance. Using more than the recommended dose can cause crop injury, residues, poisoning, and environmental harm.

## Target Pest

The target pest is the pest, disease, or weed for which the pesticide is approved.

Do not use a pesticide on pests not mentioned on the label unless recommended by a local agricultural authority.

## Crop Approval

A pesticide must be approved for the crop being treated.

A pesticide safe in one crop may damage another crop or create illegal residues.

## Pre-Harvest Interval

Pre-Harvest Interval, also called PHI or waiting period, is the minimum time required between the last pesticide application and harvest.

PHI protects consumers from unsafe pesticide residues.

## Re-Entry Interval

Re-Entry Interval, also called REI, is the minimum time workers must wait before entering a treated field without protective equipment.

REI protects workers from exposure to fresh pesticide residues.

## Restricted Entry

Some pesticides require workers to stay out of the treated area for a specific period. Entering too soon can cause skin, eye, breathing, or poisoning risks.

## Signal Words

Some labels use signal words to indicate toxicity level.

Common signal words may include:

* Danger
* Warning
* Caution

Signal words vary by country and regulation. Always follow local label classification.

# Before Buying Pesticides

## Safe Purchase Checklist

Before buying a pesticide:

* Identify the crop.
* Identify the pest, disease, or weed.
* Confirm pest severity.
* Check whether pesticide use is necessary.
* Prefer IPM and non-chemical methods when suitable.
* Buy only from licensed or trusted dealers.
* Check product registration.
* Check crop approval.
* Check expiry date.
* Check seal and packaging condition.
* Check batch number.
* Check label language and instructions.
* Avoid unlabelled, leaking, fake, expired, or repacked products.
* Avoid buying pesticides in food or drink bottles.
* Buy only the quantity needed.

## Avoid These Products

Do not buy:

* Unlabelled pesticide bottles
* Pesticides without batch number
* Expired pesticides
* Leaking containers
* Repacked pesticides
* Products sold in soft drink or water bottles
* Products without proper invoice
* Products not approved for the crop
* Products banned or restricted by local law
* Products recommended only by word-of-mouth without label support

# Pesticide Transport Safety

## Transport Guidelines

When transporting pesticides:

* Keep pesticides in original containers.
* Keep containers upright and tightly closed.
* Do not transport pesticides with food, feed, seed, clothes, medicines, or animals.
* Keep pesticides away from passengers.
* Protect containers from heat and direct sunlight.
* Prevent puncture, breakage, or leakage.
* Use a separate box or crate.
* Secure containers to prevent falling.
* Carry absorbent material for small spills where possible.

## If Leakage Occurs During Transport

* Stop safely.
* Wear gloves and protection if available.
* Keep people and animals away.
* Avoid touching spilled pesticide.
* Prevent pesticide from entering drains or water bodies.
* Absorb liquid spill with soil, sand, sawdust, or absorbent material.
* Collect contaminated material safely.
* Contact local pesticide authority, dealer, or emergency service if spill is large.

# Pesticide Storage Safety

## Storage Location

Pesticides should be stored:

* In a locked storage area.
* Away from children.
* Away from animals and livestock.
* Away from food, feed, seed, fertilizer, and medicines.
* Away from drinking water sources.
* Away from direct sunlight.
* Away from heat and fire.
* In a dry, cool, and well-ventilated area.
* On shelves or pallets, not directly on the floor.
* In original containers with labels intact.

## Storage Room Features

A good pesticide storage area should have:

* Lockable door
* Warning sign
* Ventilation
* Dry floor
* Spill containment
* Fire safety precautions
* Separate storage from household items
* Separate storage from animal feed
* Emergency contact numbers
* Basic spill kit
* Measuring tools stored safely
* Inventory record

## Storage Mistakes to Avoid

Never:

* Store pesticides inside the house.
* Store pesticides near kitchen items.
* Store pesticides near animal feed.
* Store pesticides in drink bottles.
* Store pesticides without labels.
* Store leaking containers.
* Store pesticides where children can reach.
* Store pesticides near wells or ponds.
* Store pesticides under direct sunlight.
* Store pesticides with seeds unless label allows.
* Store herbicides near sprayers used for sensitive crops without proper cleaning.

# Personal Protective Equipment

Personal Protective Equipment, or PPE, protects pesticide handlers from exposure through skin, eyes, mouth, nose, and lungs.

The PPE required depends on the pesticide product, toxicity, formulation, application method, and exposure risk. Always follow the label.

## Common PPE Items

* Chemical-resistant gloves
* Long-sleeved shirt
* Long pants
* Closed shoes or boots
* Apron for mixing and loading
* Face shield or goggles
* Mask or respirator when required
* Hat or head cover
* Waterproof clothing for high-exposure tasks

## PPE for Mixing and Loading

Mixing and loading are high-risk activities because concentrated pesticide is handled.

Recommended PPE usually includes:

* Chemical-resistant gloves
* Eye protection
* Long sleeves
* Long pants
* Boots
* Apron
* Mask or respirator if label requires

## PPE for Spraying

During spraying, PPE may include:

* Gloves
* Long-sleeved clothing
* Long pants
* Boots
* Eye protection
* Mask or respirator if needed
* Head protection if overhead spraying

## PPE for Cleaning Sprayers

Sprayer cleaning can expose workers to residues.

Wear:

* Gloves
* Boots
* Eye protection
* Apron
* Long-sleeved clothing

## PPE Care

After use:

* Wash reusable gloves and boots before removing.
* Remove PPE carefully.
* Wash hands and face immediately.
* Wash contaminated clothing separately.
* Dry PPE away from children and animals.
* Store clean PPE separately from pesticides.
* Replace damaged gloves, masks, filters, goggles, or clothing.

## PPE Mistakes to Avoid

Do not:

* Spray barefoot.
* Mix pesticides with bare hands.
* Use torn gloves.
* Use cloth masks when a respirator is required.
* Reuse contaminated clothing without washing.
* Wash pesticide clothing with family laundry.
* Store clean PPE inside pesticide-contaminated areas.
* Eat, drink, smoke, or chew tobacco while handling pesticides.

# Routes of Pesticide Exposure

Pesticides can enter the body through four main routes.

## 1. Skin Exposure

Skin exposure is one of the most common routes. It can occur while mixing, spraying, cleaning equipment, touching treated plants, or handling contaminated clothing.

High-risk body areas:

* Hands
* Forearms
* Face
* Neck
* Scalp
* Feet
* Groin area

## 2. Eye Exposure

Eyes absorb pesticides quickly and can be damaged by splashes, drift, dust, or vapour.

## 3. Inhalation Exposure

Inhalation occurs when a person breathes pesticide mist, dust, vapour, smoke, or fumes.

Risk increases with:

* Fine droplets
* Fogging
* Dust formulations
* Fumigants
* Spraying in closed spaces
* Spraying against wind
* Poor mask or respirator use

## 4. Oral Exposure

Oral exposure can occur when pesticides are swallowed accidentally.

Common causes:

* Eating while spraying
* Drinking while spraying
* Smoking while spraying
* Storing pesticides in drink bottles
* Using mouth to clear blocked nozzles
* Contaminated hands touching food
* Children accessing pesticide containers

# Mixing and Loading Safety

## Before Mixing

* Read label instructions.
* Wear PPE.
* Use clean water.
* Use clean measuring tools.
* Mix in a well-ventilated area.
* Keep children and animals away.
* Prepare only required quantity.
* Check compatibility before tank mixing.
* Do not mix near wells, ponds, canals, or drains.

## Mixing Order

Follow the product label. If no specific label instruction is available and tank mixing is allowed, general mixing order may follow:

1. Water-soluble packets
2. Wettable powders
3. Water-dispersible granules
4. Suspension concentrates
5. Soluble liquids
6. Emulsifiable concentrates
7. Oils or adjuvants

This order can vary by product. Always follow label guidance.

## Tank Mixing Safety

Do not tank mix pesticides unless the label or local expert recommendation supports it.

Before tank mixing:

* Check crop safety.
* Check physical compatibility.
* Check biological compatibility.
* Check water pH and hardness if relevant.
* Do a jar test.
* Avoid mixing strongly acidic and alkaline products.
* Avoid mixing pesticides randomly.
* Avoid mixing herbicides with insecticides or fungicides unless recommended.
* Avoid mixing pesticides with foliar fertilizers unless recommended.

## Mixing Mistakes to Avoid

Never:

* Mix with bare hands.
* Smell pesticide directly.
* Use household utensils.
* Use drink bottles for measuring.
* Pour pesticide from height causing splash.
* Prepare spray solution near drinking water.
* Use mouth to siphon pesticide.
* Use overdose.
* Store leftover mixed spray solution for long periods.
* Leave open pesticide containers unattended.

# Sprayer Calibration

Sprayer calibration ensures the correct amount of pesticide is applied over the correct area.

## Why Calibration Is Important

Poor calibration can cause:

* Overdose
* Underdose
* Poor pest control
* Crop injury
* Residue problems
* Wasted pesticide
* Environmental contamination
* Resistance development

## Calibration Factors

Calibration depends on:

* Nozzle type
* Nozzle output
* Sprayer pressure
* Walking speed
* Tractor speed
* Spray volume
* Boom height
* Swath width
* Tank capacity
* Target crop canopy

## Calibration Checklist

Before spraying:

* Check nozzle condition.
* Replace worn nozzles.
* Check for leaks.
* Measure sprayer output.
* Maintain uniform pressure.
* Maintain uniform walking speed.
* Confirm tank volume.
* Calculate area covered per tank.
* Calculate pesticide quantity per tank.
* Use correct spray volume.

# Weather Safety for Spraying

Weather strongly affects pesticide safety and performance.

## Avoid Spraying During

* Strong wind
* Rain
* Rain expected soon
* Very high temperature
* Very low temperature
* Drought stress
* Waterlogged conditions
* Foggy inversion conditions
* Stormy weather
* Midday heat
* When bees are actively foraging
* When drift may reach people, animals, water, or sensitive crops

## Best Time to Spray

In many situations, safer spraying times are:

* Early morning
* Late evening
* Calm weather
* Mild temperature
* No immediate rain forecast
* Low wind speed
* Crop not under stress

## Wind and Drift

Spray drift occurs when pesticide droplets move away from the target area.

Drift can harm:

* Nearby crops
* Vegetables
* Orchards
* Flowers
* Water bodies
* Livestock
* People
* Pollinators
* Fish
* Natural enemies

## Drift Reduction Practices

* Use correct nozzle.
* Avoid high pressure.
* Avoid very fine droplets when drift risk is high.
* Keep boom height low and correct.
* Do not spray during wind.
* Use drift-reducing nozzles where suitable.
* Maintain buffer zones.
* Avoid spraying near sensitive crops.
* Avoid aerial drift-prone methods unless professionally managed.
* Spray with wind direction away from sensitive areas.

## Before Spraying

* Confirm pest identity.
* Confirm pesticide selection.
* Check crop approval.
* Read label.
* Wear PPE.
* Calibrate sprayer.
* Check weather.
* Inform nearby workers if needed.

## During Spraying

* Avoid overlapping spray passes.

# Re-Entry Interval

Re-entry interval is the waiting time after pesticide application before workers can enter the treated field without special protective equipment.

## Why REI Matters

Fresh pesticide residues can remain on leaves, soil, irrigation water, trellis wires, plastic mulch, and crop surfaces. Workers entering too early can be exposed through skin, eyes, or inhalation.

## REI Safety Rules

* Follow label REI.
* Do not allow children or animals into the field.
* Do not harvest during REI.
* Do not weed, prune, tie, or irrigate manually during REI unless label allows with PPE.
* Put warning signs where required.
* Inform workers about treated fields.

---

# Pre-Harvest Interval

Pre-harvest interval is the minimum number of days between the last pesticide spray and harvest.

## Why PHI Matters

PHI helps ensure pesticide residues decline to legally acceptable levels before food is harvested.

## PHI Safety Rules

* Follow label PHI.
* Do not harvest early.
* Keep spray records.
* Do not apply pesticides close to harvest unless label permits.
* Use low-residue or biological options when harvest is near.
* Follow export residue standards if crop is for export.

---

# Pesticide Residue Safety

Pesticide residues are small amounts of pesticide remaining on or in crops after application.

## Causes of Unsafe Residues

* Overdose
* Too many sprays
* Spraying too close to harvest
* Using pesticide not approved for crop
* Wrong formulation
* Poor washing or processing
* Contaminated irrigation water
* Drift from nearby fields
* Mixing incompatible pesticides
* Ignoring PHI

## Residue Reduction Practices

* Follow label dose.
* Follow PHI.
* Avoid unnecessary sprays.
* Rotate pesticides.
* Use IPM.
* Use biological or botanical products near harvest where suitable.
* Wash produce before consumption.
* Keep spray records.
* Avoid banned or unregistered pesticides.

# Pollinator Safety

Pollinators include bees, butterflies, flies, moths, and other insects that help crop pollination.

## Pollinator Protection Practices

* Avoid spraying during flowering.
* Avoid spraying when bees are active.
* Spray late evening if pesticide use is unavoidable.
* Avoid drift to flowering weeds and nearby flowering crops.
* Use bee-safe options where possible.
* Inform nearby beekeepers before spraying.
* Remove flowering weeds before spraying if safe and practical.
* Avoid dust formulations near flowering crops.
* Follow label bee warnings.

# Pesticide Spill Management

## Small Spill Response

If a small spill occurs:

1. Keep people and animals away.
2. Wear PPE.
3. Stop the leak if safe.
4. Prevent spread.
5. Prevent entry into water, drains, or soil cracks.
6. Absorb liquid with sand, soil, sawdust, or absorbent material.
7. Sweep contaminated material into a labelled container.
8. Dispose according to local rules.
9. Wash contaminated surface if label or authority allows.
10. Report large or dangerous spills.

## Spill Kit Items

A pesticide spill kit may include:

* Chemical-resistant gloves
* Goggles or face shield
* Apron
* Boots
* Respirator if needed
* Absorbent material
* Sand or soil
* Broom
* Dustpan
* Plastic bags or containers
* Warning tape
* Soap
* Clean water
* Emergency contact numbers

## Large Spill Response

For large spills:

* Do not attempt cleanup without training.
* Keep people away.
* Call emergency services or local pesticide authority.
* Prevent water contamination if safe.
* Follow official instructions.

# First Aid for Pesticide Exposure

## General First Aid Rules

If pesticide exposure occurs:

* Stop exposure immediately.
* Move the person away from contaminated area.
* Call medical help or poison control.
* Keep pesticide label or container with the patient.
* Do not give anything by mouth to an unconscious person.
* Do not induce vomiting unless label or medical authority instructs.
* Remove contaminated clothing.
* Wash exposed skin with clean water and soap.
* Flush eyes with clean water if exposed.
* Protect yourself while helping the victim.

## Skin Exposure

If pesticide contacts skin:

1. Remove contaminated clothing immediately.
2. Wash skin with plenty of clean water.
3. Use soap if available.
4. Wash hair and under nails if contaminated.
5. Continue washing for at least 15 minutes when significant exposure occurs.
6. Seek medical help if irritation, burns, dizziness, vomiting, breathing difficulty, or weakness occurs.

## Eye Exposure

If pesticide enters the eye:

1. Hold eyelids open.
2. Rinse gently with clean running water.
3. Continue rinsing for at least 15 minutes.
4. Do not add chemicals or medicines to wash water.
5. Seek medical help immediately.

## Inhalation Exposure

If pesticide is inhaled:

1. Move the person to fresh air.
2. Loosen tight clothing.
3. Keep the person calm and warm.
4. If breathing is difficult, seek emergency medical care.
5. If trained, provide rescue breathing or CPR if needed.
6. Avoid exposing yourself to fumes.

## Swallowing Exposure

If pesticide is swallowed:

1. Call medical help or poison control immediately.
2. Read label first-aid instructions.
3. Do not induce vomiting unless instructed.
4. Do not give milk, alcohol, oil, or home remedies unless medical authority advises.
5. Do not give anything by mouth if the person is unconscious, drowsy, or convulsing.
6. Take the pesticide label or container to the doctor.

Symptoms depend on pesticide type, dose, route of exposure, and person’s health.

## Mild Symptoms

* Headache
* Dizziness
* Weakness
* Sweating
* Nausea
* Eye irritation
* Skin irritation
* Throat irritation
* Coughing

## Moderate Symptoms

* Vomiting
* Stomach pain
* Blurred vision
* Excess salivation
* Muscle twitching

# Empty Container Disposal

## General Rules

* Follow label instructions.
* Do not reuse pesticide containers.
* Do not use containers for drinking water, food, fuel, milk, oil, or animal feed.
* Triple-rinse containers if label recommends.
* Add rinse water to spray tank if allowed.
* Puncture or crush containers to prevent reuse.
* Return containers to authorized collection centers where available.
* Dispose according to local regulations.
* Do not burn containers unless approved by local authority.
* Do not throw containers into fields, canals, ponds, wells, or roadside areas.

## Triple Rinsing

Triple rinsing means washing the empty container three times and adding the rinse water to the spray tank when allowed by label.

General process:

1. Empty container fully into spray tank.
2. Add clean water to about one-fourth of container.
3. Close and shake.
4. Pour rinse water into spray tank.
5. Repeat three times.
6. Puncture container after rinsing.
7. Dispose safely.

# Resistance Management Safety

## Resistance Prevention

* Rotate pesticide mode of action groups.
* Avoid repeated use of the same active ingredient.
* Use recommended dose.
* Avoid underdosing.
* Spray only when needed.
* Use mixtures only when recommended.
* Control surviving pests.
* Combine chemical and non-chemical methods.
* Keep records.

Safety rules:

* Keep children away from pesticides.
* Do not allow children in treated fields during REI.
* Do not store pesticides at home where children can reach.
* Do not reuse pesticide containers.

Pregnant women should avoid pesticide handling and exposure whenever possible.

# Food and Household Safety

## Never Do These

* Do not store pesticides in the kitchen.
* Do not store pesticides in food containers.
* Do not use pesticide containers for household storage.
* Do not wash pesticide equipment near drinking water.
* Do not keep pesticides near grains.
* Do not dry pesticide-contaminated clothes near food.
* Do not eat or smoke while spraying.
* Do not bring contaminated clothes into living areas without washing.

# Common Pesticide Safety Mistakes

## Mistake 1: Not Reading the Label

The label contains critical instructions for safe and legal use.

## Mistake 2: Spraying Without PPE

Many pesticide exposures happen through skin and eyes.

## Mistake 3: Using Overdose

Overdose can cause poisoning, residues, crop injury, and environmental harm.

## Mistake 4: Spraying in Wind

Wind causes drift and exposes people, animals, water bodies, and nearby crops.

## Mistake 5: Harvesting Too Soon

Ignoring PHI can lead to unsafe residues.

## Mistake 6: Entering Field Too Early

Ignoring REI can expose workers to fresh residues.

## Mistake 7: Reusing Containers

Pesticide containers should never be reused for food, water, fuel, or household use.

## Mistake 8: Clearing Nozzle by Mouth

This can cause poisoning. Use a brush or clean water.

## Mistake 9: Random Tank Mixing

Unapproved mixtures can cause crop injury, poor control, toxicity, residues, or sprayer blockage.

## Mistake 10: Poor Storage

Unsafe storage can poison children, animals, or contaminate food and water.

# Emergency Response Templates

## Skin Exposure Template

If pesticide contacts your skin, remove contaminated clothing immediately and wash the skin with plenty of clean water and soap. Continue washing for at least 15 minutes if exposure is significant. Do not reuse contaminated clothing until washed separately. Seek medical help if irritation, vomiting, dizziness, weakness, breathing difficulty, or burning occurs.

## Eye Exposure Template

If pesticide enters your eye, hold the eyelid open and rinse gently with clean running water for at least 15 minutes. Do not add chemicals or medicines to the water. Seek medical help immediately and take the pesticide label or container.

## Inhalation Exposure Template

Move the person to fresh air immediately. Loosen tight clothing and keep the person calm. If breathing is difficult, call emergency medical help. Do not enter a contaminated area without protection.

## Swallowing Exposure Template

If pesticide is swallowed, call medical help or poison control immediately. Do not induce vomiting unless the label or a medical professional instructs you. Do not give anything by mouth to an unconscious person. Take the pesticide label or container to the doctor.

# RAG-Friendly Knowledge Chunks

## Chunk 1: What is pesticide safety?

Pesticide safety means using, handling, storing, applying, and disposing of pesticides in a way that protects people, crops, animals, food, water, soil, beneficial organisms, and the environment.

## Chunk 2: What is the most important pesticide safety rule?

The most important rule is to read and follow the pesticide label. The label gives instructions for crop use, dose, PPE, application, re-entry interval, pre-harvest interval, first aid, storage, and disposal.

## Chunk 3: Why is PPE important when using pesticides?

PPE protects pesticide handlers from exposure through skin, eyes, mouth, nose, and lungs. Gloves, long clothing, boots, goggles, aprons, and respirators may be required depending on the pesticide label.

## Chunk 4: What should a farmer do before spraying pesticide?

Before spraying, the farmer should identify the pest, confirm crop approval, read the label, wear PPE, calibrate the sprayer, check weather, remove people and animals from the area, and prepare only the required spray solution.

## Chunk 5: When should pesticides not be sprayed?

Pesticides should not be sprayed during strong wind, rain, extreme heat, drought stress, waterlogging, active bee foraging, or when drift may reach people, animals, water bodies, or sensitive crops.

## Chunk 6: What is re-entry interval?

Re-entry interval is the minimum time after pesticide application before workers can enter the treated field without special protective equipment.

## Chunk 7: What is pre-harvest interval?

Pre-harvest interval is the minimum waiting time between the last pesticide spray and harvest. It helps prevent unsafe pesticide residues in food.

## Chunk 8: What should be done if pesticide contacts skin?

Remove contaminated clothing immediately and wash the skin with clean water and soap. Continue washing for at least 15 minutes if exposure is significant. Seek medical help if symptoms occur.

## Chunk 9: What should be done if pesticide enters the eye?

Hold the eyelid open and rinse the eye gently with clean running water for at least 15 minutes. Seek medical help immediately and take the pesticide label or container.

## Chunk 10: Can pesticide containers be reused?

No. Empty pesticide containers should never be reused for food, water, milk, fuel, oil, animal feed, or household storage. They should be rinsed and disposed of according to label and local regulations.

## Chunk 11: Why is pesticide overdose dangerous?

Overdose can cause crop injury, unsafe residues, human poisoning, environmental contamination, beneficial insect harm, and increased cost.

## Chunk 12: Why should sprayer nozzles not be cleaned by mouth?

Cleaning nozzles by mouth can cause pesticide poisoning through oral exposure. Use a brush, soft tool, or clean water instead.

## Chunk 13: Why should pesticides be stored safely?

Safe storage prevents poisoning of children, animals, and workers. Pesticides should be locked away, kept in original containers, and stored away from food, feed, seed, medicine, water, and household items.

## Chunk 14: How can pesticide drift be reduced?

Drift can be reduced by avoiding windy weather, using correct nozzles, avoiding high pressure, keeping boom height correct, using larger droplets when suitable, maintaining buffer zones, and avoiding spraying near sensitive areas.

## Chunk 15: What should be done with leftover spray solution?

Prepare only the required amount. Leftover spray should not be poured into drains, wells, ponds, canals, or soil. If allowed by label, it may be applied to the labelled target area without exceeding the recommended dose.

# Frequently Asked Questions

## What is pesticide safety?

Pesticide safety is the responsible use of pesticides to control pests while protecting humans, animals, crops, food, water, soil, beneficial insects, and the environment.

## Why should farmers read the pesticide label?

The label gives legal and technical instructions for safe use, including dose, crop, target pest, PPE, PHI, REI, first aid, storage, and disposal.

## What PPE is needed for pesticide spraying?

Common PPE includes gloves, long-sleeved clothing, long pants, boots, goggles or face shield, apron during mixing, and respirator if required by the label.

## Can pesticides be mixed by hand?

No. Pesticides should not be mixed with bare hands. Use proper measuring tools and wear gloves and protective equipment.

## Can I spray pesticide in the afternoon?

Avoid spraying during hot afternoon sunlight because heat can increase evaporation, drift, crop injury, and worker stress. Early morning or late evening is often safer, depending on label and weather.

## Can I spray pesticide before rain?

Avoid spraying before rain unless the label allows it. Rain can wash pesticide away, reduce control, increase runoff, and contaminate water.

## What should I do if pesticide falls on my skin?

Remove contaminated clothing and wash the skin with clean water and soap. Seek medical help if symptoms develop.

## What should I do if pesticide goes into my eye?

Rinse the eye gently with clean running water for at least 15 minutes and seek medical help immediately.

## What should I do if pesticide is swallowed?

Call medical help or poison control immediately. Do not induce vomiting unless instructed by the label or medical professional. Take the pesticide label or container to the doctor.

## Can empty pesticide bottles be reused?

No. Empty pesticide containers must not be reused for water, food, milk, fuel, oil, or storage. Dispose them safely according to label and local rules.

## What is PHI?

PHI means Pre-Harvest Interval. It is the minimum number of days required between the last pesticide spray and crop harvest.

## What is REI?

REI means Re-Entry Interval. It is the minimum waiting time before workers can safely enter a treated field without special protective equipment.

## Why is pesticide record keeping important?

Records help farmers track pesticide use, avoid residue problems, manage resistance, identify crop injury causes, and follow market or export requirements.

# Safety Disclaimer

Pesticide safety instructions vary by product, formulation, crop, country, regulation, toxicity class, and application method. Always follow the product label and local agricultural authority guidance. In case of pesticide poisoning, serious exposure, eye contamination, breathing difficulty, swallowing, unconsciousness, or convulsions, seek emergency medical help immediately and take the pesticide label or container to the doctor.
