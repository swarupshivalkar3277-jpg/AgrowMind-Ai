# PPE Usage for Pesticide Safety

# Overview

Personal Protective Equipment, commonly called PPE, is protective clothing and equipment worn by farmers, spray workers, pesticide handlers, and agricultural laborers to reduce pesticide exposure during pesticide mixing, loading, spraying, cleaning, storage, transport, and disposal.

PPE is important because pesticides can enter the human body through:

* Skin contact
* Eye contact
* Inhalation
* Accidental swallowing
* Contaminated clothing
* Contaminated hands
* Spray drift
* Leaking equipment

Correct PPE use helps protect farmers from pesticide poisoning, skin irritation, eye injury, breathing problems, chemical burns, long-term health risks, and accidental exposure.

PPE should be used whenever handling:

* Insecticides
* Fungicides
* Herbicides
* Miticides
* Nematicides
* Rodenticides
* Seed treatment chemicals
* Fumigants
* Plant growth regulators
* Tank mixtures
* Concentrated pesticide formulations

PPE does not make unsafe pesticide use safe. Farmers must still follow label instructions, use correct dose, avoid unnecessary sprays, and follow Integrated Pest Management.

---

# What is PPE?

PPE means protective equipment used to reduce exposure to pesticides and other agricultural chemicals.

PPE may include:

* Chemical-resistant gloves
* Face mask
* Respirator
* Goggles
* Face shield
* Long-sleeved shirt
* Long trousers
* Coverall
* Apron
* Boots
* Head cover
* Waterproof clothing
* Chemical-resistant suit where required

The correct PPE depends on pesticide label, formulation, toxicity, application method, exposure risk, crop height, weather, and work activity.

---

# Why PPE is Important

Pesticides can be useful for crop protection, but they can harm humans if handled carelessly.

PPE helps reduce pesticide exposure during:

* Opening pesticide containers
* Measuring pesticide
* Mixing concentrated pesticide
* Loading spray tank
* Spraying crop
* Cleaning blocked nozzles
* Washing sprayer
* Handling treated seeds
* Applying granules
* Entering treated fields
* Disposing empty containers
* Managing pesticide spills

PPE is especially important during mixing and loading because concentrated pesticide is handled at this stage.

FAO guidance explains that concentrated pesticide formulations can present greater hazard because pesticide is present at the highest concentration before dilution.

---

# Basic PPE Set for Farmers

A basic PPE set for pesticide spraying should include:

* Long-sleeved shirt
* Long trousers
* Chemical-resistant gloves
* Closed shoes or rubber boots
* Face mask or respirator as required
* Goggles or eye protection
* Head cover or hat
* Apron during mixing
* Clean water and soap nearby

For highly toxic pesticides, fumigants, or specialized applications, additional PPE may be required according to label and expert guidance.

---

# PPE is Label-Dependent

The pesticide label is the main guide for PPE.

The label may mention:

* Gloves required
* Respirator required
* Eye protection required
* Protective clothing required
* Apron required
* Boots required
* Re-entry interval
* Waiting period
* First aid
* Toxicity category
* Antidote
* Handling precautions

Farmers should not ignore PPE instructions on the label.

If label says specific PPE is required, use it.

FAO pesticide registration guidance notes that some pesticides are registered with the condition that specific PPE must be used, and the protection depends on correct equipment quality, maintenance, and use.

---

# Main Routes of Pesticide Exposure

---

# Skin Exposure

Skin exposure is the most common route of pesticide exposure.

It may happen when:

* Pesticide splashes during mixing
* Spray mist contacts skin
* Leaking sprayer wets clothing
* Contaminated gloves touch skin
* Worker walks through sprayed crop
* Clothes absorb pesticide
* Pesticide spills on hands or feet

Skin protection is very important.

---

# Eye Exposure

Eye exposure may happen from:

* Splashing during mixing
* Spray drift
* Nozzle burst
* Wind-blown spray
* Rubbing eyes with contaminated hands
* Leaking equipment

Eye exposure can cause irritation, pain, watering, burning, blurred vision, or serious injury.

---

# Inhalation Exposure

Inhalation means breathing pesticide into the body.

It may happen when:

* Mixing powders
* Spraying mist
* Using fumigants
* Spraying in closed spaces
* Spraying in greenhouse or polyhouse
* Standing downwind
* Cleaning clogged nozzles by blowing

Never blow blocked nozzles by mouth.

---

# Oral Exposure

Oral exposure may happen when:

* Eating during spraying
* Drinking during spraying
* Smoking during spraying
* Touching mouth with contaminated hands
* Storing pesticide in food or drink bottles
* Reusing pesticide containers
* Keeping pesticides near food

Oral exposure can be very dangerous.

---

# PPE for Different Activities

---

# PPE During Pesticide Purchase and Transport

When buying or transporting pesticides:

* Keep pesticide in original sealed container.
* Carry pesticide separately from food, feed, seed, and household items.
* Do not carry leaking containers.
* Keep container upright.
* Avoid carrying pesticide inside passenger area.
* Keep bill and label.
* Use gloves if handling damaged or leaking packaging.

---

# PPE During Mixing and Loading

Mixing and loading require high protection because concentrated pesticide is handled.

Recommended PPE:

* Chemical-resistant gloves
* Goggles or face shield
* Mask or respirator as label requires
* Long-sleeved clothing
* Long trousers
* Apron
* Rubber boots
* Head cover

Safety practices:

* Mix in open air.
* Stand upwind.
* Use clean water.
* Measure pesticide carefully.
* Avoid splashing.
* Keep children and animals away.
* Do not eat, drink, or smoke.
* Wash immediately if pesticide touches skin.

---

# PPE During Spraying

During spraying, use:

* Long-sleeved shirt
* Long trousers
* Gloves
* Boots or closed shoes
* Mask or respirator where required
* Goggles if drift or overhead spraying risk exists
* Head cover

Safety practices:

* Spray with wind direction.
* Avoid spray drift.
* Avoid spraying in strong wind.
* Avoid spraying during hot afternoon.
* Avoid walking into spray mist.
* Keep workers, animals, and children away.
* Stop spraying if feeling dizzy, weak, or nauseous.

---

# PPE During Herbicide Application

Herbicides can injure crops and may expose workers.

Use:

* Gloves
* Long clothing
* Boots
* Mask where label requires
* Eye protection during mixing
* Separate sprayer for herbicide where possible

Important:

* Avoid drift to neighboring crops.
* Wash herbicide sprayer carefully.
* Do not use herbicide-contaminated sprayer for vegetables or sensitive crops unless cleaned properly.

---

# PPE During Fungicide Application

Fungicides may be powders, liquids, granules, or suspensions.

Use:

* Gloves
* Mask, especially for powders
* Goggles during mixing
* Long clothing
* Boots
* Apron during mixing

Wettable powders and dust-like formulations can create inhalation exposure during mixing.

---

# PPE During Insecticide Application

Insecticides can be more acutely toxic than many other pesticide groups, depending on active ingredient.

Use:

* Gloves
* Mask or respirator as required
* Goggles during mixing
* Long clothing
* Boots
* Head cover
* Apron during mixing

Avoid spraying insecticides during flowering when bees are active unless label and expert guidance allow.

---

# PPE During Seed Treatment

Seed treatment chemicals are concentrated and can expose hands and lungs.

Use:

* Gloves
* Mask or respirator
* Goggles
* Apron
* Long clothing
* Boots

Safety:

* Treat seeds in a ventilated area.
* Do not use bare hands.
* Do not eat treated seed.
* Do not feed treated seed to animals.
* Label treated seed bags clearly.
* Keep away from children and livestock.

---

# PPE During Fumigation

Fumigants are extremely dangerous and should only be handled by trained and authorized persons.

Fumigation may require:

* Special respirator or breathing protection
* Gas detection equipment
* Sealed area management
* Warning signs
* Restricted entry
* Emergency plan
* Professional supervision

Farmers should not attempt fumigation without training and authorization.

---

# PPE During Sprayer Cleaning

After spraying, pesticide residues remain in tank, hose, nozzle, and filter.

Use:

* Gloves
* Boots
* Eye protection
* Long clothing
* Mask if strong smell or mist exists

Do not wash sprayer in ponds, canals, wells, or drinking water sources.

---

# PPE During Empty Container Disposal

Empty containers may still contain pesticide residues.

Use:

* Gloves
* Long clothing
* Boots
* Eye protection if rinsing containers

Do not reuse empty pesticide containers for water, food, oil, seed, feed, or household storage.

---

# Types of PPE

---

# Chemical-Resistant Gloves

Gloves protect hands from pesticide exposure.

## Suitable Materials

Common suitable glove materials may include:

* Nitrile
* Neoprene
* Butyl rubber
* Barrier laminate
* PVC for some uses

Always follow label recommendations.

## Avoid

Do not use:

* Cotton gloves
* Leather gloves
* Canvas gloves
* Wool gloves
* Torn gloves
* Thin household gloves if not chemical-resistant

Fabric and leather gloves can absorb pesticide and hold it against the skin.

Pesticide safety guidance commonly recommends unlined chemical-resistant gloves and warns against canvas, leather, cotton, or fabric gloves unless the pesticide label specifically allows them.

---

# Correct Glove Use

Use gloves correctly:

* Wear gloves before opening pesticide container.
* Check for holes or tears.
* Keep gloves outside sleeves when spraying downward.
* Keep sleeves over gloves when spraying overhead to prevent liquid entering gloves.
* Wash gloves before removing.
* Remove gloves last.
* Wash hands after removing gloves.

Do not touch face, phone, food, or water bottle with contaminated gloves.

---

# Masks and Respirators

Masks and respirators protect the breathing system.

## Cloth Mask

A cloth mask gives very limited pesticide protection and is not enough for many pesticide operations.

## Disposable Particulate Mask

May reduce dust exposure from powders but may not protect against vapors.

## Cartridge Respirator

May be needed for some pesticide vapors or higher-risk applications, depending on label.

## Important Notes

* Use the respirator type mentioned on label.
* Ensure proper fit.
* Replace cartridges as required.
* Do not use damaged respirator.
* Do not use pesticide respirator for household purposes.

---

# Goggles and Eye Protection

Goggles protect eyes from splashes and spray drift.

Use goggles when:

* Mixing pesticide
* Loading tank
* Spraying overhead
* Using power sprayer
* Spraying orchards
* Handling powders
* Cleaning nozzles
* Handling corrosive or irritating products

A face shield can protect face, but goggles may still be needed for full eye protection.

---

# Face Shield

A face shield protects face from splashes during mixing and loading.

Use face shield:

* When pouring concentrated pesticide
* When opening containers
* When mixing large quantities
* When handling highly irritating products

Face shield should not replace respirator if inhalation protection is required.

---

# Protective Clothing

Protective clothing reduces skin exposure.

Minimum clothing:

* Long-sleeved shirt
* Long trousers
* Closed footwear
* Head cover

Better protection:

* Coverall
* Chemical-resistant apron
* Waterproof clothing where needed

Avoid clothing that is torn, loose, or heavily contaminated.

---

# Apron

A chemical-resistant apron is very useful during mixing and loading.

It protects:

* Chest
* Stomach
* Thighs
* Clothing underneath

Use apron when handling concentrated pesticide.

---

# Boots

Boots protect feet from spills and spray.

Use:

* Rubber boots
* Chemical-resistant boots
* Closed footwear when boots are unavailable

Avoid:

* Barefoot spraying
* Open sandals
* Cloth shoes
* Absorbent footwear

Wear trousers outside boots when spraying downward so pesticide runs outside, not into boots.

---

# Head Cover

Head cover protects scalp and neck from spray drift.

Use:

* Wide-brim hat
* Cap with neck cover
* Head scarf used only for pesticide work
* Hood where available

Wash head cover after spraying.

---

# PPE Selection Guide

| Task                    | Minimum PPE                                                         |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Buying sealed pesticide | Avoid food contact, handle carefully                                |
| Transporting pesticide  | Keep separate from food and feed                                    |
| Mixing liquid pesticide | Gloves, goggles, apron, mask, long clothing, boots                  |
| Mixing powder pesticide | Gloves, goggles, mask or respirator, apron, long clothing, boots    |
| Spraying field crop     | Long clothing, gloves, boots, mask where required                   |
| Spraying orchard        | Long clothing, gloves, boots, goggles, respirator if label requires |
| Seed treatment          | Gloves, mask, goggles, apron                                        |
| Cleaning sprayer        | Gloves, boots, goggles                                              |
| Disposal of containers  | Gloves, boots, long clothing                                        |
| Fumigation              | Only trained professionals with special PPE                         |

---

# PPE Wearing Order

Before wearing PPE:

* Remove jewelry.
* Tie long hair.
* Wear clean inner clothing.
* Check PPE condition.
* Keep drinking water away from spray area.

## Suggested Wearing Order

1. Long trousers and long-sleeved shirt or coverall
2. Boots
3. Apron if mixing
4. Mask or respirator
5. Goggles or face shield
6. Head cover
7. Gloves last before handling pesticide

Make sure there are no exposed skin areas.

---

# PPE Removal Order

Removing PPE incorrectly can contaminate the body.

A safe removal order is:

1. Wash gloves and boots before removing if water is available.
2. Remove head cover.
3. Remove goggles or face shield.
4. Remove mask or respirator.
5. Remove apron.
6. Remove coverall or outer clothing.
7. Remove boots.
8. Remove gloves last.
9. Wash hands with soap.
10. Bathe as soon as possible.

FAO/WHO-related PPE guidance emphasizes careful doffing, including washing gloves and boots before removal, removing face/head protection and coveralls, removing boots, removing gloves last, and washing hands or showering after.

---

# PPE Cleaning

PPE must be cleaned after use.

## Gloves

* Wash outside of gloves before removing.
* Wash again after removing.
* Dry in shade.
* Check for holes.

## Goggles and Face Shield

* Wash with soap and clean water.
* Dry with clean cloth.
* Store safely to avoid scratches.

## Respirator

* Wipe facepiece.
* Replace filters or cartridges as recommended.
* Store in clean sealed bag or box.
* Do not wash cartridges unless manufacturer allows.

## Clothing

* Wash pesticide clothing separately.
* Do not mix with family clothes.
* Use soap and plenty of water.
* Dry in sunlight if suitable.
* Wear clean clothes next time.

## Boots

* Wash outside.
* Dry upside down if wet inside.
* Store away from children.

---

# PPE Storage

Store PPE properly.

Good storage:

* Clean and dry place
* Separate from pesticides
* Away from sunlight where material may degrade
* Away from children and animals
* Away from food and feed
* In a sealed bag or box for respirator
* In a dedicated pesticide safety kit

Do not store dirty PPE inside living rooms or kitchen.

---

# PPE Replacement

Replace PPE when damaged.

Replace gloves if:

* Torn
* Cracked
* Sticky
* Hard
* Swollen
* Leaking
* Chemical smell remains after washing

Replace mask or respirator if:

* Damaged
* Breathing becomes difficult
* Filters are expired
* Chemical smell is detected while wearing
* Straps are loose
* Face seal is poor

Replace clothing if:

* Torn
* Heavily contaminated
* Cannot be cleaned
* Chemical smell remains

---

# PPE for Hot Weather

Farmers may avoid PPE because of heat. However, pesticide exposure can be dangerous.

Heat-safe practices:

* Spray early morning or late evening.
* Take breaks in shade.
* Drink clean water before and after work.
* Avoid spraying during hot afternoon.
* Use breathable but protective clothing where possible.
* Do not remove gloves or mask during pesticide handling.
* Stop work if dizziness, weakness, or nausea occurs.

Heat stress and pesticide poisoning symptoms can look similar. Take symptoms seriously.

---

# PPE for Women Farmers

Women farmers should use PPE that fits properly.

Important points:

* Gloves should fit hand size.
* Clothing should not be loose near moving equipment.
* Mask should seal properly.
* Head cover should not block vision.
* Pregnant women should avoid pesticide handling where possible.
* Women should not wash heavily contaminated pesticide clothes with household laundry.

Women involved in nursery, vegetable harvesting, seed treatment, and spraying support should also use PPE.

---

# PPE for Hired Spray Workers

Farm owners should ensure spray workers have PPE.

Important responsibilities:

* Provide correct PPE.
* Explain pesticide label instructions.
* Provide clean water and soap.
* Avoid asking workers to spray in unsafe weather.
* Do not allow barefoot spraying.
* Provide medical help in case of poisoning.
* Keep children away from spraying work.
* Respect re-entry interval.

---

# PPE for Drone Spray Support

Drone spraying reduces direct operator exposure but does not remove all risk.

Workers still need PPE for:

* Mixing pesticide
* Filling drone tank
* Handling concentrated pesticide
* Cleaning drone tank
* Managing spills
* Entering sprayed area

Drone operators should follow legal, technical, and safety requirements.

---

# PPE for Greenhouse and Polyhouse Spraying

Spraying in enclosed structures has higher inhalation risk.

Extra precautions:

* Use respirator as label requires.
* Improve ventilation.
* Avoid entering immediately after spraying.
* Follow re-entry interval.
* Avoid spraying during high temperature.
* Use lower-risk products where possible.
* Keep workers out until safe.

---

# PPE for Orchard Spraying

Orchard spraying often causes more exposure because spray is directed upward and canopy may drip.

Use:

* Waterproof hat or hood
* Goggles
* Respirator or mask as required
* Gloves
* Coverall
* Boots
* Apron during mixing

Avoid standing under dripping branches.

---

# PPE for Vegetable Crops

Vegetable crops may require frequent spraying and harvesting.

Important:

* Follow waiting period.
* Use PPE during every spray.
* Keep spray records.
* Avoid spraying close to harvest.
* Wash PPE after each use.
* Avoid children harvesting soon after spray.

---

# PPE and Re-Entry Interval

Re-entry interval means the time after spraying before workers can enter the treated field safely without special protection.

Farmers should:

* Follow label re-entry interval.
* Keep workers and animals out.
* Place warning sign where possible.
* Avoid harvesting immediately after spray.
* Use PPE if emergency entry is required and label allows.

---

# PPE and Waiting Period

Waiting period is the minimum time between last pesticide application and harvest.

PPE protects workers, but waiting period protects consumers.

Both are important.

Farmers should:

* Record spray date.
* Know waiting period.
* Avoid harvest before safe interval.
* Inform harvesting workers.

---

# PPE and Pesticide Poisoning

PPE reduces poisoning risk but cannot prevent all exposure if used incorrectly.

Possible poisoning symptoms:

* Headache
* Dizziness
* Sweating
* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Skin irritation
* Eye burning
* Chest tightness
* Breathing difficulty
* Weakness
* Blurred vision
* Muscle twitching
* Confusion

Stop work immediately and seek medical help if symptoms occur.

Take pesticide label or container to the doctor.

---

# First Aid and PPE

If pesticide gets on clothing:

1. Remove contaminated clothing immediately.
2. Wash skin with soap and water.
3. Wear gloves while handling contaminated clothes.
4. Wash contaminated clothes separately.
5. Seek medical help if symptoms occur.

If pesticide gets in eyes:

1. Rinse eyes with clean water for several minutes.
2. Do not rub eyes.
3. Seek medical help.

If pesticide is inhaled:

1. Move person to fresh air.
2. Loosen tight clothing.
3. Seek medical help if symptoms continue.

If swallowed:

1. Do not induce vomiting unless doctor says.
2. Do not give anything to unconscious person.
3. Take label or container to hospital.

---

# Common PPE Mistakes

Farmers should avoid:

* Spraying barefoot
* Using cloth mask for all pesticide work
* Using cotton or leather gloves
* Wearing torn gloves
* Removing mask while spraying
* Touching phone or face with contaminated gloves
* Eating or drinking during spraying
* Washing pesticide clothes with family clothes
* Reusing pesticide containers
* Blowing blocked nozzles by mouth
* Storing PPE with pesticides without cleaning
* Entering field too soon after spraying
* Thinking low-smell pesticide is harmless
* Ignoring label PPE instructions

---

# Low-Cost PPE Improvement

If full professional PPE is not immediately available, farmers should still improve protection.

Minimum safer approach:

* Long-sleeved shirt
* Long trousers
* Rubber gloves
* Closed shoes or boots
* Eye protection
* Mask suitable for pesticide work
* Separate pesticide work clothes
* Soap and clean water
* Avoid spraying in wind or heat

However, minimum protection should not replace label-required PPE for more hazardous products.

# PPE Decision Guide

| Situation                     | Recommended PPE Action                                |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Mixing concentrated pesticide | Use gloves, goggles, apron, mask or respirator        |
| Spraying tall crop            | Use head cover, goggles, long clothing, gloves, boots |
| Spraying orchard              | Use stronger face and respiratory protection          |
| Handling powder pesticide     | Use mask or respirator and avoid inhaling dust        |
| Using herbicide               | Use gloves and avoid sprayer contamination            |
| Cleaning nozzle               | Use gloves; never blow by mouth                       |
| Washing sprayer               | Use gloves, boots, eye protection                     |
| PPE gets wet with pesticide   | Stop, remove safely, wash skin, change clothing       |
| Feeling dizzy during spray    | Stop immediately and seek fresh air and help          |
| Label requires respirator     | Do not spray without correct respirator               |

# PPE Usage Checklist

## Before Work

* Read pesticide label.
* Identify required PPE.
* Check PPE condition.
* Wear clean protective clothing.
* Keep soap and water ready.
* Keep children and animals away.
* Check sprayer for leaks.
* Avoid spraying in unsafe weather.

## During Work

* Wear PPE continuously.
* Avoid touching face.
* Do not eat, drink, or smoke.
* Stay upwind.
* Avoid spray drift.
* Stop if equipment leaks.
* Stop if feeling unwell.

## After Work

* Wash gloves and boots.
* Remove PPE carefully.
* Wash hands and body.
* Wash clothes separately.
* Clean sprayer safely.
* Store PPE after drying.
* Record spray details.

---

# Farmer Recommendations

Farmers are advised to:

1. Use PPE every time pesticides are handled.
2. Wear stronger PPE during mixing and loading.
3. Follow the PPE instructions written on the pesticide label.
4. Use chemical-resistant gloves, not cotton or leather gloves.
5. Protect eyes during mixing, loading, and overhead spraying.
6. Use correct mask or respirator according to pesticide risk.
7. Wash PPE and pesticide clothes separately after use.
8. Replace damaged PPE immediately.
9. Never reuse pesticide containers.
10. Seek medical help quickly if poisoning symptoms occur.

---

# Important Notes

* PPE reduces exposure but does not replace safe pesticide practices.
* Concentrated pesticide is more hazardous than diluted spray.
* PPE must fit properly and be maintained.
* Wrong PPE can give false confidence.
* Cloth masks are not enough for many pesticide operations.
* Gloves should be chemical-resistant and unlined.
* Pesticide label instructions must be followed.
* PPE should be removed carefully to avoid contamination.
* Children, pregnant women, elderly persons, and sick people should avoid pesticide handling.
* In suspected poisoning, take the pesticide label or container to the doctor.
