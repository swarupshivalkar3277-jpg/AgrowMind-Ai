# Pesticide Residue Management

## Overview

Pesticide residue management means using pesticides in a way that protects crops while keeping pesticide residues in harvested produce within legal and safe limits. Residue management is important for food safety, farmer income, consumer trust, export acceptance, and environmental protection.

Pesticide residues can remain on or inside crops after pesticide application. Residues may come from insecticides, fungicides, herbicides, acaricides, nematicides, plant growth regulators, fumigants, seed treatments, or post-harvest treatments.

Good residue management requires:

* Correct pesticide selection.
* Correct dose.
* Correct spray timing.
* Correct crop stage.
* Correct pre-harvest interval.
* Correct number of sprays.
* Correct spray method.
* Avoiding banned or unregistered pesticides.
* Avoiding unnecessary pesticide mixtures.
* Following label instructions.
* Maintaining spray records.
* Testing produce where required.

---

## 1. What Are Pesticide Residues?

Pesticide residues are small amounts of pesticide active ingredient, metabolites, breakdown products, or related substances that may remain in or on harvested crops, food, feed, soil, water, or processed products after pesticide use.

### Common Sources of Residues

* Foliar sprays.
* Soil application.
* Seed treatment.
* Fruit dipping.
* Post-harvest treatment.
* Fumigation.
* Spray drift from nearby fields.
* Contaminated irrigation water.
* Contaminated sprayer or tank.
* Repeated pesticide use.
* Wrong pesticide dose.
* Harvesting too soon after spray.

### Residues May Be Found On

* Fruits.
* Vegetables.
* Grains.
* Pulses.
* Oilseeds.
* Spices.
* Tea.
* Coffee.
* Cottonseed.
* Fodder.
* Animal feed.
* Milk, meat, and eggs if animals consume contaminated feed.

### Farmer Guidance

Residues are not only caused by spraying directly before harvest. They can also result from wrong dose, repeated sprays, drift, contaminated equipment, or use of pesticides not approved for the crop.

---

## 2. Why Residue Management Is Important

Residue management protects consumers, farmers, traders, and the environment.

### Importance for Farmers

* Avoids crop rejection in the market.
* Helps meet food-safety standards.
* Protects export opportunities.
* Reduces financial loss.
* Improves buyer confidence.
* Supports safe and responsible farming.
* Reduces risk of legal action.
* Improves long-term farm reputation.

### Importance for Consumers

* Reduces dietary exposure to pesticide residues.
* Improves food safety.
* Builds confidence in fresh produce.
* Protects vulnerable groups such as children, elderly people, and pregnant women.

### Importance for Export and Trade

* Export markets may have strict MRL requirements.
* Different countries may have different residue limits.
* Produce can be rejected if residues exceed the buyer or importing-country standard.
* Some buyers may require residue test reports.
* GlobalG.A.P., organic, and export supply chains require strict pesticide records.

## 3. Maximum Residue Limit

Maximum Residue Limit, or MRL, is the highest level of pesticide residue legally allowed in or on food or feed when pesticides are applied correctly according to Good Agricultural Practice.

MRLs are commonly expressed as:

* mg/kg.
* ppm, which means parts per million.

### Important Points About MRL

* MRL is a legal limit, not a recommended pesticide dose.
* MRL is crop-specific.
* MRL is pesticide-specific.
* MRL may differ between countries.
* MRL may differ between fresh and processed commodities.
* MRL depends on registered use pattern and residue data.
* Food with residues below MRL is generally considered compliant with the legal residue standard.
* Exceeding MRL can lead to market rejection.

### Example Explanation

If the MRL of a pesticide on tomato is 0.5 mg/kg, the harvested tomato should not contain pesticide residue above 0.5 mg/kg for that pesticide under the relevant regulation.

### Farmer Guidance

Farmers do not directly control MRL values, but they can manage residue levels by using the correct pesticide, correct dose, correct spray interval, and correct pre-harvest interval.

---

## 4. Difference Between MRL and Toxicity

MRL and toxicity are related to pesticide safety, but they are not the same thing.

### MRL

MRL is the legal maximum residue level allowed in food or feed.

### Toxicity

Toxicity describes how harmful a pesticide can be to humans, animals, beneficial insects, aquatic life, or the environment.

### Important Difference

A pesticide can be highly toxic but have residues below MRL if used correctly. A pesticide can also be less acutely toxic but still become a residue violation if used wrongly, too frequently, or too close to harvest.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not assume that a pesticide is safe just because the crop looks healthy. Residue safety depends on correct use and harvest timing.

---

## 5. Pre-Harvest Interval

Pre-Harvest Interval, or PHI, is the minimum time required between the last pesticide application and harvest.

### Why PHI Matters

PHI allows pesticide residues to decline before harvest. Residues may reduce due to plant growth, sunlight, rain, washing, metabolism, evaporation, microbial breakdown, and time.

### PHI Depends On

* Pesticide active ingredient.
* Crop.
* Dose.
* Formulation.
* Number of applications.
* Weather.
* Growth rate of crop.
* Harvested plant part.
* Local label and regulation.

### Example

If a pesticide label says PHI is 7 days for chilli, the farmer should wait at least 7 full days after the last spray before harvesting chilli.

### Farmer Guidance

Never harvest before completing the PHI. Washing cannot reliably replace the pre-harvest interval.

---

## 6. Re-Entry Interval

Re-Entry Interval, or REI, is the minimum time people should wait before entering a treated field without protective equipment.

### Why REI Matters

REI protects farmers, labourers, harvest workers, scouts, and family members from pesticide exposure.

### Common REI Activities

Avoid entering a treated field too soon for:

* Harvesting.
* Weeding.
* Irrigation work.
* Pruning.
* Training vines.
* Tying plants.
* Scouting.
* Fertilizer application.
* Intercultural operations.

### Farmer Guidance

REI protects worker health. PHI protects harvested food safety. Both are important.

---

## 7. Factors That Increase Pesticide Residues

Residue levels may increase when pesticides are misused or environmental conditions slow residue breakdown.

### Major Causes

* Spraying above recommended dose.
* Spraying too frequently.
* Harvesting before PHI.
* Using pesticide not registered for the crop.
* Using pesticide not recommended for the target pest.
* Applying late-season sprays close to harvest.
* Using repeated tank mixtures.
* Applying pesticide during wrong crop stage.
* Using contaminated sprayer.
* Spray overlap.
* Poor calibration.
* Applying during unsuitable weather.
* Using illegal or banned pesticides.
* Using low-quality or counterfeit pesticides.
* Reusing pesticide containers.
* Post-harvest pesticide misuse.
* Drift from neighboring fields.
* Irrigation water contamination.

### Farmer Guidance

Residue violation usually happens because of wrong pesticide choice, wrong dose, repeated spraying, or harvesting too soon after the final spray.

---

## 8. Factors That Reduce Residue Risk

Residue risk can be reduced by following Good Agricultural Practice.

### Best Practices

* Use only approved pesticides for the crop.
* Follow label dose.
* Follow label spray interval.
* Follow PHI.
* Avoid unnecessary sprays.
* Use pest monitoring before spraying.
* Use economic threshold levels where available.
* Use non-chemical pest management where possible.
* Rotate pesticides by mode of action.
* Use correct sprayer calibration.
* Avoid spray overlap.
* Avoid drift.
* Use clean water.
* Clean sprayer after use.
* Maintain spray records.
* Test produce when required.
* Follow buyer or export-market residue standards.

---

## 9. Good Agricultural Practice for Residue Management

Good Agricultural Practice, or GAP, means using pesticides and crop-management methods correctly to produce safe food while protecting workers, consumers, and the environment.

### GAP Includes

* Correct pest diagnosis.
* Correct product selection.
* Correct dose.
* Correct dilution.
* Correct spray volume.
* Correct nozzle.
* Correct timing.
* Correct application method.
* Correct PHI.
* Correct REI.
* Proper storage.
* Proper disposal.
* Proper recordkeeping.
* Avoiding illegal pesticides.
* Integrated Pest Management.

### Farmer Guidance

Residue management is not one step. It is the result of safe decisions from pest diagnosis to harvest.

---

## 10. Integrated Pest Management and Residue Reduction

Integrated Pest Management, or IPM, reduces unnecessary pesticide use by combining chemical and non-chemical methods.

### IPM Practices That Reduce Residue Risk

* Field scouting.
* Pest monitoring.
* Use of economic threshold levels.
* Resistant varieties.
* Crop rotation.
* Trap crops.
* Pheromone traps.
* Sticky traps.
* Light traps where suitable.
* Biological control agents.
* Neem-based products where suitable.
* Removal of infected or infested plant parts.
* Proper spacing.
* Balanced nutrition.
* Irrigation management.
* Sanitation.
* Mechanical control.
* Need-based pesticide application.

### Farmer Guidance

Less unnecessary spraying means lower residue risk, lower cost, and slower pesticide resistance development.

# Pesticide Selection for Low Residue Risk

## 11. Use Crop-Approved Pesticides Only

A pesticide should be used only on crops where it is registered or officially recommended.

### Why Crop Approval Matters

* Residue trials are crop-specific.
* Crop metabolism differs between crops.
* PHI differs between crops.
* MRL differs between crops.
* A safe use in one crop may be illegal or unsafe in another crop.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not use a pesticide on a crop just because it worked on another crop. Always check label crop approval.

---

## 12. Avoid Banned or Restricted Pesticides

Banned, restricted, or unapproved pesticides can cause serious residue violations and market rejection.

### Risks

* Food-safety violation.
* Export rejection.
* Legal penalty.
* Consumer health risk.
* Soil and water contamination.
* Harm to beneficial organisms.
* Loss of buyer trust.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not buy pesticides without proper label, bill, batch number, manufacturing date, and registration details.

---

## 13. Avoid Last-Minute Sprays Before Harvest

Spraying close to harvest is one of the biggest causes of residue violations.

### High-Risk Situations

* Fruit borer attack near harvest.
* Disease outbreak near harvest.
* Market pressure to harvest quickly.
* Repeated buyer demand for clean-looking produce.
* Labour or transport schedule forcing early harvest.
* Wrong belief that washing removes all residues.

### Safer Alternatives Near Harvest

* Use non-chemical control where practical.
* Remove damaged produce.
* Use physical control methods.
* Harvest only after PHI.
* Consult local expert for low-residue or short-PHI options.
* Use biological products only if approved and safe for the crop.

### Farmer Guidance

If harvest is near, do not spray without checking PHI. A last-minute spray may save crop appearance but cause market rejection.

---

## 14. Pesticide Dose and Residues

Correct dose is essential for residue safety and pest control.

### Overdosing Can Cause

* High residues.
* Crop injury.
* Pest resistance.
* Higher cost.
* Environmental contamination.
* Beneficial insect mortality.
* Market rejection.

### Underdosing Can Cause

* Poor pest control.
* Repeat spraying.
* Resistance development.
* Unpredictable residues.
* Higher long-term pesticide use.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not increase dose to get “stronger” control. Use recommended dose and correct spray coverage.

---

## 15. Number of Sprays and Residue Build-Up

Repeated pesticide applications can increase residue risk, especially when sprays are close together or close to harvest.

### Risk Factors

* Multiple sprays of same pesticide.
* Short interval between sprays.
* Late crop-stage spraying.
* Tank mixtures of many pesticides.
* Long-residual pesticides.
* Poor recordkeeping.

### Farmer Guidance

Before spraying again, check when the previous spray was done, what product was used, and whether another spray is really needed.

---

# Harvest and Post-Harvest Residue Management

## 16. Harvest Timing

Harvest timing is one of the most important residue-control steps.

### Safe Harvest Checklist

* Was pesticide applied recently?
* What was the last pesticide used?
* What is the PHI?
* Has the PHI fully passed?
* Was the correct dose used?
* Was the crop sprayed directly?
* Was there drift from another field?
* Is the produce for local or export market?
* Does buyer require residue testing?

### Farmer Guidance

Keep a “last spray date” visible in farm records. Never harvest a plot without checking the last spray date and PHI.

---

## 17. Washing and Residue Reduction

Washing can remove dust, soil, and some surface residues, but it cannot reliably remove all pesticide residues.

### Washing May Help Remove

* Soil particles.
* Dust.
* Surface residues.
* Some water-soluble residues.
* Loose spray deposits.

### Washing May Not Remove

* Systemic pesticide residues inside plant tissue.
* Wax-bound residues.
* Residues absorbed into fruit skin.
* Residues from repeated sprays.
* Residues above legal limits.

### Farmer Guidance

Washing is good hygiene but not a substitute for correct pesticide use and PHI.

---

## 18. Peeling, Cooking, and Processing

Processing may reduce some pesticide residues, but the effect depends on pesticide chemistry, crop type, and processing method.

### Processing Methods

* Peeling.
* Washing.
* Blanching.
* Cooking.
* Milling.
* Polishing.
* Drying.
* Juicing.
* Fermentation.
* Oil extraction.
* Pickling.

### Important Note

Processing does not guarantee residue compliance. Some residues may concentrate during drying, oil extraction, or processing.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not depend on cooking or processing to correct residue misuse. Produce should be safe before it enters the market.

---

## 19. Residues in Dried Crops and Spices

Dried commodities may show higher residue concentration because water is removed during drying.

### Examples

* Chilli.
* Turmeric.
* Coriander.
* Cumin.
* Tea.
* Dried fruits.
* Medicinal plants.
* Dehydrated vegetables.

### Why Risk Is Higher

* Water loss concentrates residues.
* Multiple sprays may be used before harvest.
* Drying surface may be contaminated.
* Post-harvest treatment may be misused.
* Export markets may have strict limits.

### Farmer Guidance

For spices and dried crops, residue planning must start before harvest. Follow buyer-specific approved pesticide lists and PHI.

---

## 20. Residues in Fodder and Animal Products

Pesticide residues can enter livestock through contaminated fodder, crop residues, or feed.

### Possible Animal Product Concerns

* Milk.
* Meat.
* Eggs.
* Honey.
* Fish.
* Animal fat.
* Feed products.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not feed animals crop residues from recently sprayed fields unless label and local guidance allow it. Follow grazing and fodder restrictions.

# Export and Market Compliance

## 21. Domestic Market Compliance

Produce sold in the domestic market must comply with national food-safety regulations and pesticide residue limits.

### Farmer Guidance

Use pesticides approved for the crop and follow national residue standards. In India, food safety regulations specify pesticide residue limits for food commodities, and FSSAI updates contaminant and residue regulations periodically.

---

## 22. Export Market Compliance

Export markets may have MRLs that differ from domestic standards.

### Export Risk Factors

* Importing country has lower MRL.
* Pesticide not approved in importing country.
* Buyer has stricter private standards.
* Organic or residue-free claim requirements.
* Lack of farm spray records.
* Mixed produce from different farms.
* Drift contamination.
* Post-harvest contamination.
* No residue test report.

### Export Farmer Guidance

Before spraying export crops, check:

* Pesticide approved by buyer.
* Pesticide approved by importing country.
* MRL of importing country.
* PHI required by buyer.
* Number of sprays allowed.
* Residue test requirement.
* Prohibited pesticide list.

---

## 23. Buyer-Specific Residue Standards

Some buyers and exporters set stricter residue standards than government MRLs.

### Examples

* Supermarket standards.
* Exporter approved pesticide list.
* Organic certification.
* Residue-free produce standards.
* Baby food standards.
* Processing industry standards.
* Contract farming pesticide schedules.

### Farmer Guidance

For contract farming or export crops, follow buyer pesticide schedule, not only the local pesticide shop recommendation.

# Spray Drift and Cross-Contamination

## 24. Spray Drift Residues

Spray drift occurs when pesticide droplets move away from the target area to nearby crops, water bodies, homes, animals, or non-target plants.

### Drift Can Cause

* Residues on nearby crops.
* Crop injury.
* Organic crop contamination.
* Export rejection.
* Water contamination.
* Human exposure.
* Bee and beneficial insect mortality.

### Drift Prevention

* Do not spray during strong wind.
* Use correct nozzle.
* Use low-drift nozzle where appropriate.
* Maintain correct boom height.
* Avoid high pressure that creates fine droplets.
* Use buffer zones.
* Use shielded spraying.
* Avoid spraying near harvest-ready crops.
* Inform neighboring farmers where needed.

### Farmer Guidance

A crop can fail residue testing even if that farmer did not spray it, because drift from nearby fields can contaminate produce.

---

## 25. Sprayer Contamination

Residues can occur when a sprayer is not cleaned properly after pesticide use.

### Common Problem

A sprayer used for herbicide may later be used for vegetables or fruits, causing crop injury or residue contamination.

### Prevention

* Use separate sprayers for herbicides where possible.
* Clean tank, nozzle, hose, and filter after use.
* Rinse multiple times.
* Dispose wash water safely.
* Do not wash sprayer near wells, ponds, or canals.
* Label sprayers by use type.

### Farmer Guidance

A contaminated sprayer can damage crops and create illegal residues even if the correct pesticide is used later.

---

## 26. Pesticide Storage and Residue Risk

Poor storage can lead to misuse, contamination, and accidental residue problems.

### Safe Storage Practices

* Store pesticides in original containers.
* Keep labels intact.
* Store in a locked place.
* Keep away from food, feed, seed, medicines, and household items.
* Separate herbicides from insecticides and fungicides.
* Do not store leaking containers.
* Keep away from children and animals.
* Check expiry date before use.
* Do not transfer pesticide into drink bottles or food containers.

### Farmer Guidance

Unlabeled pesticide containers are dangerous. Never use a pesticide if the label is missing or unreadable.

## 29. Residue Testing

Residue testing checks whether produce contains pesticide residues and whether they are within allowed limits.

### When Testing May Be Needed

* Export shipment.
* Contract farming.
* Supermarket supply.
* Spices and dried commodity trade.
* Organic certification.
* High-risk crops.
* Suspected misuse.
* Buyer requirement.
* Government monitoring.

### Sample Collection Tips

* Collect representative samples.
* Avoid contamination during sampling.
* Use clean bags or containers.
* Label samples correctly.
* Keep sample chain of custody.
* Send to accredited laboratory where required.

### Farmer Guidance

Residue test results are only useful if the sample is representative and collected properly.

# Special Residue Terms

## 36. Acceptable Daily Intake

Acceptable Daily Intake, or ADI, is an estimate of the amount of a pesticide that can be consumed daily over a lifetime without appreciable health risk, based on scientific evaluation.

### Farmer Guidance

Farmers do not calculate ADI. Regulators use ADI when setting food-safety standards and assessing consumer exposure.

---

## 37. Acute Reference Dose

Acute Reference Dose, or ARfD, is an estimate of the amount of a pesticide that can be consumed in a short period, usually one meal or one day, without appreciable health risk.

### Farmer Guidance

ARfD is important for pesticides that may cause short-term health concerns if residues are high.

---

## 38. Limit of Quantification

Limit of Quantification, or LOQ, is the lowest concentration of a pesticide residue that a laboratory method can reliably measure.

### Farmer Guidance

If a pesticide is not approved for a crop or market, even a very small detectable residue may cause rejection.

---

## 39. Default MRL

Default MRL is a general legal limit applied when no specific MRL exists for a pesticide-crop combination in a regulation or market.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not assume no MRL means free use. It may mean the limit is very strict.

## 42. If PHI Has Not Passed

If the pre-harvest interval has not passed, the crop should not be harvested for sale.

### Recommended Action

* Delay harvest until PHI is complete.
* Inform buyer if harvest must be rescheduled.
* Keep sprayed plot separate.
* Do not mix with unsprayed or safe-harvest produce.
* Consult local agricultural expert if crop is perishable.
* Consider residue testing if market risk is high.

### Farmer Guidance

Do not mix high-risk produce with safe produce. Mixing can contaminate the whole lot.

---

## 43. If Wrong Pesticide Was Used

If a pesticide not approved for the crop was used, residue risk can be serious.

### Recommended Action

* Stop harvest from that plot.
* Record product, dose, date, and crop stage.
* Contact local agriculture officer or residue expert.
* Check whether the pesticide has any MRL for that crop and market.
* Consider residue testing.
* Keep produce separate.
* Do not sell as export or premium residue-controlled produce unless cleared.

### Farmer Guidance

Using a pesticide not approved for the crop may create residue violations even if PHI is followed.

---

## 44. If Dose Was Too High

If overdose happened, normal PHI may not be enough.

### Recommended Action

* Do not harvest immediately.
* Record actual dose used.
* Contact expert.
* Extend harvest interval where advised.
* Conduct residue testing for high-value or export crops.
* Keep produce from treated plot separate.

### Farmer Guidance

PHI is based on label dose. If dose is exceeded, residue decline may not match label expectations.

---

## 45. If Spray Drift Occurred

If pesticide drift reached another crop, especially near harvest, treat that crop as residue-risk produce.

### Recommended Action

* Identify pesticide that drifted.
* Estimate date and exposure.
* Record affected area.
* Avoid harvesting affected produce until expert advice.
* Consider residue testing.
* Inform affected farmer or buyer where required.
* Prevent future drift by changing spray practice.

### Farmer Guidance

Drift contamination can create residue violation even without direct spraying.

---

# Common Mistakes in Residue Management

## 46. Mistakes Farmers Should Avoid

* Spraying without pest diagnosis.
* Using pesticide-shop advice without checking crop label.
* Using “cocktail” mixtures of many pesticides.
* Harvesting too soon after spray.
* Ignoring PHI.
* Using the same sprayer for herbicides and vegetables without cleaning.
* Spraying during high wind.
* Using unregistered pesticides.
* Using expired pesticides.
* Increasing dose for faster pest control.
* Spraying just before sending produce to market.
* Mixing produce from sprayed and unsprayed plots.
* Not keeping spray records.
* Ignoring export-market MRLs.
* Feeding recently sprayed crop residues to animals.

## 47. Safer Alternatives to Reduce Residue Risk

### Non-Chemical and Low-Residue Methods

* Pest monitoring.
* Resistant varieties.
* Net houses.
* Sticky traps.
* Pheromone traps.
* Light traps where suitable.
* Biological control agents.
* Neem-based products where appropriate and approved.
* Mechanical removal of pests.
* Removal of diseased plant parts.
* Field sanitation.
* Crop rotation.
* Balanced fertilizer.
* Proper irrigation.
* Mulching.
* Soil solarization.
* Grafting in some vegetable crops.
* Bagging fruits.
* Pruning for air circulation.
* Timely harvesting of damaged fruits.

### Farmer Guidance

Near harvest, non-chemical methods are often safer than emergency pesticide sprays.

# Residue-Safe Communication for Advisory Systems

## 48. Safe Advisory Rules for AI or RAG Systems

A crop advisory system should avoid giving unsafe pesticide advice.

### AI Should Always Ask or Consider

* Crop name.
* Pest or disease name.
* Crop stage.
* Days to harvest.
* Last pesticide used.
* Last spray date.
* Country or market.
* Whether crop is for export.
* Whether harvesting is continuous.
* Whether pesticide is registered for that crop.
* PHI and REI.

### AI Should Avoid

* Recommending banned pesticides.
* Recommending pesticide without crop and pest context.
* Suggesting higher-than-label dose.
* Suggesting harvest before PHI.
* Saying washing removes all residues.
* Ignoring export MRL differences.
* Recommending pesticide cocktails without label support.
* Giving advice that replaces local regulation.

### Safe AI Response Pattern

If residue risk is possible, the AI should say:

“Check the product label for crop approval, dose, PHI, and REI. Do not harvest until the PHI is complete. For export or residue-sensitive markets, confirm the pesticide is allowed by the buyer and consider residue testing.”

# Farmer Questions and Answers

## Q1. What is pesticide residue?

Pesticide residue is the small amount of pesticide or its breakdown product that may remain in or on harvested produce, food, feed, soil, or water after pesticide use.

## Q2. What is MRL?

MRL means Maximum Residue Limit. It is the highest pesticide residue level legally allowed in or on food or feed when the pesticide is used correctly.

## Q3. What is PHI?

PHI means Pre-Harvest Interval. It is the minimum time between the last pesticide application and harvest.

## Q4. What is REI?

REI means Re-Entry Interval. It is the minimum time people should wait before entering a sprayed field without protective equipment.

## Q5. Can washing remove pesticide residues?

Washing can remove some surface residues, dust, and soil, but it cannot reliably remove all pesticide residues. It cannot replace correct pesticide use and PHI.

## Q6. Can cooking remove pesticide residues?

Cooking may reduce some residues, but it does not guarantee residue compliance. Some residues may remain, and some may concentrate during drying or processing.

## Q7. Why does produce fail residue testing?

Produce may fail due to wrong pesticide, high dose, repeated sprays, harvesting before PHI, drift, contaminated sprayer, unapproved pesticide use, or strict export MRLs.

## Q8. What should a farmer do before spraying near harvest?

Check days to harvest, pesticide label, PHI, crop approval, buyer restrictions, and safer alternatives. Do not spray if harvest will happen before PHI is complete.

## Q9. Are export MRLs the same as local MRLs?

No. MRLs can differ between countries and buyers. Export crops must meet the importing-country and buyer standards.

## Q10. What should be recorded after spraying?

Record crop, field, pesticide name, active ingredient, dose, water volume, spray date, pest, applicator, PHI, safe harvest date, and actual harvest date.

## Q11. Can pesticide drift cause residue problems?

Yes. Drift from nearby fields can leave residues on unsprayed crops and may cause residue test failure.

## Q12. Is MRL the same as pesticide dose?

No. Dose is the amount of pesticide applied in the field. MRL is the legal residue limit allowed in harvested produce.

## Q13. What happens if crop is harvested before PHI?

Residues may exceed legal limits. The crop may be unsafe, rejected by buyers, or fail residue testing.

## Q14. What should a farmer do if wrong pesticide was sprayed?

Stop harvest from that plot, record details, keep produce separate, contact an expert, and consider residue testing.

## Q15. Why are leafy vegetables high risk for residues?

Leafy vegetables are directly sprayed, eaten fresh, and harvested quickly. This gives less time for residues to decline.

## Q16. Why are spices high risk for residues?

Spices are often dried, and drying can concentrate residues. Export markets may also have strict residue standards.

## Q17. Can treated seed be eaten?

No. Treated seed must not be used for food, feed, or oil extraction.

## Q18. Can animals eat crop residues from recently sprayed fields?

Only if the pesticide label and local guidance allow it. Some pesticides have grazing and fodder restrictions.

## Q19. What is default MRL?

Default MRL is a general legal limit used when a specific pesticide-crop MRL is not fixed. It can be very strict.

## Q20. How can farmers reduce residue risk?

Use approved pesticides, follow label dose, follow PHI, avoid unnecessary sprays, prevent drift, clean sprayers, keep records, and use IPM.
