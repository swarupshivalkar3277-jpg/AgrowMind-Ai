# Fusarium Wilt in Tomato

## Overview

Fusarium wilt is a serious fungal disease affecting tomato crops worldwide. The disease attacks the vascular system of the plant, blocking water and nutrient movement, which results in wilting, yellowing, and eventual plant death.

The disease is soil-borne and can survive in soil for many years, making management difficult once fields become infected.

Fusarium wilt can cause:

* Severe wilting
* Stunted growth
* Reduced fruit production
* Premature plant death
* Significant economic losses

# Distribution

Fusarium wilt occurs widely in:

* Tropical regions
* Subtropical climates
* Temperate tomato-growing areas

The disease is especially common in:

* Warm soil conditions
* Continuous tomato cultivation areas
* Poorly managed fields

---

# Symptoms of Fusarium Wilt

The disease mainly affects the vascular tissues of tomato plants.

---

# Early Symptoms

* Yellowing of lower leaves
* Wilting during daytime
* Partial recovery at night
* One-sided leaf yellowing in some plants

---

# Advanced Symptoms

* Permanent wilting
* Progressive leaf yellowing
* Drying of leaves
* Stunted plant growth
* Plant death

Symptoms often begin on one side of the plant before spreading.

---

# Stem Symptoms

When the stem is cut open:

* Brown discoloration of vascular tissues is visible

This is an important diagnostic symptom.

---

# Root Symptoms

Roots may show:

* Browning
* Reduced growth
* Decay in severe infection

---

# Disease Cycle

The fungus survives in:

* Soil
* Crop residues
* Infected plant material
* Alternative host plants

The pathogen enters through:

* Root tips
* Root wounds
* Damaged roots

Once inside, the fungus spreads through vascular tissues.

---

# Spread of the Disease

The disease spreads through:

* Contaminated soil
* Irrigation water
* Infected seedlings
* Farm tools
* Human activities

Movement of infected soil can spread the pathogen between fields.

---

# Favorable Conditions for Disease Development

Fusarium wilt develops rapidly under:

* Warm soil temperatures
* Poor drainage
* Acidic soil conditions
* Continuous tomato cultivation
* Root injury

Stress conditions increase disease severity.

---

# Economic Importance

Severe infection may cause:

* Reduced plant population
* Lower fruit production
* Poor plant vigor
* Major yield losses
* Economic losses to farmers

Highly infected fields may become unsuitable for tomato cultivation.

---

# Integrated Disease Management (IDM)

Integrated management is essential because the fungus survives in soil for long periods.

---

# Cultural Management

## Crop Rotation

Rotate tomato with non-host crops such as:

* Cereals
* Maize
* Grasses

Avoid repeated tomato cultivation in infected fields.

---

## Use Healthy Seedlings

Always use:

* Disease-free seedlings
* Certified planting material
* Healthy nursery plants

---

## Field Sanitation

Important practices include:

* Removal of infected plants
* Destruction of crop residues
* Weed management

Sanitation helps reduce fungal survival.

---

## Proper Drainage

Good drainage is essential.

Waterlogging increases:

* Root stress
* Disease severity

Raised beds improve drainage conditions.

---

# Soil Management

Healthy soil reduces disease pressure.

## Recommended Practices

* Addition of organic matter
* Soil solarization
* Balanced fertilization
* Soil pH management

Organic matter supports beneficial microorganisms.

---

# Resistant Varieties

Resistant tomato varieties are highly effective against fusarium wilt.

Benefits include:

* Reduced disease incidence
* Better crop survival
* Improved productivity

---

# Biological Management

Biological control agents may suppress the pathogen.

## Common Biological Approaches

* Beneficial fungi
* Beneficial bacteria
* Bio-control agents

These are useful in integrated disease management systems.

---

# Chemical Management

Chemical control alone is usually not sufficient because the pathogen survives in soil.

Soil treatments and fungicides may provide limited support under integrated management.

Applications should follow agricultural recommendations.

---

# Nursery Management

Healthy nursery management helps reduce disease introduction.

## Recommended Practices

* Use sterilized nursery soil
* Avoid excess irrigation
* Maintain nursery hygiene
* Remove infected seedlings immediately

---

# Monitoring and Early Detection

Regular monitoring is important.

Farmers should inspect for:

* Yellowing leaves
* Wilting plants
* Vascular browning
* Stunted growth

Early removal reduces disease spread.

---

# Preventive Measures

Farmers should:

1. Use resistant varieties where available.
2. Practice crop rotation.
3. Maintain proper drainage.
4. Remove infected plants immediately.
5. Avoid root injury during cultivation.
6. Improve soil health regularly.

---

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

Farmers should avoid:

* Continuous tomato cultivation
* Poor drainage
* Excess irrigation
* Use of infected seedlings
* Root damage during field operations

---

# Sustainable Disease Management

Sustainable management includes:

* Crop rotation
* Soil health improvement
* Organic matter application
* Biological control
* Integrated disease management

---

# Farmer Recommendations

Farmers are advised to:

* Inspect fields regularly.
* Remove infected plants promptly.
* Maintain healthy soil conditions.
* Use resistant varieties whenever possible.
* Follow integrated disease management practices.

---

Severely infected plants may show:

* Extensive wilting
* Severe yellowing
* Drying and death of plants
* Reduced fruit production
* Large infected patches in fields
